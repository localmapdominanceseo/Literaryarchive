Modernism

Modernism in poetry, spanning roughly the 1900s through the 1940s internationally, broke decisively from Victorian convention in favor of fragmented form, difficult allusion, and a sense that traditional certainties about history, religion, and society could no longer be taken for granted. W.B. Yeats, alongside figures like T.S. Eliot and Ezra Pound, helped define the movement's preoccupation with civilizational crisis and renewal.
