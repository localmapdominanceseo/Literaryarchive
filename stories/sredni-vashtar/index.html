Sredni Vashtar

Saki (H.H. Munro) · Written 1911, Published 1911 · ~2,100 words

Published in 1911 in The Chronicles of Clovis, 'Sredni Vashtar' is Saki's darkest story. A sickly boy named Conradin keeps a large ferret he worships as a god — Sredni Vashtar, the Ferret. His guardian Mrs. De Ropp takes the ferret away. Conradin prays. Mrs. De Ropp goes to the shed. The ferret comes out alone.

Quick Summary

'Sredni Vashtar' is a story by Saki, published in 1911. Sickly ten-year-old Conradin worships a polecat-ferret named Sredni Vashtar as a dark god, making sacrifices to it in secret. His guardian Mrs. De Ropp discovers the ferret and goes to remove it. Conradin watches the shed and prays. A long time passes. The ferret emerges with a dark wet stain around its mouth. Conradin makes himself some toast.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Close third-person narration — the story is told with Saki's characteristic lightness of touch even as it approaches horror.

Themes

The child's dark fantasy of revenge against adult oppression — Sredni Vashtar is the externalization of Conradin's suppressed rage.

Historical Context

Published in The Chronicles of Clovis (1911).

Opening Extract

Conradin was ten years old, and the doctor had pronounced his professional opinion that the boy would not live another five years. The doctor was silky and effete, and counted for little, but his opinion was endorsed by Mrs. De Ropp, who counted for nearly everything. Mrs. De Ropp was Conradin's cousin and guardian, and in his eyes she represented those three-fifths of the world that are necessary and disagreeable and real.

Public domain. Published in The Chronicles of Clovis, 1911.

About Saki (H.H. Munro)

Hector Hugh Munro, known as Saki, was born on December 18, 1870, in Akyab, Burma. His wickedly comic short stories — 'The Open Window,' 'Tobermory,' 'Sredni Vashtar' — are among the finest in English. He enlisted as a private at the outbreak of World War I despite being over-age and was killed by a sniper near Beaumont-Hamel in 1916.
