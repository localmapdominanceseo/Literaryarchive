The Open Boat

Stephen Crane · Written 1897, Published 1897 · ~11,000 words

Published in 1897 in Scribner's Magazine, 'The Open Boat' is based on Stephen Crane's own experience of surviving a shipwreck off the Florida coast in January 1897. Four men — a cook, an oiler, a correspondent, and the captain — spend thirty hours in a small lifeboat in rough seas, struggling toward shore. The sea is indifferent to their suffering.

Quick Summary

'The Open Boat' is a story by Stephen Crane, published in 1897. Four men survive a shipwreck and spend thirty hours in a small lifeboat trying to reach the Florida shore. The sea is indifferent. They rage against the universe's indifference. When they finally make shore, the oiler — the strongest of them, who has rowed without rest — drowns in the surf. The story ends with the survivors standing on the beach, 'interpreters' of what they have been through.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Close third-person narration that shifts between the collective 'they' and the individual 'the correspondent' — a naturalist masterwork in which the narrator's voice is both inside and outside the experience.

Themes

Naturalism and the indifference of the universe — the sea cares nothing for the men's struggles, the tower on shore cannot see them, nature is sublime and merciless. Human solidarity (the brotherhood of the boat) is the only answer to cosmic indifference.

Historical Context

Published in Scribner's Magazine, June 1897. Based on Crane's own experience when the SS Commodore sank on January 2, 1897. Crane was a correspondent for newspapers covering the Cuban insurrection.

Key Passage

When it occurs to a man that nature does not regard him as important, and that she feels she would not maim the universe by disposing of him, he at first wishes to throw bricks at the temple, and he hates deeply the fact that there are no bricks and no temples.

Extract

None of them knew the color of the sky. Their eyes glanced level, and were fastened upon the waves that swept toward them. These waves were of the hue of slate, save for the tops, which were of foaming white, and all of the men knew the colors of the sea. The horizon narrowed and widened, and dipped and rose, and at all times its edge was jagged with waves that seemed thrust up in points like rocks.

Many a man ought to have a bath-tub larger than the boat which here rode upon the sea. These waves were most wrongfully and barbarously abrupt and tall, and each froth-top was a problem in small boat navigation.

The cook squatted in the bottom and looked with both eyes at the six inches of gunwale which separated him from the ocean. His sleeves were rolled over his fat forearms, and the two flaps of his unbuttoned vest dangled as he bent to bail out the boat.

Public domain. First published in Scribner's Magazine, June 1897.

About Stephen Crane

Stephen Crane was born on November 1, 1871, in Newark, New Jersey. He produced some of the finest American naturalist writing before dying of tuberculosis at twenty-eight: The Red Badge of Courage (1895), Maggie: A Girl of the Streets (1893), and 'The Open Boat' (1897). He died in Badenweiler, Germany, in 1900.
