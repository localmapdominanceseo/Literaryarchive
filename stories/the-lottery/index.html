The Lottery

Shirley Jackson · Written 1948, Published 1948 · ~3,400 words

Published in The New Yorker on June 26, 1948, 'The Lottery' describes a small American village conducting its annual lottery on a bright summer morning. The story's shocking ending — the winner is stoned to death by their neighbors — caused more mail to The New Yorker than any piece in its history up to that point.

Quick Summary

'The Lottery' is a short story by Shirley Jackson, published in 1948. A small village holds its annual lottery on a sunny summer morning. The townspeople draw slips of paper; the Hutchinson family draws the marked slip. Tessie Hutchinson protests the result as unfair. Her neighbors — including her own children — stone her to death. The story is a study in the blind continuation of tradition and mob violence.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Precise, flat, documentary narration — the horror of the ending is heightened by the story's calm, matter-of-fact prose throughout.

Themes

The story attacks the danger of tradition maintained without question — the villagers continue the lottery because they always have, though no one can remember why. It explores scapegoating, mob violence, and the way ordinary people participate in cruelty when it is institutionalized.

Historical Context

Published June 28, 1948. The New Yorker received hundreds of letters, mostly angry or baffled. Jackson said she was thinking about 'the pointless violence and cruelty of society.' The story is now one of the most taught in American secondary schools.

Key Passage

'It isn't fair, it isn't right,' Mrs. Hutchinson screamed, and then they were upon her.

Characters

Tessie Hutchinson

The story's nominal protagonist, though she is not introduced until the lottery is already underway. Her late arrival and her initial joking attitude — 'Wouldn't have me leave m'dishes in the sink, now, would you, Joe?' — establish her as a participant who does not question the lottery until it threatens her directly. Her final cry — 'It isn't fair, it isn't right' — is not a critique of the lottery itself but of the result for her specifically.

Old Man Warner

The oldest man in the village, who has participated in seventy-seven lotteries. He is the voice of tradition, dismissing talk of villages that have given up the lottery as 'young fools.' His longevity is a dark irony — the lottery has never killed him, and he defends it entirely on the basis that it always has been done.

Mr. Summers

The lottery's administrator, who also runs the coal company and organizes civic events. His name — Summers — and his cheerful efficiency emphasize the story's central horror: that ordinary, pleasant community life contains this ritual at its center.

Symbols & Motifs

The Black Box

The battered, faded black box from which slips are drawn is described as older than Old Man Warner himself and made partly from the wood of the original box. It symbolizes tradition itself — deteriorating, arbitrary, kept only because it has always been kept, its original meaning entirely forgotten.

The Stones

The boys collecting stones at the start of the story — which readers initially read as innocent childhood play — are in fact preparing the instruments of execution. The detail is one of Jackson's most disturbing structural choices.

The Slips of Paper

The lottery tickets — which determine who dies — are described as plain white slips of paper, modern replacements for the original chips of wood. The modernization of the instrument combined with the continuation of the barbaric outcome underscores the story's argument about unreflective tradition.

Opening Extract

The morning of June 27th was clear and sunny, with the fresh warmth of a full-summer day; the flowers were blossoming profusely and the grass was richly green. The people of the village began to gather in the square, between the post office and the bank, around ten o'clock; in some towns there were so many people that the lottery took two days and had to be started on June 26th, but in this village, where there were only about three hundred people, the whole lottery took less than two hours, so it could begin at ten o'clock in the morning and still be through in time to allow the villagers to get home for noon dinner.

The children assembled first, of course. School was recently over for the summer, and the feeling of liberty sat uneasily on most of them; they tended to gather together quietly for a while before they broke into boisterous play, and their talk was still of the classroom and the teacher, of books and reprimands. Bobby Martin had already stuffed his pockets full of stones, and the other boys soon followed his example, selecting the smoothest and roundest stones; Bobby and Harry Jones and Dickie Delacroix—the villagers pronounced this name 'Dellacroy'—eventually made a great pile of stones in one corner of the square and guarded it against the raids of the other boys.

Legacy & Influence

Published in The New Yorker on June 26, 1948, 'The Lottery' generated more reader mail than any story the magazine had published. Most letters were angry, baffled, or disgusted; many cancelled their subscriptions. Shirley Jackson received hundreds of personal letters asking where the lottery was held and whether they could attend to watch. The story has been censored and banned repeatedly while also becoming one of the most widely taught stories in American secondary schools. It established Jackson's reputation and defined a genre of American dark fiction.

Study Questions

  1. How does Jackson use the setting and tone of the opening paragraphs to set up the story's shock?
  2. What does Tessie Hutchinson's complaint — 'It isn't fair' — reveal about the nature of her objection?
  3. How does Old Man Warner function as a representative of unreflective tradition?
  4. What is the story's argument about the relationship between community and violence?
  5. What American traditions or social practices might Jackson have had in mind?

About Shirley Jackson

Shirley Jackson was born on December 14, 1916, in San Francisco. Her story 'The Lottery' (1948) provoked more reader mail than any piece The New Yorker had published. Her novel The Haunting of Hill House (1959) is considered one of the finest haunted house stories ever written. She died in North Bennington, Vermont, in 1965.
