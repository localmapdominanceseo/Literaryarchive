Harrison Bergeron

Kurt Vonnegut · Written 1961, Published 1961 · ~2,500 words

Published in 1961 in The Magazine of Fantasy and Science Fiction, 'Harrison Bergeron' is Kurt Vonnegut's satirical story set in 2081, when the government enforces absolute equality by handicapping the strong, the intelligent, and the beautiful. Harrison Bergeron, a fourteen-year-old of exceptional ability, escapes from prison and appears on live television.

Quick Summary

'Harrison Bergeron' is a story by Kurt Vonnegut, published in 1961. In 2081, the Handicapper General enforces equality by weighing down strong people, blaring noises in the ears of intelligent people, and masking beautiful people. Harrison Bergeron, enormous and brilliant, escapes prison and appears on live TV, tears off his handicaps and those of a ballerina, and they dance magnificently. The Handicapper General shoots them both dead. His parents watch it on TV and then forget what upset them.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Satirical third-person narration with a deliberately flat, numbed quality — the Bergerons' incapacity for sustained thought is enacted in the prose itself.

Themes

The story is a satire of forced equality — arguing that equality imposed by handicapping excellence produces not justice but the suppression of human greatness and the numbing of consciousness. It is also a satire of television's sedative effect.

Historical Context

Published in 1961. Collected in Welcome to the Monkey House (1968). One of the most widely taught American short stories.

Key Passage

'I am the Emperor!' cried Harrison. 'Do you hear? I am the Emperor! Everybody must do what I say at once!'

Opening Extract

THE YEAR WAS 2081, and everybody was finally equal. They weren't only equal before God and the law. They were equal every which way. Nobody was smarter than anybody else. Nobody was better looking than anybody else. Nobody was stronger or quicker than anybody else. All this equality was due to the 211th, 212th, and 213th Amendments to the Constitution, and to the unceasing vigilance of agents of the United States Handicapper General.

Some things about living still weren't quite right, though. April, for instance, still drove people crazy by not being springtime. And it was in that clammy month that the H-G men took George and Hazel Bergeron's fourteen-year-old son, Harrison, away.

Published 1961. Copyright status: under copyright. Extract shown for educational commentary.

About Kurt Vonnegut

Kurt Vonnegut was born on November 11, 1922, in Indianapolis, Indiana. His satirical novels and stories — including Slaughterhouse-Five (1969), Cat's Cradle (1963), and 'Harrison Bergeron' (1961) — combined science fiction, dark comedy, and humanist philosophy. He died in New York in 2007.
