Young Goodman Brown

Nathaniel Hawthorne · Written 1835, Published 1835 · ~6,800 words

Published in 1835 in New England Magazine, 'Young Goodman Brown' describes a Puritan man who ventures into the forest one night for a meeting with the Devil. He encounters his minister, his wife's grandmother, and nearly the entire village — all revealed as secret devil-worshippers. He may have dreamed it — but he never again trusts anyone, and he dies in gloom.

Quick Summary

'Young Goodman Brown' is a story by Nathaniel Hawthorne, published in 1835. Young Goodman Brown leaves his wife Faith at night and ventures into the dark forest to meet the Devil. He encounters respected members of his community as devil-worshippers. He sees Faith about to be initiated into the coven. He cries to her to resist the Evil One — and finds himself alone in the forest. He never knows if it was a dream. He lives out his days in bitter distrust of all he knew.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with deliberate ambiguity about whether the forest journey was real or a dream — Hawthorne leaves this unresolved.

Themes

The story attacks Puritan hypocrisy and the dangers of a theology of hidden sin — if everyone is secretly corrupt, the result is not virtue but paranoia and misanthropy. Brown's discovery destroys his faith in human goodness.

Historical Context

Published in 1835. One of Hawthorne's most discussed stories for its exploration of Puritan psychology and its relation to his own family's involvement in the Salem witch trials.

Key Passage

Had Goodman Brown fallen asleep in the forest and only dreamed a wild dream of a witch-meeting? Be it so if you will. But, alas! it was a dream of evil omen for young Goodman Brown.

Extract

Young Goodman Brown came forth at sunset, into the street of Salem village, but put his head back, after crossing the threshold, to exchange a parting kiss with his young wife. And Faith, as the wife was aptly named, thrust her own pretty head into the street, letting the wind play with the pink ribbons of her cap, while she called to Goodman Brown.

'Dearest heart,' whispered she, softly and rather sadly, when her lips were close to his ear, 'prithee, put off your journey until sunrise, and sleep in your own bed to-night. A lone woman is troubled with such dreams and such thoughts, that she's afeard of herself, sometimes. Pray, tarry with me this night, dear husband, of all nights in the year!'

Public domain. First published in New England Magazine, April 1835.

About Nathaniel Hawthorne

Nathaniel Hawthorne was born on July 4, 1804, in Salem, Massachusetts. His fiction — haunted by the Puritan legacy and his family's role in the Salem witch trials — includes The Scarlet Letter (1850) and The House of the Seven Gables (1851), along with dozens of Gothic and allegorical short stories. He died in Plymouth, New Hampshire, in 1864.
