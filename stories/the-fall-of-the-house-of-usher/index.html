The Fall of the House of Usher

Edgar Allan Poe · Written 1839, Published 1839 · ~6,800 words

Published in 1839 in Burton's Gentleman's Magazine, 'The Fall of the House of Usher' is one of Poe's masterpieces. An unnamed narrator visits his childhood friend Roderick Usher at his crumbling ancestral mansion. Usher is suffering from a mysterious malady; his twin sister Madeline is dying. The story builds to a shattering climax when Madeline — prematurely buried — breaks free and falls upon her brother.

Quick Summary

'The Fall of the House of Usher' is a story by Edgar Allan Poe, published in 1839. The narrator visits Roderick Usher, who is suffering from a hypersensitive nervous condition. Usher's twin sister Madeline dies and they bury her in the house. A week later, during a storm, Madeline breaks free from her vault — still alive — and falls upon Usher, killing him. The narrator flees as the house splits and sinks into the tarn.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person Gothic narration — the house itself functions as a character, reflecting and embodying the mental state of its inhabitants. The structure mirrors the decomposing mental state of the Usher line.

Themes

The terror of the self turning upon itself — the house and the Usher family are one organism. Madeline is the repressed half of Roderick's consciousness, and her return is both supernatural punishment and psychological inevitability.

Historical Context

Published in Burton's Gentleman's Magazine, September 1839. One of the most influential Gothic stories ever written, establishing the decaying house as a symbol of mental deterioration.

Key Passage

During the whole of a dull, dark, and soundless day in the autumn of the year, when the clouds hung oppressively low in the heavens, I had been passing alone, on horseback, through a singularly dreary tract of country.

Extract

During the whole of a dull, dark, and soundless day in the autumn of the year, when the clouds hung oppressively low in the heavens, I had been passing alone, on horseback, through a singularly dreary tract of country; and at length found myself, as the shades of the evening drew on, within view of the melancholy House of Usher. I know not how it was — but, with the first glimpse of the building, a sense of insufferable gloom pervaded my spirit. I say insufferable; for the feeling was unrelieved by any of that half-pleasurable, because poetic, sentiment, with which the mind usually receives even the sternest natural images of the desolate or terrible. I looked upon the scene before me — upon the mere house, and the simple landscape features of the domain — upon the bleak walls — upon the vacant eye-like windows — upon a few rank sedges — and upon a few white trunks of decayed trees — with an utter depression of soul which I can compare to no earthly sensation more properly than to the after-dream of the reveller upon opium.

Public domain. First published in Burton's Gentleman's Magazine, September 1839.

About Edgar Allan Poe

Edgar Allan Poe was born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts. A master of both poetry and short fiction, Poe is credited with helping originate the detective story and significantly shaping the modern horror and gothic genres. His poetry is known for its musicality, melancholy tone, and recurring themes of loss, death, and longing. He died in Baltimore in 1849 under circumstances that remain debated to this day.
