The Lottery in Babylon

Jorge Luis Borges · Written 1941, Published 1941 · ~2,800 words

Published in 1941, 'The Lottery in Babylon' describes a lottery in ancient Babylon that has evolved from a simple game of chance into an all-encompassing system governing every aspect of life — good and bad outcomes distributed at random.

Quick Summary

'The Lottery in Babylon' is a story by Borges, published in 1941. A Babylonian narrator describes how the lottery evolved from a game of chance to a system that governs everything — not just prizes but punishments, not just wealthy men but everyone. Eventually the Company that administers it becomes invisible and omnipotent. Perhaps it does not exist. Perhaps it always has.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A narrator recounting the history of an institution — the mock-historical voice giving the absurdist premise the feeling of documentary truth.

Themes

The arbitrary nature of fate institutionalized into a system — the lottery is a metaphor for the way random chance becomes organized religion or government. Power becomes invisible precisely when it becomes total.

Historical Context

Published in El jardín de senderos que se bifurcan (1941). Collected in Ficciones (1944).

Opening Extract

Like all the men of Babylon, I have been proconsul; like all, I have been a slave. I have known omnipotence, ignominy, imprisonment. Look here — my right hand has no index finger. Look here — through this gash in my cape you can see on my stomach a crimson tattoo — it is the second symbol, Beth. On nights when the moon is full, this symbol gives me power over men whose mark is Gimel, but it subjects me to those marked Aleph.

Public domain in many jurisdictions. Published 1941. Translated from Spanish.

About Jorge Luis Borges

Jorge Luis Borges was born on August 24, 1899, in Buenos Aires, Argentina. His short story collections Ficciones (1944) and El Aleph (1949) established him as one of the most influential fiction writers of the twentieth century. His labyrinthine, philosophical stories — mixing detective fiction, fantasy, and metaphysics — founded what would become known as magical realism. He died in Geneva in 1986.
