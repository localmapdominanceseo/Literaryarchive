Araby

James Joyce · Written 1905, Published 1914 · ~2,800 words

Published in 1914 in Dubliners, 'Araby' is one of Joyce's most perfectly realized stories of youthful disillusionment. A boy in Dublin is obsessed with the older sister of his friend Mangan. She mentions that she wishes she could go to the Araby bazaar but cannot. He promises to bring her something from it. He arrives late to the bazaar to find it dark and closing — and is struck by his own vanity and foolishness.

Quick Summary

'Araby' is a story by James Joyce, published in 1914 in Dubliners. A Dublin boy falls in love with Mangan's sister and promises to bring her something from the Araby bazaar. He waits impatiently for his uncle to give him money to go. He arrives to find the bazaar almost closed, the lights going out. He picks up a vase but cannot buy anything. Standing in the darkness, he recognizes his foolishness: 'Gazing up into the darkness I saw myself as a creature driven and derided by vanity.'

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person narration with Joyce's characteristic epiphany structure — the story builds to a moment of sudden self-recognition.

Themes

The disillusionment of romantic idealism — the bazaar, exotic and promising in imagination, is tawdry and almost dark in reality. The boy's love is exposed as self-dramatization.

Historical Context

Published in Dubliners (1914). The third story in the collection, one of the 'childhood' stories.

Opening Extract

North Richmond Street, being blind, was a quiet street except at the hour when the Christian Brothers' School set the boys free. An uninhabited house of two storeys stood at the blind end, detached from its neighbours in a square ground. The other houses of the street, conscious of decent lives within them, gazed at one another with brown imperturbable faces.

Public domain. Published in Dubliners, Grant Richards, London, 1914.

About James Joyce

James Joyce was born on February 2, 1882, in Dublin, Ireland. His works — Dubliners (1914), A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man (1916), Ulysses (1922), and Finnegans Wake (1939) — redefined what the novel could be. Ulysses is widely considered the greatest novel of the twentieth century. He died in Zurich in 1941.
