An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge

Ambrose Bierce · Written 1890, Published 1890 · ~3,900 words

Published in 1890 in the San Francisco Examiner, 'An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge' is Ambrose Bierce's most celebrated Civil War story. A Southern plantation owner, Peyton Farquhar, is about to be hanged from a railroad bridge. The rope breaks. He escapes into the stream, swims to safety, runs through the forest, and reaches his home — where his wife comes toward him. Then the rope breaks his neck and he is dead.

Quick Summary

'An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge' is a story by Ambrose Bierce, published in 1890. Peyton Farquhar is about to be hanged from a bridge by Union soldiers. The rope snaps. He plunges into the river, escapes through the water, and runs through the forest toward his home. His wife comes toward him with outstretched arms. Then he is hanged — the entire escape was a hallucination in the moment of death.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Three-part structure: the hanging scene (present), the backstory of how Farquhar was captured (past), and the escape (present, but revealed as hallucination). Bierce disguises the fantasy section as realism until the final line.

Themes

The mind's desperate creation of escape from death — the story is about the way consciousness refuses to accept its own ending.

Historical Context

Published in the San Francisco Examiner in July 1890. One of the most technically accomplished American short stories, famous for its time-manipulation and twist ending.

Key Passage

Peyton Farquhar was dead; his body, with a broken neck, swung gently from side to side beneath the timbers of the Owl Creek bridge.

Extract

A man stood upon a railroad bridge in northern Alabama, looking down into the swift water twenty feet below. The man's hands were behind his back, the wrists bound with a cord. A rope closely encircled his neck. It was attached to a stout cross-timber above his head and the slack fell to the level of his knees. Some loose boards laid upon the sleepers supporting the metals of the railway supplied a footing for him and his executioners — two private soldiers of the Federal army, directed by a sergeant who in civil life may have been a deputy sheriff. At a short remove upon the same temporary platform was an officer in the uniform of his rank, armed. He was a captain.

Public domain. First published in the San Francisco Examiner, July 13, 1890.

About Ambrose Bierce

Ambrose Bierce was born on June 24, 1842, in Horse Cave Creek, Ohio. A Civil War veteran, he became one of the most mordant American journalists and fiction writers of the Gilded Age, best known for his Civil War stories and The Devil's Dictionary. He disappeared in Mexico in 1913 or 1914 under unknown circumstances.
