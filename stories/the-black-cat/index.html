The Black Cat

Edgar Allan Poe · Written 1843, Published 1843 · ~4,000 words

Published in 1843 in The Saturday Evening Post, 'The Black Cat' is one of Poe's most disturbing stories. An unnamed narrator describes how alcoholism drove him to torture and hang his beloved black cat Pluto, then to adopt another, eerily similar cat — which ultimately leads him to murder his wife and wall up her body in the cellar.

Quick Summary

'The Black Cat' is a story by Poe, published in 1843. An alcoholic narrator kills his cat Pluto, then adopts a similar cat that will not leave him. He murders his wife with an axe when she tries to stop him killing the second cat. He walls up her body. The police come; he boasts of the solid walls — and the cat, walled up with the corpse, reveals his crime with its cry.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person confession on the eve of execution — the narrator insists his account is rational even as it reveals complete irrationality.

Themes

The perverseness of human nature — Poe's concept of 'the imp of the perverse,' the human compulsion to do what one knows is wrong precisely because one knows it is wrong.

Historical Context

Published in The Saturday Evening Post, August 1843.

Extract

For the most wild, yet most homely narrative which I am about to pen, I neither expect nor solicit belief. Mad indeed would I be to expect it, in a case where my very senses reject their own evidence. Yet, mad am I not — and very surely do I not dream. But to-morrow I die, and to-day I would unburthen my soul. My immediate purpose is to place before the world, plainly, succinctly, and without comment, a series of mere household events.

Public domain. First published in The Saturday Evening Post, August 1843.

About Edgar Allan Poe

Edgar Allan Poe was born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts. A master of both poetry and short fiction, Poe is credited with helping originate the detective story and significantly shaping the modern horror and gothic genres. His poetry is known for its musicality, melancholy tone, and recurring themes of loss, death, and longing. He died in Baltimore in 1849 under circumstances that remain debated to this day.
