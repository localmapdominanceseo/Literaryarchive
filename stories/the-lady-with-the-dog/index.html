The Lady with the Dog

Anton Chekhov · Written 1899, Published 1899 · ~7,000 words

Published in 1899 in the Russian journal Russkoye Bogatstvo, 'The Lady with the Dog' is widely regarded as one of the greatest short stories ever written. Dmitri Gurov, a bored Moscow banker, has an affair with Anna Sergeyevna while both are holidaying in Yalta. He expects it to be another forgettable encounter — but finds instead that he is genuinely in love for the first time in his life.

Quick Summary

'The Lady with the Dog' is a short story by Anton Chekhov, published in 1899. Gurov, a cynical married man, has a brief affair with Anna Sergeyevna in Yalta. Back in Moscow he cannot forget her and secretly travels to her town. They meet again and begin a secret relationship in Moscow. The story ends with both understanding they love each other deeply but see no way out of their situation — the most difficult part of their life is only just beginning.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Four sections of close third-person narration that gradually moves deeper inside Gurov's consciousness — from cynicism to genuine feeling to love, all shown through behavior and thought rather than declaration.

Themes

The transformation of a cynical man by genuine love — Gurov has always treated women as inferior, has had many affairs, and expects this one to be the same. Instead, love arrives and makes his comfortable life seem empty. The story refuses a happy ending but refuses a tragic one too.

Historical Context

Published 1899. Nabokov called it one of the greatest stories ever written. It influenced virtually every serious short story writer who followed Chekhov.

Key Passage

And it seemed to them that they were within an inch of arriving at a decision, and that then a new, beautiful life would begin. And they both realized that the end was still a long, long way away, and that the hardest, most complicated part was only just beginning.

Extract

People were telling one another that a new arrival had been seen on the sea-front: a lady with a little dog. Dmitri Dmitrievich Gurov had been a fortnight in Yalta, and was used to its ways, and he, too, had begun to take an interest in new arrivals. Sitting in Vernet's pavilion, he watched the young lady walking along the sea-front — she was not very tall, fair, and wore a béret; a white Pomeranian trotted along beside her.

And afterwards he met her in the park, and in the square, several times a day. She was walking alone, always wearing the same béret, and always with the white dog; nobody knew who she was, and everyone called her simply 'the lady with the little dog.'

'If she's here without her husband and without any friends,' thought Gurov, 'it wouldn't be a bad idea to make her acquaintance.'

Public domain. First published in Russkoye Bogatstvo, 1899. Translated from Russian.

About Anton Chekhov

Anton Chekhov was born on January 29, 1860, in Taganrog, Russia. Working as a physician while writing, he produced hundreds of short stories that revolutionized the form — replacing plot-driven narratives with atmosphere, implication, and the depiction of ordinary life's quiet suffering. He is also considered the father of modern drama. He died of tuberculosis in Badenweiler, Germany, in 1904.
