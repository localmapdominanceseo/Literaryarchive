The Tell-Tale Heart

Edgar Allan Poe · Written 1843, Published 1843 · ~2,100 words

Published in 1843 in The Pioneer, 'The Tell-Tale Heart' is Poe's masterpiece of psychological horror. An unnamed narrator insists on their sanity while confessing to murdering an old man because of his 'vulture eye' — and describes how the victim's beating heart, which only they can hear, drove them to confess to the police.

Quick Summary

'The Tell-Tale Heart' is a short story by Edgar Allan Poe, published in 1843. The narrator murders an old man because of his pale blue 'vulture eye,' then dismembers and hides the body under the floorboards. When police arrive, the narrator hears the dead man's heart beating louder and louder until they confess. The story is a study in guilt, paranoia, and the self-defeating nature of madness.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person confession, the narrator addressing the reader directly and insisting on their sanity — which their narration constantly contradicts. The rising rhythm of the prose mirrors the narrator's psychological breakdown.

Themes

Guilt and the unreliable narrator — the beating heart is the narrator's own guilt externalized. The story is also a study in obsession: the murder is committed not out of hatred or greed but out of irrational fixation on the eye.

Historical Context

Published in The Pioneer in January 1843. One of Poe's most anthologized and psychologically sophisticated stories, and a foundational text in the literature of the unreliable narrator.

Key Passage

True! nervous, very, very dreadfully nervous I had been and am; but why will you say that I am mad?

Characters

The Narrator

Unnamed, possibly of either gender, the narrator insists obsessively on their own sanity while demonstrating complete irrationality. They murdered the old man not out of hatred or greed but because of irrational fixation on his 'pale blue eye.' The confession structure — the story is addressed to 'you,' presumably investigating police or a judge — means the narrator is condemning themselves while trying to defend themselves.

The Old Man

The narrator claims to love the old man and says he never wronged them. He is defined entirely by his 'vulture eye' — the one detail the narrator cannot control their response to. He is a victim whose individuality is erased by the narrator's obsession.

Symbols & Motifs

The Vulture Eye

The old man's pale blue eye, filmed over, which the narrator cannot stand. It is the symbol of the irrational nature of the narrator's obsession — there is no rational reason to kill someone for the appearance of their eye. The eye represents everything the narrator cannot control about the external world.

The Beating Heart

The sound the narrator hears — growing louder and louder until they confess — is the classic symbol of guilt. The heart cannot literally beat after death; the sound is the narrator's own guilt and fear projected outward. The story demonstrates that the mind cannot escape its own crimes.

The Lantern

The narrator uses a dark lantern, opening it just enough to let a thin ray fall on the old man's eye. The deliberate control of light and darkness reflects the narrator's belief that they can control their crime — a belief entirely undermined by the ending.

Opening Extract

TRUE! — nervous — very, very dreadfully nervous I had been and am; but why will you say that I am mad? The disease had sharpened my senses — not destroyed — not dulled them. Above all was the sense of hearing acute. I heard all things in the heaven and in the earth. I heard many things in hell. How, then, am I mad? Hearken! and observe how healthily — how calmly I can tell you the whole story.

It is impossible to say how first the idea entered my brain; but once conceived, it haunted me day and night. Object there was none. Passion there was none. I loved the old man. He had never wronged me. He had never given me insult. For his gold I had no desire. I think it was his eye! yes, it was this! He had the eye of a vulture — a pale blue eye, with a film over it. Whenever it fell upon me, my blood ran cold; and so by degrees — very gradually — I made up my mind to take the life of the old man, and thus rid myself of the eye forever.

Public domain. First published in The Pioneer, January 1843.

Legacy & Influence

Published in 1843, 'The Tell-Tale Heart' is one of Poe's most anthologized stories and a defining text of the psychological horror genre. The unreliable first-person narrator — a confessing murderer who simultaneously denies their guilt — became a template for fiction from Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment to countless twentieth-century crime novels. The story is remarkable for the way its rhythm accelerates to match the narrator's panic.

Study Questions

  1. How does Poe use narrative structure to implicate the reader in the narrator's guilt?
  2. What does the story suggest about the relationship between intelligence and sanity?
  3. How does the rhythm and pacing of the prose enact the narrator's psychological state?
  4. What is the significance of the narrator's claim to love the old man?
  5. How does the story define 'madness' — is the narrator mad because they killed, or because of how they killed?

About Edgar Allan Poe

Edgar Allan Poe was born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts. A master of both poetry and short fiction, Poe is credited with helping originate the detective story and significantly shaping the modern horror and gothic genres. His poetry is known for its musicality, melancholy tone, and recurring themes of loss, death, and longing. He died in Baltimore in 1849 under circumstances that remain debated to this day.
