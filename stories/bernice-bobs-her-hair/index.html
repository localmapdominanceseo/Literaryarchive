Bernice Bobs Her Hair

F. Scott Fitzgerald · Written 1920, Published 1920 · ~9,000 words

Published in 1920 in The Saturday Evening Post, 'Bernice Bobs Her Hair' is a sharp comedy of manners about female social competition. Bernice visits her glamorous cousin Marjorie, who tutors her in how to attract boys. Marjorie then tricks Bernice into bobbing her hair as a dare, destroying her appeal. Bernice gets revenge.

Quick Summary

'Bernice Bobs Her Hair' is a story by Fitzgerald, published in 1920. Awkward Bernice is transformed by her glamorous cousin Marjorie into a social success through coaching. Marjorie then tricks her into following through on a dare to bob her hair, destroying Bernice's appeal. Before leaving, Bernice cuts off Marjorie's braids while she sleeps and flings them on Warren's porch.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with close attention to social dynamics — a comedy of manners about the cruelty beneath social surfaces.

Themes

Female social competition and the arbitrariness of the codes that govern it — the story is both a critique of the Marjories of the world and an acknowledgment that Bernice can play the game too.

Historical Context

Published in The Saturday Evening Post, May 1920. One of Fitzgerald's finest early stories.

Extract

After dark on Saturday night one could stand on the first tee of the golf-course and see the country-club windows as a yellow expanse over a very black and wavy ocean. The waves of this ocean, so to speak, were the heads of many curious caddies, a few of the more ingenious chauffeurs, the golf professional's deaf sister — and there were usually several stray, diffident waves who might have rolled inside had they so desired.

Public domain. First published in The Saturday Evening Post, May 1920.

About F. Scott Fitzgerald

Francis Scott Key Fitzgerald was born on September 24, 1896, in St. Paul, Minnesota. The Great Gatsby (1925) is his masterpiece — the definitive novel of the American Dream's promise and betrayal. His short stories, collected in Flappers and Philosophers and Tales of the Jazz Age, are equally important to the period. He died in Hollywood in 1940.
