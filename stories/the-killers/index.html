The Killers

Ernest Hemingway · Written 1927, Published 1927 · ~3,800 words

Published in 1927 in Scribner's Magazine, 'The Killers' is one of Hemingway's finest and most purely minimalist stories. Two hired killers come to a diner looking for a Swede named Ole Andreson. After they leave, Nick Adams goes to warn Ole — who is lying on his bed, facing the wall, knowing they are coming and having decided not to run.

Quick Summary

'The Killers' is a story by Hemingway, published in 1927. Two gangsters come to a small-town diner looking for Ole Andreson, a former heavyweight fighter. They tie up the cook and Nick Adams and wait. Ole doesn't come. Nick goes to warn Ole, who lies on his bed facing the wall. He knows they are coming. He has done something wrong to someone and will not run. Nick doesn't know what to do.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Almost entirely dialogue — the iceberg theory applied to a thriller premise. The violence and Ole's backstory are never explained.

Themes

The code of acceptance — Ole's decision to wait for the killers, his refusal to run, is presented without explanation or judgment. Nick's initiation into a world where men are hunted and do not flee.

Historical Context

Published in Scribner's Magazine, March 1927. Collected in Men Without Women (1927).

Opening Extract

The door of Henry's lunchroom opened and two men came in. They sat down at the counter.

'What's yours?' George asked them.

'I don't know,' one of the men said. 'What do you want to eat, Al?'

'I don't know,' said Al. 'I don't know what I want to eat.'

Outside it was getting dark. The street-light came on outside the window. The two men at the counter read the menu. From the other end of the counter Nick Adams watched them.

Published 1927. Copyright status: under copyright. Extract shown for educational commentary.

About Ernest Hemingway

Ernest Hemingway was born on July 21, 1899, in Oak Park, Illinois. His prose style — stripped of adjectives, relying on dialogue and implication — transformed American fiction. His novels include The Sun Also Rises (1926) and A Farewell to Arms (1929). He won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1954. He died by suicide in Ketchum, Idaho, in 1961.
