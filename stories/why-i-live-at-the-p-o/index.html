Why I Live at the P.O.

Eudora Welty · Written 1941, Published 1941 · ~5,500 words

Published in 1941 in Atlantic Monthly, 'Why I Live at the P.O.' is Eudora Welty's most celebrated comic story. Narrated by the postmistress of China Grove, Mississippi, it is a one-sided account of a family feud that drives her to move out of the house and into the post office — told with complete self-righteousness that makes her own unreliability obvious.

Quick Summary

'Why I Live at the P.O.' is a story by Eudora Welty, published in 1941. The narrator, called Sister, is the postmistress of China Grove, Mississippi. When her younger sister Stella-Rondo returns from a failed marriage with a child she claims to have adopted, the whole family sides with Stella-Rondo against Sister. Sister takes her belongings and moves into the post office, declaring she is better off there.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person comic monologue — the narrator's complete self-righteousness reveals her own contribution to the chaos she describes.

Themes

The comedy of family dysfunction told from the inside — Sister's account is transparently unreliable, making the reader simultaneously sympathize and laugh.

Historical Context

Published in The Atlantic Monthly, April 1941. Collected in A Curtain of Green (1941). One of the funniest stories in American literature.

Extract

I was getting along fine with Mama, Papa-Daddy and Uncle Rondo until my sister Stella-Rondo just separated from her husband and came back home again. Mr. Whitaker! Of course I went with Mr. Whitaker first, when he first appeared here in China Grove, taking 'Pose Yourself' photos, and Stella-Rondo broke us up. Told him I was one-sided. Bigger on one side than the other, which is a deliberate, calculated falsehood: I'm the same.

Published 1941. Copyright status: under copyright. Extract shown for educational commentary.

About Eudora Welty

Eudora Welty was born on April 13, 1909, in Jackson, Mississippi. One of the finest American short story writers, she spent her entire career in Mississippi, writing stories of extraordinary observation and tenderness. Her novel The Optimist's Daughter (1972) won the Pulitzer Prize. She died in Jackson in 2001.
