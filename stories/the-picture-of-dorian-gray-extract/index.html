The Picture of Dorian Gray (Extract)

Oscar Wilde · Written 1890, Published 1890 · ~78,000 words

Published in 1890 in Lippincott's Monthly Magazine, 'The Picture of Dorian Gray' is Oscar Wilde's only novel. Beautiful young Dorian Gray wishes that his portrait would age instead of him. The wish is granted. Dorian pursues a life of sin and decadence while remaining young; the portrait grows monstrous. He eventually stabs the portrait — and is found dead, aged and hideous, while the portrait is restored to its original beauty.

Quick Summary

'The Picture of Dorian Gray' is a novel by Oscar Wilde, published in 1890. Dorian Gray, influenced by the hedonistic Lord Henry Wotton, wishes his portrait would age in his place. The wish is granted. He lives a life of sin — abandoning and indirectly killing the actress Sibyl Vane, murdering Basil Hallward, driving others to ruin — while his portrait records his corruption. He eventually stabs the portrait and is found dead, hideous and aged.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A Gothic novel with Wilde's characteristic epigrams and paradoxes embedded throughout — the prose style itself enacts Lord Henry's philosophy of beautiful surfaces.

Themes

The separation of aesthetics from morality, the corruption of influence, and the consequences of treating life as art. Dorian's wish separates appearance from reality — and the separation destroys him.

Historical Context

Published in Lippincott's Monthly Magazine in 1890, expanded for book publication in 1891. Wilde added a preface of epigrams to defend the novel against moral criticism.

Extract

The studio was filled with the rich odour of roses, and when the light summer wind stirred amidst the trees of the garden, there came through the open door the heavy scent of the lilac, or the more delicate perfume of the pink-flowering thorn.

From the corner of the divan of Persian saddle-bags on which he was lying, smoking, as was his custom, innumerable cigarettes, Lord Henry Wotton could just catch the gleam of the honey-sweet and honey-coloured blossoms of a laburnum, whose tremulous branches seemed hardly able to bear the burden of a beauty so flame-like as theirs.

Public domain. Published in Lippincott's Monthly Magazine, 1890.

About Oscar Wilde

Oscar Wilde was born on October 16, 1854, in Dublin, Ireland. A poet, playwright, novelist, and wit, he is best known for The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890), The Importance of Being Earnest (1895), and his fairy tales. He was imprisoned for two years for 'gross indecency' in 1895 and died in Paris in 1900.
