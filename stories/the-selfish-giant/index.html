The Selfish Giant

Oscar Wilde · Written 1888, Published 1888 · ~1,800 words

Published in 1888 in The Happy Prince and Other Tales, 'The Selfish Giant' tells of a giant who fences off his garden, forbidding children to play there. Permanent winter settles over his garden. When children sneak back in, spring returns. The giant repents. Years later he finds a small child in his garden — bearing the wounds of the nails — and understands he has been visited by Christ.

Quick Summary

'The Selfish Giant' is a fairy tale by Oscar Wilde, published in 1888. A giant's garden is permanently winter because he has fenced it off from children. When children sneak in, spring returns to their corners. The giant repents and opens the garden. Years later he finds one child he cannot reach — a small boy with the prints of nails on hands and feet. Christ tells him he let the giant play in His garden long ago, and he will come with him to Paradise.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A classic fairy tale structure — sin, consequence, repentance, and redemption — in Wilde's characteristically simple and beautiful prose.

Themes

Selfishness, repentance, and Christian redemption — the story is an allegory of the soul that excludes love and suffers for it, then opens itself and is rewarded.

Historical Context

Published in The Happy Prince and Other Tales (1888).

Opening Extract

Every afternoon, as they were coming from school, the children used to go and play in the Giant's garden.

It was a large lovely garden, with soft green grass. Here and there over the grass stood beautiful flowers like stars, and there were twelve peach-trees that in the spring-time broke out into delicate blossoms of pink and pearl, and in the autumn bore rich fruit. The birds sat on the trees and sang so sweetly that the children used to stop their games in order to listen to them.

Public domain. Published in The Happy Prince and Other Tales, 1888.

About Oscar Wilde

Oscar Wilde was born on October 16, 1854, in Dublin, Ireland. A poet, playwright, novelist, and wit, he is best known for The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890), The Importance of Being Earnest (1895), and his fairy tales. He was imprisoned for two years for 'gross indecency' in 1895 and died in Paris in 1900.
