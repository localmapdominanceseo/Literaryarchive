The Dead

James Joyce · Written 1907, Published 1914 · ~15,000 words

The final and longest story in James Joyce's Dubliners (1914), 'The Dead' is widely considered one of the greatest short stories in the English language. Gabriel Conroy attends his aunts' annual Christmas party in Dublin, delivers a self-conscious speech, and then, after the party, discovers that his wife Gretta has been thinking all evening of a boy who loved her in Galway and died young — a revelation that shatters Gabriel's complacency about himself and his marriage.

Quick Summary

'The Dead' is a short story by James Joyce, published in 1914 in Dubliners. Gabriel Conroy attends his aunts' Christmas party, gives a speech, and feels superior to those around him. That night, he discovers his wife Gretta has been moved by a song to think of a boy named Michael Furey who loved her in her youth and who died of pneumonia after standing in the rain outside her window before she left for a convent. Gabriel realizes the depth of something he has never felt — and meditates on the snow falling over all of Ireland, over the living and the dead.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration gradually moving into Gabriel's interior consciousness, culminating in one of the most celebrated final passages in all of fiction — a prose-poem meditation on snow and death.

Themes

The paralysis of the living compared to the passion of the dead — Michael Furey, though dead, is more alive in Gretta's heart than Gabriel, who is present and alive, has ever been. The snow that falls at the end falls equally over the quick and the dead, equalizing them.

Historical Context

Written in 1907, published in Dubliners (1914). Joyce added 'The Dead' to the collection after the other stories were complete. He later called it his best work in Dubliners.

Key Passage

His soul swooned slowly as he heard the snow falling faintly through the universe and faintly falling, like the descent of their last end, upon all the living and the dead.

Extract

Lily, the caretaker's daughter, was literally run off her feet. Hardly had she brought one gentleman into the little pantry behind the office on the ground floor and helped him off with his overcoat than the wheezy hall-door bell clanged again and she had to scamper along the bare hallway to let in another guest. It was well for her she had not to attend to the ladies also. But Miss Kate and Miss Julia had thought of that and had converted the bathroom upstairs into a ladies' dressing-room. Miss Kate and Miss Julia were there, gossiping and laughing and fussing, walking after each other to the head of the stairs, peering down over the banisters and calling down to Lily to ask her who had come.

It was always a great affair, the Misses Morkan's annual dance. Everybody who knew them came to it, members of the family, old friends of the family, the members of Julia's choir, any of Kate's pupils that were grown up enough, and even some of Mary Jane's pupils too.

Public domain. Published in Dubliners, Grant Richards, London, 1914.

About James Joyce

James Joyce was born on February 2, 1882, in Dublin, Ireland. His works — Dubliners (1914), A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man (1916), Ulysses (1922), and Finnegans Wake (1939) — redefined what the novel could be. Ulysses is widely considered the greatest novel of the twentieth century. He died in Zurich in 1941.
