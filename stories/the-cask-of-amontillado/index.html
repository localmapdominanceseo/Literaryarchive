The Cask of Amontillado

Edgar Allan Poe · Written 1846, Published 1846 · ~2,300 words

Published in 1846 in Godey's Lady's Book, 'The Cask of Amontillado' is told by Montresor, who lures his enemy Fortunato into the wine cellars beneath his palazzo during carnival season, chains him to the wall, and bricks him in alive — all with perfect composure and courtesy.

Quick Summary

'The Cask of Amontillado' is a short story by Edgar Allan Poe, published in 1846. Montresor has been insulted by Fortunato and plans perfect revenge. He lures Fortunato into his family's wine vaults during carnival on the pretext of tasting a rare wine. He chains Fortunato to the wall deep in the catacombs and walls him in alive with bricks and mortar. The crime goes undiscovered for fifty years.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person confession told from fifty years' distance, the narrator describing his perfect crime with quiet pride and no remorse.

Themes

Revenge, pride, and the perfect crime — Montresor's criteria for revenge are that the avenger must not be caught and the victim must know who is punishing them. The story never reveals what Fortunato did to deserve this fate.

Historical Context

Published in Godey's Lady's Book in November 1846. One of Poe's most tightly constructed stories, and a masterclass in the unreliable narrator who reveals more about themselves than they intend.

Key Passage

In pace requiescat! [May he rest in peace!] — the last words Montresor speaks into the sealed vault.

Extract

THE thousand injuries of Fortunato I had borne as I best could; but when he ventured upon insult, I vowed revenge. You, who so well know the nature of my soul, will not suppose, however, that I gave utterance to a threat. At length I would be avenged; this was a point definitively settled — but the very definitiveness with which it was resolved, precluded the idea of risk. I must not only punish, but punish with impunity. A wrong is unredressed when retribution overtakes its redressor. It is equally unredressed when the avenger fails to make himself felt as such to him who has done the wrong.

It must be understood, that neither by word nor deed had I given Fortunato cause to doubt my good will. I continued, as was my wont, to smile in his face, and he did not perceive that my smile now was at the thought of his immolation.

Public domain. First published in Godey's Lady's Book, November 1846.

About Edgar Allan Poe

Edgar Allan Poe was born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts. A master of both poetry and short fiction, Poe is credited with helping originate the detective story and significantly shaping the modern horror and gothic genres. His poetry is known for its musicality, melancholy tone, and recurring themes of loss, death, and longing. He died in Baltimore in 1849 under circumstances that remain debated to this day.
