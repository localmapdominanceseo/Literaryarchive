The Gift of the Magi

O. Henry · Written 1905, Published 1905 · ~2,100 words

Published in 1905 in the New York Sunday World magazine and later collected in The Four Million (1906), 'The Gift of the Magi' is O. Henry's most celebrated story. A young couple, Della and Jim, each secretly sell their most prized possession to buy a Christmas gift for the other — only to find their gifts are now useless because of what they sacrificed.

Quick Summary

'The Gift of the Magi' is a short story by O. Henry, published in 1905. Della sells her beautiful long hair to buy Jim a platinum watch chain for Christmas. Jim sells his gold watch to buy Della a set of expensive combs for her hair. Each gift is now useless — but the story argues that their mutual sacrifice makes them the wisest gift-givers of all, like the Magi.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with O. Henry's characteristic warm, ironic narrative voice — addressing the reader directly and building to the famous twist ending.

Themes

Selfless love and sacrifice — the story's central irony is that each act of generosity cancels out the other, leaving the couple poorer than before but richer in demonstrated love. O. Henry frames them as wiser than the Magi.

Historical Context

Published in 1905. The story has become a Christmas classic and is widely studied as an example of situational irony and the O. Henry twist ending.

Key Passage

Here I have lamely related to you the uneventful chronicle of two foolish children in a flat who most unwisely sacrificed for each other the greatest treasures of their house.

Characters

Della

A young woman deeply in love with her husband Jim and desperate to buy him a worthy Christmas gift despite having only $1.87. Her sacrifice — cutting and selling her beautiful long hair — is impulsive and passionate. She is the story's emotional center.

Jim

Della's husband, who earns only $20 a week and is struggling financially. He has sold his prized gold watch to buy Della a set of combs for her long hair. His sacrifice is revealed only at the story's end. His reaction to Della's shorn hair — complex, still, quietly devastated but not unkind — is one of the story's most delicate moments.

Symbols & Motifs

The Hair and the Watch

The two most prized possessions — Della's hair and Jim's watch — represent their identities and their pride. By sacrificing them for each other, they demonstrate that they value their love more than their identity. The irony is that the gifts become useless, but this uselessness is the story's point: the sacrifice was the gift.

The Magi

O. Henry's closing comparison to the Magi — the wise men who brought gifts to the infant Jesus — frames Della and Jim's exchange as an act of genuine wisdom. The Magi invented the tradition of gift-giving; Della and Jim, O. Henry argues, have perfected it.

Opening Extract

One dollar and eighty-seven cents. That was all. And sixty cents of it was in pennies. Pennies saved one and two at a time by bulldozing the grocer and the vegetable man and the butcher until one's cheeks burned with the silent imputation of parsimony that such close dealing implied. Three times Della counted it. One dollar and eighty-seven cents. And the next day would be Christmas.

There was clearly nothing to do but flop down on the shabby little couch and howl. So Della did it. Which instigates the moral reflection that life is made up of sobs, sniffles, and smiles, with sniffles predominating.

While the mistress of the home is gradually subsiding from the first stage to the second, take a look at the home. A furnished flat at $8 per week. It did not exactly beggar description, but it certainly had that word on the lookout for the mendicancy squad.

Public domain. First published in the New York Sunday World, December 10, 1905.

Legacy & Influence

Published in 1905, 'The Gift of the Magi' is O. Henry's most celebrated story and one of the most widely read Christmas stories in the world. It has been adapted for film, television, and stage dozens of times. The story established O. Henry's signature technique — the twist ending that recontextualizes everything preceding it — as a standard form of the American short story.

Study Questions

  1. How does O. Henry use irony in the story?
  2. What does the narrator's direct address to the reader accomplish?
  3. Is the story sentimental? Can it be both sentimental and meaningful?
  4. What does the comparison to the Magi suggest about O. Henry's view of love?
  5. How does the story's poverty setting affect its emotional resonance?

About O. Henry

William Sydney Porter, known by his pen name O. Henry, was born on September 11, 1862, in Greensboro, North Carolina. He wrote nearly 600 short stories celebrated for their wit, wordplay, and surprise twist endings. His most famous works include 'The Gift of the Magi' and 'The Last Leaf.' He died in New York in 1910.
