Chickamauga

Ambrose Bierce · Written 1889, Published 1889 · ~3,200 words

Published in 1889 in Tales of Soldiers and Civilians, 'Chickamauga' is Bierce's most devastating Civil War story. A six-year-old deaf-mute boy wanders from home playing soldier and falls asleep in the woods. He wakes among crawling, groaning men — the survivors of the Battle of Chickamauga — and leads them, laughing, as if they are his toy soldiers. His house is in flames. His mother is dead.

Quick Summary

'Chickamauga' is a story by Ambrose Bierce, published in 1889. A six-year-old boy, deaf and mute, wanders into the forest playing soldier. He wakes among badly wounded men crawling from the Battle of Chickamauga. Unable to hear their groans, he laughs and plays among them. He leads them toward a fire — his burning house. He finds his mother's mutilated body and makes an inarticulate sound: his first.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration from the child's perspective — the horror emerges from the gap between what the child sees (adventure, play) and what the reader understands.

Themes

The innocence of the child as an ironic lens on the horror of war — the boy's deafness makes him unable to understand what surrounds him, making the reader's comprehension of the horror all the more acute.

Historical Context

Published in Tales of Soldiers and Civilians (1891). Based on the Battle of Chickamauga (1863), one of the bloodiest of the Civil War.

Extract

One sunny autumn afternoon a child strayed away from its rude home in a small field and entered a forest unobserved. It was happy in a new sense of freedom from control, happy in the opportunity of exploration and adventure; for this child's spirit, in bodies of its ancestors, had for thousands of years been trained to memorable feats of discovery and conquest.

Public domain. First published in the San Francisco Examiner, January 1889.

About Ambrose Bierce

Ambrose Bierce was born on June 24, 1842, in Horse Cave Creek, Ohio. A Civil War veteran, he became one of the most mordant American journalists and fiction writers of the Gilded Age, best known for his Civil War stories and The Devil's Dictionary. He disappeared in Mexico in 1913 or 1914 under unknown circumstances.
