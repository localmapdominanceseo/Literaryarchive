Eveline

James Joyce · Written 1904, Published 1914 · ~2,100 words

Published in 1914 in Dubliners, 'Eveline' describes a young Dublin woman who has promised to leave Ireland with her sailor lover Frank for a new life in Buenos Aires. She sits by the window remembering her life — her dead mother, her violent father, her obligations — and on the quay she cannot get on the boat. She is paralysed.

Quick Summary

'Eveline' is a story by James Joyce, published in 1914. Eveline Hill sits by her window reviewing her life and her decision to emigrate with Frank to Buenos Aires. She recalls her promises to her dying mother, her father's violence, the drudgery of her work. On the quay, as Frank tries to lead her onto the boat, she grips the railing and cannot move. Her eyes show no love or recognition — like a helpless animal.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Close third-person narration of Eveline's consciousness — her paralysis is rendered through the accumulation of memory and obligation.

Themes

Paralysis — Joyce's central theme in Dubliners. Eveline cannot leave because her sense of duty and fear are stronger than her desire for freedom.

Historical Context

Published in Dubliners (1914). Part of the 'adolescence' section of the collection.

Opening Extract

She sat at the window watching the evening invade the avenue. Her head was leaned against the window curtains and in her nostrils was the odour of dusty cretonne. She was tired.

Few people passed. The man out of the last house passed on his way home; she heard his footsteps clacking along the concrete pavement and afterwards crunching on the cinder path before the new red houses. One time there used to be a field there in which they used to play every evening with other people's children.

Public domain. Published in Dubliners, Grant Richards, London, 1914.

About James Joyce

James Joyce was born on February 2, 1882, in Dublin, Ireland. His works — Dubliners (1914), A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man (1916), Ulysses (1922), and Finnegans Wake (1939) — redefined what the novel could be. Ulysses is widely considered the greatest novel of the twentieth century. He died in Zurich in 1941.
