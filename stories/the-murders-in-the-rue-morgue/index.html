The Murders in the Rue Morgue

Edgar Allan Poe · Written 1841, Published 1841 · ~14,000 words

Published in 1841 in Graham's Magazine, 'The Murders in the Rue Morgue' is the first modern detective story. C. Auguste Dupin — the prototype for Sherlock Holmes — investigates the brutal murders of two women in a locked Paris apartment. The solution: an escaped orangutan.

Quick Summary

'The Murders in the Rue Morgue' is a story by Edgar Allan Poe, published in 1841. C. Auguste Dupin investigates the seemingly impossible murders of two women in a locked Paris apartment. Through brilliant deductive analysis — examining the physical evidence, the hair found in the victim's hands, the voice heard — Dupin concludes that the murderer was not human: it was an escaped orangutan belonging to a French sailor.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person narration by Dupin's unnamed companion — the template that Conan Doyle would adopt for Watson and Holmes.

Themes

Poe invented the genre of detective fiction here: the locked-room mystery, the eccentric genius detective, the less-clever narrator companion, the solution through pure reason.

Historical Context

Published in Graham's Magazine in April 1841. The first modern detective story. Conan Doyle acknowledged Poe as the inventor of the genre.

Extract

The mental features discoursed of as the analytical, are, in themselves, but little susceptible of analysis. We appreciate them only in their effects. We know of them, among other things, that they are always to their possessor, when inordinately possessed, a source of the liveliest enjoyment. As the strong man exults in his physical ability, delighting in such exercises as call his muscles into action, so glories the analyst in that moral activity which disentangles.

Public domain. First published in Graham's Magazine, April 1841.

About Edgar Allan Poe

Edgar Allan Poe was born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts. A master of both poetry and short fiction, Poe is credited with helping originate the detective story and significantly shaping the modern horror and gothic genres. His poetry is known for its musicality, melancholy tone, and recurring themes of loss, death, and longing. He died in Baltimore in 1849 under circumstances that remain debated to this day.
