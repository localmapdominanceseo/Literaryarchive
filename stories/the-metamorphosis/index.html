The Metamorphosis

Franz Kafka · Written 1912, Published 1915 · ~22,000 words

Published in 1915, 'The Metamorphosis' (Die Verwandlung) is Franz Kafka's most celebrated work. Gregor Samsa wakes one morning to find himself transformed into a giant insect. The story follows the consequences for Gregor and his family as they struggle to adapt to and eventually reject his new form.

Quick Summary

'The Metamorphosis' is a novella by Franz Kafka, published in 1915. Gregor Samsa wakes one morning transformed into a giant insect. His first concern is that he will be late for work. His family is initially horrified, then adapt to managing around him, then begin to resent him. Gregor dies quietly in his room. The family, freed from his burden, feels immediate relief and makes plans for the future.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Close third-person narration following Gregor's perspective with surreal matter-of-factness — the transformation is accepted as literal fact and the story focuses on the mundane practical consequences.

Themes

The story has been read as an allegory of alienated labor (Gregor was a traveling salesman trapped by family debt), of disability and the family's response to a member who can no longer contribute economically, and of Kafka's own sense of monstrousness and isolation from his family.

Historical Context

Written in 1912, published 1915 in Die weißen Blätter. One of the most influential works of twentieth-century literature, it established the 'Kafkaesque' mode of treating the impossible as ordinary.

Key Passage

As Gregor Samsa awoke one morning from uneasy dreams he found himself transformed in his bed into a gigantic insect.

Characters

Gregor Samsa

A traveling salesman who wakes one morning as a giant insect. Gregor is defined by his function: he supports his entire family with his earnings and has no identity beyond his economic usefulness. His transformation removes his economic function and reveals how thoroughly his family's relationship with him was transactional. Even as an insect, Gregor worries about missing work and supporting his family.

Grete Samsa

Gregor's sister, who initially takes care of him with genuine tenderness — cleaning his room, bringing food, learning his preferences. As months pass, she grows resentful of the burden and is the one who finally declares the family must get rid of him. Her transformation from loving sister to cold pragmatist mirrors the family's overall trajectory.

The Father

Mr. Samsa is physically and psychologically revitalized by Gregor's transformation. Previously passive and defeated, he now wears a bank uniform proudly and resumes authority in the household. He twice attacks Gregor violently — throwing apples at him, injuring him fatally. His revival comes entirely at Gregor's expense.

The Mother

Wavering between guilt and revulsion, the mother cannot look at Gregor without fainting. She represents a more humanized response to the situation but is ultimately unable to act on her maternal feelings.

Symbols & Motifs

The Transformation

The insect transformation has been read as an allegory for alienated labor (Gregor is literally dehumanized by his work), for disability and family dynamics, for Kafka's own sense of monstrousness, and for the Marxist idea of the worker reduced to a unit of production. The transformation is both literal and metaphorical — Gregor was already being treated as less than human before the story began.

The Picture of the Woman in Furs

The framed picture Gregor has cut from a magazine and hung on his wall. When Grete and the mother clear his room, it is the one object he clings to. It represents the last fragment of his human identity and his connection to beauty and desire.

The Three Lodgers

The three lodgers who come to stay represent the economic logic that has replaced familial love. They occupy the best room, eat the best food, and are eventually driven out by the sound of Grete's violin — the same violin that once gave Gregor joy but now disturbs the lodgers who pay the bills.

Opening Extract

As Gregor Samsa awoke one morning from uneasy dreams he found himself transformed in his bed into a gigantic insect. He was lying on his hard, as it were armor-plated, back and when he lifted his head a little he could see his dome-like brown belly divided into stiff arched segments on top of which the bed quilt was about to slide off entirely. His numerous legs, which were pitifully thin compared to the rest of his bulk, flickered helplessly before his eyes.

What has happened to me? he thought. It was no dream. His room, a regular human bedroom, only rather too small, lay quiet between the four familiar walls. Above the table on which a collection of cloth samples was unpacked and spread out — Samsa was a commercial traveller — hung the picture which he had recently cut out of an illustrated magazine and put into a pretty gilt frame. It showed a lady, with a fur cap on and a fur stole, sitting upright and holding out to the spectator a huge fur muff into which the whole of her forearm had vanished.

Public domain. First published in Die weißen Blätter, 1915. Translated from German.

Legacy & Influence

Published in 1915, 'The Metamorphosis' became one of the defining texts of twentieth-century literature. The word 'Kafkaesque' — meaning a situation of surreal bureaucratic horror — derives from Kafka's body of work, with The Metamorphosis as its most accessible expression. The story influenced Albert Camus, Samuel Beckett, Gabriel García Márquez, and virtually every writer of the absurdist and existentialist traditions. W.H. Auden called Kafka 'the most important writer of our time.'

Study Questions

  1. What does Gregor's first concern — being late for work — reveal about his character and situation?
  2. How does Kafka use the family's financial recovery as a commentary on capitalism?
  3. What is the significance of Grete's transformation from caretaker to judge?
  4. How does the story present the relationship between economic utility and human worth?
  5. Why does Kafka never explain the cause of the transformation?

About Franz Kafka

Franz Kafka was born on July 3, 1883, in Prague, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Writing in German, he produced some of the most disquieting and influential fiction of the twentieth century: 'The Metamorphosis,' 'The Trial,' 'The Castle,' and 'In the Penal Colony.' Most of his work was published posthumously by his friend Max Brod, against Kafka's explicit instructions to burn it. He died of tuberculosis in Kierling, Austria, in 1924.
