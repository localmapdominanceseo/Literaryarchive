A Good Man Is Hard to Find

Flannery O'Connor · Written 1953, Published 1953 · ~6,600 words

Published in 1953 in A Good Man Is Hard to Find and Other Stories, Flannery O'Connor's most famous story follows a Southern family on a road trip. The grandmother manipulates the family into taking a detour to see an old plantation — causing an accident that leads to their encounter with a fugitive called The Misfit, who has all of them killed.

Quick Summary

'A Good Man Is Hard to Find' is a story by Flannery O'Connor, published in 1953. A Southern family travels to Florida. The grandmother causes an accident by hiding her cat and then, when the car overturns, they are found by The Misfit, an escaped convict. The Misfit has his men kill the family one by one while he discusses religion with the grandmother. In her final moment of terror, she reaches out and touches him — he recoils and shoots her three times.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with O'Connor's characteristic sharp, satirical eye for Southern manners and pretensions, the comedy darkening steadily until the final violent confrontation.

Themes

Grace, violence, and the moment of recognition — O'Connor was a devout Catholic and read the story as being about a moment of grace: the grandmother's final touch of The Misfit, recognizing him as one of her own children. The Misfit's theological argument — if Jesus raised the dead, there is no meaning without following him; if he didn't, there is no meaning either — is central.

Historical Context

Published in 1953. O'Connor's most anthologized story. She wrote a famous essay explaining her intentions called 'A Reasonable Use of the Unreasonable.'

Key Passage

'She would of been a good woman,' The Misfit said, 'if it had been somebody there to shoot her every minute of her life.'

Characters

The Grandmother

The story's central consciousness and its most complex character. She is a self-righteous, manipulative, class-conscious Southern woman whose manipulation of the family causes the accident that leads to their deaths. Yet in her final moment of terror, she reaches out to The Misfit and calls him 'one of my own children' — an act of genuine grace that O'Connor considered the story's climax.

The Misfit

An escaped convict and mass murderer who is, paradoxically, the story's most theologically serious character. He has thought deeply about Jesus and the Resurrection: if Jesus raised the dead, then one must follow him entirely; if he did not, then there is no meaning and one might as well 'enjoy the few minutes you got left the best way you can — by killing somebody or burning down his house or doing some other meanness to him.' He cannot find his way to faith.

Bailey

The grandmother's son, largely passive and dominated by his mother. His failure to control his family — letting the grandmother bring the cat, being talked into the detour — has fatal consequences. He is the story's portrait of weak male authority.

Symbols & Motifs

The Cat

Pitty Sing, the grandmother's cat she has secretly brought on the trip despite Bailey's prohibition, escapes from the basket and causes the car accident. The cat — smuggled in, hidden, uncontrolled — represents the grandmother's deceptions and their consequences.

The Hearse-Like Car

The car the family is driving is described as a hearse-like vehicle. The detail foreshadows their fate.

The Sky

The sky at the story's climax is described as 'cloudless and cold blue' — O'Connor's characteristic use of the natural world to signal divine presence or judgment.

Opening Extract

The grandmother didn't want to go to Florida. She wanted to visit some of her connections in east Tennessee and she was seizing at every chance to change Bailey's mind. Bailey was the son she lived with, her only boy. He was sitting on the edge of his chair at the table, bent over the orange sports section of the Journal. 'Now look here, Bailey,' she said, 'see here, read this,' and she stood with one hand on her thin hip and the other rattling the newspaper at his bald head. 'Here this fellow that calls himself The Misfit is aloose from the Federal Pen and headed toward Florida and you read here what it says he did to these people. Just you read it. I wouldn't take my children in any direction with a criminal like that aloose in it. I couldn't answer to my conscience if I did.'

Legacy & Influence

Published in 1953, 'A Good Man Is Hard to Find' is the most discussed of O'Connor's stories and one of the most analyzed in American literature. O'Connor's essay 'A Reasonable Use of the Unreasonable' (1963) explains her intention: she was writing about a moment of grace — 'the grandmother's head clear for an instant' — which she believed only violence could bring about in a 'hard of hearing' secular audience.

Study Questions

  1. How does O'Connor use the grandmother's self-righteousness to prepare for her moment of grace?
  2. What is the theological argument The Misfit makes, and why does O'Connor take it seriously?
  3. How does the story function as a critique of superficial Christianity?
  4. What does The Misfit mean when he says the grandmother 'would of been a good woman if it had been somebody there to shoot her every minute of her life'?
  5. How does O'Connor use dark comedy in the story?

About Flannery O'Connor

Flannery O'Connor was born on March 25, 1925, in Savannah, Georgia. A devout Catholic writing in the Protestant South, she produced two novels and thirty-two short stories that combined grotesque comedy with fierce theological insight. Her most celebrated works include 'A Good Man Is Hard to Find' and 'The Violent Bear It Away.' She died of lupus in Milledgeville, Georgia, in 1964.
