The Secret Life of Walter Mitty

James Thurber · Written 1939, Published 1939 · ~1,900 words

Published in 1939 in The New Yorker, 'The Secret Life of Walter Mitty' is James Thurber's most famous story. Walter Mitty drives his wife into the city for her weekly hair appointment, running errands between her instructions — and between these mundane activities, he escapes into elaborate heroic fantasies in which he is a Navy commander, a brilliant surgeon, a deadly assassin, and more.

Quick Summary

'The Secret Life of Walter Mitty' is a story by James Thurber, published in 1939. Walter Mitty drives to town with his domineering wife. In the car, in the parking lot, in the drugstore, and outside a building, he has vivid daydreams — as a Navy commander, as a surgeon performing a miracle operation, as a crack pistol shot. Each fantasy is interrupted by reality: his wife, a parking lot attendant, a woman passing. The story ends with Mitty against a wall, a cigarette in his mouth, facing a firing squad in his final fantasy.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Alternating passages of mundane third-person narration and vivid first-person-flavored fantasy, the contrast building the comedy and the pathos.

Themes

The gap between the life we live and the life we imagine — Mitty's fantasies are grandiose precisely because his actual life is so thoroughly managed by others. The story is both comedy and quiet tragedy.

Historical Context

Published in The New Yorker, March 18, 1939. 'Walter Mitty' has entered the language as a common noun for a habitual daydreamer.

Key Passage

He picked up an old copy of Liberty and sank down into the chair. 'Can Germany Conquer the World Through the Air?' Walter Mitty looked at the pictures of bombing planes and of ruined streets.

Extract

'We're going through!' The Commander's voice was like thin ice breaking. He wore his full-dress uniform, with the heavily braided white cap pulled down rakishly over one cold gray eye. 'We can't make it, sir. It's spoiling for a hurricane, if you ask me.' 'I'm not asking you, Lieutenant Berg,' said the Commander. 'Throw on the power lights! Rev her up to 8,500! We're going through!' The pounding of the cylinders increased: ta-pocketa-pocketa-pocketa-pocketa-pocketa. The Commander stared at the ice forming on the pilot window. He walked over and twisted a row of complicated dials. 'Switch on No. 8 auxiliary!' he shouted. 'Switch on No. 8 auxiliary!' repeated Lieutenant Berg. 'Full strength in No. 3 turret!' shouted the Commander. 'Full strength in No. 3 turret!' The crew, bending to their various tasks in the huge, hurtling eight-engined Navy hydroplane, looked at each other and grinned. 'The Old Man'll get us through,' they said to one another. 'The Old Man ain't afraid of Hell!' . . .

'Not so fast! You're driving too fast!' said Mrs. Mitty. 'What are you driving so fast for?'

About James Thurber

James Thurber was born on December 8, 1894, in Columbus, Ohio. A longtime staff writer and cartoonist at The New Yorker, he produced some of the finest comic writing in American literature. 'The Secret Life of Walter Mitty' (1939) is his most celebrated story. He died in New York in 1961.
