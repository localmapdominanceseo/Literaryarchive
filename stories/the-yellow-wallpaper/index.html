The Yellow Wallpaper

Charlotte Perkins Gilman · Written 1890, Published 1892 · ~6,000 words

Published in 1892 in The New England Magazine, 'The Yellow Wallpaper' is Charlotte Perkins Gilman's semi-autobiographical tale of a woman confined to a nursery by her physician husband as a 'rest cure' for nervous depression. Narrated through her secret journal, the story tracks her increasing obsession with the yellow wallpaper on the walls — and her eventual identification with the woman she imagines trapped behind its pattern.

Quick Summary

'The Yellow Wallpaper' is a short story by Charlotte Perkins Gilman, published in 1892. A woman suffering from post-partum depression is prescribed total rest by her physician husband and confined to a room with yellow wallpaper. She becomes obsessed with the wallpaper's pattern and eventually believes a woman is trapped behind it. The story is a feminist critique of the 'rest cure' and of male authority over women's bodies and minds.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person journal entries, the form fragmenting as the narrator's mental state deteriorates — a structural enactment of her psychological unraveling.

Themes

The story attacks the 'rest cure' prescribed by S. Weir Mitchell, which Gilman herself underwent. It explores the oppression of women by medical and domestic authority, the connection between creativity and mental health, and the way enforced passivity drives the narrator into psychosis rather than healing her.

Historical Context

Gilman wrote the story after her own experience of the rest cure and sent a copy to Mitchell, who reportedly changed his treatment practices. The story was largely ignored for decades before being rediscovered by feminist critics in the 1970s as a foundational text.

Key Passage

The front pattern does move—and no wonder! The woman behind shakes it! Sometimes I think there are a great many women behind, and sometimes only one, and she crawls around fast, and her crawling shakes it all over.

Extract

It is very seldom that mere ordinary people like John and myself secure ancestral halls for the summer.

A colonial mansion, a hereditary estate, I would say a haunted house, and reach the height of romantic felicity—but that would be asking too much of fate!

Still I will proudly declare that there is something queer about it.

Else, why should it be let so cheaply? And why have stood so long untenanted?

John laughs at me, of course, but one expects that in marriage.

John is practical in the extreme. He has no patience with faith, an intense horror of superstition, and he scoffs openly at any talk of things not to be felt and seen and put down in figures.

John is a physician, and perhaps—(I would not say it to a living soul, of course, but this is dead paper and a great relief to my mind)—perhaps that is one reason I do not get well faster.

You see he does not believe I am sick!

And what can one do?

If a physician of high standing, and one's own husband, assures friends and relatives that there is really nothing the matter with one but temporary nervous depression—a slight hysterical tendency—what is one to do?

My brother is also a physician, and also of high standing, and he says the same thing.

So I take phosphates or phosphites—whichever it is, and tonics, and journeys, and air, and exercise, and am absolutely forbidden to 'work' until I am well again.

Personally, I disagree with their ideas.

Personally, I believe that congenial work, with excitement and change, would do me good.

But what is one to do?

Public domain. First published in The New England Magazine, January 1892.

About Charlotte Perkins Gilman

Charlotte Perkins Gilman was born on July 3, 1860, in Hartford, Connecticut. A pioneering feminist intellectual, she is best known for her semi-autobiographical story 'The Yellow Wallpaper' (1892), which drew on her own experience of the rest cure. Her non-fiction work Women and Economics (1898) was internationally influential. She died in Pasadena, California, in 1935.
