The Bet

Anton Chekhov · Written 1889, Published 1889 · ~3,500 words

Published in 1889, 'The Bet' describes a dinner party argument between a banker and a young lawyer about which is crueler — capital punishment or life imprisonment. The banker bets two million rubles that the lawyer cannot endure fifteen years of voluntary solitary confinement. The lawyer agrees. Fifteen years later, on the eve of winning the bet, the lawyer writes a letter renouncing the money.

Quick Summary

'The Bet' is a short story by Anton Chekhov, published in 1889. A banker bets a lawyer two million rubles that he cannot endure fifteen years of voluntary solitary confinement. The lawyer agrees and spends fifteen years in a lodge, reading voraciously. The night before the bet expires, the banker — now nearly bankrupt — plans to kill him. He finds a letter: the lawyer, having read everything and learned everything, has decided that worldly goods are worthless and will leave before the fifteen years expire, forfeiting the money.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A framing narrative — the story of the bet told in retrospect by the banker — gives the story its ironic shape.

Themes

The pursuit of wisdom and its consequences — the lawyer's fifteen years of reading have given him a philosophical detachment that makes the money meaningless to him. The story argues that genuine wisdom leads to renunciation of worldly ambition.

Historical Context

Published in 1889 in Novoye Vremya. One of Chekhov's most philosophical stories.

Key Passage

You are out of your mind. In all fifteen years I have studied earthly life. It is true I have become wiser from your books; I have renounced all worldly goods. You have lost your bet.

Extract

It was a dark autumn night. The old banker was walking up and down his study and remembering how, fifteen years before, he had given a party one autumn evening. There had been many clever men there, and there had been interesting conversations. Among other things they had talked of capital punishment. The majority of the guests, among whom were many journalists and intellectual men, disapproved of the death penalty. They considered that form of punishment out of date, immoral, and unsuitable for Christian States. In the opinion of some of them the death penalty ought to be replaced everywhere by imprisonment for life.

'I don't agree with you,' said their host the banker. 'I have not tried either the death penalty or imprisonment for life, but if one may judge a priori, the death penalty is more moral and more humane than imprisonment for life. Capital punishment kills a man at once, but lifelong imprisonment kills him slowly. Which executioner is the more humane, he who kills you in a few minutes or he who drags the life out of you in the course of many years?'

Public domain. First published in Novoye Vremya, 1889. Translated from Russian.

About Anton Chekhov

Anton Chekhov was born on January 29, 1860, in Taganrog, Russia. Working as a physician while writing, he produced hundreds of short stories that revolutionized the form — replacing plot-driven narratives with atmosphere, implication, and the depiction of ordinary life's quiet suffering. He is also considered the father of modern drama. He died of tuberculosis in Badenweiler, Germany, in 1904.
