The Diamond as Big as the Ritz

F. Scott Fitzgerald · Written 1922, Published 1922 · ~17,000 words

Published in 1922 in The Smart Set, 'The Diamond as Big as the Ritz' is F. Scott Fitzgerald's satirical fantasy about the American worship of wealth. John T. Unger visits his prep-school friend Percy Washington at his Montana estate, where the Washington family has kept secret a diamond the size of a mountain — and killed anyone who might expose it — for over a hundred years.

Quick Summary

'The Diamond as Big as the Ritz' is a story by F. Scott Fitzgerald, published in 1922. John visits Percy Washington whose family owns a diamond mountain in Montana, hidden from the world. The family has enslaved workers and shoots down planes that fly too close. When the government discovers the estate, Percy's father tries to bribe God. The estate is bombed. John and two sisters escape, their illusions shattered.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with a deliberately fairy-tale quality — the story is a fable, not a realist story, and Fitzgerald signals this from the opening.

Themes

The corruption of the American Dream by the worship of money — the Washington family's diamond represents the extreme endpoint of American capitalist ambition: beautiful, isolated, maintained by violence and slavery, and ultimately destroyed.

Historical Context

Published in The Smart Set, June 1922. Collected in Tales of the Jazz Age (1922). Written in 1921 while Fitzgerald was working on The Great Gatsby.

Key Passage

'It was a great sin if I did it,' said Mr. Washington. 'Washington never did anything without being sure it was absolutely necessary.'

Extract

John T. Unger came from a family that had been well known in Hades — a small town on the Mississippi River — for several generations. John's father had held the amateur golf championship through many a heated contest; Mrs. Unger was known 'from hot-box to hot-box,' as the local phrase went, for her political addresses; and young John T. Unger, who had just turned sixteen, had danced all the latest dances from New York before he put on long trousers. And now, for a certain time, he was to go to St. Midas' School near Boston — Hades was too small to hold him any longer.

Public domain. First published in The Smart Set, June 1922.

About F. Scott Fitzgerald

Francis Scott Key Fitzgerald was born on September 24, 1896, in St. Paul, Minnesota. The Great Gatsby (1925) is his masterpiece — the definitive novel of the American Dream's promise and betrayal. His short stories, collected in Flappers and Philosophers and Tales of the Jazz Age, are equally important to the period. He died in Hollywood in 1940.
