Bartleby, the Scrivener

Herman Melville · Written 1853, Published 1853 · ~14,000 words

Published in 1853 in Putnam's Magazine, 'Bartleby, the Scrivener' follows a Manhattan lawyer who hires a new copyist named Bartleby. Bartleby works diligently at first, then begins refusing tasks with the simple phrase 'I would prefer not to,' until he refuses everything and simply stops living.

Quick Summary

'Bartleby, the Scrivener' is a story by Herman Melville, published in 1853. A Wall Street lawyer hires Bartleby as a copyist. Bartleby works well at first, then begins refusing tasks — 'I would prefer not to.' He refuses to copy, to leave the office, to speak, to eat. He is removed to a prison, where he dies, having preferred not to eat. The story ends with the lawyer's meditation: Ah Bartleby! Ah humanity!

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person narration from the bewildered, well-meaning, but ultimately helpless lawyer — whose gradual paralysis in the face of Bartleby mirrors Bartleby's own.

Themes

Passive resistance, the alienation of clerical labor, and the limits of liberal benevolence — the lawyer wants to help Bartleby but cannot penetrate his refusal. Bartleby has been read as a proto-existentialist figure who opts out of participation in society entirely.

Historical Context

Published in Putnam's Monthly Magazine in 1853. Largely unappreciated in Melville's lifetime, 'Bartleby' is now considered one of the greatest American stories.

Extract

I am a rather elderly man. The nature of my avocations for the last thirty years has brought me into more than ordinary contact with what would seem an interesting and somewhat singular set of men, of whom as yet nothing that I know of has ever been written: — I mean the law-copyists or scriveners. I have known very many of them, professionally and privately, and if I pleased, could relate divers histories, at which good-natured gentlemen might smile, and sentimental souls might weep. But I waive the biographies of all other scriveners for a few passages in the life of Bartleby, who was a scrivener of the strangest I ever saw or heard of.

Public domain. First published in Putnam's Monthly Magazine, 1853.

About Herman Melville

Herman Melville was born on August 1, 1819, in New York City. His novel Moby-Dick (1851) is widely considered the greatest American novel. His short fiction, including 'Bartleby, the Scrivener' (1853) and 'Benito Cereno,' is equally celebrated. He died in New York in 1891, largely forgotten, to be rediscovered in the twentieth century.
