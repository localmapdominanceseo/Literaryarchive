The Country of the Blind

H.G. Wells · Written 1904, Published 1904 · ~9,000 words

Published in 1904 in The Strand Magazine, 'The Country of the Blind' describes Nunez, a mountaineer who stumbles upon a hidden valley where all the inhabitants have been blind for fifteen generations and have no concept of sight. He believes 'in the country of the blind, the one-eyed man is king' — but finds instead that his sight is considered a disability.

Quick Summary

'The Country of the Blind' is a story by H.G. Wells, published in 1904. Nunez falls into a hidden valley where all inhabitants have been blind for fifteen generations. He assumes he will rule them. Instead, they pity him — his constant references to 'seeing' make him seem deluded. He falls in love with a woman whose family says he must have his eyes removed to be normal. He refuses and escapes into the mountains.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person adventure narration with philosophical undercurrent — Wells structures the story as an ironic reversal of the proverb 'In the country of the blind, the one-eyed man is king.'

Themes

Relativism and the definition of normality — in a society that has never known sight, sight is not an advantage but a disability. The story questions whether progress and civilization are absolute values or merely conventional ones.

Historical Context

Published in The Strand Magazine in 1904. One of Wells's finest short stories.

Key Passage

In the Country of the Blind the One-eyed Man is King... He had come upon a place where the usual axiom did not apply.

Extract

Three hundred miles and more from Chimborazo, one hundred miles from the snows of Cotopaxi, in the wildest wastes of Ecuador's Andes, there lies that mysterious mountain valley, cut off from the world of men, the Country of the Blind. Long years ago that valley lay so far open to the world that men might come at last through frightful gorges and over an icy pass into its equable meadows; and thither indeed men came, a family or so of Peruvian half-breeds fleeing from the lust and tyranny of an evil Spanish ruler. Then came the stupendous outbreak of Mindobamba, when it was night in Quito for seventeen days, and the water was boiling at Yaguachi and all the fish floating dying even as far as Guayaquil; everywhere along the Pacific slopes there were land-slips and swift thawings and sudden floods, and one whole side of the old Arauca crest slipped and came down in thunder, and cut off the Country of the Blind for ever from the exploring feet of men.

Public domain. First published in The Strand Magazine, April 1904.

About H.G. Wells

Herbert George Wells was born on September 21, 1866, in Bromley, Kent, England. He invented the template for modern science fiction with The Time Machine (1895), The War of the Worlds (1898), and The Invisible Man (1897), while also writing realist novels, short stories, and prolific non-fiction. He died in London in 1946.
