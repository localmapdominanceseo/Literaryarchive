The War Prayer

Mark Twain · Written 1905, Published 1923 · ~1,500 words

Written in 1905 and published posthumously in 1923, 'The War Prayer' was considered too inflammatory to publish in Twain's lifetime. A stranger enters a church where the congregation prays for victory in war and delivers the second, unspoken prayer — that God help them tear apart the enemy soldiers, make their children orphans and their wives widows.

Quick Summary

'The War Prayer' is a story by Mark Twain, written 1905, published 1923. A congregation prays passionately for their soldiers to win the war. A stranger appears and says he brings the second half of the prayer — the part they did not speak aloud: that God help them destroy the enemy utterly, lay waste to their country, make widows of their wives, orphans of their children. He is considered a lunatic.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A brief parable — the stranger articulates what the prayer for victory actually entails when made explicit.

Themes

The moral hypocrisy of war prayer — to pray for one side's victory is to pray for the other side's destruction, which no one in church wants to say aloud.

Historical Context

Written 1905 but Twain's family persuaded him not to publish it in his lifetime. Published 1923.

Opening Extract

It was a time of great and exalting excitement. The country was up in arms, the war was on, in every breast burned the holy fire of patriotism; the drums were beating, the bands playing, the toy pistols popping, the bunched firecrackers hissing and spluttering; on every hand and far down the receding and fading spread of roofs and balconies a fluttering wilderness of flags flashed in the sun.

Public domain. Written 1905, published posthumously 1923.

About Mark Twain

Samuel Langhorne Clemens, known as Mark Twain, was born on November 30, 1835, in Florida, Missouri. The Adventures of Tom Sawyer (1876), Adventures of Huckleberry Finn (1884), and The Adventures of Tom Sawyer are among the most celebrated American novels. His satirical stories and essays make him the greatest American humorist. He died in Redding, Connecticut, in 1910.
