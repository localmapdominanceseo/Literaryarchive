In the Penal Colony

Franz Kafka · Written 1914, Published 1919 · ~13,000 words

Published in 1919, 'In the Penal Colony' is Kafka's most disturbing story. A traveler visits a penal colony and witnesses a demonstration of the Old Commandant's judicial apparatus — a machine that inscribes the condemned man's sentence into his body over twelve hours, until the condemned man dies. The Officer who operates it believes in the machine absolutely.

Quick Summary

'In the Penal Colony' is a story by Franz Kafka, published in 1919. An Officer shows a visiting explorer the torture-execution machine he operates — it inscribes the condemned prisoner's sentence into his flesh over twelve hours until death. The Officer explains the old system with reverence. When the explorer refuses to support the machine, the Officer releases the prisoner and inserts himself into the machine, which malfunctions and simply kills him. The colony is already decaying.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration from the perspective of the detached, observing explorer — the horror is increased by the clinical explanations of the Officer.

Themes

The story is a parable about justice, bureaucracy, and the violence inherent in systems of law — the machine writes its judgments into flesh. The Officer's absolute faith in the system is both chilling and pitiable.

Historical Context

Written in 1914, published in 1919. One of Kafka's most explicitly political works, often read as a critique of military and legal bureaucracy.

Key Passage

'The Harrow begins to write; when it finishes the first draft of the inscription on the man's back, the layer of cotton wool turns and slowly rolls the body over, to give the Harrow fresh space for writing.'

Extract

'It is a remarkable piece of apparatus,' said the officer to the explorer and surveyed with a certain air of admiration the apparatus which was after all quite familiar to him. The explorer seemed to have accepted merely out of politeness the Commandant's invitation to witness the execution of a soldier condemned to death for disobedience and insulting behavior to a superior. Nor did the colony itself betray much interest in this execution. At least, in the small sandy valley, a deep hollow surrounded on all sides by naked crags, there was no one present save the officer, the explorer, the condemned man, who was a stupid-looking wide-mouthed creature with bewildered hair and face, and the soldier who held the heavy chain controlling the small chains locked on the prisoner's ankles, wrists and neck, chains which were themselves attached to each other by communicating links.

Public domain. First published in 1919. Translated from German.

About Franz Kafka

Franz Kafka was born on July 3, 1883, in Prague, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Writing in German, he produced some of the most disquieting and influential fiction of the twentieth century: 'The Metamorphosis,' 'The Trial,' 'The Castle,' and 'In the Penal Colony.' Most of his work was published posthumously by his friend Max Brod, against Kafka's explicit instructions to burn it. He died of tuberculosis in Kierling, Austria, in 1924.
