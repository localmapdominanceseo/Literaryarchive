The Aleph

Jorge Luis Borges · Written 1945, Published 1945 · ~6,500 words

Published in 1945 in Sur magazine, 'The Aleph' is Borges's most celebrated story. The narrator visits the insufferable poet Carlos Argentino Daneri, who is writing an epic poem describing every point on earth. In Daneri's cellar the narrator finds the Aleph — a single point of space that contains all other points — and sees everything simultaneously.

Quick Summary

'The Aleph' is a story by Jorge Luis Borges, published in 1945. The narrator, who mourns a dead woman named Beatriz Viterbo, visits her cousin Carlos Argentino, who is writing a poem describing the entire earth. In Carlos's cellar the narrator finds the Aleph — a small sphere about an inch across that contains all space at once. He sees everything: oceans, armies, letters, mirrors, his dead beloved's face. It is indescribable but he tries.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Characteristic Borges: a realistic frame story leading to a metaphysical revelation, presented with mock-scholarly apparatus.

Themes

The impossibility of representing infinity in language — the Aleph contains all space, but the narrator can only describe it sequentially in words. The story is about the limits of literature itself.

Historical Context

Published in Sur magazine, 1945. Collected in El Aleph (1949).

Extract

On the back part of the step, toward the right, I saw a small iridescent sphere of almost unbearable brilliance. At first I thought it was revolving; then I realised that this movement was an illusion created by the dizzying world it enclosed. The Aleph's diameter was probably little more than an inch, but all space was there, actual and undiminished. Each thing (a mirror's face, let us say) was infinite things, since I distinctly saw it from every angle of the universe.

Public domain in many jurisdictions. Published 1945. Translated from Spanish.

About Jorge Luis Borges

Jorge Luis Borges was born on August 24, 1899, in Buenos Aires, Argentina. His short story collections Ficciones (1944) and El Aleph (1949) established him as one of the most influential fiction writers of the twentieth century. His labyrinthine, philosophical stories — mixing detective fiction, fantasy, and metaphysics — founded what would become known as magical realism. He died in Geneva in 1986.
