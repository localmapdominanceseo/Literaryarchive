The Blue Hotel

Stephen Crane · Written 1898, Published 1898 · ~8,500 words

Published in 1898 in Collier's Weekly, 'The Blue Hotel' follows a Swede who arrives at a Nebraska hotel convinced he will be killed in the Wild West. His paranoid behavior provokes the fight he was afraid of. He is killed. The final section argues that everyone present was responsible.

Quick Summary

'The Blue Hotel' is a story by Stephen Crane, published in 1898. A Swede arrives at a Nebraska hotel convinced he will be murdered. His paranoid, aggressive behavior escalates through a card game and a fight until he leaves the hotel and picks a fight with a gambler in a saloon — who stabs him. The final section distributes blame: the Swede's fear created the conditions for his death, but everyone enabled it.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person naturalist narration with a distinctive coda that steps back to analyze responsibility.

Themes

Determinism and collective responsibility — the Swede's death is caused by his fear, but the other characters' failure to act is equally culpable.

Historical Context

Published in Collier's Weekly, November-December 1898. Collected in The Monster and Other Stories (1899).

Opening Extract

The Palace Hotel at Fort Romper was painted a light blue, a shade that is on the legs of a kind of heron, causing the bird to declare its position against any background. The Palace Hotel, then, was always screaming and howling in a way that made the dazzling winter landscape of Nebraska seem only a gray swampish hush.

Public domain. First published in Collier's Weekly, November-December 1898.

About Stephen Crane

Stephen Crane was born on November 1, 1871, in Newark, New Jersey. He produced some of the finest American naturalist writing before dying of tuberculosis at twenty-eight: The Red Badge of Courage (1895), Maggie: A Girl of the Streets (1893), and 'The Open Boat' (1897). He died in Badenweiler, Germany, in 1900.
