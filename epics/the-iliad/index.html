The Iliad

by Homer

Composed c. 8th century BCE Ancient Greek Epic Epic poem, 24 books, dactylic hexameter, c. 15,693 lines

The Iliad is an ancient Greek epic poem traditionally attributed to Homer, composed around the 8th century BCE. Set during the final weeks of the decade-long Trojan War, it follows the rage of the Greek warrior Achilles after a quarrel with Agamemnon, through to Achilles's grief-driven return to battle and his eventual restoration of the body of the Trojan prince Hector to his father.

The Iliad was originally composed in ancient Greek. Several English translations are in the public domain, including Samuel Butler's prose translation (1898) and Alexander Pope's verse translation (1715-1720). This page summarizes the work rather than reproducing any single translation in full.

Overview

The Iliad opens nine years into the Greek siege of Troy, not with the war's beginning but in the middle of a personal crisis: Agamemnon, the Greek commander, has taken the captive woman Briseis from Achilles, the Greeks' greatest warrior, as compensation for having to return another captive to her father. Humiliated, Achilles withdraws from battle and asks his divine mother, the sea-nymph Thetis, to persuade Zeus to let the Greeks suffer in his absence. What follows is not the story of the whole Trojan War, but a tightly focused account of the consequences of Achilles's wrath: the Greeks falter without him, his close companion Patroclus is killed wearing his armor, and Achilles returns to battle consumed by grief and fury, killing the Trojan prince Hector and dragging his body behind his chariot. The poem ends not in triumph but in an unexpected act of shared grief, as Hector's father Priam comes in person to beg for his son's body, and Achilles, recognizing his own father in Priam's grief, relents.

Book-by-Book Summary

Book 1

The Rage of Achilles

Agamemnon refuses to return the captive Chryseis to her father, a priest of Apollo, bringing a plague on the Greek camp. To end it, Agamemnon takes Achilles's prize, Briseis, instead. Furious, Achilles withdraws from the war and asks Thetis to convince Zeus to favor the Trojans until the Greeks recognize his worth.

Book 2

The Gathering of Forces

Zeus sends Agamemnon a false dream promising victory. The Greek army nearly mutinies and sails for home before being rallied. Both armies' forces are cataloged in detail.

Book 3

Paris and Menelaus Duel

The war's original cause, Paris, faces Helen's husband Menelaus in single combat to settle the conflict. Paris is losing badly until the goddess Aphrodite rescues him and spirits him back to Troy.

Book 4

The Armies Clash

The fragile truce collapses when a Trojan archer wounds Menelaus. Full-scale battle resumes between the two armies.

Book 5

Diomedes Fights the Gods

The Greek hero Diomedes, aided by Athena, fights with superhuman ferocity, wounding both the god Ares and the goddess Aphrodite.

Book 6

Hector and Andromache

Amid the fighting, the Trojan prince Hector returns briefly to the city, sharing a tender, sorrowful scene with his wife Andromache and infant son before returning to battle.

Book 7

Hector and Ajax Duel

Hector duels the Greek champion Ajax to a draw before nightfall. Both sides agree to a truce to bury their dead and the Greeks build a defensive wall.

Book 8

The Tide Turns

Zeus forbids the other gods from interfering and the Trojans, led by Hector, push the Greeks back toward their ships.

Book 9

Agamemnon's Embassy to Achilles

With the Greeks in danger, Agamemnon sends envoys offering Achilles immense wealth and the return of Briseis if he will rejoin the fight. Achilles, still wounded in pride, refuses.

Book 10

A Night Raid

Odysseus and Diomedes sneak into the Trojan camp at night, killing a Trojan spy and a sleeping ally contingent.

Book 11

Agamemnon's Triumph and Fall

Agamemnon fights well but is wounded and forced to withdraw. Other Greek heroes are wounded as the Trojans press their advantage toward the ships.

Book 12

The Trojans Storm the Wall

Hector leads the Trojans in breaching the Greek defensive wall, despite warnings from a comrade who reads the omens as unfavorable.

Book 13

The Battle at the Ships

The god Poseidon secretly aids the Greeks, defying Zeus, and the fighting rages around the beached ships.

Book 14

Zeus Deceived

Hera seduces and distracts Zeus so that Poseidon can openly help the Greeks push the Trojans back.

Book 15

The Achaeans at Bay

Zeus wakes, discovers the deception, and forces Poseidon to withdraw. The Trojans surge again, and Hector nearly sets a Greek ship on fire.

Book 16

Patroclus Fights and Dies

Achilles's beloved companion Patroclus persuades Achilles to let him wear his armor and lead the Myrmidons into battle. Patroclus drives the Trojans back but is killed by Hector, who takes Achilles's armor from his body.

Book 17

The Struggle Over Patroclus

A fierce battle rages over Patroclus's corpse, with both sides trying to claim the body.

Book 18

The Shield of Achilles

Achilles learns of Patroclus's death and is consumed by grief. Thetis asks the god Hephaestus to forge Achilles a magnificent new shield and armor, which is described in famous, elaborate detail.

Book 19

Achilles Arms for Battle

Achilles publicly reconciles with Agamemnon, accepts the return of Briseis, and arms himself for war, determined to avenge Patroclus.

Book 20

The Gods Enter the War

Zeus permits the gods to fight openly on both sides. Achilles rampages through the battlefield, nearly killing the Trojan ally Aeneas before the gods intervene to save him.

Book 21

Achilles Fights the River

Achilles slaughters Trojans in such numbers that the river god Scamander rises up against him in fury, nearly drowning him before other gods intervene.

Book 22

The Death of Hector

Hector, abandoned by the gods, finally stands and faces Achilles alone outside the gates of Troy. Achilles kills him, then drags his body behind his chariot back to the Greek camp.

Book 23

The Funeral Games for Patroclus

Achilles holds an elaborate funeral and athletic games in Patroclus's honor, briefly restoring a sense of order and ritual amid the grief.

Book 24

Achilles and Priam

Hector's father, King Priam, comes in secret to the Greek camp to beg for his son's body. Achilles, moved to recognize his own father in Priam's grief, relents, and Hector is returned to Troy for burial.

Themes

The Iliad's central theme is the destructive and ultimately self-defeating nature of personal pride, embodied in Achilles's withdrawal from battle over a perceived insult, which leads directly to the death of his closest companion. Honor and glory, expressed through the Greek concept of kleos, drive nearly every warrior's choices, since a hero's reputation was understood to outlive his body. The poem is equally preoccupied with fate and mortality: both Achilles and Hector know, with near certainty, that they will die young, and the gods' constant intervention raises the question of how much free will humans actually exercise within a fate the gods have already set in motion. Finally, the poem refuses an easy moral about war, portraying Greeks and Trojans with equal dignity and humanity, and ending not with triumph but with two grieving fathers recognizing shared loss in each other.

Historical Context

The Iliad is one of the oldest surviving works of Western literature, traditionally attributed to a poet named Homer and composed around the 8th century BCE, though it likely drew on a much older oral storytelling tradition passed down for generations before being written down. It is divided into 24 books and written in dactylic hexameter, the standard meter of ancient Greek epic poetry. The poem assumes its audience already knows the broader story of the Trojan War and Helen's abduction, focusing instead on a single, tightly bounded period near the war's end. Its companion epic, The Odyssey, follows the Greek hero Odysseus's journey home after the war.
