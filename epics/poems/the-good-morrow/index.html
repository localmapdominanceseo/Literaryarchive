The Good-Morrow

by John Donne

Written 1600 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1600 and published posthumously in 1633, this aubade (morning poem) opens with the lovers waking and argues that before they found each other, they were merely sleeping children. Now that they have, their two faces form a perfect hemisphere, a world complete in itself.

Quick Answer

"The Good-Morrow" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker and his beloved wake together and the speaker asks: what were they doing before they fell in love? Merely sleeping, like children. Now awake to each other, their two souls watch over each other's face — each face reflecting the other in a perfect hemisphere — and their world, being equal and mixed, will never die.

I wonder, by my troth, what thou and I
Did, till we loved? Were we not weaned till then?
But sucked on country pleasures, childishly?
Or snorted we in the Seven Sleepers' den?
'Twas so; but this, all pleasures fancies be.
If ever any beauty I did see,
Which I desired, and got, 'twas but a dream of thee.

And now good morrow to our waking souls,
Which watch not one another out of fear;
For love, all love of other sights controls,
And makes one little room an everywhere.
Let sea-discoverers to new worlds have gone,
Let maps to other, worlds on worlds have shown,
Let us possess one world; each hath one, and is one.

My face in thine eye, thine in mine appears,
And true plain hearts do in the faces rest;
Where can we find two better hemispheres,
Without sharp north, without declining west?
Whatever dies was not mixed equally;
If our two loves be one, or, thou and I
Love so alike, that none do slacken, none can die.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three seven-line stanzas with a complex rhyme scheme, each stanza building from the witty question of the first stanza toward the metaphysical resolution of the third.

Themes

Love as a complete world that supersedes all other experience. The geographical conceit — each lover's face is a hemisphere, and together they form a perfect sphere, a world unto themselves — is one of Donne's most elegant. The closing argument is that perfect, equal love cannot decay.

Historical Context

Part of Songs and Sonnets, published posthumously 1633. Widely considered one of Donne's finest love poems for its movement from playful wit to genuine philosophical argument.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
