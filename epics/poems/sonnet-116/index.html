Sonnet 116 (Let Me Not to the Marriage of True Minds)

by William Shakespeare

Written 1595 Published 1609 English Renaissance Public Domain

One of Shakespeare's most celebrated sonnets, published in 1609, this poem defines love by what it is not — not a thing that changes when circumstances change, or bends when tested — arguing that love is a fixed, permanent star that guides ships through storms.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 116 is one of Shakespeare's most famous definitions of love, published in 1609. It argues that true love is immovable and constant — it does not change when circumstances change, does not bend when the beloved changes, and endures through time until the very end of life. The speaker stakes his reputation as a poet on this claim: if he is wrong, he has never written, and no man has ever loved.

Let me not to the marriage of true minds
Admit impediments. Love is not love
Which alters when it alteration finds,
Or bends with the remover to remove:
O, no! it is an ever-fixed mark,
That looks on tempests and is never shaken;
It is the star to every wandering bark,
Whose worth's unknown, although his height be taken.
Love's not Time's fool, though rosy lips and cheeks
Within his bending sickle's compass come;
Love alters not with his brief hours and weeks,
But bears it out even to the edge of doom.
If this be error and upon me proved,
I never writ, nor no man ever loved.

Public domain. Text per the Folger Shakespeare Library.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter with the rhyme scheme ABAB CDCD EFEF GG, structured as a series of negative definitions — love is not this, not that — before ending in the bold wager of the couplet.

Themes

The permanence and unconditional nature of true love. Each quatrain eliminates a form of false or conditional love: love that alters when circumstances alter, love that is swayed by obstacles, love that decays with time and physical beauty. What remains — the 'ever-fixed mark,' the 'star' — is an absolute that guides without being fully understood.

Historical Context

Published in the 1609 quarto Shake-speares Sonnets as part of the Fair Youth sequence, this sonnet is one of the most frequently quoted definitions of love in the English language, read at weddings, memorialized in speeches, and cited across popular culture.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
