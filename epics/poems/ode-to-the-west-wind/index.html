Ode to the West Wind

by Percy Bysshe Shelley

Written 1819 Published 1820 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written in a single sitting on October 25, 1819, near Florence, Italy, and published in 1820, this ode addresses the West Wind as both destroyer and preserver, asking it to scatter the poet's words across the world as it does the autumn leaves.

Quick Answer

"Ode to the West Wind" is a poem by Percy Bysshe Shelley, written in 1819 and published in 1820. In five linked sonnets, the speaker addresses the West Wind, a force of both destruction and renewal that strips leaves in autumn and spreads seeds for spring. The speaker, feeling trapped and diminished, begs the Wind to take him up and scatter his thoughts and words across humanity as it does the autumn leaves.

O wild West Wind, thou breath of Autumn's being,
Thou, from whose unseen presence the leaves dead
Are driven, like ghosts from an enchanter fleeing,

Yellow, and black, and pale, and hectic red,
Pestilence-stricken multitudes: O thou,
Who chariotest to their dark wintry bed

The winged seeds, where they lie cold and low,
Each like a corpse within its grave, until
Thine azure sister of the Spring shall blow

Her clarion o'er the dreaming earth, and fill
(Driving sweet buds like flocks to feed in air)
With living hues and odours plain and hill:

Wild Spirit, which art moving everywhere;
Destroyer and Preserver; hear, O hear!

Thou on whose stream, 'mid the steep sky's commotion,
Loose clouds like Earth's decaying leaves are shed,
Shook from the tangled boughs of Heaven and Ocean,

Angels of rain and lightning: there are spread
On the blue surface of thine airy surge,
Like the bright hair uplifted from the head

Of some fierce Maenad, even from the dim verge
Of the horizon to the zenith's height,
The locks of the approaching storm. Thou dirge

Of the dying year, to which this closing night
Will be the dome of a vast sepulchre,
Vaulted with all thy congregated might

Of vapours, from whose solid atmosphere
Black rain, and fire, and hail will burst: O hear!

Thou who didst waken from his summer dreams
The blue Mediterranean, where he lay,
Lull'd by the coil of his crystalline streams,

Beside a pumice isle in Baiae's bay,
And saw in sleep old palaces and towers
Quivering within the wave's intenser day,

All overgrown with azure moss and flowers
So sweet, the sense faints picturing them! Thou
For whose path the Atlantic's level powers

Cleave themselves into chasms, while far below
The sea-blooms and the oozy woods which wear
The sapless foliage of the ocean, know

Thy voice, and suddenly grow grey with fear,
And tremble and despoil themselves: O hear!

If I were a dead leaf thou mightest bear;
If I were a swift cloud to fly with thee;
A wave to pant beneath thy power, and share

The impulse of thy strength, only less free
Than thou, O Uncontrollable! If even
I were as in my boyhood, and could be

The comrade of thy wanderings over Heaven,
As then, when to outstrip thy skiey speed
Scarce seem'd a vision; I would ne'er have striven

As thus with thee in prayer in my sore need.
Oh! lift me as a wave, a leaf, a cloud!
I fall upon the thorns of life! I bleed!

A heavy weight of hours has chain'd and bow'd
One too like thee: tameless, and swift, and proud.

Make me thy lyre, even as the forest is:
What if my leaves are falling like its own!
The tumult of thy mighty harmonies

Will take from both a deep, autumnal tone,
Sweet though in sadness. Be thou, Spirit fierce,
My spirit! Be thou me, impetuous one!

Drive my dead thoughts over the universe
Like wither'd leaves to quicken a new birth!
And, by the incantation of this verse,

Scatter, as from an unextinguish'd hearth
Ashes and sparks, my words among mankind!
Be through my lips to unawaken'd Earth

The trumpet of a prophecy! O Wind,
If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five sections, each a fourteen-line sonnet in terza rima (the Italian interlocking rhyme scheme aba bcb cdc ded ee used in Dante's Divine Comedy), creating a continuous forward motion across all five sections rather than five self-contained units.

Themes

The poem moves from the Wind as an observed force of nature in sections one through three, to the speaker's personal identification with the Wind in section four, to a plea for the Wind to use the poet as its instrument in section five. The famous closing question, 'If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?', argues that destruction and renewal are inseparable, and that the scattering of old forms is the precondition for new ones — a political as much as a natural claim, written in the immediate aftermath of the Peterloo Massacre.

Historical Context

Shelley wrote the poem on October 25, 1819, in the Cascine wood near Florence. The political context was charged: the Peterloo Massacre of August 1819, when soldiers fired on a crowd of reform protesters in Manchester, had appalled him. He wrote several other directly political poems at the same time, and 'Ode to the West Wind' can be read as an indirect expression of his hope that radical ideas, like seeds, would survive their suppression and germinate in a future spring.

About Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley was born on August 4, 1792, in Horsham, England, and became one of the major poets of English Romanticism despite controversy during his lifetime over his radical political and religious views. His poetry combines lyrical intensity with sustained engagement with politics, philosophy, and the natural world. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy in 1822, at the age of twenty-nine.
