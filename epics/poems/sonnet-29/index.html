Sonnet 29 (When, in Disgrace with Fortune and Men's Eyes)

by William Shakespeare

Written 1595 Published 1609 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1609, this sonnet opens with the speaker at his lowest — outcast, envious of others, cursing his fate — before the thought of his beloved's love lifts him so completely that he would not exchange his state for that of kings.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 29 is a Shakespeare sonnet published in 1609. The speaker begins in a state of disgrace and self-pity, envying other men their skills and opportunities, feeling himself cursed by fortune. Then the thought of his beloved's love transforms his mood entirely — like a lark rising at dawn to sing at heaven's gate — and he concludes that with this love, he would not trade places with any king.

When, in disgrace with fortune and men's eyes,
I all alone beweep my outcast state,
And trouble deaf heaven with my bootless cries,
And look upon myself and curse my fate,
Wishing me like to one more rich in hope,
Featured like him, like him with friends possessed,
Desiring this man's art and that man's scope,
With what I most enjoy contented least;
Yet in these thoughts myself almost despising,
Haply I think on thee, and then my state,
Like to the lark at break of day arising
From sullen earth, sings hymns at heaven's gate;
For thy sweet love remembered such wealth brings
That then I scorn to change my state with kings.

Public domain. Text per the Folger Shakespeare Library.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter with the rhyme scheme ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. The poem's dramatic turn arrives in line 9 with 'Yet in these thoughts myself almost despising / Haply I think on thee' — one of Shakespeare's most effective volta moments.

Themes

The redemptive power of love against self-contempt and social shame. The poem catalogs the speaker's miseries in detail — unheard prayers, disgrace, envy, self-loathing — and then dispels them all in a single thought. The lark image, rising from the 'sullen earth' to 'sing hymns at heaven's gate,' captures the sudden, complete reversal.

Historical Context

Part of the Fair Youth sequence. The opening reference to 'disgrace with Fortune and men's eyes' may reflect professional or social setbacks Shakespeare experienced, though scholars caution against reading the sonnets as straightforward autobiography.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
