In Memoriam A.H.H. (Extract: Canto 27)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1833 Published 1850 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written between 1833 and 1850 after the death of Tennyson's closest friend Arthur Henry Hallam, In Memoriam is a 133-canto elegy and one of the great long poems of the Victorian era. Canto 27 contains the famous lines about it being better to have loved and lost than never to have loved at all.

Quick Answer

"In Memoriam A.H.H." is Tennyson's 133-canto elegy for his friend Arthur Henry Hallam, published in 1850. Canto 27 — among the most quoted passages — argues that it is better to have loved and lost than never to have loved at all, even though love involves grief. The speaker would rather have known his friend and lost him than lived without him.

I envy not in any moods
The captive void of noble rage,
The linnet born within the cage,
That never knew the summer woods:

I envy not the beast that takes
His license in the field of time,
Unfetter'd by the sense of crime,
To whom a conscience never wakes;

Nor, what may count itself as blest,
The heart that never plighted troth
But stagnates in the weeds of sloth;
Nor any want-begotten rest.

I hold it true, whate'er befall;
I feel it, when I sorrow most;
'Tis better to have loved and lost
Than never to have loved at all.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Each canto of In Memoriam uses the same four-line stanza in iambic tetrameter with an ABBA rhyme scheme, now called the 'In Memoriam stanza.' The enclosed rhyme scheme creates a sense of contained, circling grief.

Themes

The relationship between love and loss, and whether love is worth the grief it entails. Tennyson's answer — better to have loved and lost — is not a consolation but a conviction: grief is the price of love, and love is worth the price.

Historical Context

Tennyson wrote the poem over seventeen years, beginning shortly after Hallam's death in 1833. Published in 1850, it became one of Queen Victoria's favorite poems and she found consolation in it after the death of Prince Albert.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
