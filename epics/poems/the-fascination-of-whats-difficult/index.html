The Fascination of What's Difficult

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1909 Published 1910 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1910 in The Green Helmet, this short poem expresses Yeats's frustration with the difficulty of managing the Abbey Theatre and Irish public affairs, which have dried up his spontaneous creativity — he swears to find out where she has gone, this Pegasus who was meant to carry him.

Quick Answer

"The Fascination of What's Difficult" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1910. The fascination of what's difficult has dried the sap out of his veins and rent spontaneous joy and natural content out of his heart. Management of theatre and public affairs have killed the winged Pegasus. He swears he'll find out where she has gone and leave both theatre and public affairs.

The fascination of what's difficult
Has dried the sap out of my veins, and rent
Spontaneous joy and natural content
Out of my heart. There's something ails our colt
That must, as if it had not holy blood,
Nor on an Olympus leaped from cloud to cloud,
Shiver under the lash, strain, sweat and jolt
As though it dragged road-metal. My curse on plays
That have to be set up in fifty ways,
On the day's war with every knave and dolt,
Theatre business, management of men.
I swear before the dawn comes round again
I'll find the stable and pull out the bolt.

Public domain (published 1910). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twelve lines of iambic pentameter in irregular rhyme, the poem's complaint expressed directly and with bitter humor.

Themes

The conflict between administrative duties and creative freedom, between public responsibility and the joy of spontaneous art.

Historical Context

Published in The Green Helmet (1910). Yeats co-managed the Abbey Theatre in Dublin and found the work exhausting and creatively draining.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
