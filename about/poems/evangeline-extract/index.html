Evangeline (Extract)

by Henry Wadsworth Longfellow

Written 1847 Published 1847 American Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1847, Evangeline is Longfellow's epic poem about the expulsion of the Acadians from Nova Scotia in 1755. The opening passage, with its celebrated first line, sets the scene of the peaceful Acadian village of Grand-Pré before the catastrophe falls.

Quick Answer

"Evangeline" is Longfellow's 1847 epic poem about the expulsion of Acadians from Nova Scotia, following Evangeline and her lost love Gabriel across the continent. The opening, beginning 'This is the forest primeval,' establishes the murmuring pines and hemlocks of the primeval forest and introduces the village of Grand-Pré in its lost pastoral happiness.

This is the forest primeval. The murmuring pines and the hemlocks,
Bearded with moss, and in garments green, indistinct in the twilight,
Stand like Druids of eld, with voices sad and prophetic,
Stand like harpers hoar, with beards that rest on their bosoms.
Loud from its rocky caverns, the deep-voiced neighboring ocean
Speaks, and in accents disconsolate answers the wail of the forest.

This is the forest primeval; but where are the hearts that beneath it
Leaped like the roe, when he hears in the woodland the voice of the huntsman?
Where is the thatch-roofed village, the home of Acadian farmers,—
Men whose lives glided on like rivers that water the woodlands,
Darkened by shadows of earth, but reflecting an image of heaven?
Waste are those pleasant farms, and the farmers forever departed!
Scattered like dust and leaves, when the mighty blasts of October
Seize them, and whirl them aloft, and sprinkle them far o'er the ocean.
Naught but tradition remains of the beautiful village of Grand-Pré.

Ye who believe in affection that hopes, and endures, and is patient,
Ye who believe in the beauty and strength of woman's devotion,
List to the mournful tradition, still sung by the pines of the forest;
List to a Tale of Love in Acadie, home of the happy.

Public domain. Published 1847.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Dactylic hexameter — the meter of Homer and Virgil — applied to an American subject, Longfellow's deliberate attempt to write an American epic in the classical tradition.

Themes

The loss of a paradise and the endurance of love through separation. The forest primeval that opens the poem frames everything that follows as lost — the poem is an elegy for a world that no longer exists.

Historical Context

Published in 1847, it was the first major American poem in classical hexameter and immediately became Longfellow's most celebrated work. It was based on the historical Deportation of the Acadians in 1755.

About Henry Wadsworth Longfellow

Henry Wadsworth Longfellow was born on February 27, 1807, in Portland, Maine, and became the most popular and widely read American poet of the nineteenth century. His accessible style, traditional meters, and frequent use of historical and legendary American subject matter, as in 'Paul Revere's Ride' and The Song of Hiawatha, made his work a fixture of American education for generations. He taught modern languages at Harvard for many years and died in Cambridge, Massachusetts, in 1882.
