Auguries of Innocence (Opening)

by William Blake

Written 1803 Published 1863 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1803 and published posthumously in 1863, this poem opens with four of the most famous lines in English poetry — 'To see a World in a Grain of Sand' — before continuing through a long series of moral couplets. This is the celebrated opening passage.

Quick Answer

"Auguries of Innocence" is a poem by William Blake, written around 1803 and published posthumously in 1863. It opens with the famous quatrain describing the ability to see a world in a grain of sand and hold infinity in an hour, before continuing through moral couplets about the cruelty of caging birds and the consequences of treating living creatures with contempt.

To see a World in a Grain of Sand
And a Heaven in a Wild Flower,
Hold Infinity in the palm of your hand
And Eternity in an hour.

A Robin Red breast in a Cage
Puts all Heaven in a Rage.
A Dove house fill'd with Doves & Pigeons
Shudders Hell thro' all its regions.
A dog starv'd at his Master's Gate
Predicts the ruin of the State.
A Horse misus'd upon the Road
Calls to Heaven for Human blood.
Each outcry of the hunted Hare
A fibre from the Brain does tear.
A Skylark wounded in the wing,
A Cherubim does cease to sing.
The Game Cock clipp'd & arm'd for fight
Does the Rising Sun affright.
Every Wolf's & Lion's howl
Raises from Hell a Human Soul.
The wild deer, wand'ring here & there,
Keeps the Human Soul from Care.
The Lamb misus'd breeds Public Strife
And yet forgives the Butcher's knife.

Public domain. Written c.1803, published 1863.

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem in full runs to 132 lines of rhyming couplets, but the opening quatrain stands apart as a concentrated statement of Blake's visionary perception, followed by paired moral observations in the voice of a prophet.

Themes

The possibility of visionary perception that sees the infinite in the particular, and the moral consequences of treating living creatures and children cruelly. Each couplet in the body of the poem presents an action and its cosmic consequence.

Historical Context

Blake wrote the poem in his notebook around 1803 but it was not published until 1863 in Alexander Gilchrist's biography of Blake. The opening quatrain has become one of the most quoted passages in English literature.

About William Blake

William Blake was born on November 28, 1757, in London, England, and worked as a poet, painter, and printmaker, producing illustrated volumes of poetry that he engraved, printed, and hand-colored himself. Largely unrecognized during his lifetime, he is now considered one of the founding figures of English Romanticism. His best-known collection, Songs of Innocence and of Experience, pairs poems of childlike wonder with darker counterparts exploring suffering and moral complexity. He died in London in 1827.
