The Road to Mandalay

by Rudyard Kipling

Written 1890 Published 1890 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1890 in Barrack-Room Ballads, this poem is spoken by a British soldier who has returned home from Burma (Myanmar) and longs to go back, haunted by memories of the temple bells, the Burmese girl by the old Moulmein Pagoda, and the road that leads back to Mandalay.

Quick Answer

"The Road to Mandalay" is a poem by Rudyard Kipling, published in 1890. A British soldier who served in Burma and has returned to London longs to go back to Mandalay — the Burmese city where he remembers a girl by a pagoda, flying fishes, the dawn coming up like thunder, and a life that felt freer and more real than gloomy England.

By the old Moulmein Pagoda, lookin' eastward to the sea,
There's a Burma girl a-settin', and I know she thinks o' me;
For the wind is in the palm-trees, and the temple-bells they say:
"Come you back, you British soldier; come you back to Mandalay!"
Come you back to Mandalay,
Where the old Flotilla lay:
Can't you 'ear their paddles chunkin' from Rangoon to Mandalay?
On the road to Mandalay,
Where the flyin'-fishes play,
An' the dawn comes up like thunder outer China 'crost the Bay!

'Er petticoat was yaller an' 'er little cap was green,
An' 'er name was Supi-yaw-lat—jes' the same as Theebaw's Queen,
An' I seed her first a-smokin' of a whackin' white cheroot,
An' a-wastin' Christian kisses on an 'eathen idol's foot:
Bloomy' an' fragrant—I remember how she'd say:
"Come you back, you British soldier; come you back to Mandalay!"
Come you back to Mandalay...

Ship me somewheres east of Suez, where the best is like the worst,
Where there aren't no Ten Commandments an' a man can raise a thirst;
For the temple-bells are callin', an' it's there that I would be—
By the old Moulmein Pagoda, looking lazy at the sea;
On the road to Mandalay,
Where the old Flotilla lay,
With our sick beneath the awnings when we went to Mandalay!
On the road to Mandalay,
Where the flyin'-fishes play,
An' the dawn comes up like thunder outer China 'crost the Bay!

Public domain. Published 1890 in Barrack-Room Ballads.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six stanzas of eight lines each in ballad meter, written in Cockney/working-class dialect to capture the voice of a common soldier rather than an officer or educated speaker, with a highly musical refrain that drives the poem's nostalgic longing.

Themes

Nostalgia for a romanticized colonial past and the contrast between the soldier's drab return to England and his vivid memories of Burma. The poem is one of Kipling's most famous Barrack-Room Ballads, capturing the perspective of the ordinary British soldier in the empire with sympathy, even as it reflects the era's imperialist assumptions.

Historical Context

Kipling published the poem in 1890 in Barrack-Room Ballads, a collection written from the perspective of ordinary British soldiers in the empire. He had spent time in India and traveled through Burma, drawing on those experiences. The poem was set to music and became one of the most popular songs of the late Victorian era.

About Rudyard Kipling

Rudyard Kipling was born on December 30, 1865, in Bombay (now Mumbai), British India, and became one of the most popular British writers of the late Victorian and Edwardian periods. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1907. His work, including The Jungle Book, Kim, and the Barrack-Room Ballads, drew heavily on his experiences of British India and of ordinary soldiers and working-class voices. He died in London in 1936.
