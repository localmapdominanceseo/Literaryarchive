Hope is the Thing with Feathers

by Emily Dickinson

Written 1861 Published 1891 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1861 and published posthumously in 1891, this poem defines hope through an extended metaphor of a bird that perches in the soul and never stops singing, even in the worst storms.

Quick Answer

"Hope is the Thing with Feathers" is a poem by Emily Dickinson, written around 1861 and published posthumously in 1891. It defines hope as a bird perching in the soul, singing wordlessly without pause, sustaining the speaker through storms and cold, and never once asking for anything in return.

'Hope' is the thing with feathers—
That perches in the soul—
And sings the tune without the words—
And never stops—at all—

And sweetest—in the Gale—is heard—
And sore must be the storm—
That could abash the little Bird
That kept so many warm—

I've heard it in the chillest land—
And on the strangest Sea—
Yet—never—in Extremity,
It asked a crumb—of Me.

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains in Dickinson's characteristic common meter (alternating lines of eight and six syllables), with slant rhyme rather than perfect rhyme — 'gale' and 'soul,' 'tune' and 'on' — creating the subtly off-kilter quality typical of her work.

Themes

Hope as an innate, resilient, and selfless force within the human spirit. The bird metaphor emphasizes that hope requires nothing to sustain itself — it asks 'nothing of me' even in 'the chillest land' and 'the strangest Sea.' Unlike other comforts, hope is simply present, persistent, and unconditional.

Historical Context

Dickinson wrote the poem around 1861, during the early years of the American Civil War, a period of tremendous national grief and uncertainty. She published almost none of her work during her lifetime; the poem first appeared in 1891, five years after her death, in a posthumous collection edited by Mabel Loomis Todd and Thomas Wentworth Higginson.

About Emily Dickinson

Emily Dickinson was born on December 10, 1830, in Amherst, Massachusetts, and spent nearly her entire life there. Though she wrote nearly 1,800 poems, fewer than a dozen were published during her lifetime. Her work, marked by unconventional capitalization, slant rhyme, and dashes, was largely discovered and published after her death in 1886. She is now considered one of the most important and original voices in American poetry, known for her meditations on death, immortality, nature, and the inner life.
