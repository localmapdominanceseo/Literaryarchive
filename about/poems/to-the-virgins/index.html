To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time

by Robert Herrick

Written 1648 Published 1648 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1648 in Hesperides, this short carpe diem lyric uses the image of the rose that blooms and quickly fades to urge young women to marry while they are young, before time steals their youth and opportunity passes.

Quick Answer

"To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time" is a poem by Robert Herrick, published in 1648. Using the image of a rose that flowers and fades within a day, the speaker urges young women to gather their rosebuds while they may — that is, to marry and embrace life's pleasures while young — because time moves quickly and youth, once gone, cannot be recovered.

Gather ye rosebuds while ye may,
Old Time is still a-flying;
And this same flower that smiles today
Tomorrow will be dying.

The glorious lamp of heaven, the sun,
The higher he's a-getting,
The sooner will his race be run,
And nearer he's to setting.

That age is best which is the first,
When youth and blood are warmer;
But being spent, the worse, and worst
Times still succeed the former.

Then be not coy, but use your time,
And while ye may, go marry;
For having lost but once your prime,
You may forever tarry.

Public domain. Published 1648 in Hesperides.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of alternating tetrameter and trimeter with an ABAB rhyme scheme, in the traditional ballad-like carpe diem mode. The poem's light, musical quality belies its subject — the relentless loss of time and beauty.

Themes

Carpe diem — seize the day before time takes it. The rose is a traditional symbol of transient beauty; the sun 'nearer he's to setting' echoes classical arguments about the shortness of life. The poem is gentler and less morbid than Marvell's 'To His Coy Mistress,' using the same structure of argument but with floral rather than funereal imagery.

Historical Context

Herrick published the poem in Hesperides (1648), his enormous collection of more than 1,400 poems. It is his most anthologized work and gave rise to the popular phrase 'gather ye rosebuds while ye may.' Herrick was influenced by the Latin poets Horace, Catullus, and Ovid, and this poem closely follows the classical carpe diem tradition.

About Robert Herrick

Robert Herrick was born in London in 1591 and became one of the most prolific of the Cavalier poets, writing elegant, sensuous lyrics drawing on classical models. His collection Hesperides (1648) contained over 1,400 poems, including his most famous work 'To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time.' He served as a vicar in Devon and died in 1674.
