Prospice

by Robert Browning

Written 1861 Published 1864 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1861 shortly after the death of his wife Elizabeth Barrett Browning, and published in 1864, the title means 'look forward' in Latin. The poem is a defiant anticipation of death as a final struggle to be met head-on, ending in reunion with the beloved.

Quick Answer

"Prospice" is a poem by Robert Browning, published in 1864, written after his wife Elizabeth Barrett Browning's death. The speaker — identifying himself as a fighter who will not be led away passively — confronts approaching death as a fierce final battle, welcoming the darkness and the elements, before arriving at the moment of reunion with his beloved, 'with God be the rest!'

Fear death?—to feel the fog in my throat,
The mist in my face,
When the snows begin, and the blasts denote
I am nearing the place,
The power of the night, the press of the storm,
The post of the foe;
Where he stands, the Arch Fear in a visible form,
Yet the strong man must go:
For the journey is done and the summit attained,
And the barriers fall,
Though a battle's to fight ere the guerdon be gained,
The reward of it all.
I was ever a fighter, so—one fight more,
The best and the last!
I would hate that death bandaged my eyes, and forebore,
And bade me creep past.
No! let me taste the whole of it, fare like my peers
The heroes of old,
Bear the brunt, in a minute pay glad life's arrears
Of pain, darkness and cold.
For sudden the worst turns the best to the brave,
The black minute's at end,
And the elements' rage, the fiend-voices that rave,
Shall dwindle, shall blend,
Shall change, shall become first a peace out of pain,
Then a light, then thy breast,
O thou soul of my soul! I shall clasp thee again,
And with God be the rest!

Public domain. Published 1864 in Dramatis Personae.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-eight lines of varied stress patterns and lengths, with an insistent forward momentum created by anaphora and the accumulation of obstacles the speaker embraces.

Themes

The defiant facing of death and the hope of reunion beyond it. Rather than passive acceptance or resignation, the speaker actively embraces death's approach as a warrior embraces battle — because on the other side is the person he loves. The poem is both a statement of Victorian courage in the face of mortality and a very personal expression of Browning's grief.

Historical Context

Browning wrote the poem in 1861 after the death of Elizabeth Barrett Browning in Florence on June 29, 1861. The title, 'Prospice' (Latin: 'look forward'), signals its forward orientation. Published in 1864 in Dramatis Personae.

About Robert Browning

Robert Browning was born on May 7, 1812, in Camberwell, London, and became one of the foremost Victorian poets, celebrated above all for his mastery of the dramatic monologue — a form in which a speaker inadvertently reveals more about themselves than they intend. His collection Men and Women (1855) and his verse novel The Ring and the Book (1868-1869) are considered his greatest achievements. He married Elizabeth Barrett in 1846 and they lived in Italy until her death in 1861. He died in Venice in 1889.
