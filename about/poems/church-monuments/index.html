Church Monuments

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 in The Temple, this poem meditates on church monuments — the marble tombs and carved epitaphs — and uses them to argue that our bodies are dust, and should learn from the monuments how quickly beauty crumbles to the same dust.

Quick Answer

"Church Monuments" is a poem by George Herbert, published in 1633. While his body rests in church while he prays, his flesh is sent to spell its elements and find its dust. The monuments around teach the body what it shall become — the marble itself will crumble to dust in time, which is more than the body. Let the body learn its lesson early.

While that my soul repairs to her devotion,
Here I entomb my flesh, that it betimes
May take acquaintance of this heap of dust;
To which the blast of death's incessant motion,
Fed with the exhalation of our crimes,
Drives all at last. Therefore I gladly trust

My body to this school, that it may learn
To spell his elements, and find his birth
Written in dusty heraldry and lines;
Which dissolution sure doth best discern,
Comparing dust with dust, and earth with earth.
These laugh at jet and marble put for signs,

To sever the good fellowship of dust,
And spoil the meeting. What shall point out them,
When they shall bow, and kneel, and fall down flat
To kiss those heaps, which now they have in trust?
Dear flesh, while I do pray, learn here thy stem
And true descent; that when thou shalt grow fat,

And wanton in thy cravings, thou mayst know,
That flesh is but the glass, which holds the dust
That measures all our time; which also shall
Be crumbled into dust. Mark here below
How tame these ashes are, how free from lust,
That thou mayst fit thyself against thy fall.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six six-line stanzas of iambic pentameter with ABABCC rhyme, the poem's meditation on dissolution formally controlled even as it describes dissolution.

Themes

The leveling power of death — marble and flesh alike return to dust, and the monuments that were meant to preserve memory themselves decay. The poem uses this to argue for spiritual priorities over bodily ones.

Historical Context

Published in The Temple (1633). One of Herbert's most original meditations on mortality, unusual in sending the body alone to the church while the soul prays separately.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
