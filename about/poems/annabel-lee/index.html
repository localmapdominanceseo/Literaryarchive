Annabel Lee

by Edgar Allan Poe

Written 1849 Published 1849 American Gothic Romanticism Public Domain

Poe's final complete poem, written in 1849 and published shortly after his death that same year. It tells of a love so powerful that even envious angels could not let it rest, as the speaker mourns a young bride taken from him and laid in a tomb by the sea.

Quick Answer

"Annabel Lee" is Edgar Allan Poe's final completed poem, written in 1849 and published shortly after his death that year. It describes a young speaker's unbreakable love for Annabel Lee, who dies after envious angels send a chilling wind, and ends with the speaker still lying beside her tomb by the sea each night.

It was many and many a year ago,
   In a kingdom by the sea,
That a maiden there lived whom you may know
   By the name of Annabel Lee;
And this maiden she lived with no other thought
   Than to love and be loved by me.

I was a child and she was a child,
   In this kingdom by the sea:
But we loved with a love that was more than love—
   I and my Annabel Lee;
With a love that the winged seraphs of heaven
   Coveted her and me.

And this was the reason that, long ago,
   In this kingdom by the sea,
A wind blew out of a cloud, chilling
   My beautiful Annabel Lee;
So that her highborn kinsman came
   And bore her away from me,
To shut her up in a sepulchre
   In this kingdom by the sea.

The angels, not half so happy in heaven,
   Went envying her and me—
Yes!—that was the reason (as all men know,
   In this kingdom by the sea)
That the wind came out of the cloud by night,
   Chilling and killing my Annabel Lee.

But our love it was stronger by far than the love
   Of those who were older than we—
   Of many far wiser than we—
And neither the angels in heaven above,
   Nor the demons down under the sea,
Can ever dissever my soul from the soul
   Of the beautiful Annabel Lee:

For the moon never beams, without bringing me dreams
   Of the beautiful Annabel Lee;
And the stars never rise, but I feel the bright eyes
   Of the beautiful Annabel Lee;
And so, all the night-tide, I lie down by the side
Of my darling—my darling—my life and my bride,
   In her sepulchre there by the sea,
   In her tomb by the sounding sea.

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org), from The Works of the Late Edgar Allan Poe, Vol. II, 1850.

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem uses a flowing anapestic meter and heavy repetition of the name 'Annabel Lee' and the phrase 'kingdom by the sea,' creating an incantatory, song-like rhythm typical of Poe's belief that poetry should achieve a musical, hypnotic effect on the reader.

Themes

The poem explores idealized, eternal love that death cannot sever, a theme Poe returned to often. It also reflects his recurring fascination with the death of a beautiful young woman, which he described as the most poetic of all subjects, and frames grief as something the speaker refuses to relinquish even after the beloved's death.

Historical Context

Poe wrote "Annabel Lee" in 1849, the year of his own death, and many scholars have connected it to the loss of his young wife, Virginia Clemm, who died of tuberculosis two years earlier. The poem was sold to a magazine before his death but was not printed until after it, appearing in multiple publications within weeks of one another.

About Edgar Allan Poe

Edgar Allan Poe was born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts. A master of both poetry and short fiction, Poe is credited with helping originate the detective story and significantly shaping the modern horror and gothic genres. His poetry is known for its musicality, melancholy tone, and recurring themes of loss, death, and longing. He died in Baltimore in 1849 under circumstances that remain debated to this day.
