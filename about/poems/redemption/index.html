Redemption

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 in The Temple, this sonnet is structured as a parable: the speaker is a tenant seeking his Lord to ask for a new lease, and after searching in great courts and resorts, finds him at last among thieves and murderers, who is just dying — and grants the suit.

Quick Answer

"Redemption" is a poem by George Herbert, published in 1633. A tenant seeks his rich Lord to cancel his old lease and grant a new, small one. He searches heaven, then great courts, then a resort of fine things — only to find the Lord in the street, among thieves and murderers. The Lord hears him, grants the suit, and dies.

Having been tenant long to a rich Lord,
Not thriving, I resolved to be bold,
And make a suit unto him, to afford
A new small-rented lease, and cancel th' old.
In heaven at his manour I him sought:
They told me there, that he was lately gone
About some land, which he had dearly bought
Long since on earth, to take possession.
I straight returned, and knowing his great birth,
Sought him accordingly in great resorts;
In cities, theatres, gardens, parks, and courts:
At length I heard a ragged noise and mirth
Of thieves and murderers: there I him espied,
Who straight, Your suit is granted, said, and died.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A fourteen-line sonnet in iambic pentameter, the parable structure (tenant and Lord) concealing its religious content (sinner and Christ) until the shocking final line.

Themes

The Incarnation and Crucifixion figured as a landlord granting a new lease — Christ is found not in heavenly courts but among the poor, the criminal, and the dying. The shock of finding the Lord 'among thieves and murderers' is the poem's central revelation.

Historical Context

Published in The Temple (1633). One of Herbert's most structurally brilliant short poems, using the extended parable to conceal and then reveal its theological subject.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
