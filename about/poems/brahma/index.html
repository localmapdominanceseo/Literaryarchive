Brahma

by Ralph Waldo Emerson

Written 1856 Published 1857 American Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1857 in The Atlantic Monthly, this short poem is spoken by Brahma, the Hindu creator deity, who confounds all opposites — slayer and slain are the same, the strong gods long for him, and the one who comes to him finds him.

Quick Answer

"Brahma" is a poem by Ralph Waldo Emerson, published in 1857, spoken in the voice of the Hindu deity Brahma. It argues that all opposites — slayer and slain, doubt and faith, far and near — are illusions that Brahma transcends. If the red slayer thinks he slays, or the slain thinks he is slain, they both fail to know the subtle ways of Brahma.

If the red slayer think he slays,
Or if the slain think he is slain,
They know not well the subtle ways
I keep, and pass, and turn again.

Far or forgot to me is near;
Shadow and sunlight are the same;
The vanished gods to me appear;
And one to me are shame and fame.

They reckon ill who leave me out;
When me they fly, I am the wings;
I am the doubter and the doubt,
I am the hymn the Brahmin sings.

The strong gods pine for my abode,
And pine in vain the sacred Seven;
But thou, meek lover of the good!
Find me, and turn thy back on heaven.

Public domain. Published 1857 in The Atlantic Monthly.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of iambic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme, clean and compressed, the formal control enacting the deity's transcendent perspective above all contradiction.

Themes

The unity of all apparent opposites from a transcendent perspective — the Hindu concept of Brahman as the ultimate reality underlying all apparent distinctions. The poem draws on the Bhagavad Gita's argument that the soul cannot truly be killed.

Historical Context

Published in The Atlantic Monthly in 1857 and collected in May-Day and Other Pieces (1867). One of Emerson's most direct engagements with Hindu philosophy, which profoundly influenced his Transcendentalism.

About Ralph Waldo Emerson

Ralph Waldo Emerson was born on May 25, 1803, in Boston, Massachusetts, and became the central figure of American Transcendentalism — the philosophical and literary movement that emphasized the divinity of nature, individual intuition, and the unity of all things. His Essays (1841 and 1844) and his poetry exerted enormous influence on American literature and thought, and his lecture 'The American Scholar' (1837) has been called America's intellectual Declaration of Independence. He died in Concord, Massachusetts, in 1882.
