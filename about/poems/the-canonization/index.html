The Canonization

by John Donne

Written 1600 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1600 and published posthumously in 1633, this poem responds to an imagined critic who tells the speaker his love is inappropriate or inconvenient, by arguing that love is its own world and its own religion — and that the lovers themselves will be canonized as saints of love.

Quick Answer

"The Canonization" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker tells someone who objects to his love to mind their own business and invest in worldly things instead — for he and his beloved are so complete in each other that they are the world. They will be canonized as saints of love, and future lovers will pray to them for patterns of true devotion.

For God's sake hold your tongue, and let me love,
Or chide my palsy, or my gout,
My five gray hairs, or ruined fortune flout,
With wealth your state, your mind with arts improve,
Take you a course, get you a place,
Observe his Honour, or his Grace,
Or the King's real, or his stampèd face
Contemplate; what you will, approve,
So you will let me love.

Alas, alas, who's injured by my love?
What merchant's ships have my sighs drowned?
Who says my tears have overflowed his ground?
When did my colds a forward spring remove?
When did the heats which my veins fill
Add one more to the plaguy bill?
Soldiers find wars, and lawyers find out still
Litigious men, which quarrels move,
Though she and I do love.

Call us what you will, we are made such by love;
Call her one, me another fly,
We're tapers too, and at our own cost die,
And we in us find the Eagle and the Dove.
The Phoenix riddle hath more wit
By us; we two being one, are it.
So, to one neutral thing both sexes fit,
We die and rise the same, and prove
Mysteriousbylaw of love.

We can die by it, if not live by love,
And if unfit for tombs and hearse
Our legend be, it will be fit for verse;
And if no piece of chronicle we prove,
We'll build in sonnets pretty rooms;
As well a well-wrought urn becomes
The greatest ashes, as half-acre tombs,
And by these hymns all shall approve
Us canonized for love.

And thus invoke us: You whom reverend love
Made one another's hermitage;
You, to whom love was peace, that now is rage;
Who did the whole world's soul contract, and drove
Into the glasses of your eyes
(So made such mirrors, and such spies,
That they did all to you epitomize)
Countries, towns, courts: Beg from above
A pattern of your love!

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five nine-line stanzas with a complex ABABBCCC rhyme scheme, building through argument to the audacious final claim that the lovers will become saints.

Themes

The self-sufficiency of love against the world's demands and judgments. The poem moves through several conceits — the lovers as flies, tapers, the Eagle and Dove, the Phoenix — before arriving at the paradox that by dying to the world, they become a legend that posterity will invoke.

Historical Context

Part of Donne's Songs and Sonnets, published posthumously in 1633. The title and religious framing (canonization, saints, prayer) are characteristically Metaphysical, fusing erotic and religious registers.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
