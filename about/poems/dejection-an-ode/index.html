Dejection: An Ode

by Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Written 1802 Published 1802 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1802, Dejection: An Ode was written partly in response to Wordsworth's 'Immortality Ode' and laments that Coleridge can no longer feel the beauty he sees — his imagination has dried up and he can no longer find joy in nature.

Quick Answer

Dejection: An Ode is a poem by Coleridge, published in 1802. The speaker can see the beauty of the night sky and the stars but cannot feel it — there is a grief that has come upon him, a stifled drowsy unimpassioned grief. He knows that joy must come from within, and he has lost the power to project joy onto the world.

Late, late yestreen I saw the new Moon,
With the old Moon in her arms;
And I fear, I fear, my Master dear!
We shall have a deadly storm.

Well! If the Bard was weather-wise, who made
The grand old ballad of Sir Patrick Spence,
This night, so tranquil now, will not go hence
Unroused by winds, that ply a busier trade
Than those which mould yon cloud in lazy flakes,
Or the dull sobbing draft, that moans and rakes
Upon the strings of this Eolian lute,
Which better far were mute.
For lo! the New-moon winter-bright!
And overspread with phantom light,
(With swimming phantom light o'erspread
But rimmed and circled by a silver thread)
I see the old Moon in her lap, foretelling
The coming-on of rain and squally blast.

Public domain. Published 1802.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight irregular stanzas in varying line lengths and rhyme schemes, the formal variety enacting the poem's emotional instability.

Themes

The loss of the imaginative power to find joy in nature — what Wordsworth calls the 'visionary gleam' in the Immortality Ode. Coleridge's dejection is specifically the failure of the inner life to respond to the outer world.

Historical Context

Published in The Morning Post on October 4, 1802 — Wordsworth's wedding day. The poem was originally a much longer verse letter to Sara Hutchinson (the woman Coleridge loved, who married Wordsworth's brother).

About Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was born on October 21, 1772, in Ottery St Mary, Devon, England, and became one of the founders of the English Romantic movement alongside his friend William Wordsworth. Together they published Lyrical Ballads in 1798, a collection that launched Romanticism in English literature. Coleridge's own most celebrated contributions to the collection, 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' and 'Kubla Khan,' are among the most distinctive and widely studied poems in the language. He died in Highgate, London, in 1834.
