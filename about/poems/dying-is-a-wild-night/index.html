Dying Is a Wild Night and a New Road

by Emily Dickinson

Written 1862 Published 1929 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1862 and published posthumously, this two-line poem is one of Dickinson's most compressed: dying is a wild night and a new road.

Quick Answer

"Dying Is a Wild Night and a New Road" is one of Emily Dickinson's most compressed poems. In two lines it presents dying not as an ending but as a wild night and a new road — unknown, energetic, forward-moving.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two lines — Dickinson at her most compressed.

Themes

Death as adventure rather than ending. The 'wild night' suggests storm and danger; the 'new road' suggests continuation and discovery. Neither element is peaceful or consolatory — the poem is exhilarating.

Historical Context

One of Dickinson's many brief poems on death, this one notable for its compression and its unusual tone of excitement rather than grief or calm acceptance.

About Emily Dickinson

Emily Dickinson was born on December 10, 1830, in Amherst, Massachusetts, and spent nearly her entire life there. Though she wrote nearly 1,800 poems, fewer than a dozen were published during her lifetime. Her work, marked by unconventional capitalization, slant rhyme, and dashes, was largely discovered and published after her death in 1886. She is now considered one of the most important and original voices in American poetry, known for her meditations on death, immortality, nature, and the inner life.
