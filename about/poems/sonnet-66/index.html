Sonnet 66 (Tired with All These, for Restful Death I Cry)

by William Shakespeare

Written 1595 Published 1609 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1609, this sonnet catalogues the injustices of the world — merit begging, faith violated, honor misplaced, art muzzled by authority — until the speaker cries for restful death, held back only by the thought of leaving the beloved alone.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 66 is Shakespeare's catalogue of the world's injustices, published in 1609. The speaker lists everything that makes him long for death — beggarly merit, wronged faith, misplaced honor, muzzled art, foolish authority over skill — before the closing couplet: he would gladly die, except that dying means leaving his beloved alone.

Tired with all these, for restful death I cry,
As to behold desert a beggar born,
And needy nothing trimm'd in jollity,
And purest faith unhappily forsworn,
And gilded honour shamefully misplaced,
And maiden virtue rudely strumpeted,
And right perfection wrongfully disgraced,
And strength by limping sway disabled,
And art made tongue-tied by authority,
And folly, doctor-like, controlling skill,
And simple truth miscalled simplicity,
And captive good attending captain ill:
Tired with all these, from these would I be gone,
Save that, to die, I leave my love alone.

Public domain. Text per the Folger Shakespeare Library.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter. Lines 2 through 12 form an almost unbroken list — eleven parallel clauses all grammatically subordinate to the opening 'Tired with all these' — before the couplet snaps the list shut.

Themes

Disillusionment with the world's moral disorder and the only thing that makes life worth enduring — love. The poem reads almost like a modernist catalogue of injustice, remarkable for its directness and lack of consolation before the turn.

Historical Context

Part of the Fair Youth sequence. Boris Pasternak's famous Russian translation of this sonnet made it one of the best-known Shakespeare sonnets in the Russian literary tradition, often read as a veiled political statement about Soviet censorship of art.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
