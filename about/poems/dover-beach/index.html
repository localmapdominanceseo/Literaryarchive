Dover Beach

by Matthew Arnold

Written 1851 Published 1867 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1851 and published in 1867, this poem begins with a calm sea on a moonlit night at Dover, then turns to the 'Sea of Faith' that once surrounded the world but is now retreating with a melancholy, long, withdrawing roar — and ends with the plea to be true to one another in a world with neither joy nor love nor light.

Quick Answer

"Dover Beach" is a poem by Matthew Arnold, published in 1867. The sea is calm tonight. The speaker listens to the grating roar of pebbles as the waves draw back and fling them again. He hears in it the eternal note of sadness. The Sea of Faith was once at full tide but is now retreating. The world has neither joy nor love nor light nor peace — and all they can do is be true to one another.

The sea is calm tonight.
The tide is full, the moon lies fair
Upon the straits; on the French coast the light
Gleams and is gone; the cliffs of England stand,
Glimmering and vast, out in the tranquil bay.
Come to the window, sweet is the night-air!
Only, from the long line of spray
Where the sea meets the moon-blanched land,
Listen! you hear the grating roar
Of pebbles which the waves draw back, and fling,
At their return, up the high strand,
Begin, and cease, and then again begin,
With tremulous cadence slow, and bring
The eternal note of sadness in.

Sophocles long ago
Heard it on the Ægean, and it brought
Into his mind the turbid ebb and flow
Of human misery; we
Find also in the sound a thought,
Hearing it by this distant northern sea.

The Sea of Faith
Was once, too, at the full, and round earth's shore
Lay like the folds of a bright girdle furled.
But now I only hear
Its melancholy, long, withdrawing roar,
Retreting, to the breath
Of the night-wind, down the vast edges drear
And naked shingles of the world.

Ah, love, let us be true
To one another! for the world, which seems
To lie before us like a land of dreams,
So various, so beautiful, so new,
Hath really neither joy, nor love, nor light,
Nor certitude, nor peace, nor help for pain;
And we are here as on a darkling plain
Swept with confused alarms of struggle and flight,
Where ignorant armies clash by night.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four stanzas of irregular free verse with occasional rhyme, the poem's formal freedom matching its subject of a world without anchoring certainties.

Themes

The retreat of religious faith from the modern world and the isolation it leaves behind. Without the Sea of Faith, the world is a darkling plain where ignorant armies clash by night — and the only response is private love and fidelity.

Historical Context

Arnold wrote the poem on his honeymoon in 1851, published in New Poems (1867). It is now one of the most frequently taught Victorian poems and a touchstone for discussions of secularization.

About Matthew Arnold

Matthew Arnold was born on December 24, 1822, in Laleham, Middlesex, England, the son of the famous headmaster Thomas Arnold of Rugby. He became both a significant poet and the foremost Victorian literary and cultural critic, arguing for culture as 'the best that has been thought and said.' His poetry, including 'Dover Beach' and 'The Scholar Gipsy,' engages directly with the crisis of religious faith in the modern world. He died in Liverpool in 1888.
