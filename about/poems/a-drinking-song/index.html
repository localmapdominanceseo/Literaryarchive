A Drinking Song

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1910 Published 1910 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1910 in The Green Helmet, this three-line lyric — one of Yeats's shortest — states that wine comes in at the mouth and love comes in at the eye, and that is all we shall know for truth before we grow old and die.

Quick Answer

"A Drinking Song" is a three-line poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1910. Wine comes in at the mouth, love comes in at the eye — that is all we shall know for truth before we grow old and die. I lift the glass to my mouth, I look at you, and I sigh.

Wine comes in at the mouth
And love comes in at the eye;
That's all we shall know for truth
Before we grow old and die.
I lift the glass to my mouth,
I look at you, and I sigh.

Public domain (published 1910). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three lines — Yeats at his most compressed, the lyric reduced to its essential statement.

Themes

The two entry points of pleasure and passion — wine through the mouth, love through the eye — and the brevity of life in which they are the only certain truths.

Historical Context

Published in The Green Helmet (1910). A miniature in a collection containing some of Yeats's most politically engaged verse.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
