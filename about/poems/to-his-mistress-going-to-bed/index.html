To His Mistress Going to Bed

by John Donne

Written 1595 Published 1654 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1595 and published posthumously in 1654, this elegy is one of Donne's most audacious poems — an extended address to a woman undressing for bed, using the metaphor of America newly discovered to celebrate the body.

Quick Answer

"To His Mistress Going to Bed" is an elegy by John Donne, written around 1595 and published posthumously in 1654. The speaker watches his mistress undress for bed, addressing each article of clothing and her body beneath, culminating in the America metaphor: her body is a new-found land, he its first discoverer, and the licence of full nakedness is 'My America, my new found land.'

Come, Madam, come, all rest my powers defy,
Until I labour, I in labour lie.
The foe oft-times having the foe in sight,
Is tired with standing though he never fight.
Off with that girdle, like heaven's Zone glistering,
But a far fairer world encompassing.
Unpin that spangled breastplate which you wear,
That th'eyes of busy fools may be stopped there.
Unlace yourself, for that harmonious chyme,
Tells me from you, that now it is bed time.
Off with that happy busk, which I envy,
That still can be, and still can stand so nigh.
Your gown going off, such beautious state reveals,
As when from flowry meads th'hills shadow steals.
Off with that wyerie Coronet and shew
The haiery Diadem which on you doth grow:
Now off with those shoes, and then safely tread
In this love's hallow'd temple, this soft bed.
In such white robes, heaven's Angels used to be
Received by men; Thou Angel bringst with thee
A heaven like Mahomet's Paradise; and though
Ill spirits walk in white, we easly know,
By this these Angels from an evil sprite,
Those set our hairs, but these our flesh upright.
Licence my roaving hands, and let them go,
Before, behind, between, above, below.
O my America! my new-found-land,
My kingdome, safeliest when with one man man'd,
My Mine of precious stones: My Emperie,
How blest am I in this discovering thee!
To enter in these bonds, is to be free;
Then where my hand is set, my seal shall be.
Full nakedness! All joyes are due to thee,
As souls unbodied, bodies uncloth'd must be,
To taste whole joyes.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1654.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Forty-eight lines of iambic pentameter in rhyming couplets, an Ovidian elegy in the tradition of erotic classical poetry, deliberately and playfully provocative.

Themes

The celebration of the beloved's body through the metaphor of geographical discovery — an extended conceit comparing undressing to the exploration of a new world, with the lover as Columbus discovering America.

Historical Context

One of Donne's elegies, circulated in manuscript during his lifetime. The poem was considered too explicit for inclusion in the 1633 posthumous collection and was first published in 1654.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
