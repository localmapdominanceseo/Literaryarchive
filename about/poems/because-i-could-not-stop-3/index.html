This World Is Not Conclusion

by Emily Dickinson

Written 1862 Published 1896 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1862 and published posthumously in 1896, this poem asserts that this world is not conclusion but the beginning of another — though the species who have gone cannot be located, and the argument from faith fails to convince even the narrator.

Quick Answer

"This World Is Not Conclusion" is a poem by Emily Dickinson, written around 1862. It opens by asserting that this world is not conclusion — a species stands beyond. But as the poem continues, the evidence for this thins: the scholars cannot tell us where the gone have gone, and the narcotics of faith only numb the ache.

This World is not Conclusion.
A Species stands beyond—
Invisible, as Music—
But positive, as Sound—
It beckons, and it baffles—
Philosophy—don't know—
And through a Riddle, at the last—
Sagacity, must go—
To guess it, puzzles scholars—
To gain it, Men have borne
Contempt of Generations
And Crucifixion, shown—
Faith slips—and laughs, and rallies—
Blushes, if any see—
Plucks at a twig of Evidence—
And asks a Vane, the way—
Much Gesture, from the Pulpit—
Strong Hallelujahs roll—
Narcotics cannot still the Tooth
That nibbles at the soul—

Public domain. Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five quatrains in common meter with slant rhyme, the poem's formal confidence ironically undercutting its thematic uncertainty.

Themes

The gap between the assertion of faith and its failure to convince fully — the poem begins with a confident declaration and ends in the 'Narcotic' of faith barely masking the ache of doubt.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1896 in Poems: Third Series. One of Dickinson's many poems engaging directly with questions of immortality and faith.

About Emily Dickinson

Emily Dickinson was born on December 10, 1830, in Amherst, Massachusetts, and spent nearly her entire life there. Though she wrote nearly 1,800 poems, fewer than a dozen were published during her lifetime. Her work, marked by unconventional capitalization, slant rhyme, and dashes, was largely discovered and published after her death in 1886. She is now considered one of the most important and original voices in American poetry, known for her meditations on death, immortality, nature, and the inner life.
