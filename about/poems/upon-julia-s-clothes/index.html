Upon Julia's Clothes

by Robert Herrick

Written 1648 Published 1648 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1648 in Hesperides, this six-line poem describes the speaker watching Julia walk in her silks — their liquefaction, their brave vibration each way free — and being unable to account for what he sees.

Quick Answer

"Upon Julia's Clothes" is a poem by Robert Herrick, published in 1648. When Julia walks in silks, their liquefaction makes the speaker wild. Next, when he casts his eyes and sees the brave vibration each way free, oh, how that glittering taketh me.

Whenas in silks my Julia goes,
Then, then, methinks, how sweetly flows
That liquefaction of her clothes.

Next, when I cast mine eyes and see
That brave vibration each way free,
O how that glittering taketh me!

Public domain. Published 1648 in Hesperides.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two tercets of iambic tetrameter with AAB CCB rhyme — six lines that say everything necessary and nothing more.

Themes

The erotic power of beauty in motion, specifically the silks' movement as Julia walks. The poem is entirely about visual sensation and the response it provokes, with no argument or reflection.

Historical Context

Published in Hesperides (1648). One of the most frequently anthologized of all short English lyrics for its extreme compression and sensory precision.

About Robert Herrick

Robert Herrick was born in London in 1591 and became one of the most prolific of the Cavalier poets, writing elegant, sensuous lyrics drawing on classical models. His collection Hesperides (1648) contained over 1,400 poems, including his most famous work 'To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time.' He served as a vicar in Devon and died in 1674.
