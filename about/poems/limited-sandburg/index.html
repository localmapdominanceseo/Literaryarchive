Limited

by Carl Sandburg

Written 1916 Published 1916 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1916 in Chicago Poems, this six-line poem describes riding on a limited express train through the Midwest — and the speaker, who knows all the faces of those riding it, knows they will all be lost, knows the cars will be junked, knows the conductor plays with his watch.

Quick Answer

"Limited" is a poem by Carl Sandburg, published in 1916. I am riding a limited express train, one of the crack trains of the nation. I am hurtling across the prairie and I know all the faces of the people. In the cars there are faces and hearts under the ribs — lost too, in the rush. The conductor plays with his watch charm.

I am riding on a limited express, one of the crack trains
of the nation.
Hurtling across the prairie into blue haze and dark air
go fifteen all-steel coaches holding a thousand people.
(All the coaches shall be scrap and rust and all the men
and women laughing in the diners and sleepers shall
pass to ashes.)
I ask a man in the smoker where he is going and he
answers: 'Omaha.'

Public domain (published 1916 in Chicago Poems).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six lines of free verse, compressed and imagistic.

Themes

The anonymity and impermanence of modern American life — the rush of the train carrying all these faces toward oblivion, the conductor indifferent with his watch charm.

Historical Context

Published in Chicago Poems (1916). One of Sandburg's briefest and most compressed city poems.

About Carl Sandburg

Carl Sandburg was born on January 6, 1878, in Galesburg, Illinois, to Swedish immigrant parents, and became one of the most popular American poets of the twentieth century. He worked many jobs — farm worker, soldier, journalist — before establishing himself as a poet with Chicago Poems (1916). He won three Pulitzer Prizes, two for poetry and one for his biography of Abraham Lincoln. He died in Flat Rock, North Carolina, in 1967.
