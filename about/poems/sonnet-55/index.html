Sonnet 55 (Not Marble, nor the Gilded Monuments)

by William Shakespeare

Written 1595 Published 1609 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1609, this sonnet argues that poetry outlasts all physical monuments — marble, bronze, gilded tombs — and will preserve the beloved's memory until the Day of Judgment, when they will rise to live again in the eyes of posterity.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 55 is one of Shakespeare's boldest claims for the power of poetry, published in 1609. Not marble nor the gilded monuments of princes shall outlive these rhymes, the speaker declares — war, time, and oblivion will destroy all physical memorials, but the beloved will live in verse until the end of time itself.

Not marble, nor the gilded monuments
Of princes, shall outlive this powerful rhyme;
But you shall shine more bright in these contents
Than unswept stone, besmear'd with sluttish time.
When wasteful war shall statues overturn,
And broils root out the work of masonry,
Nor Mars his sword nor war's quick fire shall burn
The living record of your memory.
'Gainst death and all-oblivious enmity
Shall you pace forth; your praise shall still find room
Even in the eyes of all posterity
That wear this world out to the ending doom.
So, till the judgment that yourself arise,
You live in this, and dwell in lovers' eyes.

Public domain. Text per the Folger Shakespeare Library.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. The poem is unusual in the Fair Youth sequence for its confident, almost aggressive tone — the speaker is making a declaration, not questioning or pleading.

Themes

The supremacy of poetry over all other forms of memorial. Each quatrain eliminates a rival to verse: marble and gilded monuments, war and its destruction, and 'sluttish time' itself. The couplet states the final destination: the beloved lives in verse 'till the judgment that yourself arise.'

Historical Context

Part of the Fair Youth sequence. This sonnet is often paired with Sonnet 18 as Shakespeare's two most direct statements of poetry's power to confer immortality on the beloved.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
