September 1913

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1913 Published 1913 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1913 during the Dublin Lockout, this poem attacks the materialistic middle class of Ireland who have driven away the romantic spirit of the old heroes — John O'Leary, Wolfe Tone, Robert Emmet — with their rosaries and halfpence.

Quick Answer

"September 1913" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1913 during the Dublin Lockout. It attacks the Irish middle class who have traded the romantic idealism of the dead heroes — O'Leary, Tone, Emmet — for material comfort and piety. 'Romantic Ireland's dead and gone / It's with O'Leary in the grave.'

What need you, being come to sense,
But fumble in a greasy till
And add the halfpence to the pence
And prayer to shivering prayer, until
You have dried the marrow from the bone?
For men were born to pray and save:
Romantic Ireland's dead and gone,
It's with O'Leary in the grave.

Yet they were of a different kind,
The names that stilled your childish play,
They have gone about the world like wind,
But little time had they to pray
For whom the hangman's rope was spun,
And what, God help us, could they save?
Romantic Ireland's dead and gone,
It's with O'Leary in the grave.

Was it for this the wild geese spread
The grey wing upon every tide;
For this that all that blood was shed,
For this Edward Fitzgerald died,
And Robert Emmet and Wolfe Tone,
All that delirium of the brave?
Romantic Ireland's dead and gone,
It's with O'Leary in the grave.

Yet could we turn the clock to zero,
And be once more a wandering race,
The Muses' fabled idiot boy
Would say, 'the world has turned away
From all that men have thought to say
Of ancient idealism's grave.'
Romantic Ireland's dead and gone,
It's with O'Leary in the grave.

Public domain (published 1913). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four stanzas of eight lines each with a repeating refrain, giving the poem the quality of a bitter lament or a political broadside.

Themes

The death of romantic Irish nationalism under the pressure of bourgeois materialism. Yeats mourns the heroic dead who sacrificed for an Ireland that their successors have betrayed by choosing money and comfortable religion over sacrifice.

Historical Context

Written during the Dublin Lockout of 1913, when employers locked out workers who joined Jim Larkin's union. Yeats saw the employers' behavior as evidence of exactly the meanness he attacks in the poem.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
