Selected Haiku — Kobayashi Issa

by Kobayashi Issa

Written 1819 Published 1819 Japanese Haiku Public Domain

Kobayashi Issa (1763-1828) is known for haiku that combine humor, pathos, and a tender sympathy for small creatures. These include his most famous poem — 'This world of dew / is only a world of dew — / and yet... and yet...' — written after the death of his infant daughter.

Quick Answer

Kobayashi Issa is the master of haiku that combine tenderness, humor, and grief. His most famous poem was written after the death of his infant daughter and acknowledges Buddhist teaching that this world is transient as dew — and yet cannot suppress the grief of a father. These haiku represent his characteristic voice.

This world of dew
is only a world of dew—
and yet... and yet...

(1819, on the death of his daughter)

—

O snail,
climb Mount Fuji,
but slowly, slowly!

(c.1815)

—

In this world
we walk on the roof of hell
gazing at flowers.

(c.1820)

—

The dragonfly
can't quite land
on that blade of grass.

(c.1818)

Analysis

Form and Structure

Seventeen syllables in three lines in Japanese, with English translations varying. Issa's haiku are known for their sympathy toward small creatures — flies, frogs, fireflies — that reflects both Buddhist compassion and a personality shaped by personal loss.

Themes

Impermanence, small creatures' vulnerability, the coexistence of intellectual acceptance and emotional grief. Issa knew Buddhist teachings on impermanence yet could not apply them emotionlessly to his own losses.

Historical Context

Issa (1763-1828) had a difficult life marked by family conflict, poverty, and the deaths of multiple children in infancy. The poem about the world of dew was written after his daughter Sato died in 1819.

About Kobayashi Issa

Kobayashi Issa (1763-1828) is one of the four great haiku masters of Japan, known for haiku that combine humor, tenderness, and profound sadness. His life was marked by poverty and the deaths of multiple children in infancy. His most famous poem was written after his daughter's death and acknowledges Buddhist teaching on impermanence while refusing to suppress a father's grief.
