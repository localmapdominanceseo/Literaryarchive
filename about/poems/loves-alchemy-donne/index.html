Love's Alchemy

by John Donne

Written 1601 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1633, this deeply cynical poem attacks the pretensions of love poetry — the speaker has dug the mine of love and found nothing but the medicine, nothing of the pregnant pot. Love's elixir does not exist.

Quick Answer

"Love's Alchemy" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker has dug deeper than any alchemist and found love's elixir does not exist — the mummy possessed by men is not the woman's soul but merely a momentary bliss. He who thinks he has found a heaven in his woman's love will find it was only their mind's whim.

Some that have deeper digged love's mine than I,
Say, where his centric happiness doth lie;
I have loved, and got, and told,
But should I love, get, tell, till I were old,
I should not find that hidden mystery.
O, 'tis imposture all!
And as no chemic yet th' elixir got,
But glorifies his pregnant pot,
If by the way to him befall
Some odoriferous thing, or medicinal,
So, lovers dream a rich and long delight,
But get a winter-seeming summer's night.

Our ease, our thrift, our honor, and our day,
Shall we for this vain bubble's shadow pay?
Ends love in this, that my man
Can be as happy'as I can, if he can
Endure the short scorn of a bridegroom's play?
That loving wretch that swears,
'Tis not the bodies marry, but the minds,
Which he in her angelic finds,
Would swear as justly, that he hears,
In that day's rude hoarse minstrelsy, the spheres.
Hope not for mind in women; at their best,
Sweetness and wit they'are, but mummy, possessed.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two twelve-line stanzas in iambic pentameter with complex rhyme, the sustained analogy between love and alchemy structuring the entire poem.

Themes

A savage attack on idealized love — Donne strips away the Petrarchan idealization and insists that love is only physical, transient, and disappointing, that the supposed bliss of union is a fiction maintained by desire.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633. One of Donne's most cynical poems, standing in sharp contrast to the ecstatic celebrations of love in other Songs and Sonnets.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
