On the Morning of Christ's Nativity (Extract)

by John Milton

Written 1629 Published 1645 English Renaissance Public Domain

Written on Christmas morning 1629, when Milton was twenty-one, and published in 1645, this ode celebrates the Nativity and the banishment of pagan gods by Christ's arrival. The Hymn section describes the universal peace at the moment of the birth and the flight of the oracles and ancient gods.

Quick Answer

"On the Morning of Christ's Nativity" is an ode by John Milton, written in 1629 and published in 1645. The Hymn section describes the peace that fell upon the world at Christ's birth — the winds still, the ocean at rest, the stars fixed — and then the flight of the pagan gods from their temples as Christ's power supersedes them.

This is the Month, and this the happy morn
Wherein the Son of Heav'ns eternal King,
Of wedded Maid, and Virgin Mother born,
Our great redemption from above did bring;
For so the holy sages once did sing,
That he our deadly forfeit should release,
And with his Father work us a perpetual peace.

That glorious Form, that Light unsufferable,
And that far-beaming blaze of Majesty,
Wherewith he wont at Heav'ns high Councel-Table,
To sit the midst of Trinal Unity,
He laid aside; and here with us to be,
Forsook the Courts of everlasting Day,
And chose with us a darksom House of mortal Clay.

Say Heav'nly Muse, shall not thy sacred vein
Afford a present to the Infant God?
Hast thou no vers, no hymn, or solemn strein,
To welcom him to this his new abode,
Now while the Heav'n by the Suns team untrod,
Hath took no print of the approaching light,
And all the spangled host keep watch in squadrons bright?

Analysis

Form and Structure

An introductory section of four eight-line stanzas, followed by a 'Hymn' of twenty-seven stanzas in a complex rhyme scheme Milton devised for the occasion.

Themes

The displacement of the classical world by Christianity, and the meaning of the Incarnation. The pagan gods do not simply fade — they flee in terror at the presence of a greater power.

Historical Context

Written on Christmas morning 1629 while Milton was a student at Cambridge. It was the first major poem he considered worthy of preservation and became a touchstone for later Romantic poets.

About John Milton

John Milton was born on December 9, 1608, in London, England, and became one of the greatest poets in the English language, best known for his epic Paradise Lost (1667). He served as Latin Secretary to Oliver Cromwell's government during the English Interregnum and went completely blind around 1651-1652, composing his greatest works through dictation. He died in London in 1674.
