An Irish Airman Foresees His Death

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1918 Published 1919 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1919 in The Wild Swans at Coole, this poem is spoken by Robert Gregory, an Irish airman killed in World War I, who explains that he fights neither out of duty nor hatred but out of a pure, balanced love of the excitement of flight.

Quick Answer

"An Irish Airman Foresees His Death" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1919, spoken by the airman Robert Gregory before his death in battle. He fights not for love of country or hatred of the enemy, not from duty or public men's cheers — but from a lonely impulse of delight that drove him to this tumult in the clouds, balanced against all he knew and may know.

I know that I shall meet my fate
Somewhere among the clouds above;
Those that I fight I do not hate,
Those that I guard I do not love;
My country is Kiltartan Cross,
My countrymen Kiltartan's poor,
No likely end could bring them loss
Or leave them happier than before.
Nor law, nor duty bade me fight,
Nor public men, nor cheering crowds,
A lonely impulse of delight
Drove to this tumult in the clouds;
I balanced all, brought all to mind,
The years to come seemed waste of breath,
A waste of breath the years behind
In balance with this life, this death.

Public domain (published 1919). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Sixteen lines of iambic tetrameter in ABAB rhyme, the formal balance of the stanzas enacting the speaker's own balanced, rational acceptance of death.

Themes

The pure, non-ideological motivations of the man who acts from instinct and love of the act itself rather than political obligation. Gregory's death is not for a cause but from a personal, almost aesthetic decision.

Historical Context

Written for Robert Gregory, son of Yeats's patron Lady Gregory, killed in action in January 1918. Published in The Wild Swans at Coole (1919).

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
