Lines Composed a Few Miles above Tintern Abbey (Extract)

by William Wordsworth

Written 1798 Published 1798 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written on July 13, 1798, and published that same year in Lyrical Ballads, this poem is one of the central texts of English Romanticism. Returning to the Wye Valley after five years, Wordsworth meditates on how the memory of natural beauty has sustained him in the city, and on what nature now means to him as an adult compared to his boyhood experience of it.

Quick Answer

"Lines Composed a Few Miles above Tintern Abbey" is a poem by William Wordsworth, published in 1798. Returning to the Wye Valley after five years, the speaker reflects that the memory of the scene has sustained him through city life, that he hears in nature 'the still, sad music of humanity,' and that nature has become both a moral anchor and a spirit that rolls through all things.

Five years have past; five summers, with the length
Of five long winters! and again I hear
These waters, rolling from their mountain-springs
With a soft inland murmur.—Once again
Do I behold these steep and lofty cliffs,
That on a wild secluded scene impress
Thoughts of more deep seclusion; and connect
The landscape with the quiet of the sky.
The day is come when I again repose
Here, under this dark sycamore, and view
These plots of cottage-ground, these orchard-tufts,
Which at this season with their unripe fruits
Are clad in one green hue, and lose themselves
'Mid groves and copses. Once again I see
These hedge-rows, hardly hedge-rows, little lines
Of sportive wood run wild: these pastoral farms,
Green to the very door; and wreaths of smoke
Sent up, in silence, from among the trees!
With some uncertain notice, as might seem
Of vagrant dwellers in the houseless woods,
Or of some Hermit's cave, where by his fire
The Hermit sits alone.

These beauteous forms,
Through a long absence, have not been to me
As is a landscape to a blind man's eye:
But oft, in lonely rooms, and 'mid the din
Of towns and cities, I have owed to them,
In hours of weariness, sensations sweet,
Felt in the blood, and felt along the heart;
And passing even into my purer mind,
With tranquil restoration:—feelings too
Of unremembered pleasure: such, perhaps,
As have no slight or trivial influence
On that best portion of a good man's life,
His little, nameless, unremembered, acts
Of kindness and of love. Nor less, I trust,
To them I may have owed another gift,
Of aspect more sublime; that blessed mood,
In which the burthen of the mystery,
In which the heavy and the weary weight
Of all this unintelligible world,
Is lightened:—that serene and blessed mood,
In which the affections gently lead us on,—
Until, the breath of this corporeal frame
And even the motion of our human blood
Almost suspended, we are laid asleep
In body, and become a living soul:
While with an eye made quiet by the power
Of harmony, and the deep power of joy,
We see into the life of things.

Analysis

Form and Structure

One hundred and fifty-nine lines of blank verse in loose iambic pentameter, spoken as a meditation addressed partly to himself and partly to his sister Dorothy, present with him on the walk.

Themes

The relationship between the memory of natural beauty and the present experience of it; how nature's meaning changes as we age from childhood sensation to adult reflection. The famous passage beginning 'I have felt / A presence that disturbs me with the joy / Of elevated thoughts' articulates a pantheistic vision of nature as a living spiritual force.

Historical Context

Wordsworth wrote the poem in a single day after a walking tour of the Wye Valley with Dorothy in July 1798, and included it at the end of Lyrical Ballads (1798) without revision. It was the last poem added and is often read as the collection's philosophical statement.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
