The Onset

by Robert Frost

Written 1921 Published 1923 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1923 in New Hampshire, this poem describes the first onset of winter snow as an apparent ending — then asserts that spring will come again, as it always has, and the speaker knows this from experience.

Quick Answer

"The Onset" is a poem by Robert Frost, published in 1923. The speaker stands in a darkening wood as the first winter snow begins to fall, feeling as if this time it might truly be the end. But he reassures himself: he has seen this before, he knows spring will come, the snow will fail, and the trees and fields and towns will emerge again.

Always the same, when on a fated night
At last the gathered snow lets down as white
As may be in dark woods, and with a song
It shall not make again all winter long
Of hissing on the yet uncovered ground,
I almost stumble looking up and round,
As one who overtaken by the end
Gives up his errand, and lets death descend
Upon him where he is, with nothing done
To evil, no important triumph won,
More than if life had never been begun.
Yet all the precedent is on my side:
I know that winter death has never tried
The earth but it has failed: the snow may heap
In long storms an undrifted four feet deep
As measured again maple, birch, and oak,
It cannot check the peeper's silver croak;
And I shall see the snow all go down hill
In water of a slender April rill
That flashes tail through last year's withered brake
And dead weeds, like a disappearing snake.
Nothing will be left white but here a birch,
And there a clump of houses with a church.

Public domain (published 1923). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-two lines of blank verse, the open form suiting a meditative reassurance.

Themes

The fear of permanent ending and the reassurance of cyclical renewal. The poem dramatizes winter as existential threat before countering it with the authority of experience: this always passes.

Historical Context

Published in New Hampshire (1923). One of Frost's more overtly hopeful poems, unusual for its directness of reassurance.

About Robert Frost

Robert Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California, though he is most closely associated with rural New England, where he lived and farmed for much of his life. His poetry uses plain, conversational language and traditional verse forms to explore deceptively simple rural scenes that often open onto deeper questions about choice, isolation, and mortality. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry and remains one of the most widely read American poets of the twentieth century. He died in Boston in 1963.
