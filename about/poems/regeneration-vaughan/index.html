Regeneration

by Henry Vaughan

Written 1650 Published 1650 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1650 in Silex Scintillans, this allegorical poem describes a spiritual journey from a cold, dead winter landscape through a valley into a grove where a rushing wind blows — and the speaker cries to God to arise and breathe upon him.

Quick Answer

"Regeneration" is a poem by Henry Vaughan, published in 1650. The speaker walks through a dead winter landscape — stones, frozen hills, clouded skies — into a valley where spring has arrived, and then to a grove where a wind breathes through the trees. He does not know where it comes from or goes, and asks God to arise and breathe upon him.

A Ward, and still in bonds, one day
I stole abroad,
It was high spring, and all the way
Primrosed, and hung with shade;
Yet, was it frost within,
And surly winds
Blasted my infant buds, and sinne
Like Clouds ecclips'd my mind.

Storm'd thus; I straight perceived my spring
Meere stage, and show,
My walke a monstrous, mountain'd thing
Rough-cast with Rocks, and snow;
And as a Pilgrim's Eye
Far from reliefe,
Measures the melancholy sky
Then drops, and rains for griefe,

So sigh'd I upwards still, at last
'Twixt steps, and falls
I reach'd the pinnacle, where plac'd
I found a paire of scales,
I tooke them up and layd
In th'one late paines,
The other smoake, and pleasures weigh'd
But prov'd the heavier graines;

With that, some cried, Away; straight I
Obey'd, and led
Full East, a faire, fresh field could spy
Some call'd it, Iacobs Bed;
A Virgin-soile, which no
Rude feet ere trod,
Where (since he stept there,) only go
Prophets, and friends of God.

Public domain. Published 1650 in Silex Scintillans.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Ten stanzas of eight lines each in iambic tetrameter with ABABCDCD rhyme, the allegorical landscape moving from death to life.

Themes

Spiritual regeneration through divine grace figured as a spring wind that blows where it will. The poem draws on John 3:8 ('The wind bloweth where it listeth') and is one of the finest allegorical poems of the seventeenth century.

Historical Context

Published in Silex Scintillans (1650), Vaughan's major collection. Vaughan was deeply influenced by George Herbert and is generally considered the finest poet of the tradition Herbert founded.

About Henry Vaughan

Henry Vaughan was born in 1621 in Brecknockshire, Wales, and became one of the finest poets of the Metaphysical tradition, deeply influenced by George Herbert. His collection Silex Scintillans (1650, 1655) contains some of the most beautiful devotional poems in English, including 'The World,' 'The Night,' and 'Regeneration.' He worked as a physician in Wales and died in 1695.
