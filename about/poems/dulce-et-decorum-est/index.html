Dulce et Decorum Est

by Wilfred Owen

Written 1917 Published 1920 World War I Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1917 and published posthumously in 1920, this poem describes soldiers marching exhausted from the trenches when a gas attack suddenly hits, and one man who fails to get his mask on in time. The poet, haunted by this death, directly addresses those who encourage young men to enlist with the 'old lie' that dying for one's country is sweet and fitting.

Quick Answer

"Dulce et Decorum Est" is a poem by Wilfred Owen, written in 1917 and published posthumously in 1920. It describes soldiers stumbling exhausted through mud when a gas shell strikes. One man fails to get his gas mask on and dies horribly, while the speaker, haunted by the vision, rejects the old Latin phrase 'dulce et decorum est pro patria mori' — it is sweet and fitting to die for one's country — calling it 'the old lie.'

Bent double, like old beggars under sacks,
Knock-kneed, coughing like hags, we cursed through sludge,
Till on the haunting flares we turned our backs
And towards our distant rest began to trudge.
Men marched asleep. Many had lost their boots
But limped on, blood-shod. All went lame; all blind;
Drunk with fatigue; deaf even to the hoots
Of tired, outstripped Five-Nines that dropped behind.

Gas! Gas! Quick, boys!—An ecstasy of fumbling,
Fitting the clumsy helmets just in time;
But someone still was yelling out and stumbling,
And flound'ring like a man in fire or lime...
Dim, through the misty panes and thick green light,
As under a green sea, I saw him drowning.
In all my dreams, before my helpless sight,
He plunges at me, guttering, choking, drowning.

If in some smothering dreams you too could pace
Behind the wagon that we flung him in,
And watch the white eyes writhing in his face,
His hanging face, like a devil's sick of sin;
If you could hear, at every jolt, the blood
Come gargling from the froth-corrupted lungs,
Obscene as cancer, bitter as the cud
Of vile, incurable sores on innocent tongues,
My friend, you would not tell with such high zest
To children ardent for some desperate glory,
The old Lie: Dulce et decorum est
Pro patria mori.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-eight lines of loose iambic pentameter in two unequal stanzas with a sestet transition, rhyming loosely ABAB. The poem deliberately breaks its own formal control under the weight of what it describes, with irregular meter and half-rhyme enacting the chaos of the gas attack.

Themes

The brutal reality of industrial warfare versus the romantic patriotic propaganda used to recruit young men. Owen specifically targets the Latin motto used on military memorials — 'dulce et decorum est pro patria mori' — and argues that any adult who had seen what he describes would never use such language to send young men to war.

Historical Context

Owen wrote the poem at Craiglockhart War Hospital in Edinburgh in 1917, where he was being treated for shell shock after serving on the Western Front. He revised it through several drafts and dedicated an early version to the poet Jessie Pope, who wrote patriotic recruiting verse. Owen was killed in action on November 4, 1918, one week before the Armistice. The poem was published posthumously in 1920.

About Wilfred Owen

Wilfred Owen was born on March 18, 1893, in Oswestry, Shropshire, England, and is now considered the greatest poet of the First World War. He enlisted in 1915, served on the Western Front, and was sent to Craiglockhart War Hospital in 1917 suffering from shell shock, where he met Siegfried Sassoon, who encouraged and shaped his work. Owen was killed in action on November 4, 1918, exactly one week before the Armistice, at the age of twenty-five. Most of his poems were published posthumously in 1920.
