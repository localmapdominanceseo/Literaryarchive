After Apple-Picking

by Robert Frost

Written 1914 Published 1914 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1914 in North of Boston, this poem describes a tired apple-picker at the end of a long harvest, drowsing on his ladder, unable to separate the dream-state from the waking-state, wondering whether what is coming is ordinary sleep or something longer.

Quick Answer

"After Apple-Picking" is a poem by Robert Frost, published in 1914. An apple-picker, exhausted at the end of harvest, has a barrel left to fill but can't keep going. He's drowsy from looking through a pane of ice at the world this morning, still feeling the instep arch and the ladder-rung sway. He wonders whether the sleep coming is ordinary or the longer sleep.

My long two-pointed ladder's sticking through a tree
Toward heaven still,
And there's a barrel that I didn't fill
Beside it, and there may be two or three
Apples I didn't pick upon some bough.
But I am done with apple-picking now.
Essence of winter sleep is on the night,
The scent of apples: I am drowsing off.
I cannot rub the strangeness from my sight
I got from looking through a pane of glass
I skimmed this morning from the drinking trough
And held against the world of hoary grass.
It melted, and I let it fall and break.
But I was well
Upon my way to sleep before it fell,
And I could tell
What form my dreaming was about to take.
Magnified apples appear and disappear,
Stem end and blossom end,
And every fleck of russet showing clear.
My instep arch not only keeps the ache,
It keeps the pressure of a ladder-round.
I feel the ladder sway as the boughs bend.
And I keep hearing from the cellar bin
The rumbling sound
Of load on load of apples coming in.
For I have had too much
Of apple-picking: I am overtired
Of the great harvest I myself desired.
There were ten thousand thousand fruit to touch,
Cherish in hand, lift down, and not let fall.
For all
That struck the earth,
No matter if not bruised or spiked with stubble,
Went surely to the cider-apple heap
As of no worth.
One can see what will trouble
This sleep of mine, whatever sleep it is.
Were he not gone,
The woodchuck could say whether it's like his
Long sleep, as I describe its coming on,
Or just some human sleep.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Forty-two lines of blank verse with irregular rhymes that come and go — not quite free verse, not quite formal verse, enacting the drowsy half-dream state of the poem's subject.

Themes

The ambiguity between the end of a season's work (legitimate exhaustion) and the approach of death (the long sleep). Apple-picking has been read as a metaphor for any completed life's work. The poem refuses to resolve whether the speaker is just tired or something more.

Historical Context

Published in North of Boston (1914). The apple harvest setting is characteristic New England — but the philosophical resonance of the poem extends well beyond its literal subject.

About Robert Frost

Robert Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California, though he is most closely associated with rural New England, where he lived and farmed for much of his life. His poetry uses plain, conversational language and traditional verse forms to explore deceptively simple rural scenes that often open onto deeper questions about choice, isolation, and mortality. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry and remains one of the most widely read American poets of the twentieth century. He died in Boston in 1963.
