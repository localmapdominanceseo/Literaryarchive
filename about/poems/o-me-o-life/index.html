O Me! O Life!

by Walt Whitman

Written 1865 Published 1865 American Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1865 in Leaves of Grass, this short poem poses the question of why we continue when faced with all the emptiness and failure of life — and answers that the answer is you yourself, that you are here, that life exists, and that you can contribute a verse.

Quick Answer

"O Me! O Life!" is a poem by Walt Whitman, published in 1865. The speaker asks what it all amounts to — the faithless, cities full of foolish people, the recurring question of what good is it. The answer: that you are here, that life exists and identity, that the powerful play goes on and you may contribute a verse.

Oh me! Oh life! of the questions of these recurring,
Of the endless trains of the faithless, of cities fill'd with the foolish,
Of myself forever reproaching myself, (for who more foolish than I, and who more faithless?)
Of eyes that vainly crave the light, of the objects mean, of the struggle ever renew'd,
Of the poor results of all, of the plodding and sordid crowds I see around me,
Of the empty and useless years of the rest, with the rest me intertwined,
The question, O me! so sad, recurring—What good amid these, O me, O life?

Answer.
That you are here—that life exists and identity,
That the powerful play goes on, and you may contribute a verse.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Fourteen lines of free verse, building through the question to the answer with Whitman's characteristic declarative confidence.

Themes

The meaning of individual existence within the larger human story. The answer to 'what good is it?' is simply: you exist, and your existence allows you to add your voice to the ongoing play of human life.

Historical Context

Published in the 1865 edition of Leaves of Grass in the section 'Whispers of Heavenly Death.' The poem was quoted memorably in the film Dead Poets Society (1989), bringing it to wide contemporary attention.

About Walt Whitman

Walt Whitman was born on May 31, 1819, on Long Island, New York. His landmark collection Leaves of Grass, first published in 1855 and expanded throughout his life, broke from traditional verse forms in favor of free verse and sweeping, democratic subject matter. Whitman served as a nurse during the American Civil War, an experience that shaped much of his later work, including his elegy for Abraham Lincoln. He is widely regarded, alongside Emily Dickinson, as one of the founders of a distinctly American poetic voice.
