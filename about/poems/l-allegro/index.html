L'Allegro (Extract)

by John Milton

Written 1631 Published 1645 English Renaissance Public Domain

Written around 1631 and published in 1645, L'Allegro ('The Merry Man') is a companion poem to Il Penseroso. It celebrates the life of cheerful mirth — the lark, the cock, the plowman, the milkmaid, the shepherd, the village festivities — and the pleasures of comedy, music, and sociable entertainment.

Quick Answer

"L'Allegro" is a poem by John Milton, published in 1645, celebrating the merry, social life of pleasure and company. It begins by banishing Melancholy and calling on Mirth to come with her companions — Sport, Laughter, Liberty — then moves through a day of cheerful sights and sounds from dawn to evening festivities.

Hence loathed Melancholy
Of Cerberus, and blackest midnight born,
In Stygian Cave forlorn
'Mongst horrid shapes, and shreiks, and sights unholy,
Find out som uncouth cell,
Wher brooding darknes spreads his jealous wings,
And the night-Raven sings;
There under Ebon shades, and low-brow'd Rocks,
As ragged as thy Locks,
In dark Cimmerian desert ever dwell.
But com thou Goddes fair and free,
In Heav'n ycleap'd Euphrosyne,
And by men, heart-easing Mirth,
Whom lovely Venus at a birth
With two sister Graces more
To Ivy-crownèd Bacchus bore;
Or whether (as som Sager sing)
The frolick Wind that breathes the Spring,
Zephir with Aurora playing,
As he met her once a Maying,
There on Beds of Violets blew,
And fresh-blown Roses washt in dew,
Fill'd her with thee a daughter fair,
So buxsom, blith, and debonair.
Haste thee nymph, and bring with thee
Jest and youthful Jollity,
Quips and Cranks, and wanton Wiles,
Nods, and Becks, and Wreathed Smiles,
Such as hang on Hebe's cheek,
And love to live in dimple sleek;
Sport that wrincled Care derides,
And Laughter holding both his sides.
Com, and trip it as ye go
On the light fantastick toe,
And in thy right hand lead with thee,
The Mountain Nymph, sweet Liberty.

Public domain. Published 1645.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A long poem in octosyllabic couplets (eight-syllable rhyming lines), the same form as Il Penseroso, with a short introductory section banishing the opposite temperament before the main celebration begins.

Themes

The pleasures of sociable, cheerful engagement with the world — from nature at dawn to comedy in the evening — as a complete and valid way of life, contrasted with Il Penseroso's solitary contemplation.

Historical Context

Written at Cambridge around 1631 alongside Il Penseroso. Published in Poems (1645). The two companion poems represent Milton's early exploration of temperament and artistic vocation.

About John Milton

John Milton was born on December 9, 1608, in London, England, and became one of the greatest poets in the English language, best known for his epic Paradise Lost (1667). He served as Latin Secretary to Oliver Cromwell's government during the English Interregnum and went completely blind around 1651-1652, composing his greatest works through dictation. He died in London in 1674.
