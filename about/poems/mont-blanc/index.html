Mont Blanc

by Percy Bysshe Shelley

Written 1816 Published 1817 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1817, this meditation on Mont Blanc argues that the mountain's power — the ravine, the glaciers, the avalanches — teaches the human mind something about the power of nature and of thought itself. The mountain is the home of silence and solitude.

Quick Answer

"Mont Blanc" is a poem by Shelley, published in 1817. Standing beneath the mountain, Shelley meditates on the universe of things flowing through the mind — the ravine is the mind's theatre. The mountain's glaciers carve out valleys as if preparing for something. The wilderness teaches human powers and their limits. If it were not heard or seen, would silence and solitude be vacancy?

The everlasting universe of things
Flows through the mind, and rolls its rapid waves,
Now dark—now glittering—now reflecting gloom—
Now lending splendour, where from secret springs
The source of human thought its tribute brings
Of waters—with a sound but half its own,
Such as a feeble brook will oft assume,
In the wild woods, among the mountains lone,
Where waterfalls around it leap for ever,
Where woods and winds contend, and a vast river
Over its rocks ceaselessly bursts and raves.

Thus thou, Ravine of Arve—dark, deep Ravine—
Thou many-coloured, many-voiced vale,
Over whose pines, and crags, and caverns sail
Fast cloud-shadows and sunbeams: awful scene,
Where Power in likeness of the Arve comes down
From the ice-gulphs that gird his secret throne,
Bursting through these dark mountains like the flame
Of lightning through the tempest; thou dost lie,
Thy giant brood of pines around thee clinging,
Children of elder time, in whose devotion
The chainless winds still come and ever came
To drink their odours, and their mighty swinging
To hear—an old and solemn harmony;
Thine earthly rainbows stretched across the sweep
Of the ethereal waterfall, whose veil
Robes some unsculptured image; the strange sleep
Which when the voices of the desert fail
Wraps all in its own deep eternity;—
Thy caverns echoing to the Arve's commotion,
A loud, lone sound no other sound can tame;
Thou art pervaded with that ceaseless motion,
Thou art the path of that unresting sound—
Dizzy Ravine! and when I gaze on thee
I seem as in a trance sublime and strange
To muse on my own separate fantasy,
My own, my human mind, which passively
Now renders and receives fast influencings,
Holding an unremitting interchange
With the clear universe of things around.

Analysis

Form and Structure

One hundred and forty-four lines of irregular verse, loosely blank with occasional rhymes, the form reflecting the poem's irregular, powerful subject.

Themes

The relationship between the mind and the external world — the mountain is both independent of human thought and deeply implicated in it. Shelley wavers between idealism (all things flow through the mind) and realism (the mountain exists independently and powerfully).

Historical Context

Written in 1816 while Shelley was visiting the Alps with Mary Godwin (later Mary Shelley). Published in the preface of Mary Shelley's History of a Six Weeks' Tour (1817).

About Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley was born on August 4, 1792, in Horsham, England, and became one of the major poets of English Romanticism despite controversy during his lifetime over his radical political and religious views. His poetry combines lyrical intensity with sustained engagement with politics, philosophy, and the natural world. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy in 1822, at the age of twenty-nine.
