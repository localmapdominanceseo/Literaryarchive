Air and Angels

by John Donne

Written 1601 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1601 and published posthumously in 1633, this poem meditates on what form love should take — the beloved's face and hair are too pure for love to inhabit, so love must take a subtler form: it settles in the beloved's love itself, as angels take air for their sphere.

Quick Answer

"Air and Angels" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker loved his beloved before he saw her, then found her too lovely to be inhabited by his love — he tried her face and hair as its sphere, but they were too bright. As angels take on air as their sphere without being air, so love must take the beloved's love as its sphere.

Twice or thrice had I loved thee,
Before I knew thy face or name;
So in a voice, so in a shapeless flame
Angels affect us oft, and worshipped be;
Still when, to where thou wert, I came,
Some lovely glorious nothing I did see.
But since my soul, whose child love is,
Takes limbs of flesh, and else could nothing do,
More subtle than the parent is
Love must not be, but take a body too;
And therefore what thou wert, and who,
I bid Love ask, and now
That it assume thy body, I allow,
And fix itself in thy lip, eye, and brow.

Whilst thus to ballast love I thought,
And so more steadily to have gone,
With wares which would sink admiration,
I saw I had love's pinnace overfraught;
Ev'ry thy hair for love to work upon
Is much too much; some fitter must be sought;
For, nor in nothing, nor in things
Extreme, and scatt'ring bright, can love inhere;
Then as an angel, face and wings
Of air, not pure as it, yet pure doth wear,
So thy love may be my love's sphere;
Just such disparity
As is 'twixt air and angels' purity,
'Twixt women's love, and men's, will ever be.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two stanzas of fourteen lines each, the complex rhyme scheme and argument making this one of Donne's most difficult love poems.

Themes

The philosophical problem of how immaterial love relates to a specific material person — the beloved is too bright and pure to serve as love's exact vessel, so the poem ends with the resolution that her love is the right sphere for his, as air is for angels.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633 in Songs and Sonnets. One of Donne's most densely argued lyric poems.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
