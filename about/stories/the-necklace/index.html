The Necklace

Guy de Maupassant · Written 1884, Published 1884 · ~2,700 words

Published in 1884 in Le Gaulois, 'The Necklace' (La Parure) is Guy de Maupassant's most famous story. Mathilde Loisel, a beautiful woman of modest means who longs for wealth, borrows a diamond necklace from a rich friend for a grand ball — and loses it. She and her husband spend ten years working off the debt to replace it, only to discover at the end that the original necklace was fake.

Quick Summary

'The Necklace' is a short story by Guy de Maupassant, published in 1884. Mathilde Loisel borrows a diamond necklace from her rich friend Madame Forestier for a grand ball. She loses it. She and her husband secretly replace it with a real diamond necklace, spending ten years in poverty to pay off the debt. When they finally have paid, she meets Madame Forestier and confesses — only to learn the original was paste, worth only five hundred francs.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration, tightly controlled and ironic, moving swiftly through years of suffering to deliver the twist ending with maximum economy.

Themes

The story attacks vanity and the desire for social status — Mathilde's longing for wealth destroys her actual life. The irony is savage: ten years of real suffering to replace something that was never real.

Historical Context

Published in Le Gaulois on February 17, 1884. One of the most anthologized stories in the world and the model for the modern twist-ending short story.

Key Passage

'Oh, my poor Mathilde! But mine was imitation. It was worth at most five hundred francs!'

Characters

Mathilde Loisel

A beautiful woman of modest means who is consumed by longing for the wealthy life she believes she deserves. Her vanity and discontentment drive the story's plot — she borrows the necklace to appear at the ball as the woman she imagines herself to be, and the consequences destroy exactly the life she was dissatisfied with.

Monsieur Loisel

Mathilde's devoted husband, a minor government clerk, who sacrifices his savings for her dress and then his entire financial future to replace the necklace. He is defined by his uncomplaining devotion to a wife who consistently undervalues him.

Madame Forestier

Mathilde's wealthy friend, whose casual generosity with the necklace sets the plot in motion. Her revelation at the story's end — 'Oh, my poor Mathilde! But mine was imitation' — is delivered simply, without drama, which makes it more devastating.

Symbols & Motifs

The Necklace

The necklace is the story's central symbol and its central irony. It represents the appearance of wealth that Mathilde craves — and it turns out to be an appearance only, fake, worth almost nothing. Ten years of real suffering to replace a false jewel: Maupassant's critique of social pretension is precise and merciless.

The Ball

The ball at the Ministry represents the social world Mathilde longs to belong to. Her one evening there — 'intoxicated with pleasure,' admired by everyone — is the single moment she achieves what she wants, and it costs her everything.

Opening Extract

She was one of those pretty and charming girls who are sometimes, as if by a mistake of destiny, born in a family of clerks. She had no dowry, no expectations, no means of being known, understood, loved, wedded by any rich and distinguished man; and she let herself be married to a little clerk at the Ministry of Public Instruction.

She dressed plainly because she could not dress well, but she was as unhappy as though she had really fallen from her proper station; since with women there is neither caste nor rank, and beauty, grace, and charm act instead of family and birth. Natural fineness, instinct for what is elegant, suppleness of wit, are the sole hierarchy, and make from women of the people the equals of the very greatest ladies.

Public domain. First published in Le Gaulois, February 17, 1884. Translated from French.

Legacy & Influence

Published in 1884, 'The Necklace' is the most widely translated and anthologized French short story in the world. It established the twist-ending story as a popular form and influenced generations of short story writers. The story's brutal efficiency — its complete lack of sentimentality toward Mathilde's suffering — is characteristic of Maupassant's naturalist approach.

Study Questions

  1. Is Mathilde a sympathetic character? Does Maupassant intend her to be?
  2. What does the story suggest about the relationship between social aspiration and suffering?
  3. How does the story's setting — Third Republic France with its rigid class distinctions — shape Mathilde's psychology?
  4. What is the significance of Monsieur Loisel's uncomplaining devotion?
  5. How does Maupassant's prose style — spare, precise, emotionally detached — serve the story's themes?

About Guy de Maupassant

Guy de Maupassant was born on August 5, 1850, in Tourville-sur-Arques, France. A protégé of Flaubert, he wrote nearly 300 short stories and six novels in a career lasting barely a decade before his mental collapse. His masterpieces include 'The Necklace,' 'Boule de Suif,' and 'The Horla.' He died in Paris in 1893.
