To Build a Fire

Jack London · Written 1908, Published 1908 · ~7,000 words

Published in 1908 in The Century Magazine, 'To Build a Fire' is Jack London's most celebrated story. A man travels alone through the Yukon in temperatures of seventy-five below zero against the advice of an old-timer. His dog knows it is too cold to travel. The man falls through ice, gets his feet wet, and cannot build a fire to warm himself.

Quick Summary

'To Build a Fire' is a story by Jack London, published in 1908. A man travels alone in the Yukon in extreme cold, accompanied only by a dog. He falls through ice and his feet get wet. He tries to build a fire but fails — first when snow falls from a tree and extinguishes it, then when his numbing hands cannot hold a match. He tries to kill the dog for warmth but cannot grip it. He dies in the snow. The dog trots off to find other men.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Close third-person narration that is deliberately flat and scientific — the narrator observes without sentimentality, as if reporting a natural experiment.

Themes

Naturalism and the indifference of nature — the man's fatal flaw is that he cannot imagine, only think. He knows the temperature is dangerous but cannot grasp what that means for his body. The dog has instinct he lacks.

Historical Context

Published in The Century Magazine, August 1908. London's most anthologized story and a masterwork of American naturalism.

Key Passage

The trouble with him was that he was without imagination. He was quick and alert in the things of life, but only in the things, and not in the significances.

Characters

The Man

Unnamed, emphasizing his representative status. He is not unintelligent or reckless — he knows the temperature is dangerous and takes precautions. His fatal flaw is that he can think but not imagine: he knows the facts of cold without understanding what they mean for his body. He cannot project forward into consequences.

The Dog

The man's companion, who knows instinctively that it is too cold to travel. The dog's knowledge — embodied, pre-rational, purely physical — is the knowledge the man lacks. The dog survives by following instinct; the man dies by following thought.

Symbols & Motifs

The Cold

The Yukon cold — seventy-five below zero — is not malevolent but simply physical. London's point is not that nature is cruel but that it is completely indifferent to human survival. The cold does not want to kill the man; it simply is what it is.

The Tree That Drops Snow on the Fire

The man builds his first fire under a spruce tree, and the heat from the fire causes snow to fall from the tree onto the flames. This random, mechanical cause-and-effect — with no malice involved — is London's most precise naturalist image. The man is undone not by malice but by physics.

The Man's Hands

As his hands freeze and become useless, the man loses the ability to act. The progressive paralysis of his hands tracks the story's arc: he can think but cannot act; thinking without the ability to act is useless.

Opening Extract

Day had broken cold and gray, exceedingly cold and gray, when the man turned aside from the main Yukon trail and climbed the high earth-bank, where a dim and little-travelled trail led eastward through the fat spruce timberland. It was a steep bank, and he paused for breath at the top, excusing the act to himself by looking at his watch. It was nine o'clock. There was no sun nor hint of sun, though there was not a cloud in the sky. It was a clear day, and yet there seemed an intangible pall over the face of things, a subtle gloom that made the day dark, and that was due to the absence of sun. This fact did not worry the man. He was used to the lack of sun. It had been days since he had seen the sun, and he knew that a few more days must pass before that cheerful orb, due south, would just peep above the sky-line and dip immediately from view.

Legacy & Influence

Published in 1908, 'To Build a Fire' is London's most celebrated story and a masterwork of American naturalism. London actually wrote an earlier, more didactic version in 1902; the 1908 version strips out the moral lesson and lets the facts speak. The story has been anthologized continuously since publication and is one of the most studied American stories in secondary and university education.

Study Questions

  1. What is the significance of the man remaining unnamed?
  2. How does London contrast the man's rational thinking with the dog's instinct?
  3. What does the story argue about the relationship between knowledge and understanding?
  4. How does the story function as a critique of human pride in rationality?
  5. What is the effect of London's flat, unemotional prose style on the story's impact?

About Jack London

Jack London was born on January 12, 1876, in San Francisco, California. His adventure novels and stories — The Call of the Wild (1903), White Fang (1906), and 'To Build a Fire' (1908) — made him the highest-paid American writer of his time. He was also a committed socialist and journalist. He died in Glen Ellen, California, in 1916.
