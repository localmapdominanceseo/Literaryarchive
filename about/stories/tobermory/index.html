Tobermory

Saki (H.H. Munro) · Written 1911, Published 1911 · ~3,800 words

Published in 1911 in The Chronicles of Clovis, 'Tobermory' is Saki's most celebrated long comic story. A guest at a country house party announces he has taught the cat Tobermory to speak. Tobermory proceeds to repeat the private conversations of every guest — with devastating accuracy.

Quick Summary

'Tobermory' is a story by Saki, published in 1911. At a country house party, a man named Appin announces he has taught the cat Tobermory to speak. Tobermory proceeds to reveal the private conversations of every guest — their affairs, their contempt for each other, their secrets. The guests decide Tobermory must be killed. He disappears. Later Appin is killed by an elephant in Germany.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Comic third-person narration — the comedy builds as each guest realizes what Tobermory knows about them.

Themes

The comedy of social hypocrisy exposed — Tobermory's literal mind repeats exactly what he has heard, with no sense of social tact.

Historical Context

Published in The Chronicles of Clovis (1911).

Opening Extract

It was a chill, rain-washed afternoon of a late August day, that indefinite season when partridges are still in security or cold storage, and there is nothing to hunt — unless one is bounded on the north by the Bristol Channel, in which case one may lawfully gallop after fat red stags. Lady Blemley's house-party was not bounded on the north by the Bristol Channel, hence there was a full gathering of her guests round the tea-table on this particular afternoon.

Public domain. Published in The Chronicles of Clovis, 1911.

About Saki (H.H. Munro)

Hector Hugh Munro, known as Saki, was born on December 18, 1870, in Akyab, Burma. His wickedly comic short stories — 'The Open Window,' 'Tobermory,' 'Sredni Vashtar' — are among the finest in English. He enlisted as a private at the outbreak of World War I despite being over-age and was killed by a sniper near Beaumont-Hamel in 1916.
