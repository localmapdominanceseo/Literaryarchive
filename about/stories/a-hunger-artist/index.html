A Hunger Artist

Franz Kafka · Written 1922, Published 1922 · ~4,000 words

Published in 1922, one of Kafka's last stories, 'A Hunger Artist' describes a professional faster — an entertainer who fasts in a cage for public spectacle. Fasting loses its public appeal; the hunger artist is forgotten in his cage and eventually dies. His final secret: he never found food he liked.

Quick Summary

'A Hunger Artist' is a story by Kafka, published in 1922. A professional faster entertains crowds by starving himself in a cage for forty days. The art of fasting falls out of fashion; he is forgotten in his cage at a circus and finally removed, dying, to reveal what he was fasting for. He confesses to the overseer: he could never find food he liked. A panther is put in his empty cage and draws the crowds.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with an unusual plural perspective — 'we' — implying the narrator is one of the crowd who watched and then forgot.

Themes

The artist's isolation and the public's fickle attention — the hunger artist's incomprehensible devotion to his art cannot be explained to an audience that simply wants entertainment. His final secret makes his whole career a cosmic misunderstanding.

Historical Context

Published in Die neue Rundschau (1922). One of Kafka's last stories, written while he was dying of tuberculosis.

Opening Extract

During these last decades the interest in professional fasting has markedly diminished. It used to pay very well to stage such great performances under one's own management, but today that is quite impossible. We live in a different world now. At one time the whole town took a lively interest in the hunger artist; from day to day of his fast the excitement mounted; everybody wanted to see him at least once a day.

Public domain. Published 1922. Translated from German.

About Franz Kafka

Franz Kafka was born on July 3, 1883, in Prague, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Writing in German, he produced some of the most disquieting and influential fiction of the twentieth century: 'The Metamorphosis,' 'The Trial,' 'The Castle,' and 'In the Penal Colony.' Most of his work was published posthumously by his friend Max Brod, against Kafka's explicit instructions to burn it. He died of tuberculosis in Kierling, Austria, in 1924.
