Rip Van Winkle

Washington Irving · Written 1819, Published 1819 · ~7,500 words

Published in 1819 in The Sketch Book, 'Rip Van Winkle' tells of a Dutch-American villager in the Catskill Mountains who meets mysterious men, drinks their liquor, falls asleep, and wakes twenty years later to find the world completely changed.

Quick Summary

'Rip Van Winkle' is a story by Washington Irving, published in 1819. Rip Van Winkle, a good-natured but lazy villager, encounters strange men in the Catskill Mountains, drinks their mysterious liquor, and falls asleep. He wakes twenty years later. His wife is dead, his children grown, and the American Revolution has happened. He has slept through history.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Presented as a manuscript found among the papers of a fictional Dutch historian — Irving's favorite frame device, lending mock-scholarly legitimacy to the tall tale.

Themes

The story is simultaneously a comic tale about laziness and a meditation on time, change, and the American Revolution. Rip sleeps through the founding of the nation and must adapt to a world transformed.

Historical Context

Published in The Sketch Book of Geoffrey Crayon, Gent. (1819). Based loosely on a German folk tale. One of the first internationally celebrated works of American fiction.

Opening Extract

Whoever has made a voyage up the Hudson must remember the Kaatskill mountains. They are a dismembered branch of the great Appalachian family, and are seen away to the west of the river, swelling up to a noble height, and lording it over the surrounding country. Every change of season, every change of weather, indeed, every hour of the day, produces some change in the magical hues and shapes of these mountains, and they are regarded by all the good wives, far and near, as perfect barometers.

Public domain. Published in The Sketch Book of Geoffrey Crayon, Gent., 1819.

About Washington Irving

Washington Irving was born on April 3, 1783, in New York City. He was the first American writer to achieve international fame, best known for 'Rip Van Winkle' and 'The Legend of Sleepy Hollow,' both published in The Sketch Book (1819-20). He died in Tarrytown, New York, in 1859.
