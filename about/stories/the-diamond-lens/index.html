The Diamond Lens

Fitz-James O'Brien · Written 1858, Published 1858 · ~9,500 words

Published in 1858 in The Atlantic Monthly, 'The Diamond Lens' is an early science fiction story about a microscopist who murders a neighbor to steal a diamond needed to make a perfect microscope lens. Through the lens he sees a microscopic world — and a beautiful woman living in a drop of water. He falls in love with her. The water evaporates.

Quick Summary

'The Diamond Lens' is a story by Fitz-James O'Brien, published in 1858. A young microscopist murders his neighbor for a perfect diamond to make the ultimate microscope lens. Through it he discovers a tiny world in a drop of water and falls in love with its beautiful inhabitant, Animula. He neglects everything to watch her. The water evaporates; Animula dies; he goes mad.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person confession — the narrator tells his story with complete self-awareness about his obsession.

Themes

The fatal nature of scientific obsession and idealized love — the narrator destroys himself through the pursuit of a perfection that is both literally and figuratively unreachable.

Historical Context

Published in The Atlantic Monthly, January 1858. One of the earliest American science fiction stories.

Opening Extract

From my earliest youth I have been a lover of the microscope. Not the plaything microscopes of commerce, those clumsy instruments, not worthy the name — but the true microscope, as it should be, the microscope of the future, the real instrument of wonder.

Public domain. First published in The Atlantic Monthly, January 1858.

About Fitz-James O'Brien

Fitz-James O'Brien was born around 1828 in County Limerick, Ireland. He emigrated to New York and became one of the most inventive American fiction writers of the 1850s, producing early science fiction and horror stories. 'The Diamond Lens' (1858) and 'What Was It?' are his masterpieces. He died of a wound received in the American Civil War in 1862.
