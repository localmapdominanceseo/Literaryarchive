A Country Doctor

Franz Kafka · Written 1917, Published 1919 · ~2,500 words

Published in 1919, 'A Country Doctor' describes a doctor's nightmarish attempt to answer an urgent night call. Everything goes wrong: his horse is dead, mysterious horses and a groom appear from his pigsty, the groom attacks his maidservant, the boy the doctor is summoned to see has a terrible wound — and the doctor cannot get home.

Quick Summary

'A Country Doctor' is a story by Kafka, published in 1919. A doctor is summoned on a winter night call. His horse is dead; mysterious horses appear from his pigsty, but the groom attacks his servant Rose. He cannot go back. The sick boy has a rose-colored wound in his side — a wound the doctor cannot heal. He is undressed and placed in bed with the boy. He escapes but rides naked through the winter night, unable to return home. He has answered the false alarm bell.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person present-tense narration — the dream-logic of the story is enacted by the prose's forward momentum that cannot stop or reverse.

Themes

The failure of professional and moral duty — the doctor cannot save the boy, cannot protect Rose, cannot return home. He has been stripped of everything by the impossible demands placed on him.

Historical Context

Published in A Country Doctor: Fourteen Little Stories (1919).

Opening Extract

I was in great difficulty. An urgent journey lay before me. A seriously ill patient was waiting for me in a village ten miles away. A great snowstorm filled the long stretch of road between us. I had a carriage — light, with large wheels, exactly right for our country roads; muffled in fur, bag of instruments in hand, I stood in the courtyard ready to go; but the horse was missing, the horse.

Public domain. Published 1919. Translated from German.

About Franz Kafka

Franz Kafka was born on July 3, 1883, in Prague, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Writing in German, he produced some of the most disquieting and influential fiction of the twentieth century: 'The Metamorphosis,' 'The Trial,' 'The Castle,' and 'In the Penal Colony.' Most of his work was published posthumously by his friend Max Brod, against Kafka's explicit instructions to burn it. He died of tuberculosis in Kierling, Austria, in 1924.
