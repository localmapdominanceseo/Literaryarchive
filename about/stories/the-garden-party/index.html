The Garden Party

Katherine Mansfield · Written 1921, Published 1922 · ~5,700 words

Published in 1922 in The Weekly Westminster Gazette, 'The Garden Party' follows Laura Sheridan as her wealthy family prepares for their garden party. When she learns that a carter's husband has been killed in an accident nearby, she wants to cancel — but is dissuaded. After the party, she takes a basket of leftover food to the dead man's family and is confronted with death for the first time.

Quick Summary

'The Garden Party' is a story by Katherine Mansfield, published in 1922. Laura Sheridan's family is preparing a garden party when they learn a working-class man has been killed nearby. Laura wants to cancel; her mother says they should not — it would be absurd. The party is a great success. Afterward, Laura takes leftovers to the dead man's cottage and sees his body. She is moved beyond words. She meets her brother Laurie on the road and tries to explain: 'Isn't life—' She cannot finish.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Close third-person narration that inhabits Laura's consciousness with Mansfield's characteristic impressionistic style — the physical world rendered with intense sensory vividness.

Themes

The collision of class privilege with mortality — Laura's instinct is right (the party is obscene in the circumstances) but she is overridden by social convention. Her encounter with the dead man is the first time her sheltered consciousness confronts reality.

Historical Context

Published in 1922, collected in The Garden Party and Other Stories (1922). One of Mansfield's most celebrated stories.

Key Passage

'Isn't life,' she stammered, 'isn't life—' But what life was she couldn't explain. No matter. He quite understood.

Opening Extract

And after all the weather was ideal. They could not have had a more perfect day for a garden-party if they had ordered it. Windless, warm, the sky without a cloud. Only the blue was veiled with a haze of light gold, as it is sometimes in early summer. The gardener had been up since dawn, mowing the lawns and sweeping them, until the grass and the dark flat rosettes where the daisy plants had been seemed to shine. As for the roses, you could not help feeling they understood that roses are the only flowers that impress people at garden-parties; the only flowers that everybody is certain of knowing. Hundreds, yes, literally hundreds, had come out in a single night; the green bushes bowed down as though they had been visited by archangels.

Public domain. First published in The Weekly Westminster Gazette, 1922.

About Katherine Mansfield

Katherine Mansfield was born on October 14, 1888, in Wellington, New Zealand. She moved to London and became one of the most important short story writers in the English language, known for her impressionistic style, her rendering of consciousness, and her attention to the hidden lives of women. She died of tuberculosis in Fontainebleau, France, in 1923.
