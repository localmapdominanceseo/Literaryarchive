A Clean, Well-Lighted Place

Ernest Hemingway · Written 1933, Published 1933 · ~1,500 words

Published in 1933 in Scribner's Magazine, 'A Clean, Well-Lighted Place' is Hemingway at his most minimalist. Two waiters discuss an old deaf man who drinks alone late at night. The younger waiter wants to close; the older understands that the old man needs the light and the cleanness — a defense against the nada, the nothingness.

Quick Summary

'A Clean, Well-Lighted Place' is a story by Hemingway, published in 1933. Two waiters watch a deaf old man drinking brandy alone late at night. The young waiter wants to go home. The older waiter understands: some people need a clean, well-lighted place — a refuge against the nothingness. He stays open. After closing, he goes to a bar and thinks of nada: nothing, emptiness.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Almost entirely dialogue and minimal narration — Hemingway's iceberg theory at its most compressed. The older waiter's prayer replaces 'nuestro' and 'Señor' with 'nada.'

Themes

The existential need for order and light against the encroaching nothingness — the older waiter's compassion for the lonely old man is an acknowledgment of universal human isolation.

Historical Context

Published in Scribner's Magazine, March 1933. Collected in Winner Take Nothing (1933).

Extract

It was late and every one had left the café except an old man who sat in the shadow the leaves of the tree made against the electric light. In the day time the street was dusty, but at night the dew settled the dust and the old man liked to sit late because he was deaf and now at night it was quiet and he felt the difference.

About Ernest Hemingway

Ernest Hemingway was born on July 21, 1899, in Oak Park, Illinois. His prose style — stripped of adjectives, relying on dialogue and implication — transformed American fiction. His novels include The Sun Also Rises (1926) and A Farewell to Arms (1929). He won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1954. He died by suicide in Ketchum, Idaho, in 1961.
