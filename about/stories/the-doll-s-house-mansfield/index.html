The Doll's House

Katherine Mansfield · Written 1921, Published 1922 · ~3,500 words

Published in 1922 in The Nation and Athenaeum, 'The Doll's House' describes the Burnell children who receive a magnificent doll's house. They are allowed to show it to schoolmates — but not to the Kelvey girls, children of a washerwoman, who are social outcasts. The youngest Burnell girl shows it to them anyway. A lamp inside the doll's house — that lamp — is the story's still centre.

Quick Summary

'The Doll's House' is a story by Katherine Mansfield, published in 1922. The Burnell children receive a doll's house and invite schoolmates to see it. The Kelvey girls — social outcasts, daughters of a washerwoman — are excluded. Little Kezia Burnell shows the house to the Kelveys anyway, briefly, before her aunt drives them away. The Kelveys walk on. 'I seen the little lamp,' says Else Kelvey, smiling.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration moving between the perspectives of the privileged Burnells and the excluded Kelveys — Mansfield's irony operating throughout.

Themes

Class cruelty and the innocent who sees past it — the lamp inside the doll's house is small and perfect, and Else's recognition of it is the story's act of grace against the cruelty surrounding it.

Historical Context

Published in The Nation and Athenaeum, February 1922. Collected in The Dove's Nest (1923).

Opening Extract

When dear old Mrs. Hay went back to town after staying with the Burnells she sent the children a doll's house. It was so big that the carter and Pat carried it into the courtyard, and there it stayed, propped up on two wooden boxes beside the feed-room door. No harm could come to it; it was summer. And perhaps the smell of paint would have gone off by the time it had to be taken in.

Public domain. Published 1922.

About Katherine Mansfield

Katherine Mansfield was born on October 14, 1888, in Wellington, New Zealand. She moved to London and became one of the most important short story writers in the English language, known for her impressionistic style, her rendering of consciousness, and her attention to the hidden lives of women. She died of tuberculosis in Fontainebleau, France, in 1923.
