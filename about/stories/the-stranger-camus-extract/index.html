The Guest

Albert Camus · Written 1957, Published 1957 · ~5,800 words

Published in 1957 in the collection L'Exil et le royaume (Exile and the Kingdom), 'The Guest' is set in Algeria during the colonial period. A French schoolteacher named Daru is ordered to escort an Arab prisoner to the police at Tinguit. He cannot bring himself to deliver the man to justice. He gives him food and money and shows him the road to freedom — and the road to prison — and lets him choose. The Arab walks toward prison.

Quick Summary

'The Guest' is a story by Albert Camus, published in 1957. Daru, a French schoolteacher in colonial Algeria, is ordered to escort an Arab prisoner to the police. He refuses to make himself the man's jailer but cannot release him. He gives him food, shelter, money, and shows him the two roads — to freedom and to prison — and lets him choose. The Arab chooses prison. Daru returns to his school to find a message on the blackboard: 'You handed over our brother. You will pay for this.'

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration from Daru's perspective — Camus's characteristic precise, spare prose.

Themes

The impossibility of neutrality in colonial Algeria — Daru's refusal to take sides satisfies no one. The Arab chooses prison; the Arab nationalists blame Daru. The absurd situation has no good outcome.

Historical Context

Published in L'Exil et le royaume (1957). Set during the Algerian War of Independence.

Opening Extract

The schoolmaster was watching the two men climb toward him. One was on horseback, the other on foot. They had not yet tackled the abrupt rise leading to the schoolhouse built on the hillside. They were toiling onward, making slow progress in the snow, among the stones, on the vast expanse of the high, deserted plateau.

Public domain in many jurisdictions. Published 1957. Translated from French.

About Albert Camus

Albert Camus was born on November 7, 1913, in Mondovi, Algeria, then a French colony. His novels The Stranger (1942) and The Plague (1947) and his philosophical essay The Myth of Sisyphus (1942) established him as the leading voice of literary existentialism. He won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1957. He died in a car accident near Sens, France, in 1960.
