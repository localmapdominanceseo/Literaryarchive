Winter Dreams

F. Scott Fitzgerald · Written 1922, Published 1922 · ~8,500 words

Published in 1922 in Metropolitan Magazine, 'Winter Dreams' is often called the first draft of The Great Gatsby. Dexter Green, a caddy's son, is obsessed with Judy Jones, the carelessly beautiful daughter of wealth, and spends years pursuing and losing her — until he finally learns she has become ordinary and his dream is gone.

Quick Summary

'Winter Dreams' is a story by Fitzgerald, published in 1922. Dexter Green, from modest origins, becomes successful in business while obsessively pursuing the beautiful, carelessly cruel Judy Jones. She takes him up and drops him repeatedly. He becomes engaged to a good woman, then Judy appears again and he drops the engagement. Years later he learns Judy has become plain and ordinary — and he realizes his dream of her is gone and cannot be recovered.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration covering many years in swift, impressionistic strokes — the story's structure mimics the way the past is remembered selectively.

Themes

The American Dream of self-creation and its emotional cost — Dexter's winter dreams are about class aspiration as much as love. When Judy becomes ordinary, he loses his dream of who he could become through her.

Historical Context

Published in Metropolitan Magazine, December 1922. Collected in All the Sad Young Men (1926).

Opening Extract

Some of the caddies were poor as sin and lived in one-room houses with a neurasthenic cow in the front yard, but Dexter Green's father owned the second best grocery-store in Black Bear — the best one was 'The Hub,' patronized by the wealthy people from Sherry Island — and Dexter caddied only for pocket-money.

Public domain. First published in Metropolitan Magazine, December 1922.

About F. Scott Fitzgerald

Francis Scott Key Fitzgerald was born on September 24, 1896, in St. Paul, Minnesota. The Great Gatsby (1925) is his masterpiece — the definitive novel of the American Dream's promise and betrayal. His short stories, collected in Flappers and Philosophers and Tales of the Jazz Age, are equally important to the period. He died in Hollywood in 1940.
