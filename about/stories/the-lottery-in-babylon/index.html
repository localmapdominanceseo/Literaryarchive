The Library of Babel

Jorge Luis Borges · Written 1941, Published 1941 · ~4,500 words

Published in 1941, 'The Library of Babel' is Borges's most celebrated metaphysical story. The narrator describes an infinite library — the universe — that contains every possible book of 410 pages. Most books are gibberish; a tiny fraction contain meaning. The librarians search for the catalogue of catalogues and the book that contains all books. The library is total and eternal.

Quick Summary

'The Library of Babel' is a story by Borges, published in 1941. The universe is a vast library of hexagonal rooms containing all possible books — every combination of letters in 410 pages. Most books are meaningless noise; a tiny fraction contain coherent text. Librarians have searched for the Crimson Hexagon containing a book that is the perfect compendium of all others. The narrator concludes: the library is unlimited and cyclical.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A philosophical essay in the guise of fiction — Borges presents the library with mathematical precision and footnotes, as if it were real.

Themes

The infinite and the random — the library contains all truth but buried in infinite noise. The story is about the human condition of searching for meaning in what may be fundamentally random.

Historical Context

Published in El jardín de senderos que se bifurcan (1941). Collected in Ficciones (1944).

Extract

The universe (which others call the Library) is composed of an indefinite, perhaps infinite number of hexagonal galleries. In the center of each gallery is a ventilation shaft, bounded by a low railing. From any hexagon one can see the floors above and below — one after another, endlessly. The arrangement of the galleries is always the same: twenty bookshelves, five to each side, line four of the hexagon's six sides; the height of the bookshelves, floor to ceiling, is hardly greater than the height of a normal librarian.

Public domain in many jurisdictions. Published 1941. Translated from Spanish.

About Jorge Luis Borges

Jorge Luis Borges was born on August 24, 1899, in Buenos Aires, Argentina. His short story collections Ficciones (1944) and El Aleph (1949) established him as one of the most influential fiction writers of the twentieth century. His labyrinthine, philosophical stories — mixing detective fiction, fantasy, and metaphysics — founded what would become known as magical realism. He died in Geneva in 1986.
