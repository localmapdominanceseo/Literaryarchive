The Fly

Katherine Mansfield · Written 1922, Published 1922 · ~2,200 words

Published in 1922, 'The Fly' by Katherine Mansfield describes a businessman called 'the boss' who shows an old friend the portrait of his dead son, killed in the war. Alone afterward, he torments a fly that has fallen into his inkpot — dropping ink on it each time it recovers — until it dies. He cannot remember what he was thinking about.

Quick Summary

'The Fly' is a story by Katherine Mansfield, published in 1922. The boss shows an old friend his dead son's portrait — the son was killed in the Great War six years ago. Alone, he discovers a fly in his inkpot and rescues it. Each time it cleans itself and recovers, he drops more ink on it. Finally it dies. He cannot remember what he was thinking of before — the grief he was about to feel has been drowned by the act of cruelty.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration of extreme compression — the story is barely 2,000 words and achieves its effect through implication.

Themes

The way grief is suppressed and displaced — the boss cannot face his son's death and instead enacts a small cruelty that uses up whatever feeling he had available. The fly is both his son and himself.

Historical Context

Published in The Dove's Nest and Other Stories (1923).

Extract

"Y'are very snug in here," piped old Mr. Woodifield, and he peered out of the great, green leather armchair by his friend the boss's desk as a baby peers out of its pram. His talk was over; it was time for him to be off. But he did not want to go. Since he had retired, since his... stroke, the wife and the girls kept him boxed up in the house every day of the week except Tuesday.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1923.

About Katherine Mansfield

Katherine Mansfield was born on October 14, 1888, in Wellington, New Zealand. She moved to London and became one of the most important short story writers in the English language, known for her impressionistic style, her rendering of consciousness, and her attention to the hidden lives of women. She died of tuberculosis in Fontainebleau, France, in 1923.
