The Happy Prince

Oscar Wilde · Written 1888, Published 1888 · ~4,000 words

Published in 1888 in The Happy Prince and Other Tales, 'The Happy Prince' tells of a golden statue of a prince who enlists a swallow to strip his jewels and gold leaf to give to the poor of the city. The swallow, delayed too long by love for the prince, dies in the cold. The city melts down the broken statue. God asks an angel to bring him the two most precious things in the city — the leaden heart and the dead bird.

Quick Summary

'The Happy Prince' is a fairy tale by Oscar Wilde, published in 1888. The gilded statue of the Happy Prince sends a passing swallow to carry his ruby, his sapphire eyes, and his gold leaf to the poor. The swallow stays too long and dies in the cold at the prince's feet. The city authorities melt down the statue. God tells an angel to bring the two most precious things in the city: the leaden heart and the dead bird.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A fairy tale in Wilde's ornate, lyrical prose — apparently simple but deeply ironic about the city's treatment of beauty and charity.

Themes

The story is about selfless love and its destruction by the indifference of society — the prince and swallow give everything and are discarded. Only God recognises their worth.

Historical Context

Published in The Happy Prince and Other Tales (1888). Written for Wilde's sons.

Opening Extract

High above the city, on a tall column, stood the statue of the Happy Prince. He was gilded all over with thin leaves of fine gold, for eyes he had two bright sapphires, and great red ruby glowed on his sword-hilt.

He was very much admired indeed. 'He is as beautiful as a weathercock,' remarked one of the Town Councillors who wished to gain a reputation for having artistic tastes; 'only not quite so useful,' he added, fearing lest people should think him unpractical, which he really was not.

Public domain. Published in The Happy Prince and Other Tales, 1888.

About Oscar Wilde

Oscar Wilde was born on October 16, 1854, in Dublin, Ireland. A poet, playwright, novelist, and wit, he is best known for The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890), The Importance of Being Earnest (1895), and his fairy tales. He was imprisoned for two years for 'gross indecency' in 1895 and died in Paris in 1900.
