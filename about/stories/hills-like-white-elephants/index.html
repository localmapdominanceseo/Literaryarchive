Hills Like White Elephants

Ernest Hemingway · Written 1927, Published 1927 · ~1,500 words

Published in 1927 in transition magazine, 'Hills Like White Elephants' is Hemingway's most celebrated application of his iceberg theory. A couple waits for a train at a Spanish bar. Their conversation circles around a decision the woman must make — never named in the text, but understood to be an abortion — while the man pushes, gently but relentlessly, for her to agree.

Quick Summary

'Hills Like White Elephants' is a story by Ernest Hemingway, published in 1927. A man and a woman wait for a train at a bar in Spain. They drink and talk about an unnamed operation the woman is to have. The man reassures her it is simple and natural and that he will love her afterward. The woman looks at the hills and says they look like white elephants. Nothing is resolved.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Almost entirely dialogue, with minimal narration — the iceberg theory in its purest form: the subject of the story (an abortion) is never named, and its meaning must be inferred entirely from what the characters say and don't say.

Themes

The power imbalance between the man and the woman, the pressure exerted through apparent reasonableness, and the woman's isolation and suppressed resistance. The hills — barren on one side, fertile on the other — are the story's central image.

Historical Context

Published in transition magazine in 1927, collected in Men Without Women (1927). One of the most analyzed stories in the American short fiction canon.

Key Passage

'I don't care about me.' 'Well, I care about you.' 'Oh, yes. But I don't care about me. And I'll do it and then everything will be fine.'

Extract

The hills across the valley of the Ebro were long and white. On this side there was no shade and no trees and the station was between two lines of rails in the sun. Close against the side of the station there was the warm shadow of the building and a curtain, made of strings of bamboo beads, hung across the open door into the bar, to keep out flies. The American and the girl with him sat at a table in the shade, outside the building. It was very hot and the express from Barcelona would come in forty minutes. It stopped at this junction for two minutes and went on to Madrid.

'What should we drink?' the girl asked. She had taken off her hat and put it on the table.

'It's pretty hot,' the man said.

'Let's drink beer.'

'Dos cervezas,' the man said into the curtain.

'Big ones?' a woman asked from the doorway.

'Yes. Two big ones.'

The woman brought two glasses of beer and two felt pads. She put the felt pads and the beer glasses on the table and looked at the man and the girl. The girl was looking off at the line of hills. They were white in the sun and the country was brown and dry.

About Ernest Hemingway

Ernest Hemingway was born on July 21, 1899, in Oak Park, Illinois. His prose style — stripped of adjectives, relying on dialogue and implication — transformed American fiction. His novels include The Sun Also Rises (1926) and A Farewell to Arms (1929). He won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1954. He died by suicide in Ketchum, Idaho, in 1961.
