A Rose for Emily

William Faulkner · Written 1930, Published 1930 · ~3,900 words

Published in 1930 in The Forum, 'A Rose for Emily' is William Faulkner's most famous story. Told by a collective narrator of townspeople, it describes the life and death of Emily Grierson, a reclusive Southern woman who refuses to pay taxes, refuses to let her lover leave, and whose secret is revealed only after her death.

Quick Summary

'A Rose for Emily' is a story by William Faulkner, published in 1930. The town narrates the life of Emily Grierson, a reclusive aristocratic woman. After her father dies and her Northern lover Homer Barron disappears, she becomes increasingly isolated. When Emily dies, townspeople enter her house and find Homer's decomposed body in an upstairs bedroom — and on the pillow beside him, a strand of Emily's gray hair.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Collective first-person narration ('we') — the town speaking as one — with the chronology deliberately scrambled, so the revelation at the end retroactively reorders everything the reader has been told.

Themes

The Southern past refusing to die — Emily represents the old aristocratic order that cannot adapt to change and so preserves its love objects literally. The story is about denial, both personal and regional.

Historical Context

Published in The Forum in April 1930. Faulkner's most anthologized story and a foundational text in the Southern Gothic tradition.

Key Passage

Then we noticed that in the second pillow was the indentation of a head. One of us lifted something from it, and leaning forward, that faint and invisible dust dry and acrid in the nostrils, we saw a long strand of iron-gray hair.

Extract

When Miss Emily Grierson died, our whole town went to her funeral: the men through a sort of respectful affection for a fallen monument, the women mostly out of curiosity to see the inside of her house, which no one save an old man-servant — a combined gardener and cook — had seen in at least ten years.

It was a big, squarish frame house that had once been white, decorated with cupolas and spires and scrolled balconies in the heavily lightsome style of the seventies, set on what had once been our most select street. But garages and cotton gins had encroached and obliterated even the august names of that neighborhood; only Miss Emily's house was left, lifting its stubborn and coquettish decay above the cotton wagons and the gasoline pumps — an eyestone among eyestones.

About William Faulkner

William Faulkner was born on September 25, 1897, in New Albany, Mississippi. His novels and stories set in the fictional Yoknapatawpha County — including The Sound and the Fury (1929), As I Lay Dying (1930), and Absalom, Absalom! (1936) — are among the most complex and rewarding in American literature. He won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1949. He died in Byhalia, Mississippi, in 1962.
