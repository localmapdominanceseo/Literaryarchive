The Use of Force

William Carlos Williams · Written 1933, Published 1933 · ~1,400 words

Published in 1933 in Blast, 'The Use of Force' is William Carlos Williams's most celebrated prose work. A doctor visits a sick child who refuses to open her mouth for examination. The doctor must force her to open it — and notices that the struggle has become something more than medical: he feels fury, and she resists with savage dignity.

Quick Summary

'The Use of Force' is a story by William Carlos Williams, published in 1933. A doctor makes a house call on a feverish child who refuses to let him examine her throat for diphtheria. As he tries to force her compliance, he becomes aware of his own rage — and his awareness that he and she are both caught in a struggle where he must win by force and she resists with something like dignity. He finally gets her mouth open.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

First-person narration by the doctor — startlingly honest about his own irrational fury during what is nominally a professional act.

Themes

The paradox of care that must become violence — the doctor is trying to save the child but must overpower her to do it. His self-awareness about his own anger is the story's disturbing center.

Historical Context

Published in Blast in 1933. Collected in The Farmer's Daughters (1961).

Opening Extract

They were new patients to me, all I had was the name, Olson. Please come down as soon as you can, my daughter is very sick. When I arrived I was met by the mother, a big startled-looking woman, very clean and apologetic who merely said, Is this the doctor? and let me in. In the back, she added. You must excuse us, doctor, we have her in the kitchen where it is warm. It is very damp here sometimes.

Published 1933. Copyright status: under copyright. Extract shown for educational commentary.

About William Carlos Williams

William Carlos Williams was born on September 17, 1883, in Rutherford, New Jersey, and practiced medicine as a pediatrician for most of his adult life while also becoming one of the most influential American Modernist poets. He rejected the allusive, European-influenced style of T.S. Eliot in favor of the American idiom, ordinary speech, and concrete sensory images drawn from everyday life. He died in Rutherford in 1963.
