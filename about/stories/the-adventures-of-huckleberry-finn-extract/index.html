The Man That Corrupted Hadleyburg

Mark Twain · Written 1899, Published 1899 · ~21,000 words

Published in 1899 in Harper's Monthly, 'The Man That Corrupted Hadleyburg' tells of a passing stranger who takes revenge on the smug, self-righteous town of Hadleyburg by sending each of the nineteen most prominent families a sack of gold and a letter claiming they once helped him — forcing each to lie to claim the money, ruining the town's reputation for incorruptibility.

Quick Summary

'The Man That Corrupted Hadleyburg' is a story by Mark Twain, published in 1899. An offended stranger sends a sack (apparently) of gold to Hadleyburg's nineteen leading families, each receiving a letter claiming the town's most honest citizen once helped him and should claim the gold by reciting the words he said at the time. He has forged identical letters to all nineteen. Each family lies to claim the gold; the town's famous incorruptibility is destroyed in public.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A satirical fable told in third-person narration — the community as protagonist, exposed through a trap of its own pride.

Themes

The hypocrisy of civic virtue — Hadleyburg's reputation for honesty was untested and therefore meaningless. The story is Twain's bleakest satire on human nature and community pretension.

Historical Context

Published in Harper's Monthly, December 1899. Written during Twain's darkest period.

Opening Extract

It was many years ago. Hadleyburg was the most honest and upright town in all the region round about. It had kept that reputation unsmirched during three generations, and was prouder of it than of any other of its possessions. It was so proud of it, and so anxious to insure its perpetuation, that it began to teach the principles of honest dealing to its babies in the cradle, and made the like teachings the staple of their culture thenceforward through all the years devoted to their education.

Public domain. First published in Harper's Monthly, December 1899.

About Mark Twain

Samuel Langhorne Clemens, known as Mark Twain, was born on November 30, 1835, in Florida, Missouri. The Adventures of Tom Sawyer (1876), Adventures of Huckleberry Finn (1884), and The Adventures of Tom Sawyer are among the most celebrated American novels. His satirical stories and essays make him the greatest American humorist. He died in Redding, Connecticut, in 1910.
