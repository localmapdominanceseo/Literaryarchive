The Birthmark

Nathaniel Hawthorne · Written 1843, Published 1843 · ~6,700 words

Published in 1843 in The Pioneer, 'The Birthmark' tells of Aylmer, a brilliant scientist who marries a beautiful woman named Georgiana. He becomes obsessed with the small red birthmark on her cheek — the one human flaw in her otherwise perfect beauty. He experiments to remove it. He succeeds — and she dies.

Quick Summary

'The Birthmark' is a story by Nathaniel Hawthorne, published in 1843. Aylmer, a brilliant scientist, is obsessed with the small red birthmark on his wife Georgiana's cheek — the mark of human mortality in an otherwise perfect being. He formulates an elixir to remove it. As the birthmark fades, Georgiana dies. The mark was her hold on life.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person allegorical narration — Hawthorne's characteristic mode of exploring moral themes through symbolic action.

Themes

The story is an allegory about the impossibility of human perfection and the dangers of the scientific ambition to transcend natural limits. The birthmark represents mortality itself — to remove it is to remove Georgiana's humanity.

Historical Context

Published in The Pioneer in March 1843. One of Hawthorne's most anthologized stories.

Opening Extract

In the latter part of the last century there lived a man of science, an eminent proficient in every branch of natural philosophy, who not long before our story opens had made experience of a spiritual affinity more attractive than any chemical one. He had left his laboratory to the care of an assistant, cleared his fine countenance from the furnace smoke, washed the stain of acids from his fingers, and persuaded a beautiful woman to become his wife. In those days when the comparatively recent discovery of electricity and other kindred mysteries of Nature seemed to open paths into the region of miracle, it was not unusual for the love of science to rival the love of woman in its depth and absorbing energy.

Public domain. First published in The Pioneer, March 1843.

About Nathaniel Hawthorne

Nathaniel Hawthorne was born on July 4, 1804, in Salem, Massachusetts. His fiction — haunted by the Puritan legacy and his family's role in the Salem witch trials — includes The Scarlet Letter (1850) and The House of the Seven Gables (1851), along with dozens of Gothic and allegorical short stories. He died in Plymouth, New Hampshire, in 1864.
