The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde

Robert Louis Stevenson · Written 1886, Published 1886 · ~25,000 words

Published in 1886, Robert Louis Stevenson's novella follows lawyer Mr. Utterson investigating the connection between the respectable Dr. Henry Jekyll and the monstrous Edward Hyde. The solution, revealed in Jekyll's final statement, is that they are the same man — Jekyll having discovered a drug that releases his darker self.

Quick Summary

'Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde' is a novella by Robert Louis Stevenson, published in 1886. Lawyer Utterson investigates the mysterious Mr. Hyde — a small, repellent man who tramples a child, beats a man to death, and seems to have a hold over the respectable Dr. Jekyll. Jekyll's final letter reveals that he discovered a potion to separate his good and evil selves. Hyde grew stronger, until Jekyll could no longer control the transformations and killed himself.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A mystery structure that withholds its central revelation until the final two chapters — Jekyll's own account — making the reader reassemble everything they have read.

Themes

The duality of human nature and the Victorian repression of the body and desire — Hyde is what Jekyll has suppressed, and once released he is stronger than the repressor.

Historical Context

Published in 1886. Reportedly written after a dream. Sold 40,000 copies in six months.

Extract

Mr. Utterson the lawyer was a man of a rugged countenance that was never lighted by a smile; cold, scanty and embarrassed in discourse; backward in sentiment; lean, long, dusty, dreary and yet somehow lovable. At friendly meetings, and when the wine was to his taste, something eminently human beaconed from his eye; something indeed which never found its way into his talk, but which spoke not only in these silent symbols of the after-dinner face, but more often and loudly in the acts of his life.

Public domain. Published 1886.

About Robert Louis Stevenson

Robert Louis Stevenson was born on November 13, 1850, in Edinburgh, Scotland. His adventure novels Treasure Island (1883) and Kidnapped (1886) and his novella The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde (1886) made him one of the most widely read authors of his era. He died in Samoa in 1894.
