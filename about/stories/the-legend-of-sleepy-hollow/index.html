The Legend of Sleepy Hollow

Washington Irving · Written 1820, Published 1820 · ~10,500 words

Published in 1820 in The Sketch Book of Geoffrey Crayon, Gent., 'The Legend of Sleepy Hollow' follows Ichabod Crane, a superstitious schoolteacher in a Dutch Hudson Valley community, who is pursued one night by the terrifying Headless Horseman — or so he believes.

Quick Summary

'The Legend of Sleepy Hollow' is a story by Washington Irving, published in 1820. Ichabod Crane, a lanky, superstitious schoolteacher, courts the daughter of the wealthiest farmer in Sleepy Hollow. His rival Brom Bones frightens him on a dark road by hurling what appears to be a severed head. Ichabod vanishes. The town believes Brom Bones played a prank — the thrown object was a pumpkin.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Frame narrative in Irving's gentle, mock-scholarly style — the story is presented as a legend told at a party, giving it a folksy, oral quality.

Themes

The story plays with the supernatural versus the rational, and with American myth-making — Sleepy Hollow becomes a place where old Dutch legends persist against Yankee rationalism. Ichabod's fate is ambiguous: was he frightened away or actually taken by the Horseman?

Historical Context

Published in The Sketch Book of Geoffrey Crayon, Gent. (1820). One of the first widely celebrated American short stories and a foundational text in American Gothic literature.

Extract

In the bosom of one of those spacious coves which indent the eastern shore of the Hudson, at that broad expansion of the river denominated by the ancient Dutch navigators the Tappan Zee, and where they always prudently shortened sail, and implored the protection of St. Nicholas when they crossed, there lies a small market-town or rural port, which by some is called Greensburgh, but which is more generally and properly known by the name of Tarry Town.

Not far from this village, perhaps about two miles, there is a little valley, or rather lap of land, among high hills, which is one of the quietest places in the whole world. A small brook glides through it, with just murmur enough to lull one to repose; and the occasional whistle of a quail, or tapping of a woodpecker, is almost the only sound that ever breaks in upon the uniform tranquility.

Public domain. Published in The Sketch Book of Geoffrey Crayon, Gent., 1820.

About Washington Irving

Washington Irving was born on April 3, 1783, in New York City. He was the first American writer to achieve international fame, best known for 'Rip Van Winkle' and 'The Legend of Sleepy Hollow,' both published in The Sketch Book (1819-20). He died in Tarrytown, New York, in 1859.
