A Worn Path

Eudora Welty · Written 1941, Published 1941 · ~3,800 words

Published in 1941 in The Atlantic Monthly, 'A Worn Path' follows Phoenix Jackson, an elderly Black woman in Mississippi, on her long walk from the countryside into Natchez to get medicine for her grandson. The story is a portrait of endurance, love, and dignity in the face of age and racism.

Quick Summary

'A Worn Path' is a story by Eudora Welty, published in 1941. Phoenix Jackson, an ancient Black woman, walks the worn path from her rural home to Natchez to get medicine for her chronically ill grandson. The journey is arduous — she crosses a log over a creek, gets her dress caught on thorns, crosses a field. She arrives at the clinic, briefly forgets her mission, then remembers and gets the medicine.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration following Phoenix's journey — the story is essentially a character portrait in the form of a journey narrative.

Themes

Endurance, love, and the dignity of ordinary heroism — Phoenix's journey is an act of love that the story treats with complete seriousness, though others condescend to her.

Historical Context

Published in The Atlantic Monthly, February 1941. Collected in A Curtain of Green (1941).

Extract

It was December — a bright frozen day in the early morning. Far out in the country there was an old Negro woman with her head tied in a red rag, coming along a path through the pinewoods. Her name was Phoenix Jackson. She was very old and small and she walked slowly in the dark pine shadows, moving a little from side to side in her steps, with the balanced heaviness and lightness of a pendulum in a grandfather clock.

About Eudora Welty

Eudora Welty was born on April 13, 1909, in Jackson, Mississippi. One of the finest American short story writers, she spent her entire career in Mississippi, writing stories of extraordinary observation and tenderness. Her novel The Optimist's Daughter (1972) won the Pulitzer Prize. She died in Jackson in 2001.
