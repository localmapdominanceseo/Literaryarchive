Othello

William Shakespeare · Written 1603, Published 1622

Written around 1603 and published in 1622, Othello is Shakespeare's most intimate tragedy — a domestic drama of jealousy and manipulation. Othello, a Moorish general in the Venetian army, secretly marries Desdemona. His ensign Iago, passed over for promotion, manipulates Othello into believing Desdemona is unfaithful. Othello smothers her and then, learning the truth, kills himself.

Quick Summary

Othello is a tragedy by Shakespeare, written c.1603. Othello, a Moorish general, marries Desdemona against her father's wishes. His ensign Iago, motivated by resentment, poisons Othello's mind against Desdemona through planted evidence — a handkerchief. Othello smothers Desdemona in bed, convinced of her infidelity. Emilia reveals Iago's manipulation. Iago kills Emilia. Othello kills himself over Desdemona's body.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act blank verse tragedy with a tight, claustrophobic structure — most of the action is set in Cyprus rather than Venice.

Themes

Jealousy as a consuming passion; racism and the outsider in Venetian society; Iago as a study in motiveless malignancy; the destruction of trust; honor and reputation.

Historical Context

Written c.1603, based on a tale by Cinthio (1565). Iago is one of Shakespeare's greatest villains — his motivations are deliberately unclear, making him more terrifying.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

Othello has secretly married Desdemona, daughter of the Venetian senator Brabantio. Iago, passed over for promotion, begins his scheme. Othello is summoned to deal with the Turkish fleet threatening Cyprus. Desdemona accompanies him.

Act 2

The fleet arrives in Cyprus. The Turkish threat is destroyed by a storm. Iago begins manipulating Cassio, getting him drunk and disgraced. He plants the idea of Cassio's intimacy with Desdemona in Othello's mind.

Act 3

Iago systematically destroys Othello's trust in Desdemona through insinuation and the planted handkerchief. By the end of the act, Othello has vowed to kill Desdemona and promoted Iago to lieutenant.

Act 4

Othello is consumed by jealousy. He strikes Desdemona publicly. Iago arranges for Roderigo to kill Cassio; the attempt fails and Iago kills Roderigo. Othello resolves to kill Desdemona.

Act 5

Othello smothers Desdemona in her bed. Emilia reveals the truth about the handkerchief. Iago kills Emilia and is arrested. Othello kills himself beside Desdemona's body.

Key Quotes

O, beware, my lord, of jealousy! It is the green-eyed monster which doth mock the meat it feeds on.

Iago — Act 3, Scene 3

Iago warning Othello about jealousy — while simultaneously planting it in him.

She loved me for the dangers I had passed, and I loved her that she did pity them.

Othello — Act 1, Scene 3

Othello explaining to the Venetian senate how he and Desdemona fell in love.

I am not what I am.

Iago — Act 1, Scene 1

Iago's declaration of his fundamental duplicity — the defining line of his character.

Put out the light, and then put out the light.

Othello — Act 5, Scene 2

Othello about to kill Desdemona, comparing the lamp he extinguishes to her life.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
