Hamlet

William Shakespeare · Written 1600, Published 1603

Written around 1600 and first published in 1603, Hamlet is Shakespeare's longest and most psychologically complex play. Prince Hamlet of Denmark is visited by his father's ghost, who reveals he was murdered by Hamlet's uncle Claudius, who has married Hamlet's mother and taken the throne. The play follows Hamlet's paralysis, his feigned madness, and the cascade of deaths that follows.

Quick Summary

Hamlet is a tragedy by William Shakespeare, written around 1600. Prince Hamlet learns from his father's ghost that his uncle Claudius murdered the king, married his mother Gertrude, and took the throne of Denmark. Hamlet feigns madness while planning revenge. He accidentally kills Polonius, driving Ophelia to madness and death. In the final scene, Gertrude drinks poison meant for Hamlet, Laertes wounds Hamlet with a poisoned sword, Hamlet kills Claudius, and Hamlet dies. Horatio survives to tell the story.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act blank verse tragedy with prose passages, soliloquies, and a play-within-a-play. Shakespeare's longest play at approximately 4,000 lines.

Themes

Revenge and its moral cost; the corruption of the Danish court; the nature of action and inaction ('To be or not to be'); appearance versus reality; the relationship between thought and action; death and the afterlife.

Historical Context

Written c.1600, first performed probably 1600-01. Based on a Scandinavian legend recorded by Saxo Grammaticus. The play has generated more critical commentary than any other work in English literature.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

The ghost of King Hamlet appears to the guards and to Hamlet, revealing that he was murdered by Claudius who poured poison in his ear. Hamlet vows revenge but is troubled. Polonius warns his daughter Ophelia against Hamlet's courtship.

Act 2

Hamlet adopts an 'antic disposition' — feigned madness. Claudius and Polonius spy on him. Hamlet greets a troupe of traveling players and devises a plan: he will have them perform a play mirroring his father's murder to catch the king's conscience.

Act 3

Hamlet delivers the 'To be or not to be' soliloquy. The play-within-a-play, 'The Mousetrap,' makes Claudius flee in guilt. Hamlet goes to his mother's chamber, kills Polonius hiding behind a curtain, and speaks again with his father's ghost. Claudius plans to send Hamlet to England with orders for his execution.

Act 4

Ophelia goes mad after her father's death and drowns. Laertes returns from France and swears revenge on Hamlet. Claudius plots with Laertes to kill Hamlet in a rigged fencing match — a poisoned sword and a poisoned cup.

Act 5

Hamlet returns from England, having escaped the execution plot. Ophelia's burial. The fencing match: Gertrude drinks from the poisoned cup meant for Hamlet; Laertes wounds Hamlet with the poisoned sword; Hamlet kills Laertes and then Claudius; Hamlet dies. Horatio survives. Fortinbras arrives to take the throne.

Key Quotes

To be, or not to be, that is the question.

Hamlet — Act 3, Scene 1

Hamlet's meditation on whether it is nobler to endure suffering or to end it — and why fear of death keeps us from acting.

The lady doth protest too much, methinks.

Gertrude — Act 3, Scene 2

Gertrude watching the Player Queen in the play-within-a-play, commenting on her excessive vows of fidelity.

Something is rotten in the state of Denmark.

Marcellus — Act 1, Scene 4

Marcellus sensing the corruption at the Danish court after the ghost's appearance.

Neither a borrower nor a lender be.

Polonius — Act 1, Scene 3

Polonius's advice to Laertes before he leaves for France — a string of platitudes.

What a piece of work is a man!

Hamlet — Act 2, Scene 2

Hamlet describing the wonder of humanity to Rosencrantz and Guildenstern — before undercutting it: 'And yet, to me, what is this quintessence of dust?'

The rest is silence.

Hamlet — Act 5, Scene 2

Hamlet's last words as he dies.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
