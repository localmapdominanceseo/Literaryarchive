A Midsummer Night's Dream

William Shakespeare · Written 1595, Published 1600

Written around 1595-96 and published in 1600, A Midsummer Night's Dream is Shakespeare's most enchanted comedy. Three plots interweave in a magical Athenian forest: four young lovers lost and mismatched by fairy magic; the fairy king and queen at war over a changeling boy; and a group of amateur actors rehearsing a play for the Duke's wedding.

Quick Summary

A Midsummer Night's Dream is a comedy by Shakespeare, written c.1595-96. Four young Athenians flee into an enchanted forest. Oberon, the fairy king, orders Puck to use a love potion on his sleeping queen Titania and on the young lovers — but Puck causes comic chaos by misapplying the potion. Titania falls in love with Bottom the weaver, who has been given a donkey's head. All is eventually set right. The lovers marry. Bottom and the mechanicals perform their play.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act comedy with three interwoven plots, verse and prose, and a play-within-a-play in the final act.

Themes

The irrationality of love and desire; dreams and imagination; the relationship between art and reality; the power and humor of transformation.

Historical Context

Written c.1595-96, possibly for a noble wedding. One of Shakespeare's most popular comedies and a rich source of quotations about love and dreams.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

Hermia is ordered to marry Demetrius rather than Lysander. She and Lysander plan to flee into the forest. Helena, who loves Demetrius, tells him of the plan. The mechanicals plan their play.

Act 2

The fairy world is introduced: Oberon and Titania are at war. Oberon sends Puck for a love potion; he plans to use it on Titania and tells Puck to use it on the Athenian man (Demetrius) to make him love Helena. Puck doses Lysander by mistake.

Act 3

Bottom is given a donkey's head; Titania wakes and falls in love with him. Puck doses Demetrius too — now both men love Helena, who thinks she is being mocked. Complete comic chaos ensues.

Act 4

Oberon releases Titania from the spell. Bottom's head is restored. The lovers are discovered asleep and taken back to Athens by Theseus. Bottom wakes and tries to describe his dream.

Act 5

The three couples celebrate. The mechanicals perform 'Pyramus and Thisbe' — a hilariously bad version of a tragic love story. The fairies bless the house. Puck delivers the epilogue.

Characters

Puck (Robin Goodfellow)

Oberon's mischievous sprite, who causes most of the play's comic chaos by misapplying the love potion. He is the play's most energetic presence — amoral, quicksilver, delighted by the confusion of mortals. His closing speech — 'If we shadows have offended' — frames the entire play as a dream.

Oberon

King of the fairies, who uses Titania's enchantment as leverage in their quarrel over a changeling boy. He is not cruel — he restores the lovers and eventually releases Titania — but he is comfortable wielding power over others without their knowledge.

Titania

Queen of the fairies, briefly enchanted into loving Bottom. Her speech about the natural disorder caused by her quarrel with Oberon — floods, seasons confused, diseases spreading — gives the play its most serious moment: fairy quarrels have human consequences.

Bottom

The weaver, the play's greatest comic creation. He is completely unaware of his own ridiculousness — self-confident, enthusiastic, generous. His equanimity when given a donkey's head and adored by the fairy queen is both funny and strangely touching. His attempt to explain his 'dream' afterward is one of the play's finest moments.

Helena and Hermia

The two Athenian women who represent the play's central comic confusion. Helena's misery at being unloved and her disbelief when suddenly both men pursue her — she thinks it's a joke — is the play's emotional center.

Key Quotes

The course of true love never did run smooth.

Lysander — Act 1, Scene 1

Lysander comforting Hermia about the obstacles facing their love.

Lord, what fools these mortals be!

Puck — Act 3, Scene 2

Puck observing the confusion among the four lovers caused by his misapplied potion.

I know a bank where the wild thyme blows, where oxlips and the nodding violet grows.

Oberon — Act 2, Scene 1

Oberon describing the enchanted spot where Titania sleeps.

Though she be but little, she is fierce.

Helena — Act 3, Scene 2

Helena defending Hermia's spirit despite her small stature.

Legacy & Critical History

A Midsummer Night's Dream is one of Shakespeare's most performed and most adapted plays. Mendelssohn wrote his famous incidental music for it in 1826. It has been filmed multiple times, most famously by Max Reinhardt (1935) and Peter Hall (1968). The play's treatment of love as irrational, magical, and temporary influenced the Romantic movement's understanding of imagination and desire.

Study Questions

  1. How does the play use the relationship between the fairy world and the mortal world?
  2. What does the play suggest about the nature of love — is it genuine when it is magically induced?
  3. How does Bottom function as the play's most fully human character despite being the most ridiculous?
  4. What is the significance of the 'play-within-a-play' that closes the comedy?
  5. How does Puck's epilogue frame the entire play as a dream? What effect does this have?

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
