The Merchant of Venice

William Shakespeare · Written 1596, Published 1600

Written around 1596-97 and published in 1600, The Merchant of Venice is Shakespeare's most morally complex comedy. Antonio borrows money from the Jewish moneylender Shylock to help his friend Bassanio woo the heiress Portia. The bond: if Antonio cannot repay, Shylock takes a pound of his flesh. When Antonio's ships apparently sink, Portia saves him in court by arguing Shylock may take the flesh but not one drop of blood.

Quick Summary

The Merchant of Venice is a play by Shakespeare, written c.1596-97. Antonio borrows from Shylock with a bond that forfeits a pound of his flesh. Bassanio wins Portia in the casket test. Shylock's daughter Jessica elopes with Lorenzo, taking Shylock's money. Antonio's ships are lost; Shylock demands his bond. Portia disguises as a lawyer and defeats Shylock — he may take the flesh but not blood. Shylock is forced to convert to Christianity.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act comedy with two interwoven plots: the bond plot and the casket/courtship plot, converging in the trial scene.

Themes

Mercy versus justice; the treatment of Jews in Christian Venice; friendship and love; the relationship between law and equity; appearance versus reality (the caskets).

Historical Context

Written c.1596-97. The play's attitude to Shylock is deeply contested — is he a villain or a victim? His 'Hath not a Jew eyes?' speech makes him one of Shakespeare's most humanized antagonists.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

Antonio borrows 3,000 ducats from Shylock for Bassanio. The bond is a pound of flesh. Portia's suitors must choose between gold, silver, and lead caskets.

Act 2

Bassanio travels to Belmont. Shylock's daughter Jessica elopes with the Christian Lorenzo, taking a large sum of money. Two wrong suitors choose gold and silver caskets.

Act 3

Bassanio chooses the lead casket correctly and wins Portia. News arrives that Antonio's ships have sunk. Shylock, already furious about Jessica, demands his bond.

Act 4

The trial scene. Shylock insists on his bond. Portia, disguised as the lawyer Balthazar, finds the loophole: flesh but no blood. Shylock loses his case, half his wealth, and is forced to convert.

Act 5

The couples are united at Belmont. News arrives that Antonio's ships have come in safely after all. The ring trick is resolved.

Key Quotes

Hath not a Jew eyes? Hath not a Jew hands, organs, dimensions, senses, affections, passions?

Shylock — Act 3, Scene 1

Shylock's passionate defense of his humanity to Salerio and Solanio after Jessica's elopement.

The quality of mercy is not strained. It droppeth as the gentle rain from heaven.

Portia — Act 4, Scene 1

Portia's famous speech urging Shylock to show mercy in the trial scene.

All that glitters is not gold.

Prince of Morocco — Act 2, Scene 7

The inscription inside the golden casket warning against judging by appearances.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
