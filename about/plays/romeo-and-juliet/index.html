Romeo and Juliet

William Shakespeare · Written 1594, Published 1597

Written around 1594-96 and published in 1597, Romeo and Juliet is Shakespeare's most famous love story. Romeo Montague and Juliet Capulet fall in love at first sight, not knowing they belong to feuding families. They secretly marry. A series of fatal misunderstandings leads to both their deaths in the Capulet family tomb.

Quick Summary

Romeo and Juliet is a tragedy by Shakespeare, written c.1594-96. Romeo Montague and Juliet Capulet fall in love despite their families' feud. They secretly marry. Romeo kills Tybalt in a street fight and is banished. Juliet's parents arrange her marriage to Paris; Friar Laurence gives her a sleeping potion. Romeo, thinking her dead, buys poison and kills himself beside her. Juliet wakes, finds Romeo dead, and kills herself with his dagger.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act tragedy with an unusual prologue in sonnet form that tells the audience the ending before the play begins.

Themes

The intensity and destructiveness of passionate love; the feud as a social structure that kills; fate and the star-crossed lovers; youth versus age.

Historical Context

Written c.1594-96, based on Arthur Brooke's poem The Tragicall Historye of Romeus and Juliet (1562). One of the most performed plays in the world.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

A street brawl between Montague and Capulet servants. Romeo, lovesick over Rosaline, is persuaded to attend the Capulet feast. He and Juliet meet and fall in love, then discover each other's identity.

Act 2

The balcony scene — Romeo and Juliet declare their love and plan to marry in secret. Friar Laurence agrees to marry them, hoping to end the feud.

Act 3

Tybalt challenges Romeo; Mercutio fights and is killed; Romeo kills Tybalt. Romeo is banished. Juliet's parents arrange her marriage to Paris for Thursday. Romeo and Juliet spend their wedding night together.

Act 4

Friar Laurence gives Juliet a sleeping potion that will make her appear dead. She takes it the night before the forced marriage. She is found apparently dead and placed in the Capulet tomb.

Act 5

The message about the potion fails to reach Romeo. He buys poison and goes to the tomb. He kills Paris, takes the poison, and dies beside Juliet. She wakes, finds him dead, and kills herself. The families and the Prince discover what has happened.

Characters

Romeo

A young Montague who is characterized by excessive emotion — he is lovesick over Rosaline before the play begins and transfers his passion immediately to Juliet. His impulsiveness drives the plot: he kills Tybalt in a moment of rage, he buys poison without waiting for confirmation, he acts always before thinking. His passion is genuine and total — which is both his greatness and his destruction.

Juliet

Thirteen years old, the more practically intelligent of the two lovers. She is less impulsive than Romeo — she raises the practical questions he forgets — but equally passionate. Her development over the course of the play is remarkable: she goes from an obedient daughter to a woman willing to defy her family, her nurse, and ultimately life itself.

Mercutio

Romeo's witty, irreverent friend, the play's finest talker. He belongs to neither the Montague nor the Capulet feud — he is the Prince's kinsman — and his death is the hinge of the play. When Mercutio dies, the comedy ends and the tragedy begins.

Friar Laurence

A Franciscan friar who marries the couple and devises the sleeping potion plan. He is well-intentioned but over-confident — his plan requires too many things to go right. He represents the failure of institutional religion to solve the problems created by institutional conflict.

The Nurse

Juliet's nurse, who has been closer to Juliet than her own mother. She is the play's comic character and Juliet's confidante — until she advises Juliet to marry Paris, at which point Juliet completely withdraws her trust.

Key Quotes

What's in a name? That which we call a rose by any other name would smell as sweet.

Juliet — Act 2, Scene 2

Juliet arguing that Romeo's name — Montague — is irrelevant to what he is.

Romeo, Romeo, wherefore art thou Romeo?

Juliet — Act 2, Scene 2

Juliet asking why Romeo must be Romeo — a Montague, her family's enemy. 'Wherefore' means 'why,' not 'where.'

A plague on both your houses!

Mercutio — Act 3, Scene 1

Mercutio's dying curse on both the Montague and Capulet families.

Good night, good night! Parting is such sweet sorrow that I shall say good night till it be morrow.

Juliet — Act 2, Scene 2

Juliet reluctant to end the balcony scene with Romeo.

For never was a story of more woe than this of Juliet and her Romeo.

Prince Escalus — Act 5, Scene 3

The Prince's final couplet closing the play.

Legacy & Critical History

Romeo and Juliet is the most performed play in the world and has been adapted into ballet (Prokofiev), opera, musicals (West Side Story), and dozens of films. Shakespeare's version was based on Arthur Brooke's poem, which he transformed by making the lovers more sympathetic and the timing more compressed. The play gave the language 'star-crossed lovers' and established the template for the romantic tragedy that appears whenever love is destroyed by social conflict.

Study Questions

  1. How does Shakespeare use the prologue — telling the audience the ending before the play begins — to shape how we watch the story?
  2. What role does fate play in the tragedy? How much of what happens is bad luck versus bad choices?
  3. How does Mercutio's death function as the pivot between comedy and tragedy?
  4. What does the play suggest about the relationship between love and violence?
  5. How does Shakespeare use the contrast between day and night, light and dark, throughout the play?

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
