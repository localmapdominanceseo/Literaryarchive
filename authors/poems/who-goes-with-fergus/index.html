Who Goes with Fergus?

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1893 Published 1893 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1893 in The Rose, this poem is spoken by someone inviting young men and maids to go with Fergus — who gave up his kingship to follow the druid into the woods — and brood no more on love's bitter mystery.

Quick Answer

"Who Goes with Fergus?" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1893. The speaker invites young men and maids to go with Fergus into the woods, to pierce the deep wood's shade and dance on the level shore — and brood no more on love's bitter mystery, since Fergus rules the brazen cars, the shadows of the wood, the white breast of the dim sea, and all wandering stars.

Who will go drive with Fergus now,
And pierce the deep wood's woven shade,
And dance upon the level shore?
Young man, lift up your russet brow,
And lift your tender eyelids, maid,
And brood on hopes and fear no more.

And no more turn aside and brood
Upon love's bitter mystery;
For Fergus rules the brazen cars,
And rules the shadows of the wood,
And the white breast of the dim sea
And all dishevelled wandering stars.

Public domain (published 1893). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two six-line stanzas with ABABCC rhyme in Yeats's early Celtic Twilight style, musical and dreamlike.

Themes

The invitation to escape love's painful mystery by following Fergus into a wilder, freer world ruled by ancient Irish myth. The poem is an escape from personal emotional suffering into impersonal mythic grandeur.

Historical Context

Published in The Rose (1893). James Joyce quoted the poem in both A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man and Ulysses, where Stephen Dedalus sings it — it was one of Joyce's favorite Yeats poems.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
