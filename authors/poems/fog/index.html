Fog

by Carl Sandburg

Written 1916 Published 1916 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1916 in Chicago Poems, this short imagist poem describes the fog coming in on little cat feet, sitting silently over the harbor and city, and then moving on — capturing a moment of urban nature in just six lines.

Quick Answer

"Fog" is a poem by Carl Sandburg, published in 1916. In six lines, it compares the fog to a cat: it comes on little cat feet, sits silently looking over a harbor and city on haunches, and then moves on. It is one of the most frequently anthologized short poems in American literature.

The fog comes
on little cat feet.

It sits looking
over harbor and city
on silent haunches
and then moves on.

Public domain. Published 1916 in Chicago Poems.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six lines of free verse with no rhyme, stripped to the minimum needed to convey the single image. The compression is the point — the poem works like a haiku, presenting an observation without commentary.

Themes

The quiet, temporary nature of fog as an analogy for impermanence. The cat metaphor captures the way fog arrives silently, occupies a space without disturbance, and then simply leaves — like a cat that has decided to move on. The poem asks nothing further of the comparison.

Historical Context

Sandburg published 'Fog' in Chicago Poems (1916), his first major collection, which established him as a leading voice of American modernism alongside contemporaries like Edgar Lee Masters. He was known for writing about urban industrial America and ordinary working people.

About Carl Sandburg

Carl Sandburg was born on January 6, 1878, in Galesburg, Illinois, to Swedish immigrant parents, and became one of the most popular American poets of the twentieth century. He worked many jobs — farm worker, soldier, journalist — before establishing himself as a poet with Chicago Poems (1916). He won three Pulitzer Prizes, two for poetry and one for his biography of Abraham Lincoln. He died in Flat Rock, North Carolina, in 1967.
