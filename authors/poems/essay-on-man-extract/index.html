An Essay on Man (Extract)

by Alexander Pope

Written 1733 Published 1733 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1733-1734, An Essay on Man is Pope's philosophical poem arguing that the universe is a well-ordered whole, that whatever is is right, and that man's proper study is himself. The most famous passage argues that hope springs eternal in the human breast.

Quick Answer

An Essay on Man by Alexander Pope (1733-1734) is a philosophical poem arguing for optimism about the divine order of the universe. The most famous passage describes the human condition as caught between the angel and the beast, knowing too much for a skeptic and too little for a stoic — and finding the one comfort of hope springing eternal in the human breast.

Know then thyself, presume not God to scan;
The proper study of Mankind is Man.
Placed on this isthmus of a middle state,
A being darkly wise, and rudely great:
With too much knowledge for the Sceptic side,
With too much weakness for the Stoic's pride,
He hangs between; in doubt to act, or rest,
In doubt to deem himself a God, or Beast;
In doubt his Mind or Body to prefer,
Born but to die, and reas'ning but to err;
Alike in ignorance, his reason such,
Whether he thinks too little, or too much:
Chaos of Thought and Passion, all confused;
Still by himself abused, or disabused;
Created half to rise, and half to fall;
Great lord of all things, yet a prey to all;
Sole judge of Truth, in endless Error hurled:
The glory, jest, and riddle of the world!

Hope springs eternal in the human breast:
Man never Is, but always To be blest:
The soul, uneasy and confined from home,
Rests and expatiates in a life to come.

Public domain. Published 1733-1734.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Heroic couplets, Pope's characteristic form, used here for philosophical argument with epigrammatic compression.

Themes

Theodicy — the defense of God's goodness against the apparent evidence of evil and suffering — through the argument that all partial evil is universal good, that the chain of being is perfect even if we cannot see it.

Historical Context

Published in four epistles, 1733-1734. The poem was enormously influential and was translated across Europe. Voltaire initially praised it before later attacking its optimism.

About Alexander Pope

Alexander Pope was born on May 21, 1688, in London, and became the dominant English poet of the early eighteenth century, celebrated for his mastery of the heroic couplet, his satirical wit, and the technical perfection of his verse. His most celebrated works include The Rape of the Lock (1714), An Essay on Man (1733-1734), and The Dunciad (1728). He died at Twickenham in 1744.
