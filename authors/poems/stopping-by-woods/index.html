Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening

by Robert Frost

Written 1922 Published 1923 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1923 in New Hampshire, this poem describes a traveler who stops his horse by a snowy wood on the darkest evening of the year, drawn by the beauty of the falling snow, before the horse reminds him he has promises to keep and miles to go before he sleeps.

Quick Answer

"Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening" is a poem by Robert Frost, published in 1923. A traveler stops his horse by a dark snowy wood and watches the snow fall. The horse shakes its bells as if asking why they've stopped in this lonely place. The speaker thinks of the lovely dark and deep woods but acknowledges he has promises to keep and miles to go before he sleeps.

Whose woods these are I think I know.
His house is in the village though;
He will not see me stopping here
To watch his woods fill up with snow.

My little horse must think it queer
To stop without a farmhouse near
Between the woods and frozen lake
The darkest evening of the year.

He gives his harness bells a shake
To ask if there is some mistake.
The only other sound's the sweep
Of easy wind and downy flake.

The woods are lovely, dark and deep,
But I have promises to keep,
And miles to go before I sleep,
And miles to go before I sleep.

Public domain (published 1923). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of iambic tetrameter with an interlocking rhyme scheme (AABA BBCB CCDC DDDD), the final stanza locking into four identical rhymes — the repetition of 'sleep' twice in the final couplet giving the poem its famous hypnotic quality.

Themes

The temptation of withdrawal from life versus the obligation to continue. The woods are 'lovely, dark and deep' — beautiful in a way that suggests rest, even death — but the speaker has promises (obligations to others) that require him to keep moving. The repeated final line is deliberately ambiguous: sleep as ordinary rest, or something more permanent.

Historical Context

Frost said he wrote the poem in a single sitting on a June morning in 1922 after working through the night on a longer poem. Published in New Hampshire (1923). The Frost estate is known for aggressive copyright enforcement, but the poem was published in 1923, making it public domain under US copyright law.

About Robert Frost

Robert Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California, though he is most closely associated with rural New England, where he lived and farmed for much of his life. His poetry uses plain, conversational language and traditional verse forms to explore deceptively simple rural scenes that often open onto deeper questions about choice, isolation, and mortality. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry and remains one of the most widely read American poets of the twentieth century. He died in Boston in 1963.
