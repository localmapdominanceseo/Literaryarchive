If—

by Rudyard Kipling

Written 1895 Published 1910 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1895 and published in 1910 in Rewards and Fairies, this poem addresses the speaker's son, setting out the qualities of character — patience, courage, self-reliance, humility, and perseverance — that will, if he can hold to them all, make him a man.

Quick Answer

"If—" is a poem by Rudyard Kipling, published in 1910. In four stanzas addressed to his son, the speaker lists the qualities of a truly admirable person: keeping one's head when others lose theirs, trusting oneself but allowing for doubt, meeting triumph and disaster as imposters, never giving in, using every second, and never boasting. If the son can do all these things, the world and everything in it will be his.

If you can keep your head when all about you
Are losing theirs and blaming it on you,
If you can trust yourself when all men doubt you,
But make allowance for their doubting too;
If you can wait and not be tired by waiting,
Or being lied about, don't deal in lies,
Or being hated, don't give way to hating,
And yet don't look too good, nor talk too wise:

If you can dream—and not make dreams your master;
If you can think—and not make thoughts your aim;
If you can meet with Triumph and Disaster
And treat those two impostors just the same;
If you can bear to hear the truth you've spoken
Twisted by knaves to make a trap for fools,
Or watch the things you gave your life to, broken,
And stoop and build 'em up with worn-out tools:

If you can make one heap of all your winnings
And risk it on one turn of pitch-and-toss,
And lose, and start again at your beginnings
And never breathe a word about your loss;
If you can force your heart and nerve and sinew
To serve your turn long after they are gone,
And so hold on when there is nothing in you
Except the Will which says to them: 'Hold on!'

If you can talk with crowds and keep your virtue,
Or walk with Kings—nor lose the common touch,
If neither foes nor loving friends can hurt you,
If all men count with you, but none too much;
If you can fill the unforgiving minute
With sixty seconds' worth of distance run,
Yours is the Earth and everything that's in it,
And—which is more—you'll be a Man, my son!

Public domain. Published 1910 in Rewards and Fairies.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four stanzas of eight lines each in loose iambic pentameter, structured as a single long conditional sentence — 'If you can... If you can... then yours is the Earth' — with each stanza adding more conditions before the cumulative payoff of the couplet.

Themes

The Stoic virtues of self-mastery, equanimity, and quiet endurance. The poem's most famous lines, 'If you can meet with Triumph and Disaster / And treat those two impostors just the same,' argue for emotional balance and not being deluded by either success or failure.

Historical Context

Kipling wrote the poem around 1895, reportedly inspired partly by Leander Starr Jameson, who maintained his composure and dignity after the failure of the Jameson Raid in South Africa. It was published in 1910 in Rewards and Fairies and has been voted Britain's favorite poem in multiple polls.

About Rudyard Kipling

Rudyard Kipling was born on December 30, 1865, in Bombay (now Mumbai), British India, and became one of the most popular British writers of the late Victorian and Edwardian periods. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1907. His work, including The Jungle Book, Kim, and the Barrack-Room Ballads, drew heavily on his experiences of British India and of ordinary soldiers and working-class voices. He died in London in 1936.
