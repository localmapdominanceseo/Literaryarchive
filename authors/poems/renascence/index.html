Renascence

by Edna St. Vincent Millay

Written 1912 Published 1912 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1912 when Millay was twenty years old, this long poem begins with the speaker feeling the sky pressing down with the weight of infinity, dying under it, and then being reborn through the sensation of rain on her face — discovering that the world is endless and that only an infinite heart can hold it.

Quick Answer

"Renascence" is a poem by Edna St. Vincent Millay, published in 1912 when she was twenty. The speaker feels the sky pressing down on her, experiences an overwhelming sense of all the world's suffering, dies under it, and is buried — then rain falls on her face and she is reborn, understanding that infinity is not in sky or sea but in the soul that can contain them.

All I could see from where I stood
Was three long mountains and a wood;
I turned and looked another way,
And saw three islands in a bay.
So with my eyes I traced the line
Of the horizon, thin and fine,
Straight around till I was come
Back to where I'd started from;
And all I saw from where I stood
Was three long mountains and a wood.

Over these things I could not see;
These were the things that bounded me;
And I could touch them with my hand,
Almost, I thought, from where I stand.
And all at once things seemed so small
My breath came short, and scarce at all.

But, sure, the sky is big, I said;
Miles and miles above my head;
So here upon my back I'll lie
And look my fill into the sky.
And so I looked, and, after all,
The sky was not so very tall.
The sky, I said, must somewhere stop,
And—sure enough!—I see the top!
The sky, I thought, is not so grand;
I 'most could touch it with my hand!
And reaching up my hand to try,
I screamed to feel it touch the sky.

I screamed, and—lo!—Infinity
Came down and settled over me;
And, pressing of the Undefined
The definition on my mind,
Doubt no longer could contain
Infinity, but hurried plain
With its whole weight upon me fell,
And I was blotted out as well.

Deep in the earth I rested now;
Cool is its hand upon the brow
And soft its breast beneath the head
Of one who is so gladly dead.
And all at once, and over all
The pitying rain began to fall;
I lay and heard each pattering hoof
Upon my lowly, thatched roof,
And seemed to love the sound far more
Than ever I had done before.
For rain it hath a friendly sound
To one who's six feet underground;
And scarce the friendly voice or face:
A grave is such a quiet place.

The rain, I said, is kind to come
And speak to me in my new home.
I would I were alive again
To kiss the fingers of the rain,
To drink into my eyes the shine
Of every slanting silver line,
To catch the freshened, fragrant breeze
From drenched and dripping apple-trees;
For soon the shower will be done,
And then the broad face of the sun
Will laugh above the rain-soaked earth
Until the world with answering mirth
Shakes joyously, and each round drop
Rolls, twinkling, from its grass-blade top.

How can I bear it; buried here,
While overhead the sky grows clear
And blue again after the storm?
O, multi-colored, multiform,
Beloved beauty over me,
That I shall never, never see
Again! Spring-silver, autumn-gold,
That I shall never more behold!
Leaves of the summer, lovely dark,
Where the sun strikes them in the park,
And through the bark of the old oak
The steady sap forever broke
Along its slow ascending vein
Inward to the tree's heart,—again
I never more shall see the sun.

O God, I cried, give me new birth,
And put me back upon the earth!
Upward I sprang,
And reaching up my hand to try,
Ray after ray I filled the sky
With my new light.

So through my breast she breathed the air,
Spring, summer, autumn, winter there;
And all these things were in my breast.
And lo! I had the whole earth blest,
And I was filled with beauty's best.
And I shall never more behold
The world as 'twas before; and told
That there's no God, and there's no soul,
I should not doubt it half a whit
But that the light is more than it.

The world stands out on either side
No wider than the heart is wide;
Above the world is stretched the sky,—
No higher than the soul is high.
The heart can push the sea and land
Farther away on either hand;
The soul can split the sky in two,
And let the face of God shine through.
But East and West will pinch the heart
That can not keep them pushed apart;
And he whose soul is flat—the sky
Will cave in on him by and by.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two hundred and fourteen lines of rhyming couplets in iambic tetrameter, divided into clear narrative movements — the oppression, the dying, the burial, the rain, and the rebirth — giving the long poem the structure of a spiritual journey.

Themes

The relationship between consciousness, infinity, and renewal. The poem argues that the world's infinity is not external but internal — housed in the human soul — and that renewal comes through opening oneself completely to sensation and suffering rather than shrinking from it.

Historical Context

Millay submitted the poem to The Lyric Year anthology in 1912, where it was published and created a sensation. She was twenty years old. Its publication led to her receiving a scholarship to Vassar College and launched her literary career.

About Edna St. Vincent Millay

Edna St. Vincent Millay was born on February 22, 1892, in Rockland, Maine, and became one of the most celebrated American poets of the 1920s, known for her sonnets and her embodiment of the free-spirited 'New Woman' of the Jazz Age. She was the first woman to win the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry, receiving it in 1923 for The Harp-Weaver and Other Poems. She died at Steepletop, her farm in New York, in 1950.
