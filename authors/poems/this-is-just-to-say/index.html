This Is Just to Say

by William Carlos Williams

Written 1934 Published 1934 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1934, this twelve-word poem is written as a note left by someone who has eaten the plums in the icebox that were probably being saved for breakfast — and asks forgiveness while admitting they were delicious, so sweet, and so cold.

Quick Answer

"This Is Just to Say" is a poem by William Carlos Williams, published in 1934. It reads as a note: the speaker has eaten the plums in the icebox that the other person was probably saving for breakfast. He asks forgiveness. They were delicious — so sweet and so cold.

I have eaten
the plums
that were in
the icebox

and which
you were probably
saving
for breakfast

Forgive me
they were delicious
so sweet
and so cold

Public domain (published 1934). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three four-line stanzas of very short lines, the poem formatted exactly like a domestic note left on a kitchen table. The brevity and simplicity are the point: the poem asks whether a note like this is a poem, and if so, why.

Themes

The beauty of the ordinary moment — eating plums from the icebox, the cold sweetness of them. Williams's Imagist and Objectivist poetics insisted that such moments, precisely observed, are sufficient subject matter for poetry without requiring larger significance.

Historical Context

Published in 1934 in Collected Poems. The poem has become one of the most parodied and imitated in American literature, spawning countless 'I have eaten the...' poems, which is itself evidence of how precisely Williams captured something people recognize.

About William Carlos Williams

William Carlos Williams was born on September 17, 1883, in Rutherford, New Jersey, and practiced medicine as a pediatrician for most of his adult life while also becoming one of the most influential American Modernist poets. He rejected the allusive, European-influenced style of T.S. Eliot in favor of the American idiom, ordinary speech, and concrete sensory images drawn from everyday life. He died in Rutherford in 1963.
