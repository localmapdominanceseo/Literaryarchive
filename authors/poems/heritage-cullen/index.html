Heritage

by Countee Cullen

Written 1925 Published 1925 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1925 in Color, this long poem asks what Africa means to the speaker — a Black American born in New York who has never been there — and struggles with the tension between Christian civilization and an imagined African heritage pulling at his blood.

Quick Answer

"Heritage" is a poem by Countee Cullen, published in 1925. The speaker asks 'What is Africa to me?' and answers through images of jungle, rain, wild beasts, and drums — all things he has never seen yet that seem to pulse in his blood. He struggles to reconcile his Christian faith with this ancestral pull, ultimately unable to suppress either the heritage or the doubt.

What is Africa to me:
Copper sun or scarlet sea,
Jungle star or jungle track,
Strong bronzed men, or regal black
Women from whose loins I sprang
When the birds of Eden sang?
One three centuries removed
From the scenes his fathers loved,
Spicy grove, cinnamon tree,
What is Africa to me?

So I lie, who all day long
Want no sound except the song
Sung by wild barbaric birds
Goading massive jungle herds,
Juggernauts of flesh that pass
Trembling through the jungle grass;
Pictures of a long dead dream,
Through a mist the eyes may seem
To believe,—yet seeing not
And believing, knowing what.

So I lie, who always hear,
Though I cram against my ear
Both my thumbs, and keep them there,
Great drums throbbing through the air.
So I lie, whose fount of pride,
Dear distress, and joy allied,
Is my somber flesh and skin,
With the dark blood dammed within
Like great pulsing tides of wine
That, I fear, must burst the fine
Sluices of established doom,
To be drunk in time and room.

Analysis

Form and Structure

One hundred and twenty-eight lines of octosyllabic couplets (eight-syllable rhyming lines), the regular beat of the meter giving the poem its insistent, drumlike quality.

Themes

The double consciousness of being Black and American — caught between an idealized African heritage and a Christianity that was the religion of slaveholders, between the civilization that formed him and something older he can only imagine. The poem does not resolve the tension.

Historical Context

Published in Color (1925), Cullen's first collection, published when he was twenty-two. It is one of the central texts of the Harlem Renaissance and one of the most direct engagements with W.E.B. Du Bois's concept of double consciousness.

About Countee Cullen

Countee Cullen was born on May 30, 1903, in New York City, and became one of the leading poets of the Harlem Renaissance, known for fusing traditional European forms — especially the sonnet — with themes of Black identity, heritage, and the double consciousness of being Black in America. He published his first collection, Color, in 1925 when he was twenty-two, and it immediately established him as a major voice. He died in New York in 1946.
