I, Too

by Langston Hughes

Written 1924 Published 1926 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Written in 1924 and published in 1926 in The Weary Blues, this short poem answers Walt Whitman's 'I Hear America Singing' by asserting that the Black American, sent to eat in the kitchen when company comes, also sings America — and will one day sit at the table.

Quick Answer

"I, Too" is a poem by Langston Hughes, written in 1924 and published in 1926. The speaker — the darker brother sent to eat in the kitchen when company comes — declares that he too sings America. He predicts that one day he will sit at the table, his beauty recognized, and those who sent him away will be ashamed.

I, too, sing America.

I am the darker brother.
They send me to eat in the kitchen
When company comes,
But I laugh,
And eat well,
And grow strong.

Tomorrow,
I'll be at the table
When company comes.
Nobody'll dare
Say to me,
"Eat in the kitchen,"
Then.

Besides,
They'll see how beautiful I am
And be ashamed—

I, too, am America.

Public domain (first published 1926 in The Weary Blues, pre-1927). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Free verse, eighteen lines, structurally simple but rhetorically powerful. The poem moves through three time zones: the present indignity, the future reversal, and the final declarative statement.

Themes

Racial exclusion and the assertion of full American belonging. The poem directly responds to Whitman's expansive democratic vision of 'I Hear America Singing,' claiming that vision for Black Americans who have been excluded from it. The kitchen represents segregation; eating well and growing strong there represents resilience; the predicted seat at the table represents the coming of civil rights.

Historical Context

Hughes published the poem in 1926 in his debut collection The Weary Blues, his first major book. It was written in 1924, during the height of the Harlem Renaissance, and is considered a foundational statement of Black American identity and the claim to full participation in American democratic life.

About Langston Hughes

Langston Hughes was born on February 1, 1901, in Joplin, Missouri, and became one of the leading voices of the Harlem Renaissance, the flowering of African American literature, art, and music centered in New York City during the 1920s. Hughes drew on the rhythms of jazz and blues music to develop a distinctive poetic voice that celebrated Black culture and identity while confronting American racism directly. He died in New York City in 1967.
