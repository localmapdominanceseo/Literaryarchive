If We Must Die

by Claude McKay

Written 1919 Published 1919 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Written in 1919 during the Red Summer of race riots across the United States and published that same year, this sonnet is a defiant call to resist rather than submit to mob violence, insisting that if death comes it should come while fighting back.

Quick Answer

"If We Must Die" is a sonnet by Claude McKay, published in 1919. Written during the Red Summer of race riots, it calls for dignified, fighting resistance: if we must die, let it not be like hogs hunted and penned, but like men facing their enemies, dealing them one deathblow, even if a thousand attack one. Die but fighting back.

If we must die, let it not be like hogs
Hunted and penned in an inglorious spot,
While round us bark the mad and hungry dogs,
Making their mock at our accursèd lot.
If we must die, O let us nobly die,
So that our precious blood may not be shed
In vain; then even the monsters we defy
Shall be constrained to honor us though dead!
O kinsmen! we must meet the common foe!
Though far outnumbered let us show us brave,
And for their thousand blows deal one death-blow!
What though before us lies the open grave?
Like men we'll face the murderous, cowardly pack,
Pressed to the wall, dying, but fighting back!

Public domain (published 1919). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter — a formal, traditional structure chosen deliberately for a protest poem, giving the defiance the weight and dignity of the tradition it invokes.

Themes

Resistance, dignity, and the refusal to die passively. The poem does not celebrate violence but insists that dying while fighting back is more honorable than being slaughtered without resistance. Winston Churchill quoted it in the House of Commons during World War II.

Historical Context

Written during the Red Summer of 1919, when anti-Black riots erupted in cities across the United States. McKay's sonnet was addressed to Black Americans facing mob violence, though its universality of theme led Churchill to cite it during the Blitz.

About Claude McKay

Claude McKay was born on September 15, 1889, in Sunny Ville, Jamaica, and emigrated to the United States in 1912, becoming one of the earliest and most important voices of the Harlem Renaissance. His sonnet 'If We Must Die,' written during the Red Summer of 1919, became an anthem of Black resistance and was later cited by Winston Churchill during World War II. He died in Chicago in 1948.
