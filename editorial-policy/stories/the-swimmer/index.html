The Swimmer

John Cheever · Written 1964, Published 1964 · ~5,600 words

Published in 1964 in The New Yorker, 'The Swimmer' is John Cheever's masterpiece. Neddy Merrill, at a suburban cocktail party, decides to swim home through the chain of pools in his neighbors' backyards. As he swims, summer mysteriously turns to autumn, neighbors who should be friends treat him as a stranger, and he arrives home to find his house empty and abandoned.

Quick Summary

'The Swimmer' is a story by John Cheever, published in 1964. Neddy Merrill decides to swim home through the pools of Westchester County, naming the route after his wife Lucinda. As he swims, the world changes: summer becomes autumn, neighbors who know him speak of him with pity, others have no memory of inviting him to parties. He arrives home to find his house cold and abandoned. He has been swimming through years of his life's decline.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A realistic narrative that gradually becomes surreal — the temporal slippage is so subtly handled that many readers do not realize it is happening until the end.

Themes

The decline hidden beneath suburban success — Neddy's swim is a journey through the wreckage of his life, which he has been unable to face. The story is about the alcoholic's selective memory and self-deception.

Historical Context

Published in The New Yorker, July 18, 1964. Collected in The Brigadier and the Golf Widow (1964).

Extract

It was one of those midsummer Sundays when everyone sits around saying, 'I drank too much last night.' You might have heard it whispered by the parishioners leaving church, heard it from the lips of the priest himself, struggling with his cassock in the vestiarium, heard it from the golf links and the tennis courts, heard it from the wildlife preserve where the leader of the Audubon group was suffering from a terrible hangover.

About John Cheever

John Cheever was born on May 27, 1912, in Quincy, Massachusetts. Known as 'the Chekhov of the suburbs,' he wrote stories and novels about the quiet desperation of middle-class American life, publishing extensively in The New Yorker. 'The Swimmer' (1964) is his most celebrated story. He died in Ossining, New York, in 1982.
