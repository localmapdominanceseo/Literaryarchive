Boule de Suif

Guy de Maupassant · Written 1880, Published 1880 · ~12,000 words

Published in 1880 in the collection Les Soirées de Médan, 'Boule de Suif' (Ball of Fat) made Maupassant famous overnight. During the Franco-Prussian War, a stagecoach carries passengers fleeing Rouen — including respectable citizens and a prostitute named Boule de Suif. When a Prussian officer detains the coach until Boule de Suif sleeps with him, her fellow passengers — who had earlier despised her — pressure her to comply. Once she does and they are free, they snub her again.

Quick Summary

'Boule de Suif' is a story by Maupassant, published in 1880. A coach carrying refugees from Rouen during the Prussian occupation is detained by a Prussian officer who wants Boule de Suif, a prostitute, to sleep with him. The respectable passengers — who had initially scorned her — first benefit from her food, then pressure her to submit to the Prussian for the good of all. When she does and they escape, they ignore her. She weeps alone.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with Maupassant's characteristic clinical precision — the respectable characters are observed with cold irony.

Themes

The hypocrisy of bourgeois respectability — the 'respectable' passengers use and then discard Boule de Suif, exposing their patriotism and morality as purely selfish performance.

Historical Context

Published in Les Soirées de Médan (1880). Flaubert called it a masterpiece. It established Maupassant's reputation at age thirty.

Extract

For several days in succession a few broken remnants of a defeated army had been passing through the town. They were not troops, but a broken rabble. The men had unkempt, dirty beards and tattered uniforms; they moved without energy, without pride, without courage, conquered and worn out, incapable of thought or resolution, marching out of habit merely, and falling to the ground with fatigue the moment they halted.

Public domain. Published in Les Soirées de Médan, 1880. Translated from French.

About Guy de Maupassant

Guy de Maupassant was born on August 5, 1850, in Tourville-sur-Arques, France. A protégé of Flaubert, he wrote nearly 300 short stories and six novels in a career lasting barely a decade before his mental collapse. His masterpieces include 'The Necklace,' 'Boule de Suif,' and 'The Horla.' He died in Paris in 1893.
