Désirée's Baby

Kate Chopin · Written 1892, Published 1893 · ~2,700 words

Published in 1893 in Vogue, 'Désirée's Baby' is Kate Chopin's most powerful story about race in the American South. Désirée, an orphan of unknown origins, marries the proud Armand Aubigny. When their baby is born with features suggesting mixed race, Armand blames Désirée and sends her away. She walks into the bayou and disappears. He burns her letters — and finds a letter from his own mother revealing that it was he who had Black ancestry.

Quick Summary

'Désirée's Baby' is a story by Kate Chopin, published in 1893. Désirée, of unknown parentage, marries the proud Armand. Their baby shows signs of mixed race. Armand blames Désirée — whose unknown origins make her the easy suspect — and tells her to go. She and the baby disappear into the bayou. Armand burns her letters and finds one from his mother to his father: 'our boy carries the blood of the race that is cursed with the brand of slavery' — it is Armand who has Black ancestry.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with a twist ending that Chopin has carefully prepared — the hints are there throughout.

Themes

The horror of racism turned back on the racist — Armand destroys his family because of racial prejudice, and discovers it is his own blood he was condemning.

Historical Context

Published in Vogue, January 1893. One of the most widely anthologized stories about race in American literature.

Extract

As the day was pleasant, Madame Valmondé drove over to L'Abri to see Désirée and the baby.

It made her laugh to think of Désirée with a baby. Why, it seemed but yesterday that Désirée was little more than a baby herself; when Monsieur in riding through the gateway of Valmondé had found her lying asleep in the shadow of the big stone pillar.

Public domain. Published in Vogue, January 1893.

About Kate Chopin

Kate Chopin was born on February 8, 1850, in St. Louis, Missouri. Her novel The Awakening (1899) and her short stories — including 'The Story of an Hour' and 'Désirée's Baby' — are foundational texts of American feminist literature. Largely forgotten after her death, she was rediscovered by feminist critics in the 1960s and 70s. She died in St. Louis in 1904.
