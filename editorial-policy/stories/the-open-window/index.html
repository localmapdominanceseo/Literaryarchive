The Open Window

Saki (H.H. Munro) · Written 1911, Published 1914 · ~1,100 words

Published in 1914 in Beasts and Super-Beasts, 'The Open Window' is Saki's most celebrated story. A nervous visitor calls on a stranger and is entertained by her niece, who tells him a tragic story about three men who walked out that open window and never returned. When the men walk back in through the window, the visitor flees in terror. The niece invents a new story to explain his flight.

Quick Summary

'The Open Window' is a story by Saki, published in 1914. Framton Nuttel visits Mrs. Sappleton, a stranger. Her niece tells him that three years ago Mrs. Sappleton's husband and brothers walked out that open window to shoot snipe and were swallowed by a bog — and Mrs. Sappleton keeps the window open waiting for them. When the three men walk in from shooting, Nuttel flees in terror. The niece invents a story to explain it.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

A perfectly constructed comic story of about 1,100 words — one of the finest examples of the unreliable narrator used for comedy.

Themes

The power of storytelling and the credulity of the nervous — the niece's invention is so well constructed that it destroys Nuttel completely.

Historical Context

Published in Beasts and Super-Beasts (1914). Saki (H.H. Munro) was killed in World War I in 1916.

Extract

'My aunt will be down presently, Mr. Nuttel,' said a very self-possessed young lady of fifteen; 'in the meantime you must try and put up with me.'

Framton Nuttel endeavoured to say the correct something which should duly flatter the niece of the moment without unduly discounting the aunt that was to come.

Public domain. Published in Beasts and Super-Beasts, 1914.

About Saki (H.H. Munro)

Hector Hugh Munro, known as Saki, was born on December 18, 1870, in Akyab, Burma. His wickedly comic short stories — 'The Open Window,' 'Tobermory,' 'Sredni Vashtar' — are among the finest in English. He enlisted as a private at the outbreak of World War I despite being over-age and was killed by a sniper near Beaumont-Hamel in 1916.
