The Story of an Hour

Kate Chopin · Written 1894, Published 1894 · ~1,100 words

Published in 1894 in Vogue, 'The Story of an Hour' is Kate Chopin's most celebrated short story. Mrs. Mallard, who has a heart condition, learns that her husband has been killed in a railroad accident. After weeping, she feels a strange joy — she is free. Then her husband walks in the door, alive. She dies immediately.

Quick Summary

'The Story of an Hour' is a story by Kate Chopin, published in 1894. Louise Mallard is told her husband Brently has been killed in a train crash. She weeps, then goes to her room alone. She feels an unexpected joy rising — she will be free, will live for herself. She comes downstairs; her husband walks in the door, unharmed. Louise dies of heart disease. The doctors say she died 'of joy that kills.'

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration of extraordinary compression — the story is barely over a thousand words — with Chopin's characteristic ironic distance from her narrator.

Themes

The story is a feminist critique of marriage as a constraint on women's autonomy — Louise's joy at her husband's death is not about hating him but about the prospect of self-determination. The irony of the ending is bitter: the doctors attribute her death to joy, seeing only what they expect to see.

Historical Context

Published in Vogue, December 1894. Chopin's most frequently anthologized story.

Key Passage

There would be no one to live for during those coming years; she would live for herself. There would be no powerful will bending hers in that blind persistence with which men and women believe they have a right to impose a private will upon a fellow-creature.

Extract

Knowing that Mrs. Mallard was afflicted with a heart trouble, great care was taken to break to her as gently as possible the news of her husband's death.

It was her sister Josephine who told her, in broken sentences; veiled hints that revealed in half concealing. Her husband's friend Richards was there, too, near her. It was he who had been in the newspaper office when intelligence of the railroad disaster was received, with Brently Mallard's name leading the list of 'killed.' He had only taken the time to assure himself of its truth by a second telegram, and had hastened to forestall any less careful, less tender friend in bearing the sad message.

She did not hear the story as many women have heard the same, with a paralyzed inability to accept its significance. She wept at once, with sudden, wild abandonment, in her sister's arms. When the storm of grief had spent itself she went away to her room alone. She would have no one follow her.

Public domain. First published in Vogue, December 6, 1894.

About Kate Chopin

Kate Chopin was born on February 8, 1850, in St. Louis, Missouri. Her novel The Awakening (1899) and her short stories — including 'The Story of an Hour' and 'Désirée's Baby' — are foundational texts of American feminist literature. Largely forgotten after her death, she was rediscovered by feminist critics in the 1960s and 70s. She died in St. Louis in 1904.
