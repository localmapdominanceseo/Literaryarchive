The Short Happy Life of Francis Macomber

Ernest Hemingway · Written 1936, Published 1936 · ~11,000 words

Published in 1936 in Cosmopolitan, this is Hemingway's most celebrated African safari story. Francis Macomber, a wealthy American, runs from a wounded lion in front of his wife and their guide Robert Wilson. His wife Margot takes Wilson as a lover in contempt. The next morning Macomber overcomes his cowardice hunting buffalo — and is shot by Margot. Accident or murder?

Quick Summary

'The Short Happy Life of Francis Macomber' is a story by Hemingway, published in 1936. Macomber runs from a lion on an African safari, humiliating himself before his contemptuous wife Margot and their guide Wilson. That night Margot sleeps with Wilson. The next morning Macomber discovers courage hunting buffalo — and Margot shoots him from the car. Wilson believes it was murder.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Third-person narration with shifting perspectives — including, briefly, the lion's — using Hemingway's characteristic understatement.

Themes

Cowardice, courage, and the battle of the sexes — Macomber's brief moment of happiness is the moment he becomes free of his wife's contempt. Whether she kills him deliberately is left unresolved.

Historical Context

Published in Cosmopolitan, September 1936. Collected in The Fifth Column and the First Forty-Nine Stories (1938).

Extract

It was now lunch time and they were all sitting under the double green fly of the dining tent pretending that nothing had happened.

'Will you have lime juice or lemon squash?' Macomber asked.

'I'll have a gimlet,' Robert Wilson told him.

'I'll have a gimlet too. I need something,' Macomber's wife said.

'I suppose it's the thing to do,' Macomber agreed. 'Tell him to make three gimlets.'

About Ernest Hemingway

Ernest Hemingway was born on July 21, 1899, in Oak Park, Illinois. His prose style — stripped of adjectives, relying on dialogue and implication — transformed American fiction. His novels include The Sun Also Rises (1926) and A Farewell to Arms (1929). He won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1954. He died by suicide in Ketchum, Idaho, in 1961.
