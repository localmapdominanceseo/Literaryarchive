The Man Who Would Be King

Rudyard Kipling · Written 1888, Published 1888 · ~16,000 words

Published in 1888 in The Phantom Rickshaw and Other Tales, Kipling's novella follows two British adventurers, Daniel Dravot and Peachey Carnehan, who travel to the remote kingdom of Kafiristan determined to become its kings. They succeed — until Dravot tries to take a wife, is bitten, and exposed as not a god. He is executed. Carnehan returns to tell the story.

Quick Summary

'The Man Who Would Be King' is a novella by Rudyard Kipling, published in 1888. Two British adventurers, Dravot and Carnehan, travel to Kafiristan and use their rifles and Masonic knowledge to make themselves kings. Dravot grows grand and wants a wife — but when the girl he chooses bites him during the ceremony, the Kafirs see he bleeds and is human. He is flung off a rope bridge. Carnehan is crucified and survives to tell the tale.

Literary Analysis

Form & Structure

Frame narrative — the journalist narrator tells the story as told to him by the ruined Carnehan months later.

Themes

Imperial ambition and its inevitable overreach — Dravot's desire to actually become what he has only been playing at (a king, a god) destroys everything they built.

Historical Context

Published in The Phantom Rickshaw and Other Tales (1888). Filmed by John Huston in 1975.

Extract

'You'll meet me again in Central Asia,' said the second man, 'and remember then that I know your friend.' 'What friend?' said I.

'The one you tell me about. The reporter. The one that goes about collecting information for that paper of yours.' 'But I don't know any reporter,' I said, 'unless—'

'You will know him when the time comes,' said the second man with great confidence.

Public domain. Published in The Phantom Rickshaw and Other Tales, 1888.

About Rudyard Kipling

Rudyard Kipling was born on December 30, 1865, in Bombay, British India, and became one of the most popular English writers of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. He was the first English-language writer to receive the Nobel Prize for Literature (1907). He died in London in 1936.
