Hymn to Diana

by Ben Jonson

Written 1601 Published 1601 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1601 in the play Cynthia's Revels, this hymn to Diana (the moon goddess) is one of Jonson's finest short lyrics — Queen and huntress, chaste and fair, hunting in moonlight, with Hesperus in attendance.

Quick Answer

"Hymn to Diana" is a lyric by Ben Jonson from Cynthia's Revels (1601). It addresses Diana as Queen and huntress, chaste and fair — hunting in evening's gown, sitting in silver chair, with Hesperus enthroning her. Lay thy bow of pearl apart and thy crystal shining quiver; give us quiet, and good rest.

Queen and huntress, chaste and fair,
Now the sun is laid to sleep,
Seated in thy silver chair,
State in wonted manner keep:
Hesperus entreats thy light,
Goddess excellently bright.

Earth, let not thy envious shade
Dare itself to interpose;
Cynthia's shining orb was made
Heaven to clear when day did close:
Bless us then with wished sight,
Goddess excellently bright.

Lay thy bow of pearl apart,
And thy crystal-shining quiver;
Give unto the flying hart
Space to breathe, how short soever:
Thou that mak'st a day of night,
Goddess excellently bright.

Public domain. Published 1601.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three stanzas of six lines each in iambic tetrameter and trimeter, the hymn form giving the poem its ceremonial quality.

Themes

The beauty of Diana as moon goddess and emblem of chastity, and the prayer for quiet at day's end.

Historical Context

From Cynthia's Revels (1601). Widely considered one of the finest short lyrics of the English Renaissance for its formal perfection and musical quality.

About Ben Jonson

Ben Jonson was born on June 11, 1572, in London, and became the foremost dramatist and poet of the Jacobean era, second only to Shakespeare in contemporary reputation. His plays Volpone (1606) and The Alchemist (1610) are among the greatest comedies in English. As a poet, his Epigrams, The Forest, and Under-wood collections display unrivaled technical mastery and classical learning. He died in London in 1637.
