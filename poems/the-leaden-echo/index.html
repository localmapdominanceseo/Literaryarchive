The Leaden Echo and the Golden Echo

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1882 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1882 and published posthumously in 1918, this poem poses the Leaden Echo's question — how to keep back beauty — and answers with the Golden Echo: give beauty back, give it to God.

Quick Answer

The Leaden Echo and the Golden Echo is a poem by Hopkins, published posthumously in 1918. The Leaden Echo asks: how to keep back beauty from vanishing away? There is no answer. The Golden Echo answers: give beauty back, give it to God who keeps it. Beauty is best kept there — there is a long and lazar-like life that God keeps for it.

THE LEADEN ECHO
How to kéep—is there ány any, is there none such, nowhere known some, bow or brooch or braid or brace, láce, latch or catch or key to keep
Back beauty, keep it, beauty, beauty, beauty, ... from vanishing away?

THE GOLDEN ECHO
Spare!
There ís one, yes I have one (Hush there!);
Only not within seeing of the sun,
Not within the singeing of the strong sun,
Tall sun's tingeing, or treacherous the tainting of the earth's air,
Somewhere elsewhere there is ah well where! one,
One. Yes I cán tell such a key, I do know such a place,
Where whatever's prized and passes of us, everything that's fresh and fast flying of us, seems to us sweet of us and swiftly away with, done away with, undone,
Undone, done with, soon done with, and yet dearly and dangerously sweet
Of us, the wimpled-water-dimpled, not-by-morning-matched face,
The flower of beauty, fleece of beauty, too too apt to, ah! to fleet,
Never fleets móre, fastened with the tenderest truth
To its own best being and its loveliness of youth: it is an everlastingness of, O it is an all youth!

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two long stanzas of varying line length in sprung rhythm, the Leaden Echo all falling cadences, the Golden Echo all rising ones.

Themes

The vanity of trying to preserve earthly beauty versus the surety of giving it back to God, who will restore it glorified in the resurrection.

Historical Context

Written in 1882. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
