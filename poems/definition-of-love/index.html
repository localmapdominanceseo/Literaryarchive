The Definition of Love

by Andrew Marvell

Written 1650 Published 1681 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1681, this poem defines the speaker's love as begotten by Despair upon Impossibility — a perfect love that Fate keeps apart by placing the lovers at opposite poles of the earth, because if they were united, the world's entire geometry would collapse.

Quick Answer

"The Definition of Love" is a poem by Andrew Marvell, published posthumously in 1681. The speaker's love was begotten by Despair upon Impossibility. It is a divine love, but Fate has placed the lovers at opposite poles — for if they were joined, the whole world would crumble. Their love is perfect precisely because it is impossible.

My love is of a birth as rare
As 'tis for object strange and high:
It was begotten by Despair
Upon Impossibility.

Magnanimous Despair alone
Could show me so divine a thing,
Where feeble Hope could ne'er have flown
But vainly flapped its tinsel wing.

And yet I quickly might arrive
Where my extended soul is fixed,
But Fate does iron wedges drive,
And always crowds itself betwixt.

For Fate with jealous eye does see
Two perfect loves, nor lets them close:
Their union would her ruin be,
And her tyrannic power depose.

And therefore her decrees of steel
Us as the distant poles have placed,
(Though Love's whole world on us doth wheel)
Not by themselves to be embraced:

Unless the giddy heaven fall,
And earth some new convulsion tear,
And, us to join, the world should all
Be cramped into a planisphere.

As lines, so loves oblique may well
Themselves in every angle greet:
But ours, so truly parallel,
Though infinite, can never meet.

Therefore the love which us doth bind,
But Fate so enviously debars,
Is the conjunction of the mind,
And opposition of the stars.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight quatrains of iambic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme, the formal precision mirroring the geometrical metaphors of the poem.

Themes

The paradox that perfect love is impossible by definition — or rather, that the very perfection of this love is what makes Fate keep it apart, since its union would undo the world. The poem uses astronomical and mathematical imagery throughout.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1681 in Miscellaneous Poems. One of Marvell's finest Metaphysical conceits — extending the logic of impossible love to a cosmic scale.

About Andrew Marvell

Andrew Marvell was born on March 31, 1621, in Winestead-in-Holderness, Yorkshire, England, and became one of the most significant Metaphysical poets as well as a prominent political figure, serving as a Member of Parliament. Most of his lyric poetry, including 'To His Coy Mistress,' was published posthumously in 1681. He died in London in 1678.
