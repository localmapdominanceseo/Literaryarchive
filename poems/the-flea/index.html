The Flea

by John Donne

Written 1600 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1600 and published posthumously in 1633, this poem is one of Donne's most audacious seduction arguments: a flea has bitten both the speaker and his beloved, mingling their blood inside it — and so, the speaker argues, their union in the flea has already happened, and denying him would be no worse than killing the flea.

Quick Answer

"The Flea" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker points out that a flea has sucked blood from both him and his beloved, mingling them inside it — this makes them already joined, more than marriage would. He begs her not to kill it. She kills it anyway. He recovers instantly: just as killing the flea cost her no honor, yielding to him will cost her none either.

Mark but this flea, and mark in this,
How little that which thou deniest me is;
It sucked me first, and now sucks thee,
And in this flea our two bloods mingled be;
Thou knowest that this cannot be said
A sin, nor shame, nor loss of maidenhead,
Yet this enjoys before it woo,
And pampered swells with one blood made of two,
And this, alas! is more than we would do.

Oh stay, three lives in one flea spare,
Where we almost, yea, more than married are.
This flea is you and I, and this
Our marriage bed, and marriage temple is;
Though parents grudge, and you, we're met
And cloistered in these living walls of jet.
Though use make you apt to kill me,
Let not to that self-murder added be,
And sacrilege, three sins in killing three.

Cruel and sudden, hast thou since
Purpled thy nail in blood of innocence?
Wherein could this flea guilty be,
Except in that drop which it sucked from thee?
Yet thou triumph'st, and say'st that thou
Findest not thyself nor me the weaker now.
'Tis true; then learn how false fears be:
Just so much honor, when thou yield'st to me,
Will waste, as this flea's death took life from thee.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three nine-line stanzas with a distinctive rhyme scheme (AABBCCDDD), each ending with a triplet that drives home the argument's point. The poem is structured as a three-stage argument that survives a devastating counter-move (killing the flea) and still wins.

Themes

Seduction through absurd logical argument — the Metaphysical conceit at its most extreme. The poem is witty, erotic, and genuinely funny, with the beloved's killing of the flea being the best thing that could happen to the speaker's argument. It demonstrates Donne's characteristic method: taking an unlikely premise and developing it with rigorous internal logic.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633 in Songs and Sonnets. 'The Flea' represents a tradition of flea-poems in Renaissance European literature, but Donne's version is widely considered the most brilliant — particularly for its mid-poem reversal that turns the beloved's decisive action into the speaker's final coup.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
