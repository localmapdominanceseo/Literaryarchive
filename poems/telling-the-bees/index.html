Telling the Bees

by John Greenleaf Whittier

Written 1858 Published 1858 American Romanticism Public Domain

Published 1858.

Quick Answer

Telling the Bees is a poem, published 1858.

Here is the place; right over the hill
Runs the path I took;
You can see the gap in the old wall still,
And the stepping-stones in the shallow brook.

There is the house, with the gate red-barred,
And the poplars tall;
And the barn's brown length, and the cattle-yard,
And the white horns tossing above the wall.

Before them, under the garden wall,
Forward and back,
Went drearily singing the chore-girl small,
Draping each hive with a shred of black.

Presently stole the chore-girl small
The dirge of her dead from house to house;
And the song she was singing was, after all,
'Stay at home, pretty bees, fly not hence!
Mistress Mary is dead and gone!'

About John Greenleaf Whittier

John Greenleaf Whittier was born on December 17, 1807, in Haverhill, Massachusetts, and became both a prominent abolitionist and one of the most popular American poets of the nineteenth century. His long poem Snow-Bound (1866), a winter idyll and elegy for his lost family, is considered his masterpiece. He was a Quaker and used his poetry extensively in the service of the anti-slavery cause. He died in Hampton Falls, New Hampshire, in 1892.
