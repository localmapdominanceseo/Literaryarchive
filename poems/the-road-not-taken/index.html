The Road Not Taken

by Robert Frost

Written 1915 Published 1916 American Modernism Public Domain

Written in 1915 and first published in 1916 in the collection Mountain Interval, this is Robert Frost's most famous and most widely misread poem. Frost wrote it as a gentle joke about a chronically indecisive friend, though it has since become popularly read as a sincere celebration of individualism.

Quick Answer

"The Road Not Taken" is a poem by Robert Frost, written in 1915 and published in 1916. A traveler reaches a fork in a wood and chooses one path over the other, even though the poem makes clear the two paths were really about the same. Frost intended it as a wry joke about indecision, though it is now most often read as a celebration of individual choice.

Two roads diverged in a yellow wood,
And sorry I could not travel both
And be one traveler, long I stood
And looked down one as far as I could
To where it bent in the undergrowth;

Then took the other, as just as fair,
And having perhaps the better claim,
Because it was grassy and wanted wear;
Though as for that the passing there
Had worn them really about the same,

And both that morning equally lay
In leaves no step had trodden black.
Oh, I kept the first for another day!
Yet knowing how way leads on to way,
I doubted if I should ever come back.

I shall be telling this with a sigh
Somewhere ages and ages hence:
Two roads diverged in a wood, and I -
I took the one less traveled by,
And that has made all the difference.

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem consists of four five-line stanzas written in loose iambic tetrameter, with Frost frequently substituting anapests for iambs, giving the conversational lines a natural, unforced rhythm despite their formal rhyme scheme.

Themes

Although widely quoted as a celebration of nonconformity, the poem's speaker explicitly admits the two roads 'equally lay / In leaves no step had trodden black' and were worn 'really about the same.' The real theme is how people construct meaningful narratives about their choices after the fact, turning an arbitrary decision into a defining, fateful moment only in retrospect.

Historical Context

Frost wrote the poem in 1915 while living in England, inspired by walks with his friend, the poet Edward Thomas, who habitually regretted whichever path they took. Frost intended it as a gentle joke at Thomas's expense, but audiences took it seriously from its first public reading onward, and Frost spent years trying, with little success, to correct the popular misreading.

About Robert Frost

Robert Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California, though he is most closely associated with rural New England, where he lived and farmed for much of his life. His poetry uses plain, conversational language and traditional verse forms to explore deceptively simple rural scenes that often open onto deeper questions about choice, isolation, and mortality. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry and remains one of the most widely read American poets of the twentieth century. He died in Boston in 1963.
