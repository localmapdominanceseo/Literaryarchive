The Nymph's Reply to the Shepherd

by Sir Walter Raleigh

Written 1600 Published 1600 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1600 as a reply to Marlowe's 'The Passionate Shepherd to His Love,' this poem answers the shepherd's idyllic pastoral invitation by pointing out that time will not stand still: flowers fade, rivers freeze, spring turns to winter, and no field of perpetual delight exists — so the nymph cannot be moved by his promises.

Quick Answer

"The Nymph's Reply to the Shepherd" is a poem by Sir Walter Raleigh, published in 1600, written as a response to Marlowe's 'The Passionate Shepherd to His Love.' The nymph acknowledges the shepherd's invitation but rejects it because his promises are only possible in a world where time stands still and spring lasts forever. Since time does move — flowers fade, fields frost over, the shepherd's fancy will pass — she declines.

If all the world and love were young,
And truth in every shepherd's tongue,
These pretty pleasures might me move
To live with thee and be thy love.

Time drives the flocks from field to fold,
When rivers rage and rocks grow cold,
And Philomel becometh dumb;
The rest complains of cares to come.

The flowers do fade, and wanton fields
To wayward winter reckoning yields;
A honey tongue, a heart of gall,
Is fancy's spring, but sorrow's fall.

Thy gowns, thy shoes, thy beds of roses,
Thy cap, thy kirtle, and thy posies,
Soon break, soon wither, soon forgotten,
In folly ripe, in reason rotten.

Thy belt of straw and ivy buds,
Thy coral clasps and amber studs,
All these in me no means can move
To come to thee and be thy love.

But could youth last and love still breed,
Had joys no date nor age no need,
Then these delights my mind might move
To live with thee and be thy love.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six quatrains in iambic tetrameter with AABB rhyme, closely mirroring Marlowe's form and structure — stanza for stanza, the nymph answers each of the shepherd's promises with its seasonal reality.

Themes

The corrosive power of time against the pastoral ideal. Where Marlowe imagines a permanent spring, Raleigh's nymph insists on the actual seasons and on the passing of desire with age. The poem is not bitter — it is wry and gentle — but it grounds the conversation in reality.

Historical Context

Raleigh wrote the poem around 1600 as a witty, affectionate rejoinder to Marlowe's famous poem. The two poems are often paired in anthologies as an example of poetic dialogue and as a contrast between idealistic and realistic visions of love and nature.

About Sir Walter Raleigh

Sir Walter Raleigh was born around 1554 in Devon, England, and became an explorer, soldier, courtier, and poet, one of the most flamboyant figures of the Elizabethan court. He was twice imprisoned in the Tower of London and was eventually executed in 1618. As a poet, he is best known for his witty response to Marlowe's 'The Passionate Shepherd' and for the lyric 'What Is Our Life?'
