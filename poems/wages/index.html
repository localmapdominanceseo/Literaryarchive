Wages

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1867 Published 1867 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1867, this eight-line poem argues that glory is the reward of virtue — not the pleasures of the world, which are transient, but the eternal recognition that comes from lives well lived.

Quick Answer

"Wages" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1867. Glory of warrior, glory of orator, glory of song — paid with a voice flying by to vanish, then returning to bring an echo. The wages of Virtue are dust. But she sires a race of nobler men, and that is her glory.

Glory of warrior, glory of orator, glory of song,
Paid with a voice flying by to be lost on an endless sea—
Glory of Virtue, to fight, to struggle, to right the wrong—
Nay, but she aim'd not at glory, no lover of glory she:
Give her the glory of going on, and still to be.

The wages of sin is death: if the wages of Virtue be dust,
Would she have heart to endure for the life of the worm and the fly?
She desires no isles of the blest, no quiet seats of the just,
To rest in a golden grove, or to bask in a summer sky:
Give her the wages of going on, and not to die.

Public domain. Published 1867.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight lines of iambic tetrameter with AABB CCDD rhyme.

Themes

The paradox that virtue's reward is earthly — only dust — and yet virtue persists and its long-term reward is the race of better people it creates.

Historical Context

Published in 1867. One of Tennyson's shortest philosophical poems.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
