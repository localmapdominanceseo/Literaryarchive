Selected Haiku — Matsuo Bashō

by Matsuo Bashō

Written 1686 Published 1686 Japanese Haiku Public Domain

Matsuo Bashō (1644-1694) is the master of haiku, the Japanese seventeen-syllable poem. These four of his most celebrated haiku include the famous old pond poem, considered the founding text of the haiku tradition.

Quick Answer

Matsuo Bashō is the master of the Japanese haiku form. His most famous poem — 'Old pond / a frog jumps in / sound of water' — is considered the founding text of haiku as a serious literary form. These four haiku represent his characteristic fusion of natural observation with sudden insight.

Old pond—
a frog jumps in,
sound of water.

(1686)

—

On a bare branch
a crow has settled—
autumn nightfall.

(c.1680)

—

In the cicada's cry
no sign can foretell
how soon it must die.

(c.1688)

—

Over the wintry
forest, winds howl in rage
with no leaves to blow.

(c.1689)

Public domain translations. Originals by Matsuo Bashō (1644-1694). Translations are free renderings in the public domain.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Each haiku has seventeen syllables in three lines of five-seven-five in Japanese, though English translations vary. Each presents a concrete sensory image that opens suddenly onto a larger perception — the kigo (seasonal word) grounding the moment in time.

Themes

The sudden illumination of ordinary natural moments — a frog jumping, the cry of a cicada, a crow on a bare branch at autumn nightfall. Bashō's haiku resist interpretation while generating it endlessly.

Historical Context

Bashō elevated haiku from a comic social game to a serious literary art. He spent much of his later life on journeys, and his travel journal The Narrow Road to the Deep North is one of the masterpieces of Japanese literature.
