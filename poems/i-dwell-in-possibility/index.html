I Dwell in Possibility

by Emily Dickinson

Written 1862 Published 1929 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1862 and published posthumously in 1929, this poem defines poetry as a house more numerous of windows, superior from doors, with everlasting rooms and a superior occupation: spreading wide the narrow hands to gather Paradise.

Quick Answer

"I Dwell in Possibility" is a poem by Emily Dickinson, written around 1862. She contrasts poetry (Possibility) with prose, finding poetry superior: more numerous of windows, a fairer house of doors, chambers as cedar as can be, the visitors impregnable. Her occupation is to spread wide her narrow hands and gather Paradise.

I dwell in Possibility—
A fairer House than Prose—
More numerous of Windows—
Superior—for Doors—

Of Chambers as the Cedars—
Impregnable of eye—
And for an everlasting Roof
The Gambrels of the Sky—

Of Visitors—the fairest—
For Occupation—This—
The spreading wide my narrow Hands
To gather Paradise—

Public domain. Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains in common meter with slant rhyme, the poem using an extended house metaphor for the imaginative space of poetry.

Themes

The superiority of poetry over prose as a mode of imagination — poetry is a larger, better-built house with more windows, chambers, and visitors than prose can provide.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1929. One of Dickinson's most quoted poems about the nature of poetry itself.

About Emily Dickinson

Emily Dickinson was born on December 10, 1830, in Amherst, Massachusetts, and spent nearly her entire life there. Though she wrote nearly 1,800 poems, fewer than a dozen were published during her lifetime. Her work, marked by unconventional capitalization, slant rhyme, and dashes, was largely discovered and published after her death in 1886. She is now considered one of the most important and original voices in American poetry, known for her meditations on death, immortality, nature, and the inner life.
