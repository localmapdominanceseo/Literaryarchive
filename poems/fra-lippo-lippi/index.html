Fra Lippo Lippi (Extract)

by Robert Browning

Written 1855 Published 1855 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1855 in Men and Women, this dramatic monologue is spoken by the Renaissance painter Fra Filippo Lippi, caught by the night watch in Florence and explaining himself — defending the right of art to represent the beauty of the actual world rather than transcendent ideals.

Quick Answer

"Fra Lippo Lippi" is a dramatic monologue by Robert Browning, published in 1855. The painter Fra Lippo Lippi, caught by the night watch in Florence, tells his life story — the orphan boy taken in by monks, trained to paint, but always drawn to the real world of faces and bodies. He defends painting what he sees rather than what the Church wants him to see.

I am poor brother Lippo, by your leave!
You need not clap your torches to my face.
Zooks, what's to blame? you think you see a monk!
What, 'tis past midnight, and you go the rounds,
And here you catch me at an alley's end
Where sportive ladies leave their doors ajar?
The Carmine's my cloister: hunt it up,
Do—harry out, if you must show your zeal,
Whatever rat, there, haps on his wrong hole,
And nip each softling of a wee white mouse,
Weke, weke, that's crept to keep him company!
Aha, you know your betters! Then, you'll take
Your hand away that's fiddling on my throat,
And please to know me likewise. Who am I?
Why, one, sir, who is lodging with a friend
Three streets off—he's a certain...how do I know?—
You know your betters! I am poor brother Lippo.

Public domain. Published 1855 in Men and Women.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three hundred and ninety-two lines of blank verse, one of Browning's longest and most celebrated dramatic monologues, the painter's voice vivid and specific throughout.

Themes

The conflict between the Church's demand for idealized religious art and the artist's impulse to paint the real, beautiful, sensory world. Lippi defends the value of the visible world against those who want art to transcend it.

Historical Context

Published in Men and Women (1855). Fra Filippo Lippi (c.1406-1469) was a real Florentine painter, famous for his sensuous Madonnas and his own scandalous life. Browning used historical figures throughout Men and Women to explore questions of art, love, and power.

About Robert Browning

Robert Browning was born on May 7, 1812, in Camberwell, London, and became one of the foremost Victorian poets, celebrated above all for his mastery of the dramatic monologue — a form in which a speaker inadvertently reveals more about themselves than they intend. His collection Men and Women (1855) and his verse novel The Ring and the Book (1868-1869) are considered his greatest achievements. He married Elizabeth Barrett in 1846 and they lived in Italy until her death in 1861. He died in Venice in 1889.
