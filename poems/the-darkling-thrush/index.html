The Darkling Thrush

by Thomas Hardy

Written 1900 Published 1902 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1902, written on the last day of the nineteenth century, this poem describes a thrush singing with full-hearted evensong of joy in the bleak midwinter landscape — and the speaker wondering what the bird might know that he does not.

Quick Answer

The Darkling Thrush is a poem by Thomas Hardy, published in 1902. The speaker leans on a coppice gate in the bleak December landscape. A thrush, aged and frail, flings his soul upon the growing gloom in a full-hearted evensong of joy. The speaker can think of no reason for such hope, but the thrush seems to know of some blessed hope he is unaware of.

I leant upon a coppice gate
When Frost was spectre-grey,
And Winter's dregs made desolate
The weakening eye of day.
The tangled bine-stems scored the sky
Like strings of broken lyres,
And all mankind that haunted nigh
Had sought their household fires.

The land's sharp features seemed to be
The Century's corpse outleant,
His crypt the cloudy canopy,
The wind his death-lament.
The ancient pulse of germ and birth
Was shrunken hard and dry,
And every spirit upon earth
Seemed fervourless as I.

At once a voice arose among
The bleak twigs overhead
In a full-hearted evensong
Of joy illimited;
An aged thrush, frail, gaunt, and small,
In blast-beruffled plume,
Had chosen thus to fling his soul
Upon the growing gloom.

So little cause for carolings
Of such ecstatic sound
Was written on terrestrial things
Afar or nigh around,
That I could think there trembled through
His happy good-night air
Some blessed Hope, whereof he knew
And I was unaware.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four eight-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABABCDCD rhyme.

Themes

The possibility of hope in a hopeless landscape — the thrush's inexplicable joy against the winter blankness suggests there may be something the speaker cannot perceive.

Historical Context

Written December 31, 1900, published in The Graphic in 1902. Hardy was famously pessimistic, making the thrush's hope all the more poignant.

About Thomas Hardy

Thomas Hardy was born on June 2, 1840, in Higher Bockhampton, Dorset, England, and became one of the greatest novelists and poets of the Victorian and Edwardian eras. After Jude the Obscure (1895) was attacked for immorality, he stopped writing fiction and devoted himself entirely to poetry, producing eight volumes. His elegies for his first wife Emma, written after her sudden death in 1912, are considered among the finest in the English language. He died in Dorchester in 1928.
