The Lotos-Eaters (Extract)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1832 Published 1842 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1842, The Lotos-Eaters is a dramatic poem in which Ulysses's sailors eat the lotos plant and lose all desire to return home, wishing only to stay and rest forever in the drowsy land.

Quick Answer

"The Lotos-Eaters" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1842. Ulysses's sailors eat the lotos plant and are overcome by a desire to rest and never return home. The Choric Song that follows is their sustained argument for staying — why should they toil and moil? Let them rest forever in the land where it is always afternoon.

'Courage!' he said, and pointed toward the land,
'This mounting wave will roll us shoreward soon.'
In the afternoon they came unto a land
In which it seemed always afternoon.
All round the coast the languid air did swoon,
Breathing like one that hath a weary dream.
Full-faced above the valley stood the moon;
And like a downward smoke, the slender stream
Along the cliff to fall and pause and fall did seem.

A land of streams! some, like a downward smoke,
Slow-dropping veils of thinnest lawn, did go;
And some thro' wavering lights and shadows broke,
Rolling a slumbrous sheet of foam below.
They saw the gleaming river seaward flow
From the inner land: far off, three mountain-tops,
Three silent pinnacles of aged snow,
Stood sunset-flush'd: and, dew'd with showery drops,
Up-clomb the shadowy pine above the woven copse.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A narrative introduction followed by a Choric Song in irregular Spenserian-influenced stanzas, the form enacting the sailors' drowsy abandonment of regular meter.

Themes

The temptation of rest and withdrawal from the active life — the poem presents the sailors' case for giving up sympathetically, even as it implicitly questions the morality of abandoning duty and home.

Historical Context

First published in 1832, revised for 1842. Drawn from Book IX of the Odyssey. The poem's sensuous drowsiness influenced later Aesthetic and Decadent poets.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
