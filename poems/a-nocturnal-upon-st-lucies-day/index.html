A Nocturnal upon St. Lucy's Day

by John Donne

Written 1617 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1617, possibly after the death of his wife Anne More, and published posthumously in 1633, this poem is set on the longest night of the year — St. Lucy's Day (December 13 by the old calendar) — and the speaker declares himself the epitome of nothingness, the grave of all that's nothing.

Quick Answer

"A Nocturnal upon St. Lucy's Day" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633, set on the shortest day and longest night of the year. The world is contracted to a dead and interred sun. The speaker is made of absence, darkness, death — the quintessence of nothing. Let lovers celebrate future springs; he can only prepare for his own death.

'Tis the year's midnight, and it is the day's,
Lucy's, who scarce seven hours herself unmasks;
The sun is spent, and now his flasks
Send forth light squibs, no constant rays;
The world's whole sap is sunk;
The general balm th' hydroptic earth hath drunk,
Whither, as to the bed's feet, life is shrunk,
Dead and interred; yet all these seem to laugh,
Compared with me, who am their epitaph.

Study me then, you who shall lovers be
At the next world, that is, at the next spring;
For I am every dead thing,
In whom Love wrought new alchemy.
For his art did express
A quintessence even from nothingness,
From dull privations, and lean emptiness;
He ruined me, and I am re-begot
Of absence, darkness, death—things which are not.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five nine-line stanzas in iambic pentameter and tetrameter with ABBACCCDD rhyme, Donne's most complex stanza form.

Themes

Total negation through grief — the speaker has been alchemically reduced to the quintessence of nothingness by his beloved's death. The poem is Donne's most profound meditation on grief and absence.

Historical Context

Probably written after the death of Donne's wife Anne in 1617, though the exact occasion is debated. Published posthumously in 1633. Often considered Donne's most tragic poem.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
