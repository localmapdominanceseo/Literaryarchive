Felix Randal

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1880 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in April 1880 after the death of a parishioner, a farrier named Felix Spencer, and published posthumously in 1918, this sonnet mourns the death of a powerful man reduced by illness to a child's dependence, comforted by the priest who now writes about him.

Quick Answer

"Felix Randal" is a sonnet by Gerard Manley Hopkins, written in 1880 and published posthumously in 1918. The speaker, a priest, reflects on the death of Felix Randal, a farrier — once a powerful man, 'big-boned and hardy-handsome' — who was reduced by illness to tears and was comforted by the priest's priestly ministrations. The closing image is Felix in his prime, launching a great horse on a bright street.

Felix Randal the farrier, O is he dead then? my duty all ended,
Who have watched his mould of man, big-boned and hardy-handsome
Pining, pining, till time when reason rambled in it, and some
Fatal four disorders, fleshed there, all contended?

Sickness broke him. Impatient, he cursed at first, but mended
Being anointed and all; though a heavenlier heart began some
Months earlier, since I had our sweet reprieve and ransom
Tendered to him. Ah well, God rest him all road ever he offended!

This seeing the sick endears them to us, us too it endears.
My tongue had taught thee comfort, touch had quenched thy tears,
Thy tears that touched my heart, child, Felix, poor Felix Randal;

How far from then forethought of, all thy more boisterous years,
When thou at the random grim forge, powerful amidst peers,
Didst fettle for the great grey drayhorse his bright and battering sandal!

Public domain. Published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in sprung rhythm, with Hopkins's characteristic dense sound effects, compound words, and internal alliteration carrying the emotional weight. The octave covers Felix's illness and death; the sestet turns to the speaker's emotional response and the closing memory.

Themes

The vulnerability of physical strength before illness and death; the pastoral relationship between priest and parishioner; and the way death transforms our understanding of a person's life. The final image of Felix in his prime — 'Didst fettle for the great grey drayhorse his bright and battering sandal!' — is one of the most powerful endings in Hopkins.

Historical Context

Hopkins wrote the poem in April 1880 while serving as a priest in Liverpool. The real Felix Randal was Felix Spencer, a farrier from Manchester whom Hopkins had tended in his final illness. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
