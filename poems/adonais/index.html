Adonais (Extract)

by Percy Bysshe Shelley

Written 1821 Published 1821 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written in 1821 and published the same year, Adonais is Shelley's pastoral elegy for John Keats, who had died in February 1821 at age twenty-five. The poem is modeled on the classical elegy and traces the speaker's grief through to a resolution that Keats has not died but become part of the eternal beauty he celebrated. This is the celebrated opening invocation.

Quick Answer

"Adonais" is an elegy by Percy Bysshe Shelley, written and published in 1821 for John Keats, who had died of tuberculosis in Rome at age twenty-five. Drawing on the classical pastoral elegy tradition, Shelley mourns Keats as a Miltonic figure cut down before his time, before arriving at the consolation that Keats has not perished but become part of the eternal beauty his poetry sought.

I weep for Adonais—he is dead!
Oh, weep for Adonais! though our tears
Thaw not the frost which binds so dear a head!
And thou, sad Hour, selected from all years
To mourn our loss, rouse thy obscure compeers,
And teach them thine own sorrow, say: 'With me
Died Adonais; till the Future dares
Forget the Past, his fate and fame shall be
An echo and a light unto eternity!'

Where wert thou, mighty Mother, when he lay,
When thy Son lay, pierced by the shaft which flies
In darkness? where was lorn Urania
When Adonais died? With veiled eyes,
'Mid listening Echoes, in her Paradise
She sate, while one, with soft enamoured breath,
Rekindled all the fading melodies,
With which, like flowers that mock the corse beneath,
He had adorned and hid the coming bulk of Death.

Oh, weep for Adonais—he is dead!
Wake, melancholy Mother, wake and weep!
Yet wherefore? Quench within their burning bed
Thy fiery tears, and let thy loud heart keep
Like his, a mute and uncomplaining sleep;
For he is gone, where all things wise and fair
Descend;—oh, dream not that the amorous Deep
Will yet restore him to the vital air;
Death feeds on his mute voice, and laughs at our despair.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Fifty-five Spenserian stanzas (nine lines, ABABBCBCC rhyme scheme, closing with an alexandrine — a twelve-syllable line), a highly formal, elaborate structure suited to the public gravity of a classical elegy.

Themes

Grief, consolation, and the immortality of the poet through art. Shelley argues that Keats — whom he blamed (incorrectly) for having been killed by a hostile review in the Quarterly Review — has not died but been absorbed into the 'white radiance of Eternity' and become part of the beauty he spent his short life pursuing. The poem ends with Shelley himself longing to follow.

Historical Context

Shelley wrote Adonais in April-May 1821 after learning of Keats's death in Rome on February 23, 1821. He was deeply moved and deeply wrong about the cause — Keats died of tuberculosis, not of the hostile review — but the poem is a genuine expression of grief for a fellow poet cut short. Shelley himself died in a boating accident the following year, 1822.

About Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley was born on August 4, 1792, in Horsham, England, and became one of the major poets of English Romanticism despite controversy during his lifetime over his radical political and religious views. His poetry combines lyrical intensity with sustained engagement with politics, philosophy, and the natural world. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy in 1822, at the age of twenty-nine.
