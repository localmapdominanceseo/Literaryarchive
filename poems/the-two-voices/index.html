The Two Voices (Extract)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1833 Published 1842 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1842, The Two Voices is a long philosophical poem in which a voice of despair argues for suicide and a voice of life argues for continuing — ending in a vision of a Sunday morning family going to church that restores the speaker's will to live.

Quick Answer

The Two Voices (1842) is Tennyson's long poem in which an inner voice of despair argues for suicide while a second voice argues for life. The poem ends with the speaker watching a family go to church on a Sunday morning and finding, in this ordinary human scene, the evidence that life is worth living.

A still small voice spake unto me,
'Thou art so full of misery,
Were it not better not to be?'

Then to the still small voice I said;
'Let me not cast in endless shade
What is so wonderfully made.'

To which the voice did urge reply;
'To-day I saw the dragon-fly
Come from the wells where he did lie.

'An inner impulse rent the veil
Of his old husk: from head to tail
Came out clear plates of sapphire mail.

'He dried his wings: like gauze they grew;
Thro' crofts and pastures wet with dew
A living flash of light he flew.'

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two hundred and thirty-one three-line stanzas of iambic tetrameter with AAA rhyme.

Themes

The philosophical debate between despair and hope, nihilism and faith — ending in the pragmatic resolution that the sight of ordinary human life is evidence enough for continuing.

Historical Context

Written in 1833 after the death of Arthur Hallam. Published in 1842. One of Tennyson's most explicitly philosophical poems.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
