Leda and the Swan

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1923 Published 1924 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1924, this sonnet describes Zeus's rape of Leda in the form of a swan, and asks whether she took on his knowledge and power along with his brute force — and whether the indifferent beak could let her drop before the dark wings beat and he was gone.

Quick Answer

"Leda and the Swan" is a sonnet by W.B. Yeats, published in 1924. It describes Zeus as a swan overwhelming Leda with his wings and beak, engendering Helen of Troy and the Trojan War. The poem asks whether the god put his knowledge and power into the girl before the indifferent beak let her drop.

A sudden blow: the great wings beating still
Above the staggering girl, her thighs caressed
By the dark webs, her nape caught in his bill,
He holds her helpless breast upon his breast.

How can those terrified vague fingers push
The feathered glory from her loosening thighs?
And how can body, laid in that white rush,
But feel the strange heart beating where it lies?

A shudder in the loins engenders there
The broken wall, the burning roof and tower
And Agamemnon dead.
                    Being so caught up,
So mastered by the brute blood of the air,
Did she put on his knowledge with his power
Before the indifferent beak could let her drop?

Public domain (published 1924). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

A loose sonnet in iambic pentameter, ABAB CDCD EFGEFG, the form shaken and compressed under the violence of the subject.

Themes

The moment of divine violence that initiates a historical cycle — Helen born from this union will cause the Trojan War. Yeats uses the myth to explore how history pivots on moments of overwhelming force.

Historical Context

Published in A Vision (1924). For Yeats, the rape of Leda by Zeus inaugurated the Greek historical cycle, just as the Annunciation inaugurated the Christian cycle.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
