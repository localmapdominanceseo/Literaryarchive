Yet Do I Marvel

by Countee Cullen

Written 1924 Published 1925 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1925 in Color, this sonnet catalogues the puzzling contradictions of divine creation — the mole made blind, Tantalus cursed, Sisyphus pushing his stone — before ending with the most puzzling of all: that God made a poet Black and bid him sing.

Quick Answer

"Yet Do I Marvel" is a sonnet by Countee Cullen, published in 1925. The speaker does not doubt God's goodness but marvels at God's curious ways: making the mole blind, keeping Tantalus thirsty, having Sisyphus push his stone eternally. Stranger still: that God made a poet Black and bid him sing — a bitter, defiant recognition that the gift of poetry comes with the burden of racism.

I doubt not God is good, well-meaning, kind,
And did He stoop to quibble could tell why
The little buried mole continues blind,
Why flesh that mirrors Him must some day die,
Make plain the reason tortured Tantalus
Is baited by the fickle fruit, declare
If merely brute caprice dooms Sisyphus
To struggle up a never-ending stair.
Inscrutably His ways are, and immune
To catechism by a mind too strewn
With petty cares to slightly understand
What awful brain compels His awful hand.
Yet do I marvel at this curious thing:
To make a poet black, and bid him sing!

Public domain (published 1925). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter. The formal perfection of the sonnet — one of Western civilization's most prestigious forms — is itself part of the poem's argument: a Black poet has mastered it, despite everything.

Themes

The paradox of being called to create beauty within a world that denigrates your existence. The classical references (Tantalus, Sisyphus) place the poem's concerns in the tradition of Western literature while the final couplet asserts that the Black poet's situation is more contradictory than any classical myth.

Historical Context

Published in Color (1925). The poem draws on the tradition of theodicy — attempts to reconcile God's goodness with the existence of suffering — and turns it to the specific suffering of racial oppression.

About Countee Cullen

Countee Cullen was born on May 30, 1903, in New York City, and became one of the leading poets of the Harlem Renaissance, known for fusing traditional European forms — especially the sonnet — with themes of Black identity, heritage, and the double consciousness of being Black in America. He published his first collection, Color, in 1925 when he was twenty-two, and it immediately established him as a major voice. He died in New York in 1946.
