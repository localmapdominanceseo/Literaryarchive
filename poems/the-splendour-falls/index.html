The Splendour Falls

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1847 Published 1847 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1847 as a song within The Princess, this short lyric describes bugle calls echoing across a mountain lake, the echoes dying away — and uses the image to meditate on how the echoes of our lives carry forward after we are gone.

Quick Answer

"The Splendour Falls" is a lyric by Alfred Lord Tennyson, published in 1847. Bugle calls sound across a lake and mountains, echoing in the distance until they die. The speaker calls for the bugles to blow, for the wild echoes to fly, and meditates that our echoes roll from soul to soul, growing forever and forever — carrying us forward after we die.

The splendour falls on castle walls
And snowy summits old in story:
The long light shakes across the lakes,
And the wild cataract leaps in glory.
Blow, bugle, blow, set the wild echoes flying,
Blow, bugle; answer, echoes, dying, dying, dying.

O hark, O hear! how thin and clear,
And thinner, clearer, farther going!
O sweet and far from cliff and scar
The horns of Elfland faintly blowing!
Blow, let us hear the purple glens replying:
Blow, bugle; answer, echoes, dying, dying, dying.

O love, they die in yon rich sky,
They faint on hill or field or river:
Our echoes roll from soul to soul,
And grow for ever and for ever.
Blow, bugle, blow, set the wild echoes flying,
And answer, echoes, answer, dying, dying, dying.

Public domain. Published 1847 in The Princess.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three six-line stanzas with an AABCCB rhyme scheme, building from nature imagery to the philosophical claim of the final stanza. The refrain 'Blow, bugle; answer, echoes, dying, dying, dying' enacts the very echo it describes.

Themes

The persistence of the soul's influence beyond death, figured as an echo that grows rather than fades. The natural echoes of the bugle across the lake become a metaphor for how human lives carry forward in others' memories.

Historical Context

Published as a song within The Princess (1847), Tennyson's narrative poem about a women's university. The song stands independently and is frequently anthologized separately.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
