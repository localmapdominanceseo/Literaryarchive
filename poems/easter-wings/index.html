Easter Wings

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 in The Temple, this pattern poem (a poem whose visual shape on the page enacts its meaning) consists of two stanzas that narrow to a thin line at the center and then expand again — the shape of wings — as the speaker traces humanity's fall and asks to rise with Christ.

Quick Answer

"Easter Wings" is a poem by George Herbert, published in 1633. Printed in lines that narrow to a minimum and then expand again, creating the visual shape of two pairs of wings, the poem traces humanity's fall from God's image into poverty and sin, then asks to rise with Christ like a lark at Easter, becoming most triumphant by being most thin.

Lord, who createdst man in wealth and store,
Though foolishly he lost the same,
Decaying more and more,
Till he became
Most poore:
With thee
O let me rise
As larks, harmoniously,
And sing this day thy victories:
Then shall the fall further the flight in me.

My tender age in sorrow did beginne:
And still with sicknesses and shame
Thou didst so punish sinne,
That I became
Most thinne.
With thee
Let me combine
And feel thy victorie:
For, if I imp my wing on thine,
Affliction shall advance the flight in me.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two ten-line stanzas whose lines decrease in syllable count from ten to two at the center, then expand back to ten — creating the visual shape of wings. Herbert often used shaped or pattern poetry to make the form embody the content.

Themes

The Christian narrative of fall and redemption applied personally: the speaker moves from the original richness of creation through loss and sin to the hope of rising with Christ. The most striking paradox is 'let me combine / With thee, and thy victory' — the speaker becomes most triumphant precisely at the moment of greatest weakness.

Historical Context

Published in The Temple (1633), Herbert's posthumous collection. The poem is one of the earliest and most famous examples of shape poetry in English, and Herbert was among the first English poets to use the visual arrangement of words on the page as a formal element.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
