Tell Me Not Here

by A.E. Housman

Written 1922 Published 1922 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in Last Poems (1922), this poem tells nature not to speak to the speaker — she does not love him, she does not remember him, and when he is gone she will not miss him.

Quick Answer

Tell Me Not Here is a poem by A.E. Housman, published in 1922. Tell me not here, it needs not saying, what tune the enchantress plays. She and I, we two together, in sun and wind and weather are strangers yet. She does not love me and does not miss me. I shall sleep and not return.

Tell me not here, it needs not saying,
What tune the enchantress plays
In aftermaths of soft September
Or under blanching mays,
For she and I were long acquainted
And I knew all her ways.

On russet floors, by waters idle,
The pine lets fall its cone;
The cuckoo shouts all day at nothing
In leafy dells alone;
And traveller's joy beguiles in autumn
Hearts that have lost their own.

On acres of the seeded grasses
The changing burnish heaves;
Or marshalled under moons of harvest
Stand still all night the sheaves;
Or beeches strip in storms for winter
And stain the wind with leaves.

Possess, as I possessed a season,
The countries I resign,
Where over elmy plains the highway
Would mount the hills and shine,
And full of shade the pillared forest
Would murmur and be mine.

For nature, heartless, witless nature,
Will neither care nor know
What stranger's feet may find the meadow
And trespass there and go,
Nor ask amid the dews of morning
If they are mine or no.

Public domain. Published 1922 in Last Poems.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five six-line stanzas in alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABABCC rhyme.

Themes

The indifference of nature to human beings — Housman refuses the Romantic consolation that nature sympathizes with the human soul. Nature is beautiful but utterly indifferent.

Historical Context

Published in Last Poems (1922).

About A.E. Housman

Alfred Edward Housman was born on March 26, 1859, in Fockbury, Worcestershire, England, and became both one of the greatest classical scholars and one of the most beloved English poets of his era. A Shropshire Lad (1896), published at his own expense after being rejected by publishers, became one of the most popular poetry collections ever published in English, carried in the pockets of thousands of soldiers in World War I. He died in Cambridge in 1936.
