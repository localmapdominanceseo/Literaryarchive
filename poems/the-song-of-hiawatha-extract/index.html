The Song of Hiawatha (Extract)

by Henry Wadsworth Longfellow

Written 1855 Published 1855 American Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1855, The Song of Hiawatha is Longfellow's epic poem drawing on Native American legends, written in the trochaic tetrameter of the Finnish Kalevala. The opening invocation asks the reader to listen to the story of Hiawatha.

Quick Answer

"The Song of Hiawatha" is Longfellow's 1855 epic poem based on Native American legends, especially those of the Ojibwe people. The opening invocation, in the poem's distinctive trochaic tetrameter, introduces Hiawatha and asks if the reader has heard the story of the hero of the people.

Should you ask me, whence these stories?
Whence these legends and traditions,
With the odors of the forest,
With the dew and damp of meadows,
With the curling smoke of wigwams,
With the rushing of great rivers,
With their frequent repetitions,
And their wild reverberations,
As of thunder in the mountains?
I should answer, I should tell you,
'From the forests and the prairies,
From the great lakes of the Northland,
From the land of the Ojibways,
From the land of the Dacotahs,
From the mountains, moors, and fen-lands
Where the heron, the Shuh-shuh-gah,
Feeds among the reeds and rushes.
I repeat them as I heard them
From the lips of Nawadaha,
The musician, the sweet singer.'

Public domain. Published 1855.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Unrhymed trochaic tetrameter (strong-weak stress repeated four times per line), the meter borrowed from the Finnish epic Kalevala, which Longfellow admired and used as a model for creating an American heroic tradition.

Themes

The creation of an American mythology and the noble representation of Native American culture and legend. The poem attempts to treat Native American legends with the same grandeur as classical epics.

Historical Context

Published in 1855, it was immediately popular but later criticized for romanticizing and oversimplifying Native American culture. The trochaic meter was so distinctive it spawned Longfellow parodies, most famously Lewis Carroll's Hiawatha's Photographing.

About Henry Wadsworth Longfellow

Henry Wadsworth Longfellow was born on February 27, 1807, in Portland, Maine, and became the most popular and widely read American poet of the nineteenth century. His accessible style, traditional meters, and frequent use of historical and legendary American subject matter, as in 'Paul Revere's Ride' and The Song of Hiawatha, made his work a fixture of American education for generations. He taught modern languages at Harvard for many years and died in Cambridge, Massachusetts, in 1882.
