A Hymn to God the Father

by John Donne

Written 1623 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1623 during a severe illness and published posthumously in 1633, this hymn confesses sin after sin to God and asks: when I have done, wilt thou have done? The answer: when the Son who shone is at the right hand, then the speaker has done, and Donne fears no more.

Quick Answer

"A Hymn to God the Father" is a poem by John Donne, written during his illness in 1623 and published posthumously in 1633. It confesses sins one by one and asks God: have you forgiven all this? When Donne is done confessing, has God done with forgiving? The climax is a pun on Donne's own name: when he has the Son who shone, he shall fear no more darkness — and then God has Donne.

Wilt thou forgive that sin where I begun,
Which is my sin, though it were done before?
Wilt thou forgive that sin through which I run,
And do run still, though still I do deplore?
When thou hast done, thou hast not done,
For I have more.

Wilt thou forgive that sin which I have won
Others to sin, and made my sin their door?
Wilt thou forgive that sin which I did shun
A year or two, but wallowed in a score?
When thou hast done, thou hast not done,
For I have more.

I have a sin of fear, that when I have spun
My last thread, I shall perish on the shore;
But swear by thyself that at my death thy son
Shall shine as he shines now, and heretofore;
And having done that, thou hast done;
I fear no more.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three six-line stanzas with ABABCC rhyme, each stanza ending with the refrain 'Thou hast not done, / For I have more' — which shifts in the final stanza to 'I fear no more.'

Themes

The anxiety about sin and forgiveness, the fear of dying without being forgiven, resolved by the pun on the Son/sun — Christ as the light that dispels the fear of darkness and death. Donne's characteristic wit is deployed in the service of genuine piety.

Historical Context

Donne wrote the hymn in 1623 during the dangerous illness he described in Devotions Upon Emergent Occasions. He set it to music himself. Published posthumously in 1633.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
