Ode to Evening

by William Collins

Written 1746 Published 1746 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1746, this ode addresses Evening as a goddess, asking her to teach the poet to breathe in her calm — describing the gradual darkening of the sky, the bat's flight, the beetle's drone, and the quiet that falls before night.

Quick Answer

"Ode to Evening" is a poem by William Collins, published in 1746. It addresses Evening as 'meek-eyed' and 'chaste,' asking her to teach the poet a softer strain before the bat and the beetle emerge. The poem traces the evening from the last glimmer of light to the coming of Night herself.

If aught of oaten stop, or pastoral song,
May hope, chaste Eve, to soothe thy modest ear,
Like thy own solemn springs,
Thy springs and dying gales,
O nymph reserved, while now the bright-haired sun
Sits in yon western tent, whose cloudy skirts,
With brede ethereal wove,
O'erhang his wavy bed:
Now air is hushed, save where the weak-eyed bat
With short shrill shriek flits by on leathern wing,
Or where the beetle winds
His small but sullen horn,
As oft he rises 'midst the twilight path,
Against the pilgrim borne in heedless hum:
Now teach me, maid composed,
To breathe some softened strain,
Whose numbers, stealing through thy darkening vale,
May not unseemly with its stillness suit,
As, musing slow, I hail
Thy genial loved return!

Analysis

Form and Structure

Unrhymed stanzas in iambic tetrameter and dimeter, the lack of rhyme unusual for mid-eighteenth-century verse and contributing to the poem's calm, flowing quality.

Themes

The beauty and calm of evening as a setting for poetic inspiration, and nature's gradual transition from day to night. Collins personifies Evening as a gentle goddess whose lesson is quietness and receptiveness.

Historical Context

Published in 1746 in Odes on Several Descriptive and Allegoric Subjects, a collection largely ignored at the time but recognized by later Romantic poets as anticipating their own concerns.

About William Collins

William Collins was born on December 25, 1721, in Chichester, England, and published his Odes on Several Descriptive and Allegoric Subjects in 1746 — a collection largely ignored at the time but recognized by later Romantic poets as anticipating their own sensibility. He suffered from severe mental illness in his later years and died in 1759 at age thirty-seven, leaving very little work behind.
