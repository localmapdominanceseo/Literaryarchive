Anecdote of the Jar

by Wallace Stevens

Written 1919 Published 1919 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1919 and collected in Harmonium (1923), this twelve-line poem describes placing a jar on a hill in Tennessee, and watching it become the organizing center of the wilderness around it — yet remaining unlike nature, grey and bare, giving nothing of itself.

Quick Answer

"Anecdote of the Jar" is a poem by Wallace Stevens, published in 1919. The speaker places a jar on a hill in Tennessee, and the wilderness, formerly slovenly, arranges itself around the jar as around a center. The jar dominates the surrounding landscape yet is unlike anything in nature — it takes dominion everywhere, but gives nothing back.

I placed a jar in Tennessee,
And round it was, upon a hill.
It made the slovenly wilderness
Surround that hill.

The wilderness rose up to it,
And sprawled around, no longer wild.
The jar was round upon the ground
And tall and of a port in air.

It took dominion everywhere.
The jar was gray and bare.
It did not give of bird or bush,
Like nothing else in Tennessee.

Public domain (published 1919). Text per Poetry Foundation.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains of loose iambic tetrameter with an irregular rhyme scheme, deceptively simple in structure for a poem with complex implications.

Themes

The relationship between art and nature, human order and wild disorder. The jar imposes form on the formless wilderness but is itself sterile — it gives nothing back, produces nothing, stands only as an organizing principle. The poem explores whether art enriches or merely dominates its surroundings.

Historical Context

Published in Poetry magazine in 1919 and collected in Harmonium (1923). The poem has generated extensive critical debate about whether the jar represents art, civilization, or human consciousness imposing order on nature.

About Wallace Stevens

Wallace Stevens was born on October 2, 1879, in Reading, Pennsylvania, and spent most of his career as a lawyer and insurance executive while producing some of the most philosophically ambitious poetry of the American twentieth century. He published his first major collection, Harmonium, in 1923, and won the Pulitzer Prize for poetry in 1955, the year of his death in Hartford, Connecticut.
