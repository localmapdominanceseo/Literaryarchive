La Belle Dame sans Merci

by John Keats

Written 1819 Published 1820 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written in April 1819 and first published in 1820, this ballad describes a knight-at-arms found alone on a cold hillside. He tells of meeting a beautiful fairy woman who enchanted him, brought him to her cave, and then vanished, leaving him with only a dream of pale kings and warriors who warn that she has enthralled him.

Quick Answer

"La Belle Dame sans Merci" is a ballad by John Keats, written in 1819 and published in 1820. A knight tells of meeting a beautiful fairy woman who enchanted and seduced him, took him to her elfin grot, and then left him waking alone on a cold hillside, dreaming of pale kings and warriors who cry that she — the beautiful lady without mercy — has enslaved them all.

O what can ail thee, knight-at-arms,
Alone and palely loitering?
The sedge has withered from the lake,
And no birds sing.

O what can ail thee, knight-at-arms,
So haggard and so woe-begone?
The squirrel's granary is full,
And the harvest's done.

I see a lily on thy brow,
With anguish moist and fever-dew,
And on thy cheeks a fading rose
Fast withereth too.

I met a lady in the meads,
Full beautiful—a faery's child,
Her hair was long, her foot was light,
And her eyes were wild.

I made a garland for her head,
And bracelets too, and fragrant zone;
She looked at me as she did love,
And made sweet moan.

I set her on my pacing steed,
And nothing else saw all day long,
For sidelong would she bend, and sing
A faery's song.

She found me roots of relish sweet,
And honey wild, and manna-dew,
And sure in language strange she said—
'I love thee true'.

She took me to her elfin grot,
And there she wept and sighed full sore,
And there I shut her wild wild eyes
With kisses four.

And there she lulled me asleep
And there I dreamed—Ah! woe betide!—
The latest dream I ever dreamt
On the cold hill side.

I saw pale kings and princes too,
Pale warriors, death-pale were they all;
They cried—'La Belle Dame sans Merci
Thee hath in thrall!'

I saw their starved lips in the gloam,
With horrid warning gaped wide,
And I awoke and found me here,
On the cold hill's side.

And this is why I sojourn here
Alone and palely loitering,
Though the sedge is withered from the lake,
And no birds sing.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twelve quatrains in a modified ballad form, with lines of alternating tetrameter and trimeter, and an ABCB rhyme scheme. Keats shortens the fourth line of each stanza to a haunting three syllables, giving the poem a halting, incomplete quality.

Themes

Fatal enchantment and the destruction wrought by beauty without mercy. The fairy woman may represent unattainable ideal beauty, artistic obsession, the femme fatale figure, or tuberculosis itself (the 'pale warriors' with their 'starved lips' have been read as consumptive patients). The poem refuses to explain itself or offer resolution, ending where it began on the cold hillside.

Historical Context

Keats wrote the poem in April 1819, during his most productive period. It was first published in the journal The Indicator in May 1820. Keats based the title on a medieval French poem by Alain Chartier, though his poem bears little resemblance to its source beyond the title's image of a merciless beautiful woman.

About John Keats

John Keats was born on October 31, 1795, in London, England, and died of tuberculosis at just twenty-five, yet in a creative burst during 1819 alone produced some of the most celebrated odes in the English language, including 'Ode to a Nightingale' and 'Ode on a Grecian Urn.' His poetry is known for its rich sensory detail and its preoccupation with beauty, mortality, and the relationship between art and permanence. He died in Rome in 1821.
