Hymn of Pan

by Percy Bysshe Shelley

Written 1820 Published 1820 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1820, this lyric is spoken by Pan, who describes his music-making on his pipe — liquid Peneus was flowing, and the stars, the mountains, the sea listened. Apollo's song silenced them; but Pan played on.

Quick Answer

"Hymn of Pan" is a poem by Shelley, published in 1820. Pan sings of his music-making on his pipe of reed by the flowing Peneus. The stars, the sea, and the mountains listened. Then Apollo himself sang — and the stars and the mountains and the sea were silent. Yet Pan plays on, and his music brings grief as well as joy.

From the forests and highlands
We come, we come;
From the river-girt islands,
Where loud waves are dumb
Listening to my sweet pipings.
The wind in the reeds and the rushes,
The bees on the bells of thyme,
The birds on the myrtle bushes,
The cicale above in the lime,
And the lizards below in the grass,
Were as silent as ever old Tmolus was,
Listening to my sweet pipings.

Liquid Peneus was flowing,
And all dark Tempe lay
In Pelion's shadow, outgrowing
The light of the dying day,
Speedèd by my sweet pipings.
The Sileni, and Sylvans, and Fauns,
And the Nymphs of the woods and the waves,
To the edge of the moist river-lawns,
And the brink of the dewy caves,
And all that did then attend and follow,
Were silent with love, as you now, Apollo,
With envy of my sweet pipings.

I sang of the dancing stars,
I sang of the daedal Earth,
And of Heaven—and the giant wars,
And Love, and Death, and Birth,—
And then I changed my pipings,—
Singing how down the vale of Menalus
I pursued a maiden and clasp'd a reed:
Gods and men, we are all deluded thus!
It breaks in our bosom and then we bleed:
All wept, as I think both ye now would,
If envy or age had not frozen your blood,
At the sorrow of my sweet pipings.

Public domain. Published 1820.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three eight-line stanzas in Shelley's characteristic lyric style with complex rhyme.

Themes

The competition between Pan's natural music and Apollo's divine art — and Pan's consolation that his music is real, even if less perfect.

Historical Context

Published in 1820. Written for a competition with Mary Shelley, who also wrote a Hymn of Apollo. Shelley's version is generally considered the finer poem.

About Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley was born on August 4, 1792, in Horsham, England, and became one of the major poets of English Romanticism despite controversy during his lifetime over his radical political and religious views. His poetry combines lyrical intensity with sustained engagement with politics, philosophy, and the natural world. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy in 1822, at the age of twenty-nine.
