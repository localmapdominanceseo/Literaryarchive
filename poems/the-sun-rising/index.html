The Sun Rising

by John Donne

Written 1600 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1600 and published posthumously in 1633, this playful aubade addresses the rising sun as a 'busy old fool' and argues that the sun should not disturb the lovers in bed — because their bedroom contains all the world, rendering the sun's circuits of earth unnecessary.

Quick Answer

"The Sun Rising" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker addresses the sun as a meddlesome intruder disturbing his and his lover's morning in bed. He argues that princes and kingdoms — everything the sun illuminates in its daily circuit of the earth — are mere copies of his beloved, while all wealth is concentrated in her. Since the sun's duty is to warm the world, and the world is in this bed, the sun can complete its entire job by simply shining here.

Busy old fool, unruly sun,
Why dost thou thus,
Through windows, and through curtains, call on us?
Must to thy motions lovers' seasons run?
Saucy pedantic wretch, go chide
Late schoolboys and sour prentices,
Go tell court-huntsmen that the king will ride,
Call country ants to harvest offices;
Love, all alike, no season knows nor clime,
Nor hours, days, months, which are the rags of time.

Thy beams, so reverend and strong
Why shouldst thou think?
I could eclipse and cloud them with a wink,
But that I would not lose her sight so long;
If her eyes have not blinded thine,
Look, and tomorrow late, tell me,
Whether both th' Indias of spice and mine
Be where thou leftst them, or lie here with me.
Ask for those kings whom thou saw'st yesterday,
And thou shalt hear, 'All here in one bed lay.'

She's all states, and all princes, I,
Nothing else is.
Princes do but play us; compared to this,
All honour's mimic, all wealth alchemy.
Thou, sun, art half as happy as we,
In that the world's contracted thus;
Thine age asks ease, and since thy duties be
To warm the world, that's done in warming us.
Shine here to us, and thou art everywhere;
This bed thy centre is, these walls thy sphere.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three stanzas of ten lines each, opening with short, clipped lines of three syllables ('Busy old fool') before expanding into longer iambic pentameter lines, formally mimicking the speaker's shift from irritable dismissal to expansive argument.

Themes

The absoluteness of love — its power to contain all value, all place, all time. Each stanza escalates the argument: the sun's worldly duties are trivial compared to love; his beloved outshines the treasures of the world; therefore this room is the entire world, and the sun can rest here. The poem is both comic in its audacity and serious in its claim that love supersedes all external authority.

Historical Context

An aubade (a dawn poem, typically a lament at the lovers' necessary parting at sunrise) that subverts the form by refusing to lament — the speaker simply refuses to acknowledge the sun's authority. Published posthumously in 1633 as part of Donne's Songs and Sonnets.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
