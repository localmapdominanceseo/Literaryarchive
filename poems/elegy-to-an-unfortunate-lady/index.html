Elegy to the Memory of an Unfortunate Lady

by Alexander Pope

Written 1717 Published 1717 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1717, this elegy mourns a woman who died by suicide — possibly a real person, possibly fictional — and attacks those who failed to mourn her, celebrating her noble spirit that her circumstances failed to match.

Quick Answer

Pope's "Elegy to the Memory of an Unfortunate Lady" (1717) mourns a woman who took her own life, beginning with the vision of her ghost and attacking the cold family that failed to grieve her. The poem argues that her noble spirit was too great for her circumstances, and that those who outlive her with cold hearts will themselves die unmourned.

What beck'ning ghost, along the moonlight shade
Invites my steps, and points to yonder glade?
'Tis she!—but why that bleeding bosom gor'd,
Why dimly gleams the visionary sword?
Oh ever beauteous, ever friendly! tell,
Is it, in heav'n, a crime to love too well?
To bear too tender, or too firm a heart,
To act a Lover's or a Roman's part?
Is there no bright reversion in the sky,
For those who greatly think, or bravely die?

Why bade ye else, ye Pow'rs! her soul aspire
Above the vulgar flight of low desire?
Ambition first sprung from your blest abodes;
The glorious fault of Angels and of Gods:
Thence to their Images on earth it flows,
And in the breasts of Kings and Heroes glows!
Most souls, 'tis true, but peep out once an age,
Dull sullen pris'ners in the body's cage:
Dim lights of life that burn a length of years,
Useless, unseen, as lamps in sepulchres;
Like Eastern Kings a lazy state they keep,
And close confined to their own palace sleep.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Heroic couplets, eighty-two lines, Pope's characteristic form applied here to genuine lyric feeling rather than satire.

Themes

The injustice of social condemnation of suicide, and the nobility of a spirit that outran its circumstances. Pope is unusual in treating the woman who died by her own hand with sympathy rather than censure.

Historical Context

Published in Pope's Works (1717). The identity of the 'unfortunate lady' has been debated; she may have been a real woman Pope knew, or may be largely fictional.

About Alexander Pope

Alexander Pope was born on May 21, 1688, in London, and became the dominant English poet of the early eighteenth century, celebrated for his mastery of the heroic couplet, his satirical wit, and the technical perfection of his verse. His most celebrated works include The Rape of the Lock (1714), An Essay on Man (1733-1734), and The Dunciad (1728). He died at Twickenham in 1744.
