Deer Park

by Wang Wei

Written 740 Published 740 Tang Dynasty Poetry Public Domain

Written around 740 CE, this is one of Wang Wei's celebrated Wang River poems, describing the empty mountain where no one is seen — only the sound of voices, sunlight entering the deep wood, and shining again on the green moss.

Quick Answer

"Deer Park" is a poem by Wang Wei, written around 740 CE. The empty mountain has no one in sight, yet voices can be heard. Deep in the wood, the late sun enters, shining again on the green moss. In twenty Chinese characters, it captures a quality of emptiness, light, and presence that has made it one of the most discussed poems in Chinese literary history.

Empty mountain, no man is seen,
Yet voices of men are heard.
Returning sunlight enters the deep wood,
And shines again upon the green moss.

Public domain translation. Original c.740 CE by Wang Wei (699-759 CE). Translation is a free rendering in the public domain.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four lines of five characters each in regulated verse (jueju), Wang Wei's characteristic compressed form.

Themes

The paradox of emptiness that contains presence — voices without visible speakers, light that finds green moss in a seemingly uninhabited wood. The poem reflects Wang Wei's Buddhist sensibility: emptiness is not absence but a fullness of a different kind.

Historical Context

Wang Wei (699-759 CE) was a Tang Dynasty poet and painter, a major figure in the literati tradition. The Wang River poems describe his estate and the landscape around it. This poem is often cited as an example of the Chinese poetic ideal of resonance — saying less to mean more.

About Wang Wei

Wang Wei (699-759 CE) was a Tang Dynasty poet, painter, and musician, one of the most admired figures of the Chinese literati tradition. He was also a devout Buddhist, and his poetry is known for its qualities of stillness, emptiness, and subtle revelation. Su Shi later called him 'a poet in his paintings and a painter in his poems.'
