Jerusalem (And Did Those Feet in Ancient Time)

by William Blake

Written 1804 Published 1808 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1804 and published in 1808 as the preface to Blake's prophetic poem Milton, these four quatrains ask whether Jesus walked in ancient England and whether Jerusalem was built there — then call for a militant spiritual renewal to fight against the dark Satanic Mills of industrial England.

Quick Answer

"Jerusalem" (And did those feet in ancient time) is a poem by William Blake, published in 1808. It asks whether the legendary visit of Jesus to England in ancient times is true — whether Jerusalem was actually built on England's green and pleasant land — then calls for the speaker's sword and chariot of fire to fight until that spiritual Jerusalem is built again, fighting against the dark Satanic Mills of industrial England.

And did those feet in ancient time
Walk upon England's mountains green?
And was the holy Lamb of God
On England's pleasant pastures seen?

And did the Countenance Divine
Shine forth upon our clouded hills?
And was Jerusalem builded here
Among these dark Satanic Mills?

Bring me my Bow of burning gold!
Bring me my Arrows of desire!
Bring me my Spear! O clouds, unfold!
Bring me my Chariot of fire!

I will not cease from Mental Fight,
Nor shall my Sword sleep in my hand,
Till we have built Jerusalem
In England's green and pleasant Land.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of loose iambic tetrameter, the compressed power of a hymn. Set to music by Hubert Parry in 1916 and now effectively England's unofficial national anthem.

Themes

Spiritual revolution against industrial and political oppression. The 'dark Satanic Mills' are usually read as the factories of the Industrial Revolution, though some scholars argue Blake meant the mills of rationalist philosophy. The 'mental fight' called for is a spiritual and artistic battle to restore vision against materialism.

Historical Context

Published in 1808 as the preface to Blake's prophetic epic Milton. The poem became famous when Hubert Parry set it to music in 1916 for a patriotic wartime anthology; Elgar later orchestrated Parry's setting. It is now sung at English sporting events, at the Women's Institute, and in churches.

About William Blake

William Blake was born on November 28, 1757, in London, England, and worked as a poet, painter, and printmaker, producing illustrated volumes of poetry that he engraved, printed, and hand-colored himself. Largely unrecognized during his lifetime, he is now considered one of the founding figures of English Romanticism. His best-known collection, Songs of Innocence and of Experience, pairs poems of childlike wonder with darker counterparts exploring suffering and moral complexity. He died in London in 1827.
