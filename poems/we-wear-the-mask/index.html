We Wear the Mask

by Paul Laurence Dunbar

Written 1895 Published 1896 Post-Reconstruction American Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1896 in Dunbar's collection Lyrics of Lowly Life, this poem describes the mask of forced cheerfulness that conceals pain and anger, written from the perspective of African Americans in the post-Reconstruction era but resonant for anyone forced to hide suffering behind a public face.

Quick Answer

"We Wear the Mask" is a poem by Paul Laurence Dunbar, published in 1896. It describes a mask of forced smiles and cheerfulness worn to conceal real pain from an indifferent world. Though the poem never explicitly mentions race, it is rooted in the experience of African Americans in the era following Reconstruction.

We wear the mask that grins and lies,
It hides our cheeks and shades our eyes,—
This debt we pay to human guile;
With torn and bleeding hearts we smile
And mouth with myriad subtleties.

Why should the world be over-wise,
In counting all our tears and sighs?
Nay, let them only see us, while
We wear the mask.

We smile, but oh great Christ, our cries
To thee from tortured souls arise.
We sing, but oh the clay is vile
Beneath our feet, and long the mile,
But let the world dream otherwise,
We wear the mask!

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem is written in a steady, song-like iambic tetrameter across three stanzas, using a refrain ('We wear the mask') that frames the poem the way a chorus frames a song, reinforcing its central image each time it returns.

Themes

The central theme is the gap between a performed public identity and a private interior of suffering. The mask 'grins and lies,' hiding 'torn and bleeding hearts' behind a smile, and the poem argues this performance is a survival strategy rather than genuine contentment — a direct rebuttal to the 'happy' stereotype often used to justify racial oppression in Dunbar's era.

Historical Context

Dunbar published 'We Wear the Mask' in 1896 in Lyrics of Lowly Life, his first major collection, written in the decades following Reconstruction when Black Americans faced intensifying racial violence and Jim Crow segregation despite formal emancipation. Dunbar, one of the first African American poets to gain national recognition, wrote both in standard English, as here, and in African American dialect verse.

About Paul Laurence Dunbar

Paul Laurence Dunbar was born on June 27, 1872, in Dayton, Ohio, the son of formerly enslaved parents, and became one of the first African American poets to achieve national and international recognition. He wrote both in standard English and in African American dialect verse, and his work captured the complexities of Black life in America in the decades following Reconstruction. He died in Dayton in 1906 at age 33.
