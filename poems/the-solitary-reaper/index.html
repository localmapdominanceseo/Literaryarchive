The Solitary Reaper

by William Wordsworth

Written 1805 Published 1807 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written in 1805 and published in 1807, this poem describes a traveler pausing to listen to a young Highland woman singing alone as she works in a field, unable to understand her words yet deeply moved by her voice.

Quick Answer

"The Solitary Reaper" is a poem by William Wordsworth, written in 1805 and published in 1807. A traveler passing through the Scottish Highlands pauses to listen to a young woman singing alone as she cuts grain. He cannot understand her words, speculates about what she might be singing, and carries the music with him in his heart long after he has moved on.

Behold her, single in the field,
Yon solitary Highland Lass!
Reaping and singing by herself;
Stop here, or gently pass!
Alone she cuts and binds the grain,
And sings a melancholy strain;
O listen! for the Vale profound
Is overflowing with the sound.

No Nightingale did ever chaunt
More welcome notes to weary bands
Of travellers in some shady haunt,
Among Arabian sands:
A voice so thrilling ne'er was heard
In spring-time from the Cuckoo-bird,
Breaking the silence of the seas
Among the farthest Hebrides.

Will no one tell me what she sings?--
Perhaps the plaintive numbers flow
For old, unhappy, far-off things,
And battles long ago:
Or is it some more humble lay,
Familiar matter of to-day?
Some natural sorrow, loss, or pain,
That has been, and may be again?

Whate'er the theme, the Maiden sang
As if her song could have no ending;
I saw her singing at her work,
And o'er the sickle bending;--
I listen'd, motionless and still;
And, as I mounted up the hill,
The music in my heart I bore,
Long after it was heard no more.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four eight-line stanzas written in iambic tetrameter with an ABABCCDD rhyme scheme, blending the ballad form (which Wordsworth associated with folk tradition and common speech) with the more literary ode structure.

Themes

The poem explores the power of music and beauty to transcend language and linger beyond the moment of experience. Because the speaker cannot understand the words, the song's emotional content is entirely projected and imagined, suggesting that what moves us in art is often not literal meaning but tone, feeling, and the context of solitary absorption. The image of music carried 'long after it was heard no more' anticipates the Romantic idea of the imagination as a vessel for preserving experience beyond its moment.

Historical Context

Wordsworth wrote the poem based not on his own direct observation but on a passage in his friend Thomas Wilkinson's manuscript Tours to the British Mountains, which described seeing a Highland woman singing at her work. Wordsworth never heard the actual singer himself, a fact that makes the poem's emotional intensity all the more striking. It was published in 1807 in Poems, in Two Volumes.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
