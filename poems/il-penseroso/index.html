Il Penseroso (Extract)

by John Milton

Written 1631 Published 1645 English Renaissance Public Domain

Written around 1631 and published in 1645, Il Penseroso ('The Pensive Man') is a companion poem to L'Allegro ('The Merry Man'). Where L'Allegro celebrates cheerful pleasures, Il Penseroso celebrates the life of contemplation, study, and melancholy solitude. This is the celebrated opening invocation.

Quick Answer

"Il Penseroso" is a poem by John Milton, published in 1645, celebrating the pleasures of melancholy, contemplation, and solitary study. The speaker invokes the goddess Melancholy and describes the pleasures available to the pensive person: night walks, classical music, tragedy, philosophy, and the life of the mind — culminating in the hope to live a life of such depth that old age itself brings prophetic wisdom.

Hence vain deluding joys,
The brood of folly without father bred,
How little you bested,
Or fill the fixed mind with all your toys;
Dwell in some idle brain,
And fancies fond with gaudy shapes possess,
As thick and numberless
As the gay motes that people the sunbeams,
Or likest hovering dreams
The fickle pensioners of Morpheus' train.
But hail thou Goddess, sage and holy,
Hail divinest Melancholy,
Whose saintly visage is too bright
To hit the sense of human sight;
And therefore to our weaker view,
O'erlaid with black, staid Wisdom's hue.
Black, but such as in esteem,
Prince Memnon's sister might beseem,
Or that starred Ethiope queen that strove
To set her beauty's praise above
The Sea Nymphs, and their powers offended.
Yet thou art higher far descended:
Thee bright-haired Vesta long of yore,
To solitary Saturn bore;
His daughter she (in Saturn's reign,
Such mixture was not held a stain).
Oft in glimmering bowers, and glades
He met her, and in secret shades
Of woody Ida's inmost grove,
Whilst yet there was no fear of Jove.
Come pensive nun, devout and pure,
Sober, steadfast, and demure,
All in a robe of darkest grain,
Flowing with majestic train,
And sable stole of cypress lawn,
Over thy decent shoulders drawn.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A long poem in octosyllabic couplets (eight-syllable lines in rhyming pairs), a looser, more flowing form than the sonnets, suited to its subject of contemplative wandering. The opening invocation follows classical epic convention.

Themes

The pleasures and virtues of the contemplative life — night, solitude, reading, classical learning, music, and tragedy — against the trivial pleasures of the social world. Milton argues that deep, sustained attention to serious subjects (epitomized by classical philosophy, Platonic thought, and tragedies like those of Sophocles) is a higher and more lasting pleasure than social entertainment.

Historical Context

Milton wrote both Il Penseroso and L'Allegro while at Cambridge, probably in 1631, as companion exercises. They were published together in his 1645 Poems. The pair represents two temperaments and two modes of life that Milton saw as genuine alternatives, though his own life chose the pensive mode.

About John Milton

John Milton was born on December 9, 1608, in London, England, and became one of the greatest poets in the English language, best known for his epic Paradise Lost (1667). He served as Latin Secretary to Oliver Cromwell's government during the English Interregnum and went completely blind around 1651-1652, composing his greatest works through dictation. He died in London in 1674.
