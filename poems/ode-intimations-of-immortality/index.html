Ode: Intimations of Immortality (Extract)

by William Wordsworth

Written 1802 Published 1807 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written between 1802 and 1804 and published in 1807, this is Wordsworth's most ambitious philosophical poem, arguing that children come into the world trailing clouds of glory from a pre-existent immortal state, and that growing up means a gradual forgetting of that divine origin — though the memory of it never entirely leaves us.

Quick Answer

"Ode: Intimations of Immortality" is a poem by William Wordsworth, published in 1807. It argues that children arrive in the world from a previous immortal existence, still trailing clouds of glory, but that growing up means a progressive dimming of that original light as the world closes in. The poem moves from lamentation of lost visionary childhood to a consoling acceptance that the memory of it survives in adult consciousness.

There was a time when meadow, grove, and stream,
The earth, and every common sight,
To me did seem
Apparelled in celestial light,
The glory and the freshness of a dream.
It is not now as it hath been of yore;—
Turn wheresoe'er I may,
By night or day,
The things which I have seen I now can see no more.

The Rainbow comes and goes,
And lovely is the Rose,
The Moon doth with delight
Look round her when the heavens are bare,
Waters on a starry night
Are beautiful and fair;
The sunshine is a glorious birth;
But yet I know, where'er I go,
That there hath past away a glory from the earth.

Our birth is but a sleep and a forgetting:
The Soul that rises with us, our life's Star,
Hath had elsewhere its setting,
And cometh from afar:
Not in entire forgetfulness,
And not in utter nakedness,
But trailing clouds of glory do we come
From God, who is our home:
Heaven lies about us in our infancy!
Shades of the prison-house begin to close
Upon the growing Boy,
But he beholds the light, and whence it flows,
He sees it in his joy;
The Youth, who daily farther from the east
Must travel, still is Nature's Priest,
And by the vision splendid
Is on his way attended;
At length the Man perceives it die away,
And fade into the light of common day.

What though the radiance which was once so bright
Be now for ever taken from my sight,
Though nothing can bring back the hour
Of splendour in the grass, of glory in the flower;
We will grieve not, rather find
Strength in what remains behind;
In the primal sympathy
Which having been must ever be;
In the soothing thoughts that spring
Out of human suffering;
In the faith that looks through death,
In years that bring the philosophic mind.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eleven irregular stanzas of varying length and rhyme, the formal variety mirroring the poem's movement through different emotional registers — grief, questioning, consolation — over its composition across several years.

Themes

The Platonic idea of pre-existence and the loss of visionary power through growing up. The 'celestial light' that the child sees naturally dims as adult habit and worldly experience accumulate, but the poem's resolution is that the memory of childhood vision — 'the fountain light of all our day' — remains as a resource and consolation.

Historical Context

Wordsworth began the poem in 1802 after writing the first four stanzas, then set it aside and completed it in 1804. It was published in 1807 in Poems in Two Volumes. Coleridge wrote 'Dejection: An Ode' as a partial response.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
