The Second Coming

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1919 Published 1920 Modernism

"The Second Coming" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, written in 1919 and first published in 1920. It describes a falcon spinning out of its falconer's control as a metaphor for the collapse of social and moral order after World War I, then envisions a frightening 'rough beast' arriving in place of the Christian Second Coming, suggesting a new and more violent age is about to begin.

Summary

The poem opens with a falcon flying in an ever-widening spiral so large that it can no longer hear the falconer calling it back, an image of a world that has spun beyond anyone's ability to control it. Yeats then catalogs the breakdown that follows: 'things fall apart,' anarchy is loosed, and a 'blood-dimmed tide' overtakes the world while 'the best lack all conviction' and 'the worst are full of passionate intensity.' Sensing that some revelation is at hand, the speaker invokes the Christian idea of the Second Coming, only for the vision that actually arrives to be something far stranger and more menacing: a sphinx-like creature with 'a gaze blank and pitiless as the sun,' stirring from its long sleep in the desert. The poem closes by asking what 'rough beast' is finally being born, suggesting that the new era arriving will be defined by violence rather than salvation.

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem is written in a loose, irregular blank verse across two unequal stanzas, breaking from the more tightly patterned forms Yeats often used elsewhere. The absence of a strict rhyme scheme or meter mirrors the poem's subject: a world whose structures are no longer holding.

Themes

The poem's central theme is the cyclical collapse and rebirth of civilizations, reflecting Yeats's personal theory of history as a series of interlocking 'gyres,' or spiraling cycles, that he detailed in his prose work A Vision. Rather than offering hope, the poem inverts the Christian promise of a redemptive Second Coming, replacing it with the arrival of something ominous and inhuman, reflecting Yeats's anxiety that the postwar world was entering an age of disorder rather than renewal.

Historical Context

Yeats wrote the poem in January 1919, in the immediate aftermath of World War I and amid the outbreak of the Irish War of Independence, while his pregnant wife was recovering from the 1918-1919 influenza pandemic. It was first published in the magazine The Dial in November 1920 and collected the following year in Michael Robartes and the Dancer. Its imagery of disintegration has since been invoked repeatedly during later periods of political and social crisis.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
