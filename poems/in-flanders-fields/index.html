In Flanders Fields

by John McCrae

Written 1915 Published 1915 World War I Poetry Public Domain

Written on May 3, 1915, by a Canadian military physician during the Second Battle of Ypres, and published in Punch magazine in December 1915, this poem is spoken by soldiers buried in Flanders (Belgium), asking the living to take up their torch and keep faith with their sacrifice.

Quick Answer

"In Flanders Fields" is a poem by John McCrae, written in May 1915 and published in December 1915. It is spoken from the perspective of soldiers killed in the Battle of Ypres, whose graves are marked by poppies in the Belgian fields. They ask the living to take up the quarrel against the enemy and carry on — or else they will not sleep, even in death.

In Flanders fields the poppies blow
Between the crosses, row on row,
That mark our place; and in the sky
The larks, still bravely singing, fly
Scarce heard amid the guns below.

We are the Dead. Short days ago
We lived, felt dawn, saw sunset glow,
Loved and were loved, and now we lie,
In Flanders fields.

Take up our quarrel with the foe:
To you from failing hands we throw
The torch; be yours to hold it high.
If ye break faith with us who die
We shall not sleep, though poppies grow
In Flanders fields.

Public domain. Published December 1915 in Punch.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A rondeau — a French poetic form — with fifteen lines in three stanzas of five, four, and six lines respectively. The opening two lines return as a refrain, giving the poem its circular, haunting quality.

Themes

The obligation of the living to honor the dead by continuing their cause. The poppies, which grew in disturbed soil over graves and shell craters, became the central symbol of World War I remembrance after this poem. The tone is not triumphalist but urgent — the dead soldiers speak with quiet authority, not celebration.

Historical Context

McCrae wrote the poem on May 3, 1915, the day after the death of his friend Lieutenant Alexis Helmer at Ypres. He reportedly wrote it in twenty minutes during a battle, then threw it away, before a fellow officer retrieved it. Published anonymously in Punch in December 1915, it became the most famous poem of World War I and inspired the use of the red poppy as a symbol of remembrance.

About John McCrae

John McCrae was born on November 30, 1872, in Guelph, Ontario, Canada, and became both a military physician and a poet. He served in the Second Boer War and then in World War I, where he wrote 'In Flanders Fields' on May 3, 1915, during the Second Battle of Ypres, the day after the death of a close friend. The poem became the most famous of all World War I poems and established the red poppy as the enduring symbol of remembrance. McCrae died of pneumonia in January 1918, less than a year before the war's end.
