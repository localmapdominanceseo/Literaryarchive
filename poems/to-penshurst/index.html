To Penshurst

by Ben Jonson

Written 1616 Published 1616 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1616 in The Forest, this country house poem praises Penshurst — the Sidney family estate — not for its proud towers or marble stairs but for its ancient oak, its fish and game offered freely to guests, its hospitality, its honest lord and lady.

Quick Answer

"To Penshurst" is a poem by Ben Jonson, published in 1616. Penshurst is praised not for its proud architecture but for its ancient oak, its fertile grounds that offer themselves to their lord, the Medway river that bends to provide fish, the peasants who bring their gifts voluntarily. Its lord and lady make all feel at home. This is true hospitality.

Thou art not, Penshurst, built to envious show,
Of touch, or marble; nor canst boast a row
Of polished pillars, or a roof of gold:
Thou hast no lanthorn, whereof tales are told;
Or stair, or courts; but stand'st an ancient pile,
And these grudged at, art reverenced the while.
Thou joy'st in better marks, of soil, of air,
Of wood, of water: therein thou art fair.
Thou hast thy walks for health, as well as sport:
Thy Mount, to which the Dryads do resort,
Where Pan, and Bacchus their high feasts have made,
Beneath the broad beech, and the chestnut shade;
That taller tree, which of a nut was set
At his great birth, where all the Muses met.

Public domain. Published 1616 in The Forest.

Analysis

Form and Structure

One hundred and two lines of iambic pentameter rhyming couplets, the poem establishing the country house poem as a genre in English.

Themes

True hospitality and the harmony between a great estate and the people who work it — Penshurst is praised because it is integrated with its landscape and community, not artificially imposed on it.

Historical Context

Published in The Forest (1616). The first great country house poem in English, establishing a tradition followed by Marvell (Upon Appleton House), Pope, and others. Penshurst was the Sidney family estate in Kent.

About Ben Jonson

Ben Jonson was born on June 11, 1572, in London, and became the foremost dramatist and poet of the Jacobean era, second only to Shakespeare in contemporary reputation. His plays Volpone (1606) and The Alchemist (1610) are among the greatest comedies in English. As a poet, his Epigrams, The Forest, and Under-wood collections display unrivaled technical mastery and classical learning. He died in London in 1637.
