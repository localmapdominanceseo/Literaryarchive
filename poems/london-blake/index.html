London

by William Blake

Written 1794 Published 1794 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published 1794.

Quick Answer

London is a poem published 1794.

I wander thro' each charter'd street,
Near where the charter'd Thames does flow.
And mark in every face I meet
Marks of weakness, marks of woe.

In every cry of every Man,
In every Infant's cry of fear,
In every voice: in every ban,
The mind-forg'd manacles I hear

How the Chimney-sweeper's cry
Every blackning Church appalls;
And the hapless Soldier's sigh
Runs in blood down Palace walls

But most thro' midnight streets I hear
How the youthful Harlot's curse
Blasts the new-born Infant's tear
And blights with plagues the Marriage hearse

About William Blake

William Blake was born on November 28, 1757, in London, England, and worked as a poet, painter, and printmaker, producing illustrated volumes of poetry that he engraved, printed, and hand-colored himself. Largely unrecognized during his lifetime, he is now considered one of the founding figures of English Romanticism. His best-known collection, Songs of Innocence and of Experience, pairs poems of childlike wonder with darker counterparts exploring suffering and moral complexity. He died in London in 1827.
