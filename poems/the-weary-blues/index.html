The Weary Blues

by Langston Hughes

Written 1925 Published 1926 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1926 as the title poem of Hughes's debut collection, this poem describes listening to a blues musician playing on Lenox Avenue in Harlem late at night, his body swaying, his keys moaning, his song about not having nobody and wishing he were dead — before he sleeps like a rock or a man that's dead.

Quick Answer

"The Weary Blues" is the title poem of Langston Hughes's 1926 debut collection. The speaker listens to a blues musician playing on Lenox Avenue in Harlem, his piano rocking back and forth, playing a melancholy tune about loneliness and wishing for death. When his song is done he sleeps like a rock or a man that's dead.

Droning a drowsy syncopated tune,
Rocking back and forth to a mellow croon,
I heard a Negro play.
Down on Lenox Avenue the other night
By the pale dull pallor of an old gas light
He did a lazy sway . . .
He did a lazy sway . . .
To the tune o' those Weary Blues.
With his ebony hands on each ivory key
He made that poor piano moan with melody.
O Blues!
Swaying to and fro on his rickety stool
He played that sad raggy tune like a musical fool.
Sweet Blues!
Coming from a black man's soul.
O Blues!
In a deep song voice with a melancholy tone
I heard that Negro sing, that old piano moan—
'Ain't got nobody in all this world,
Ain't got nobody but ma self.
I's gwine to quit ma frownin'
And put ma troubles on the shelf.'

Thump, thump, thump, went his foot on the floor.
He played a few chords then he sang some more—
'I got the Weary Blues
And I can't be satisfied.
Got the Weary Blues
And can't be satisfied—
I ain't happy no mo'
And I wish that I had died.'
And far into the night he crooned that tune.
The stars went out and so did the moon.
The singer stopped playing and went to bed
While the Weary Blues echoed through his head.
He slept like a rock or a man that's dead.

Public domain (published 1926). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Free verse incorporating the rhythms and language of the blues — the repetitions, the syncopated beat, the direct quotation of the musician's sung words. Hughes was among the first poets to formally integrate blues and jazz structures into verse.

Themes

The blues as simultaneously an expression of suffering and a form of transcendence — the music transforms pain into art, and the musician's performance is both lament and achievement. The closing image of sleep like the dead suggests exhaustion rather than peace.

Historical Context

Hughes won first prize in a contest with this poem in 1925, and it became the title poem of his debut collection The Weary Blues (1926). The poem established his signature approach of incorporating African American musical forms into poetry.

About Langston Hughes

Langston Hughes was born on February 1, 1901, in Joplin, Missouri, and became one of the leading voices of the Harlem Renaissance, the flowering of African American literature, art, and music centered in New York City during the 1920s. Hughes drew on the rhythms of jazz and blues music to develop a distinctive poetic voice that celebrated Black culture and identity while confronting American racism directly. He died in New York City in 1967.
