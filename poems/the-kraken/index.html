The Kraken

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1830 Published 1830 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1830, this sonnet describes the Kraken, the mythological sea monster, sleeping in the abyss beneath the ocean — and prophesying that it will rise only at the last fire, roaring as it dies.

Quick Answer

"The Kraken" is a sonnet by Tennyson, published in 1830. Below the thunders of the upper deep, far far beneath in the abysmal sea, his ancient, dreamless, uninvaded sleep the Kraken sleepeth. He shall rise once — when the latter fire shall heat the deep — then once by man and angels to be seen, he shall die roaring.

Below the thunders of the upper deep;
Far far beneath in the abysmal sea,
His ancient, dreamless, uninvaded sleep
The Kraken sleepeth: faintest sunlights flee
About his shadowy sides; above him swell
Huge sponges of millennial growth and height;
And far away into the sickly light,
From many a wondrous grot and secret cell
Unnumber'd and enormous polypi
Winnow with giant arms the slumbering green.
There hath he lain for ages, and will lie
Battening upon huge sea-worms in his sleep,
Until the latter fire shall heat the deep;
Then once by man and angels to be seen,
In roaring he shall rise and on the surface die.

Public domain. Published 1830.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A fifteen-line sonnet (expanded Petrarchan) in blank verse, one of Tennyson's earliest published poems.

Themes

The deep unconscious powers that sleep beneath the surface of the world, waking only for the apocalypse.

Historical Context

Published in Poems, Chiefly Lyrical (1830). One of Tennyson's earliest published poems, remarkable for its mature power.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
