Vastness

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1885 Published 1885 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1885, Vastness is a meditation on the insignificance of human concerns against the backdrop of cosmic space and time — accumulating examples of human striving and suffering before ending with the one fact that matters: What is it all if we all of us end but in being our own corpse-coffins at last?

Quick Answer

"Vastness" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1885. Twelve stanzas catalog human history, war, religion, love, and social strife — all against the backdrop of cosmic vastness. The poem asks: what is it all? And answers: Peace, let it be! for I loved him, and love him for ever: the dead are not dead but alive.

Many a hearth upon our dark globe sighs after many a vanish'd face,
Many a planet by many a sun may roll with the dust of a vanish'd race.

Raving politics, never at rest—as this poor earth's pale history runs,—
What is it all but a trouble of ants in the gleam of a million million of suns?

Troubles of Nature, disasters of Earth, floods, volcanoes, the fall of man,
Those are the things that trouble me most, were I fashioned to rule and plan.

Stricken in years and half-blinded ere yet I had quitted the stage,
I cry to vacant chairs and fetid air, feeble and age.

Peace, let it be! for I loved him, and love him for ever: the dead are not dead but alive.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twelve stanzas of varying length in loose tetrameter.

Themes

The tension between cosmic nihilism and personal love — the universe is vast and indifferent, but personal love remains real.

Historical Context

Published in 1885 in Tiresias and Other Poems. One of Tennyson's most despairing poems, ending in a personal declaration of love rather than cosmic resolution.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
