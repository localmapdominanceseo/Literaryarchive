The Destruction of Sennacherib

by Lord Byron

Written 1815 Published 1815 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1815 in Hebrew Melodies, this poem retells the biblical story of the Assyrian king Sennacherib's army that came to besiege Jerusalem and was destroyed overnight by an angel of God — rendered in vivid, galloping anapestic tetrameter.

Quick Answer

"The Destruction of Sennacherib" is a poem by Lord Byron, published in 1815. Drawing on the Book of Kings, it describes the Assyrian army coming like a wolf on the fold against Jerusalem, glittering in purple and gold — and then, overnight, being completely destroyed by the angel of the Lord. By morning, the widows and orphans weep and the Gentile unsmote is melted away.

The Assyrian came down like the wolf on the fold,
And his cohorts were gleaming in purple and gold;
And the sheen of their spears was like stars on the sea,
When the blue wave rolls nightly on deep Galilee.

Like the leaves of the forest when Summer is green,
That host with their banners at sunset were seen:
Like the leaves of the forest when Autumn hath blown,
That host on the morrow lay withered and strown.

For the Angel of Death spread his wings on the blast,
And breathed in the face of the foe as he pass'd;
And the eyes of the sleepers wax'd deadly and chill,
And their hearts but once heaved, and for ever grew still!

And there lay the steed with his nostril all wide,
But through it there roll'd not the breath of his pride;
And the foam of his gasping lay white on the turf,
And cold as the spray of the rock-beating surf.

And there lay the rider distorted and pale,
With the dew on his brow, and the rust on his mail:
And the tents were all silent, the banners alone,
The lances unlifted, the trumpet unblown.

And the widows of Ashur are loud in their wail,
And the idols are broke in the temple of Baal;
And the might of the Gentile, unsmote by the sword,
Hath melted like snow in the glance of the Lord!

Public domain. Published 1815 in Hebrew Melodies.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six quatrains in anapestic tetrameter (da-da-DUM da-da-DUM da-da-DUM da-da-DUM), the galloping, unstoppable meter giving the poem a musical drive that mimics the advancing army before the silent catastrophe of the final stanzas.

Themes

Divine power overturning human military might without warning. The poem contrasts the magnificent spectacle of the Assyrian army at its peak with the absolute stillness of its destruction — no battle, no resistance, just overnight annihilation. The meter itself shifts from momentum to silence.

Historical Context

Published in Hebrew Melodies (1815), the same collection as 'She Walks in Beauty,' which Byron wrote as lyrics to be set to Hebrew folk music by the composer Isaac Nathan. The poem draws on 2 Kings 19:35 and Isaiah 37:36.

About Lord Byron

George Gordon Byron, known as Lord Byron, was born on January 22, 1788, in London, and became one of the most famous and controversial figures of English Romanticism, celebrated equally for his poetry and his scandalous personal life. His work ranges from brief, polished lyrics to the sprawling satirical epic Don Juan, and the brooding, charismatic outsider figure he depicted in his writing gave rise to the term 'the Byronic hero.' He died in Greece in 1824 while supporting the Greek War of Independence.
