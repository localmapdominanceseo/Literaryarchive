To Virgil

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1882 Published 1882 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1882, this ode to Virgil was written at the request of the Mantuans to celebrate the nineteenth centenary of Virgil's death. Tennyson hails him as the wielder of the stateliest measure ever moulded by the lips of man.

Quick Answer

"To Virgil" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1882, saluting the Roman poet Virgil as the Roman Virgil, thou that singest Ilion's lofty temples robed in fire. Tennyson hails him as the wielder of the stateliest measure ever moulded by the lips of man, and sees him as a kindred spirit across the centuries.

Roman Virgil, thou that singest
Ilion's lofty temples robed in fire,
Ilion falling, Rome arising,
Wars, and filial faith, and Dido's pyre;

Landscape-lover, lord of language
More than he that sang the Works and Days,
All the chosen coin of fancy
Flashing out from many a golden phrase;

Thou that singest wheat and woodland,
Tilth and vineyard, hive and horse and herd;
All the charm of all the Muses
Often flowering in a lonely word;

Poet of the happy Tityrus
Piping underneath his beechen bowers;
Poet of the poet-satyr
Walking with the one he loved at even hours;

I salute thee, Mantovano,
I that loved thee since my day began,
Wielder of the stateliest measure
Ever moulded by the lips of man.

Public domain. Published 1882.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Nine stanzas of eight lines each in Tennyson's characteristic lyric style.

Themes

Poetic kinship across centuries — Tennyson as Victorian Poet Laureate recognizing Virgil as a literary forefather and fellow craftsman of empire.

Historical Context

Written in 1882 for the nineteenth centenary of Virgil's death, at the request of the city of Mantua, where Virgil was born.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
