Love's Philosophy

by Percy Bysshe Shelley

Written 1819 Published 1820 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1820, this short lyric argues that all nature mingles with itself — mountains with heaven, rivers with ocean, waves with each other — so why will the beloved not mingle her kiss with the speaker's?

Quick Answer

"Love's Philosophy" is a poem by Shelley, published in 1820. The fountains mingle with the river and the rivers with the ocean. The winds of heaven mix together. All things by a law divine mingle in one another. Nothing in the world is single. Why will you not mingle your kiss with mine?

The fountains mingle with the river
And the rivers with the ocean,
The winds of heaven mix for ever
With a sweet emotion;
Nothing in the world is single,
All things by a law divine
In one another's being mingle—
Why not I with thine?

See the mountains kiss high heaven,
And the waves clasp one another;
No sister-flower would be forgiven
If it disdained its brother;
And the sunlight clasps the earth,
And the moonbeams kiss the sea—
What are all these kissings worth,
If thou kiss not me?

Public domain. Published 1820.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two eight-line stanzas of alternating tetrameter and trimeter, the light, musical form matching the lyric's teasing argument.

Themes

The argument from nature to love — everything in nature mingles and unites, so love's union is natural and right. The poem uses natural philosophy as a seduction argument.

Historical Context

Published in The Literary Pocket-Book in 1820. One of Shelley's lightest and most charming short lyrics.

About Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley was born on August 4, 1792, in Horsham, England, and became one of the major poets of English Romanticism despite controversy during his lifetime over his radical political and religious views. His poetry combines lyrical intensity with sustained engagement with politics, philosophy, and the natural world. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy in 1822, at the age of twenty-nine.
