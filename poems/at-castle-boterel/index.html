At Castle Boterel

by Thomas Hardy

Written 1912 Published 1914 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1914, this poem revisits a lane in Cornwall where Hardy and his first wife Emma walked together when they were young — and he sees them still there in his memory, though he knows he will never see this lane again.

Quick Answer

At Castle Boterel is a poem by Thomas Hardy, published in 1914. Hardy drives away from Castle Boterel in the rain and looks back at the lane. He sees, in his mind, two figures — himself and Emma in their youth — climbing the lane together. That brief time had its quality. He knows he will not see this lane again.

As I drive to the junction of lane and highway,
And the drizzle bedrenches the waggonette,
I look behind at the fading byway,
And see on its slope, now glistening wet,
Beckoning from me.

A phantom figure. He turns and looks at me
Across the lane that is bordered by the mead,
And I face it; and I see him and me
As he and I once met
In that hour of long ago.

We did not sit or stand,
But climbed, before we knew,
The lane that leads to the farm and from it I turned me,
And set to walking through the brown browned leaves
With the woman who used to be.

Public domain. Published 1914.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six five-line stanzas of alternating iambic pentameter and trimeter with ABABB rhyme.

Themes

The persistence of the past in memory and place — the lovers' moment in the lane is gone but is somehow permanent in the landscape's memory. Hardy is both consoled and devastated by this.

Historical Context

Part of the Poems of 1912-13. Hardy revisited the Cornwall places associated with his courtship of Emma after her death.

About Thomas Hardy

Thomas Hardy was born on June 2, 1840, in Higher Bockhampton, Dorset, England, and became one of the greatest novelists and poets of the Victorian and Edwardian eras. After Jude the Obscure (1895) was attacked for immorality, he stopped writing fiction and devoted himself entirely to poetry, producing eight volumes. His elegies for his first wife Emma, written after her sudden death in 1912, are considered among the finest in the English language. He died in Dorchester in 1928.
