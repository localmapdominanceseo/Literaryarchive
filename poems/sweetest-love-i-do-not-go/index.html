Sweetest Love, I Do Not Go

by John Donne

Written 1601 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1601 and published posthumously in 1633, this poem is addressed to the speaker's wife before a long journey, reassuring her that his absence is not like death — for the sun returns when it goes, and so does he.

Quick Answer

"Sweetest Love, I Do Not Go" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker tells his beloved he does not go for weariness of her, or for any ill intention — the sun goes away every night and returns. He urges her not to waste her sighs and tears, which give death power over him. He will come back.

Sweetest love, I do not go
For weariness of thee,
Nor in hope the world can show
A fitter love for me;
But since that I
Must die at last, 'tis best
To use myself in jest
Thus by feigned deaths to die.

Yesternight the sun went hence,
And yet is here today;
He hath no desire nor sense,
Nor half so short a way:
Then fear not me,
But believe that I shall make
Forthwith journeys more speedy take
Than the sun can make his way.

O how feeble is man's power,
That if good fortune fall,
Cannot add another hour,
Nor a lost hour recall!
But come bad chance,
And we join to it our strength,
And we teach it art and length,
Itself o'er us to advance.

When thou sigh'st, thou sigh'st not wind,
But sigh'st my soul away;
When thou weep'st, unkindly kind,
My life's blood doth decay.
It cannot be
That thou lov'st me as thou say'st,
If in thine my life thou waste,
Thou art the best of me.

Let not thy divining heart
Forethink me any ill;
Destiny may take thy part,
And may thy fears fulfil;
But think that we
Are but turned aside to sleep;
They who one another keep
Alive, ne'er parted be.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Seven eight-line stanzas of alternating tetrameter and trimeter with ABABCDCD rhyme, the gentle, song-like form suited to its reassuring subject.

Themes

The grief of separation and the argument against excessive mourning. The poem is one of Donne's most tender love poems, without the intellectual combativeness of many Songs and Sonnets.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633 in Songs and Sonnets. Believed to have been written for Donne's wife Anne More before one of his journeys to Europe.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
