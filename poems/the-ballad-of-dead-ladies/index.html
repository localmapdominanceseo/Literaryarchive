The Ballad of Dead Ladies

by Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Written 1869 Published 1870 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1870, this is Rossetti's celebrated translation of Villon's Ballade des dames du temps jadis, with its famous refrain: But where are the snows of yester-year?

Quick Answer

The Ballad of Dead Ladies is Rossetti's translation of Villon (1870). Where is Flora the lovely Roman? Where is Thais, Alice, Beatrice? Where is Joan of Arc? But where are the snows of yester-year?

Tell me now in what hidden way is
Lady Flora the lovely Roman?
Where's Hipparchia, and where is Thais,
Neither of them the fairer woman?
Where is Echo, beheld of no man,
Only heard on river and mere,—
She whose beauty was more than human?
But where are the snows of yester-year?

Where's Héloïse, the learned nun,
For whose sake Abeillard, I ween,
Lost manhood and put priesthood on?
And where, I pray you, is the Queen
Who willed that Buridan should steer
Sewed in a sack's mouth down the Seine?
But where are the snows of yester-year?

Prince, ask me not in what region,
Nor Heaven, nor yet in Hell,
But never for this month's reign
Ask me where they are—or dwell.
Where are they now? They were fair,
But gone, like the snows of yester-year.

Public domain. Published 1870. Translation by Rossetti of Villon (c.1461).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four eight-line stanzas with ABABBCBC rhyme and a four-line envoi — the ballade form.

Themes

The vanishing of all beautiful and powerful women — the ubi sunt tradition.

Historical Context

Published in 1870. Rossetti's translation of Villon's most famous poem, which coined the phrase 'the snows of yesteryear.'

About Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Dante Gabriel Rossetti was born on May 12, 1828, in London, and became both a celebrated painter and poet, a central figure in the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. His poems, including the sonnet sequence The House of Life, are among the most elaborately crafted of the Victorian period. He died at Birchington-on-Sea in 1882.
