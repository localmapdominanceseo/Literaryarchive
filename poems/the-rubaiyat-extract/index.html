Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám (Selected Quatrains)

by Omar Khayyám

Written 1120 Published 1859 Persian Classical Poetry Public Domain

The Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám is a collection of Persian quatrains (rubai) by the mathematician and poet Omar Khayyám (1048-1131 CE), first translated into English by Edward FitzGerald in 1859 in a version that became enormously popular. These are among the most famous quatrains from FitzGerald's translation.

Quick Answer

The Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám is a collection of Persian quatrains by Omar Khayyám (1048-1131 CE), made famous in English through Edward FitzGerald's 1859 translation. The quatrains argue for seizing pleasure in the present — a loaf of bread, a jug of wine, a book of verse beside a singing companion in the wilderness — since life is short and the universe indifferent.

Here with a Loaf of Bread beneath the Bough,
A Flask of Wine, a Book of Verse—and Thou
Beside me singing in the Wilderness—
And Wilderness is Paradise enow.

—

The Moving Finger writes; and, having writ,
Moves on: nor all thy Piety nor Wit
Shall lure it back to cancel half a Line,
Nor all thy Tears wash out a Word of it.

—

Ah, make the most of what we yet may spend,
Before we too into the Dust descend;
Dust into Dust, and under Dust, to lie,
Sans Wine, sans Song, sans Singer, and—sans End!

—

Yesterday This Day's Madness did prepare;
To-morrow's Silence, Triumph, or Despair:
Drink! for you know not whence you came, nor why:
Drink! for you know not why you go, nor where.

Public domain. Edward FitzGerald's 1859 translation of the Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám (original c.1120 CE). FitzGerald's translation is in the public domain.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Each rubai is a four-line stanza rhyming AABA, FitzGerald's English version loosely following the Persian form while creating its own rhyming stanzas in iambic pentameter.

Themes

Carpe diem — seize the present pleasure since life is brief and the nature of the universe unknowable. The quatrains range from hedonistic celebration to philosophical resignation to dark fatalism, united by an acceptance that this life is all we can know.

Historical Context

Omar Khayyám (1048-1131 CE) was primarily known as a mathematician and astronomer in his lifetime. FitzGerald's 1859 translation, which took considerable liberties with the original, became one of the most popular poetry collections of the Victorian era.

About Omar Khayyám

Omar Khayyám (1048-1131 CE) was a Persian mathematician, astronomer, and poet. Edward FitzGerald's 1859 English translation of his Rubáiyát made it enormously popular in the Victorian era, capturing Khayyám's philosophical hedonism and his skeptical attitude toward religious certainty.
