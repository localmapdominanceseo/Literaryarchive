The Rose of the World

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1892 Published 1893 Modernism Public Domain

Published 1893.

Quick Answer

The Rose of the World is a poem published 1893.

Who dreamed that beauty passes like a dream?
For these red lips, with all their mournful pride,
Mournful that no new wonder may betide,
Troy passed away in one high funeral gleam,
And Usna's children died.

We and the labouring world are passing by:
Whisper, O Whisper, who but wakes to grieve
Twice over must his well-belovèd weave,
But with the shell of sorrow in his cry.

Before the world was made, or beauty found,
With God on the cloudy summit of desire,
Rose that was her name—O rose of fire!
Where was the weight of that beauty? Therein, be sure,
Night's candles are burnt out; day's love is past.

Public domain. Published 1893.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
