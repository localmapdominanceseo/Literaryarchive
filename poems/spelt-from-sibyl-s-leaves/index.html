Spelt from Sibyl's Leaves

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1884 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1884 and published posthumously in 1918, this is Hopkins's longest sonnet — a terrifying vision of the night draining all colour and distinction from the world, leaving only black and white, damned and saved.

Quick Answer

Spelt from Sibyl's Leaves is a poem by Gerard Manley Hopkins, written in 1884. As the evening world drains of colour, everything is reduced to black and white — self and its thoughts are its own torment. The heart's bones are racked in two, on a rack where self wrings self right.

Earnest, earthless, equal, attuneable, vaulty, voluminous, ... stupendous
Evening strains to be time's vást, womb-of-all, home-of-all, hearse-of-all night.
Her fond yellow hornlight wound to the west, her wild hollow hoarlight hung to the height
Waste; her earliest stars, earl-stars, stárs principal, overbend us,
Fire-féaturing heaven. For earth her being has unbound; her dapple is at end, as-
tray or aswarm, all throughther, in throngs; self ín self steepèd and páshed—qúite
Disremembering, dísmémbering áll now. Heart, you round me right
With: Óur évening is over us; óur night whelms, whelms, ánd will end us.
Only the beak-leaved boughs dragonish damask the tool-smooth bleak light; black,
Ever so black on it. Óur tale, O óur oracle! Lét life, wáned, ah lét life wind
Off hér once skéined stained véined variety upon, áll on twó spools; párt, pen, páck
Now her áll in twó flocks, twó folds—black, white; right, wrong; reckon but, reck but, mind
But thése two; wáre of a wórld where bút these twó tell, each off the óther; of a rack
Where, selfwrung, selfstrung, sheathe- and shelterless, thoughts against thoughts in groans grínd.

Public domain. Written 1884, published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

An extended sonnet of eight-stress lines — Hopkins's most ambitious formal experiment, each line carrying enormous weight.

Themes

The apocalyptic stripping away of all the particularity Hopkins celebrated in Pied Beauty, leaving only the terrible binary of damnation and salvation.

Historical Context

Written in 1884 during Hopkins's difficult years in Dublin. Published posthumously in 1918. Hopkins called it the most highly wrought of all his sonnets.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
