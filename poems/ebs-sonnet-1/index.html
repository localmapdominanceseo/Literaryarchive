Sonnets from the Portuguese: Sonnet 1

by Elizabeth Barrett Browning

Written 1845 Published 1850 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1850 as the opening sonnet of Sonnets from the Portuguese, this poem describes Death drawing the speaker backward — until a voice calls her name, and she turns to see not Death but Love.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 1 from Sonnets from the Portuguese by Elizabeth Barrett Browning (1850). The speaker is drawn backward by Death when a voice calls her name. She turns and sees — not Death but Love, standing with his eyes of flame.

I thought once how Theocritus had sung
Of the sweet years, the dear and wished-for years,
Who each one in a gracious hand appears
To bear a gift for mortals, old or young:
And, as I mused it in his antique tongue,
I saw, in gradual vision through my tears,
The sweet, sad years, the melancholy years,
Those of my own life, who by turns had flung
A shadow across me. Straightway I was 'ware,
So weeping, how a mystic Shape did move
Behind me, and drew me backward by the hair,
And a voice said in mastery, while I strove,—
'Guess now who holds thee?'—'Death,' I said. But, there,
The silver answer rang,—'Not Death, but Love.'

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter.

Themes

Love as a rescue from death and despair — love arrived unexpectedly and redirected the speaker toward life.

Historical Context

Published in Sonnets from the Portuguese (1850).

About Elizabeth Barrett Browning

Elizabeth Barrett Browning was born on March 6, 1806, in County Durham, England, and became one of the most celebrated poets of the Victorian era. Largely confined to her room by chronic illness, she corresponded with Robert Browning, who eventually persuaded her to elope with him to Italy in 1846. Her Sonnets from the Portuguese (1850), written during their courtship, is one of the great love sequences in English. Aurora Leigh (1856), her verse novel, was enormously influential on later women writers. She died in Florence in 1861.
