In the Valley of Cauteretz

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1861 Published 1864 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1864, this short poem was written when Tennyson revisited the valley in the Pyrenees where he had walked with Arthur Hallam thirty years earlier. The stream's voice is the same; everything else has changed.

Quick Answer

"In the Valley of Cauteretz" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1864. Returning to the Pyrenean valley where he walked with Arthur Hallam thirty years ago, the speaker hears the stream and finds all along the valley where he walked, the voice of the stream rang out: thy living voice to me was as the voice of the dead. And all along the valley, by rock and cave and tree, the voice of the dead was a living voice to me.

All along the valley, stream that flashest white,
Deepening thy voice with the deepening of the night,
All along the valley, where thy waters flow,
I walk'd with one I loved two and thirty years ago.
All along the valley, while I walk'd to-day,
The two and thirty years were a mist that rolls away;
For all along the valley, down thy rocky bed,
Thy living voice to me was as the voice of the dead,
And all along the valley, by rock and cave and tree,
The voice of the dead was a living voice to me.

Public domain. Published 1864.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Fourteen lines of blank verse, the poem's brevity matching the shock of memory.

Themes

The persistence of the dead in the living world — the stream sounds exactly as it did when Hallam was alive, making the dead more present than the living.

Historical Context

Published in 1864. Tennyson revisited the Pyrenees in 1861, thirty years after his 1830 journey there with Hallam.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
