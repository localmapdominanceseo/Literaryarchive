Loveliest of Trees, the Cherry Now

by A.E. Housman

Written 1896 Published 1896 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1896 in A Shropshire Lad, this poem describes the cherry tree in blossom and argues that since the speaker has only fifty years left of spring, and twenty have already passed, he had better go look at the cherry blossom now.

Quick Answer

Loveliest of Trees is a poem by A.E. Housman, published in 1896. The cherry now is hung with bloom along the bough. The speaker has only fifty years to see spring — twenty are gone and thirty remain. That's not enough. He must go look at the cherry hung with snow.

Loveliest of trees, the cherry now
Is hung with bloom along the bough,
And stands about the woodland ride
Wearing white for Eastertide.

Now, of my threescore years and ten,
Twenty will not come again,
And take from seventy springs a score,
It only leaves me fifty more.

And since to look at things in bloom
Fifty springs are little room,
About the woodlands I will go
To see the cherry hung with snow.

Public domain. Published 1896 in A Shropshire Lad.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains of iambic tetrameter with AABB rhyme, the simplicity of the form matching the directness of the argument.

Themes

Carpe diem — the urgency of appreciating beauty because life is short. The 'threescore years and ten' of the Bible minus twenty gives thirty springs left, which is not enough.

Historical Context

Published in A Shropshire Lad (1896), poem II of the sequence. One of the most anthologized poems in English.

About A.E. Housman

Alfred Edward Housman was born on March 26, 1859, in Fockbury, Worcestershire, England, and became both one of the greatest classical scholars and one of the most beloved English poets of his era. A Shropshire Lad (1896), published at his own expense after being rejected by publishers, became one of the most popular poetry collections ever published in English. He died in Cambridge in 1936.
