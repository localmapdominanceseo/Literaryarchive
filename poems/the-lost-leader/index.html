The Lost Leader

by Robert Browning

Written 1845 Published 1845 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1845 in Dramatic Romances and Lyrics, this short poem attacks a leader who abandoned his progressive cause for 'a handful of silver' and 'a riband to stick in his coat' — widely believed to be aimed at Wordsworth, who became Poet Laureate and a conservative.

Quick Answer

"The Lost Leader" is a poem by Robert Browning, published in 1845. A leader who once marched at the head of the progressive cause has left for a handful of silver and a riband to stick in his coat. Just for that the speaker and his companions will never forgive him — not even when he is dead and gone.

Just for a handful of silver he left us,
Just for a riband to stick in his coat—
Found the one gift of which fortune bereft us,
Lost all the others she lets us devote;
They, with the gold to give, doled him out silver,
So much was theirs who so little allowed:
How all our copper had gone for his service!
Rags—were they purple, his heart had been proud!
We that had loved him so, followed him, honoured him,
Lived in his mild and magnificent eye,
Learned his great language, caught his clear accents,
Made him our pattern to live and to die!
Shakespeare was of us, Milton was for us,
Burns, Shelley, were with us—they watch from their graves!
He alone breaks from the van and the freemen,
—He alone sinks to the rear and the slaves!

Public domain. Published 1845.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three stanzas of eight lines each in loose anapestic tetrameter, the driving rhythm giving the poem its sense of betrayed energy.

Themes

Political betrayal — the rage of those left behind by a leader who was once one of them and chose material comfort and establishment recognition over principle.

Historical Context

Published in Dramatic Romances and Lyrics (1845). Browning later confirmed it was aimed at Wordsworth, though he softened his position in later years. Wordsworth accepted the Poet Laureateship in 1843 after decades of conservative drift.

About Robert Browning

Robert Browning was born on May 7, 1812, in Camberwell, London, and became one of the foremost Victorian poets, celebrated above all for his mastery of the dramatic monologue — a form in which a speaker inadvertently reveals more about themselves than they intend. His collection Men and Women (1855) and his verse novel The Ring and the Book (1868-1869) are considered his greatest achievements. He married Elizabeth Barrett in 1846 and they lived in Italy until her death in 1861. He died in Venice in 1889.
