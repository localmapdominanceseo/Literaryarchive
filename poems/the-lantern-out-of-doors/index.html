The Lantern out of Doors

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1877 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1877 and published posthumously in 1918, this poem watches lantern-light fading down the road and reflects on how we lose sight of the people we glimpse and admire — though Christ minds them, Christ follows them.

Quick Answer

The Lantern out of Doors is a poem by Hopkins, published posthumously in 1918. Sometimes a lantern moves along the night — men with bright mien, eyes that watch bright things passing, tall or low — till death or distance buys them quite. Christ minds. Christ follows. He cares for them, eye after them, foot following them.

Sometimes a lantern moves along the night,
That interests our eyes. And who goes there?
I think; where from and bound, I wonder, where,
With, all down darkness wide, his wading light?

Men go by me whom either beauty bright
In mould or mind or what not else makes rare:
They rain against our much-thick and marsh air
Rich beams, till death or distance buys them quite.

Death or distance soon consumes them: wind
What most I may eye after, be in at the end
I cannot, and out of sight is out of mind.

Christ minds; Christ's interest, what to avow or amend
There, éyes them, heart wánts, care haúnts, foot fóllows kínd,
Their ránsom, théir rescue, ánd first, fást, last friénd.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter.

Themes

The pastoral care of Christ for individuals who pass briefly through our lives and then are lost — Christ keeps track of them when we cannot.

Historical Context

Written in 1877 during Hopkins's time at St Beuno's College. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
