Love (III)

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

The final poem in The Temple (1633), this short dialogue between the soul and Love (personified as God) shows the soul, dusty and guilty, refusing to enter the feast — and Love gently insisting, taking the soul's hand and serving it.

Quick Answer

"Love (III)" is the final poem of George Herbert's The Temple (1633). Love (God) bids the soul welcome to the feast, but the soul draws back, feeling unworthy. Love insists: it made the soul's eyes, it bore the blame for the soul's sin. 'You must sit down,' says Love, 'and taste my meat.' So the soul sits and eats.

Love bade me welcome: yet my soul drew back,
Guiltie of dust and sinne.
But quick-ey'd Love, observing me grow slack
From my first entrance in,
Drew nearer to me, sweetly questioning,
If I lack'd any thing.

A guest, I answer'd, worthy to be here:
Love said, You shall be he.
I the unkinde, ungratefull? Ah my deare,
I cannot look on thee.
Love took my hand, and smiling did reply,
Who made the eyes but I?

Truth Lord, but I have marr'd them: let my shame
Go where it doth deserve.
And know you not, sayes Love, who bore the blame?
My deare, then I will serve.
You must sit down, sayes Love, and taste my meat:
So I did sit and eat.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three six-line stanzas of alternating iambic pentameter and trimeter with ABABCC rhyme, the gentle back-and-forth of dialogue enacted in the stanza's short and long lines.

Themes

Divine grace meeting human unworthiness — the soul's shame and withdrawal, and Love's patient insistence on welcome. The poem ends not with theological argument but with the simple act of sitting and eating together.

Historical Context

Deliberately placed last in The Temple, this poem is Herbert's final word — the resolution of all the spiritual struggle in the collection in a quiet scene of grace. T.S. Eliot called it one of the finest poems in the English language.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
