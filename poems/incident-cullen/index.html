Incident

by Countee Cullen

Written 1924 Published 1925 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1925 in Color, this short three-stanza poem describes a Black child visiting Baltimore who sees a white child his age, smiles at him, and receives only a racist slur in return — and remembers nothing else of his months there.

Quick Answer

"Incident" is a poem by Countee Cullen, published in 1925. The speaker remembers visiting Baltimore as a small child, full of Baltimore's wonders. He saw a Baltimorean boy his size, smiled at him, and the boy stuck out his tongue and called him a racist slur. The speaker spent from May to December in Baltimore. Of all the things he saw, he remembers only that.

Once riding in old Baltimore,
Heart-filled, head-filled with glee,
I saw a Baltimorean
Keep looking straight at me.

Now I was eight and very small,
And he was no whit bigger,
And so I smiled, but he poked out
His tongue, and called me, 'Nigger.'

I saw the whole of Baltimore
From May until December;
Of all the things that happened there
That's all that I remember.

Public domain (published 1925). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains of alternating tetrameter and trimeter with ABCB rhyme — a light, simple ballad form that makes the contrast between form and content all the more devastating.

Themes

The way a single act of racism can permanently color an entire experience. The speaker spent months in Baltimore but the slur is all that survives in memory. The poem's formal lightness makes the casual, childlike cruelty of the incident more disturbing, not less.

Historical Context

Published in Color (1925), when Cullen was twenty-two. The poem is one of the most frequently anthologized Harlem Renaissance poems for its controlled, devastating understatement.

About Countee Cullen

Countee Cullen was born on May 30, 1903, in New York City, and became one of the leading poets of the Harlem Renaissance, known for fusing traditional European forms — especially the sonnet — with themes of Black identity, heritage, and the double consciousness of being Black in America. He published his first collection, Color, in 1925 when he was twenty-two, and it immediately established him as a major voice. He died in New York in 1946.
