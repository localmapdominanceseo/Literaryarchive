If Poisonous Minerals

by John Donne

Written 1609 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1633, this Holy Sonnet (IX) argues that since goats and serpents cannot be damned for their nature, why should the speaker be, when his sin comes from the same natural inheritance? God should pity or forgive, not condemn.

Quick Answer

Holy Sonnet IX by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633, argues by analogy: if poisonous minerals, lecherous goats, and venomous serpents are not damned for their nature, why should the speaker's sins, inherited from Adam, be grounds for damnation? He implores God to remember he is made of the same earth, or to forgive him as God forgot his sins.

If poisonous minerals, and if that tree
Whose fruit threw death on else immortal us,
If lecherous goats, if serpents envious
Cannot be damned, alas! why should I be?
Why should intent or reason, born in me,
Make sins, else equal, in me more heinous?
And mercy being easy, and glorious
To God; in his stern wrath, why threatens he?
But who am I, that dare dispute with thee
O God? Oh! of thine only worthy blood,
And my tears, make a heavenly Lethean flood,
And drown in it my sins' black memory.
That thou remember them, some claim as debt;
I think it mercy if thou wilt forget.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter, the argument from natural analogy building through the octave to the sestet's personal plea.

Themes

The problem of original sin and divine justice — if creatures without reason cannot be condemned for their nature, why should humans inherited sin condemn them? The poem ends with the desperate but confident appeal to divine mercy.

Historical Context

Part of Donne's Holy Sonnets (c.1609-1617), published posthumously in 1633.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
