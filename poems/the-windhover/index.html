The Windhover

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1877 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written on May 30, 1877, and published posthumously in 1918, this is Hopkins's most celebrated poem, dedicated 'To Christ our Lord,' describing the flight of a kestrel (windhover) and finding in its mastery an image of Christ's own beauty and power.

Quick Answer

"The Windhover" is a poem by Gerard Manley Hopkins, written in 1877 and published posthumously in 1918. The speaker watches a kestrel riding the morning air with mastery and beauty, and is overwhelmed — he calls its mastery a billion times more lovely than its purely physical form, because it reflects the beauty of Christ. Even humble, broken things (an ember, a ploughed furrow) can reveal divine brilliance through their very brokenness.

I caught this morning morning's minion, king-
dom of daylight's dauphin, dapple-dawn-drawn Falcon, in his riding
Of the rolling level underneath him steady air, and striding
High there, how he rung upon the rein of a wimpling wing
In his ecstasy! then off, off forth on swing,
As a skate's heel sweeps smooth on a bow-bend: the hurl and gliding
Rebuffed the big wind. My heart in hiding
Stirred for a bird,—the achieve of; the mastery of the thing!

Brute beauty and valour and act, oh, air, pride, plume, here
Buckle! AND the fire that breaks from thee then, a billion
Times told lovelier, more dangerous, O my chevalier!

No wonder of it: shéer plód makes plough down sillion
Shine, and blue-bleak embers, ah my dear,
Fall, gall themselves, and gash gold-vermillion.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in Hopkins's sprung rhythm, with the sestet introducing the 'Brute beauty and valour and act, oh, air, pride, plume, here / Buckle!' — one of the most debated lines in Victorian poetry, where 'Buckle' may mean collapse or clasp or both.

Themes

The beauty of mastery — physical, natural, and divine — and the way that even humble broken things (a blue ember revealing gold as it falls, a plough shining through the soil it cuts) reveal hidden brilliance. Hopkins sees the kestrel's flight as an image of Christ's own mastery and self-sacrifice.

Historical Context

Hopkins wrote the poem at St. Beuno's College in Wales in May 1877, during his training as a Jesuit priest. He called it 'the best thing I ever wrote.' Published posthumously by Robert Bridges in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
