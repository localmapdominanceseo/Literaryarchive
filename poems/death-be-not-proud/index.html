Death, Be Not Proud (Holy Sonnet X)

by John Donne

Written 1609 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1609 and first published posthumously in 1633, this sonnet directly addresses Death as a person and argues, point by point, that it has no real power and is, in fact, a slave to other forces.

Quick Answer

"Death, Be Not Proud" is a sonnet by John Donne, written around 1609 and published in 1633, two years after his death. The speaker personifies and directly addresses Death, arguing that it has no real power over people since it is merely a brief sleep before an eternal awakening, and concluding with the paradox that Death itself will one day die.

Death, be not proud, though some have called thee
Mighty and dreadful, for thou art not so;
For those whom thou think'st thou dost overthrow
Die not, poor death, nor yet canst thou kill me.
From rest and sleep, which but thy pictures be,
Much pleasure; then from thee much more must flow,
And soonest our best men with thee do go,
Rest of their bones, and soul's delivery.
Thou art slave to fate, chance, kings, and desperate men,
And dost with poison, war, and sickness dwell,
And poppy or charms can make us sleep as well
And better than thy stroke; why swell'st thou then?
One short sleep past, we wake eternally,
And death shall be no more; death, thou shalt die.

Public domain (composed c. 1609, first published 1633). Text per multiple independent academic sources.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A fourteen-line sonnet in iambic pentameter, blending a Petrarchan octave (ABBA ABBA) with a modified sestet ending in a couplet (CDDC EE), part of Donne's sequence of nineteen Holy Sonnets, also called Divine Meditations.

Themes

The powerlessness of death, argued through Donne's characteristic metaphysical conceit: a logical, almost legal case built point by point. Death is compared to rest and sleep, dismissed as a 'slave to Fate, Chance, kings, and desperate men,' and ultimately said to die itself once humanity 'wakes eternally,' reflecting Christian belief in resurrection and eternal life.

Historical Context

Donne likely wrote the poem between February and August 1609, during a period of religious reflection. It circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first printed in 1633, two years after his death, by which point Donne had become Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London and was known for his sermons on mortality.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
