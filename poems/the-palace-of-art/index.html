The Palace of Art (Extract)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1832 Published 1832 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1832, The Palace of Art tells the story of a Soul that builds herself a palace of art and retires there to live alone, worshipping beauty — and is eventually driven mad by her isolation and emptiness, crying out to be taken back to the world of humanity.

Quick Answer

The Palace of Art (1832) is Tennyson's poem about a Soul that builds a palace of art and beauty to live in alone. She reigns for three years in solitary magnificence — then is struck by horror and loneliness. Her palace becomes a hell. She cries to be returned to a cottage in the vale of humility, to wait until she has learned mercy.

I built my soul a lordly pleasure-house,
Wherein at ease for aye to dwell.
I said, 'O Soul, make merry and carouse,
Dear soul, for all is well.'

A huge crag-platform, smooth as burnish'd brass,
I chose. The ranged ramparts bright
From level meadow-bases of deep grass
Suddenly scaled the light.

Thereon I built it firm. Of ledge or shelf
The rock rose clear, or winding stair.
My soul would live alone unto herself
In her high palace there.

And 'while the world runs round and round,' I said,
'Reign thou apart, a quiet king,
Still as, while Saturn whirls, his steadfast shade
Sleeps on his luminous ring.'

Analysis

Form and Structure

Stanzas of four iambic pentameter lines with ABAB rhyme.

Themes

The corruption of art divorced from human sympathy — the aesthete's palace of beauty becomes a prison and a hell when it excludes the human world.

Historical Context

Published in 1832. One of Tennyson's most explicit statements about the relationship between art and society — art must serve the common life, not flee from it.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
