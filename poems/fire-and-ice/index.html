Fire and Ice

by Robert Frost

Written 1920 Published 1920 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1920 in Harper's Magazine, this nine-line poem considers two ways the world might end — in fire (desire) or ice (hate) — and concludes that either would suffice.

Quick Answer

"Fire and Ice" is a poem by Robert Frost, published in 1920. In nine lines, the speaker considers whether the world will end in fire or ice, relates fire to desire (which he knows from experience would be enough) and ice to hate (which he's also seen enough of), and concludes dryly that for destruction, ice would also suffice.

Some say the world will end in fire,
Some say in ice.
From what I've tasted of desire
I hold with those who favor fire.
But if it had to perish twice,
I think I know enough of hate
To say that for destruction ice
Is also great
And would suffice.

Public domain (published 1920). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Nine lines with a distinctive rhyme scheme (ABAABCBCB) that creates a circular, interlocking pattern. The poem is deceptively light in tone for its apocalyptic subject.

Themes

Human passion (fire/desire) and human cruelty (ice/hate) as equally sufficient agents of world-ending destruction. The poem's dry, almost comic understatement — 'I think I know enough of hate / To say that for destruction ice / Is also great / And would suffice' — gives the apocalyptic subject its characteristic Frost treatment.

Historical Context

Published in Harper's Magazine in December 1920 and collected in New Hampshire (1923). Frost is said to have written it partly in response to astronomer Harlow Shapley's description of two possible ends of the solar system: engulfed by the sun or frozen in space.

About Robert Frost

Robert Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California, though he is most closely associated with rural New England, where he lived and farmed for much of his life. His poetry uses plain, conversational language and traditional verse forms to explore deceptively simple rural scenes that often open onto deeper questions about choice, isolation, and mortality. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry and remains one of the most widely read American poets of the twentieth century. He died in Boston in 1963.
