The Tuft of Flowers

by Robert Frost

Written 1906 Published 1915 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1915 in A Boy's Will, this poem describes a man who goes to turn a field after another has mowed it and finds the mower has left a tuft of wildflowers uncut — understanding that even in solitary work, we are united by our common love of beauty.

Quick Answer

"The Tuft of Flowers" is a poem by Robert Frost, published in 1915. A man goes to turn a field of mown grass and finds a tuft of flowers left standing by the mower who worked alone before him. The butterfly leads him to it. Understanding that the mower spared the flowers from sheer love of them, the speaker realizes: 'Men work together, whether they work together or apart.'

I went to turn the grass once after one
Who mowed it in the dew before the sun.

The dew was gone that made his blade so keen
Before I came to view the levelled scene.

I looked for him behind an isle of trees;
I listened for his whetstone on the breeze.

But he had gone his way, the grass all mown,
And I must be, as he had been,—alone,

'As all must be,' I said within my heart,
'Whether they work together or apart.'

But as I said it, swift there passed me by
On noiseless wing a bewildered butterfly,

Seeking with memories grown dim o'er night
Some resting flower of yesterday's delight.

And once I marked his flight go round and round,
As where some flower lay withering on the ground.

And then he flew as far as eye could see,
And then on tremulous wing came back to me.

I thought of questions that have no reply,
And would have turned to toss the grass to dry;

But he turned first, and led my eye to look
At a tall tuft of flowers beside a brook,

A leaping tongue of bloom the scythe had spared
Beside a reedy brook the scythe had bared.

The mower in the dew had loved them thus,
By leaving them to flourish, not for us,

Nor yet to draw one thought of ours to him.
But from sheer morning gladness at the brim.

The butterfly and I had lit upon,
Nevertheless, a message from the dawn,

That made me hear the wakening birds around,
And hear his long scythe whispering to the ground,

And feel a spirit kindred to my own;
So that henceforth I worked no more alone;

But glad with him, I worked as with his aid,
And weary, sought at noon with him the shade;

And dreaming, as it were, held brotherly speech
With one whose thought I had not hoped to reach.

'Men work together,' I told him from the heart,
'Whether they work together or apart.'

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-four lines of rhyming couplets in loose iambic pentameter, the paired lines enacting the poem's theme of unseen connection.

Themes

The invisible bonds of shared sensibility that connect people even in solitary work. The mower and the speaker never meet, but the mower's act of sparing the flowers creates a connection across time.

Historical Context

Published in A Boy's Will (1913, UK; 1915 US). One of Frost's earliest and most optimistic poems, contrasting with the more ambiguous solitude of his later work.

About Robert Frost

Robert Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California, though he is most closely associated with rural New England, where he lived and farmed for much of his life. His poetry uses plain, conversational language and traditional verse forms to explore deceptively simple rural scenes that often open onto deeper questions about choice, isolation, and mortality. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry and remains one of the most widely read American poets of the twentieth century. He died in Boston in 1963.
