Stars, I Have Seen Them Fall

by A.E. Housman

Written 1896 Published 1896 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in A Shropshire Lad (1896), this short poem observes that stars fall and waters flow away and the soul of man is made like water — this is the way of all things, and it will outlast the speaker.

Quick Answer

Stars, I Have Seen Them Fall is a poem by A.E. Housman, published in 1896. Stars have I seen fall but they do not fall far. Winds blow over the water and cease. There was I, but I am not. The way of the world is this.

Stars, I have seen them fall,
But when they drop and die
No star is lost at all
From all the star-sown sky.
The toil of all that be
Helps not the primal fault;
It rains into the sea,
And still the sea is salt.

Public domain. Published 1896 in A Shropshire Lad.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two four-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter.

Themes

The consolation of cosmic perspective — individual lives end but the patterns of nature continue. The poem is as close to peace as Housman gets.

Historical Context

Published in A Shropshire Lad (1896).

About A.E. Housman

Alfred Edward Housman was born on March 26, 1859, in Fockbury, Worcestershire, England, and became both one of the greatest classical scholars and one of the most beloved English poets of his era. A Shropshire Lad (1896), published at his own expense after being rejected by publishers, became one of the most popular poetry collections ever published in English, carried in the pockets of thousands of soldiers in World War I. He died in Cambridge in 1936.
