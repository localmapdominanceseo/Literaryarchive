Sonnet 1 (From Fairest Creatures We Desire Increase)

by William Shakespeare

Written 1595 Published 1609 English Renaissance Public Domain

The opening sonnet of Shakespeare's 1609 sequence, this poem launches the 'procreation sonnets' (1-17) by urging the beautiful young man to have children so his beauty will survive his death, rather than being buried with him when he dies.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 1 opens Shakespeare's 1609 sonnet sequence by urging a beautiful young man to reproduce, so that his beauty can survive his death. The world desires increase from beautiful creatures, but this young man, 'contracted to his own bright eyes,' feeds only on himself, wasting what should be shared with the world.

From fairest creatures we desire increase,
That thereby beauty's rose might never die,
But as the riper should by time decease,
His tender heir might bear his memory:
But thou, contracted to thine own bright eyes,
Feed'st thy light's flame with self-substantial fuel,
Making a famine where abundance lies,
Thyself thy foe, to thy sweet self too cruel.
Thou that art now the world's fresh ornament,
And only herald to the gaudy spring,
Within thine own bud buriest thy content,
And, tender churl, mak'st waste in niggarding.
Pity the world, or else this glutton be,
To eat the world's due, by the grave and thee.

Public domain. Text per the Folger Shakespeare Library.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. The first of seventeen 'procreation sonnets' urging the young man to marry and have children.

Themes

The tension between self-love and the obligation to perpetuate beauty. The young man's beauty is compared to a rose that should bear new roses before it dies — but instead he hoards himself, a 'beauteous niggard' wasting his inheritance on himself alone.

Historical Context

The opening sonnet of the 1609 quarto, setting up the extended argument of sonnets 1-17 that the young man should reproduce. After sonnet 17, the sequence shifts to arguing that poetry, not children, will be his immortality.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
