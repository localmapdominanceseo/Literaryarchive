Amoretti: Sonnet 75 (One Day I Wrote Her Name)

by Edmund Spenser

Written 1594 Published 1595 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1595 in the sonnet sequence Amoretti, this sonnet shows the speaker writing his beloved's name on the strand, only for the waves to wash it away — until he argues that their love, enshrined in verse, will live in eternal fame when both are dead.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 75 from Spenser's Amoretti sequence (1595) describes writing the beloved's name on the sand, only for the waves to wash it away. The beloved says all things must die. The speaker replies: their verse will immortalize her name even after both are dead.

One day I wrote her name upon the strand,
But came the waves and washèd it away;
Agayne I wrote it with a second hand,
But came the tyde, and made my paynes his pray.
'Vayne man,' sayd she, 'that doest in vaine assay,
A mortall thing so to immortalize,
For I my selve shall lyke to this decay,
And eek my name bee wypèd out lykewize.'
'Not so,' (quod I) 'let baser things devize
To dy in dust, but you shall live by fame:
My verse your vertues rare shall eternize,
And in the heavens wryte your glorious name.
Where whenas death shall all the world subdew,
Our love shall live, and later life renew.'

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Spenserian sonnet — four interlocking quatrains (ABAB BCBC CDCD EE), Spenser's own invention, with an interlocking rhyme scheme that differs from both Petrarchan and Shakespearean forms.

Themes

The immortalizing power of poetry against the erasure of time, personified in the waves washing away the name written in sand. Spenser's response to the beloved's fatalism is to insist that verse outlasts mortality.

Historical Context

Published in Amoretti (1595), a sequence of 89 sonnets addressed to Elizabeth Boyle, whom Spenser married in 1594. Sonnet 75 is the most anthologized of the sequence.

About Edmund Spenser

Edmund Spenser was born around 1552 in London and became one of the greatest poets of the English Renaissance, best known for his unfinished epic allegory The Faerie Queene (1590-1596), dedicated to Queen Elizabeth I. His sonnet sequence Amoretti (1595) and his wedding poem Epithalamion (1595) are his most personal works. He died in London in 1599.
