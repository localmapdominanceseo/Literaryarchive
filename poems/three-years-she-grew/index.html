Three Years She Grew

by William Wordsworth

Written 1799 Published 1800 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1800 in Lyrical Ballads, this Lucy poem has Nature speak — claiming Lucy as her own, promising to make her beautiful, then leaving the speaker alone when she dies.

Quick Answer

Three Years She Grew is a poem by Wordsworth, published in 1800. Nature speaks and says she will take Lucy for her own — she will shape and mould the girl with the law of grace and the law of gravity. She will make Lucy more beautiful than any human care could make her. Then Lucy died — and the poet is left in solitude.

Three years she grew in sun and shower,
Then Nature said, 'A lovelier flower
On earth was never sown;
This Child I to myself will take;
She shall be mine, and I will make
A Lady of my own.

'Myself will to my darling be
Both law and impulse: and with me
The Girl, in rock and plain,
In earth and heaven, in glade and bower,
Shall feel an overseeing power
To kindle or restrain.

'She shall be sportive as the fawn
That wild with glee across the lawn
Or up the mountain springs;
And hers shall be the breathing balm,
And hers the silence and the calm
Of mute insensate things.

Thus Nature spake—The work was done—
How soon my Lucy's race was run!
She died, and left to me
This heath, this calm and quiet scene;
The memory of what has been,
And never more will be.

Public domain. Published 1800 in Lyrical Ballads.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six six-line stanzas of iambic tetrameter with ABABCC rhyme.

Themes

Nature claiming the beloved as her own, developing her to perfection, and then taking her in death — the speaker left desolate.

Historical Context

Published in Lyrical Ballads (1800). Part of the Lucy sequence.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
