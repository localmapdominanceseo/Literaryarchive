Song (Go and Catch a Falling Star)

by John Donne

Written 1601 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1633, this sardonic song catalogues impossible tasks alongside finding a faithful and fair woman — go and catch a falling star, tell me where all past years are, and then find a woman who is true and fair. Even if she were, by the time you got to her she would have turned false.

Quick Answer

"Song (Go and Catch a Falling Star)" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. Go and catch a falling star, tell where all past years are, teach a mermaid to sing — and find a woman who is both fair and true. Even if you found one, she would have turned false by the time you arrived. No woman is both fair and true.

Go and catch a falling star,
Get with child a mandrake root,
Tell me where all past years are,
Or who cleft the Devil's foot;
Teach me to hear mermaids singing,
Or to keep off envy's stinging,
And find
What wind
Serves to advance an honest mind.

If thou be'st born to strange sights,
Things invisible to see,
Ride ten thousand days and nights
Till Age snow white hairs on thee;
Thou, when thou return'st, wilt tell me
All strange wonders that befell thee,
And swear
Nowhere
Lives a woman true and fair.

If thou find'st one, let me know;
Such a pilgrimage were sweet.
Yet do not; I would not go,
Though at next door we might meet.
Though she were true when you met her,
And last till you write your letter,
Yet she
Will be
False, ere I come, to two or three.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three nine-line stanzas with complex ABABCCDDD rhyme, the driving, incantatory rhythm of the catalog of impossibilities giving way to the sardonic conclusion.

Themes

A cynical meditation on female fidelity — though the poem is so extreme in its skepticism that it reads as a kind of joke, a performance of misogyny rather than a sincere statement. Scholars debate how seriously to take its cynicism.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633. One of Donne's most frequently anthologized short poems for its memorable opening and its sardonic wit.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
