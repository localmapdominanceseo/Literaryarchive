Youth and Age

by Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Written 1823 Published 1828 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1828, this poem laments the difference between the speaker's youth, when life was full of hope, light, and beauty, and his present age — asking where the life has gone that was once so full.

Quick Answer

"Youth and Age" is a poem by Coleridge, published in 1828. In youth, the speaker sailed and had flowers breathing, and his whole body was one joyance. Now age has come upon him and where is the joy? Life is but thought; so think I will that youth and I are housemates still.

Verse, a breeze mid blossoms straying,
Where Hope clung feeding, like a bee—
Both were mine! Life went a-maying
With Nature, Hope, and Poesy,
When I was young!

When I was young?—Ah, woful When!
Ah! for the change 'twixt Now and Then!
This breathing house not built with hands,
This body that does me grievous wrong,
O'er aery cliffs and glittering sands,
How lightly then it flashed along:—
Like those trim skiffs, unknown of yore,
On winding lakes and rivers wide,
That ask no aid of sail or oar,
That fear no spite of wind or tide!
Nought cared this body for wind or weather
When Youth and I lived in't together.

Public domain. Published 1828.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Irregular stanzas of varying length with complex rhyme, the form loosening as the poem proceeds, enacting the speaker's loosening grip on youthful energy.

Themes

The contrast between remembered youth and present age, and the consolation that thought can still house youth within the aging mind.

Historical Context

Published in 1828 in The Bijou. Written over several years; Coleridge revised it repeatedly. One of his last significant poems.

About Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was born on October 21, 1772, in Ottery St Mary, Devon, England, and became one of the founders of the English Romantic movement alongside his friend William Wordsworth. Together they published Lyrical Ballads in 1798, a collection that launched Romanticism in English literature. Coleridge's own most celebrated contributions to the collection, 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' and 'Kubla Khan,' are among the most distinctive and widely studied poems in the language. He died in Highgate, London, in 1834.
