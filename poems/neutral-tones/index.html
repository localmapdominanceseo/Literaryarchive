Neutral Tones

by Thomas Hardy

Written 1867 Published 1898 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1867 and published in 1898, this poem describes a winter scene by a pond where a love came to its end — the white sun, the grey leaves, the woman's smile that taught the treachery of false love.

Quick Answer

Neutral Tones is a poem by Thomas Hardy, published in 1898. We stood by a pond that winter day, and the sun was white as though chidden of God. Her eyes on me were as eyes that rove over tedious riddles long ago solved. Since then, keen lessons that love deceives have shaped me in keen lessons.

We stood by a pond that winter day,
And the sun was white, as though chidden of God,
And a few leaves lay on the starving sod;
—They had fallen from an ash, and were gray.

Your eyes on me were as eyes that rove
Over tedious riddles of years ago;
And some words played between us to and fro
On which lost the more by our love.

The smile on your mouth was the deadest thing
Alive enough to have strength to die;
And a grin of bitterness swept thereby
Like an ominous bird a-wing....

Since then, keen lessons that love deceives,
And wrings with wrong, have shaped to me
Your face, and the God-curst sun, and a tree,
And a pond edged with grayish leaves.

Public domain. Published 1898.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of iambic tetrameter with ABBA rhyme, the palindrome rhyme scheme enacting the poem's sense of things folding back on themselves.

Themes

The death of love recorded in a bleached winter landscape — the neutral tones of the scene (white sun, grey leaves) matching the drained emotional content of the ending relationship.

Historical Context

Written in 1867, published in Wessex Poems (1898). One of Hardy's finest lyrics and among the best poems about the death of love in English.

About Thomas Hardy

Thomas Hardy was born on June 2, 1840, in Higher Bockhampton, Dorset, England, and became one of the greatest novelists and poets of the Victorian and Edwardian eras. After Jude the Obscure (1895) was attacked for immorality, he stopped writing fiction and devoted himself entirely to poetry, producing eight volumes. His elegies for his first wife Emma, written after her sudden death in 1912, are considered among the finest in the English language. He died in Dorchester in 1928.
