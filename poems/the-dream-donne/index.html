The Dream

by John Donne

Written 1601 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1633, this poem describes the beloved coming to the speaker's bed while he dreams of her — and waking him, which he first resents until he realizes she is better than any dream, and better than faith itself.

Quick Answer

"The Dream" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The beloved wakes the speaker from a dream in which he was dreaming of her. He is first annoyed — a dream of her was as real as she is, since both are too good for reason. But she is even better than faith, being present: a candle in the dark shows us what a torch would show. She may leave, but he will dream of her again.

Dear love, for nothing less than thee
Would I have broke this happy dream;
It was a theme
For reason, much too strong for fantasy;
Therefore thou waked'st me wisely; yet
My dream thou brok'st not, but continued'st it.
Thou art so true, that thoughts of thee suffice,
To make dreams truths, and fables histories;
Enter these arms, for since thou thought'st it best,
Not to dream all my dream, let's act the rest.

As lightning, or a taper's light,
Thine eyes, and not thy noise, waked me;
Yet I thought thee
(For thou lov'st truth) an angel, at first sight;
But when I saw thou saw'st my heart,
And knew'st my thoughts, beyond an angel's art,
When thou knew'st what I dreamt, when thou knew'st when
Excess of joy would wake me, and cam'st then,
I must confess, it could not choose but be
Profane, to think thee any thing but thee.

Coming and staying showed thee, thee,
But rising makes me doubt, that now
Thou art not thou.
That love is weak, where fear's as strong as he;
Tis not all spirit, pure and brave,
If mixture it of fear, shame, honour have.
Perchance as torches, which must ready be,
Men light and put out, so thou deal'st with me;
Thou cam'st to kindle, go'st to come; then I
Will dream that hope again, but else would die.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three ten-line stanzas of iambic pentameter with ABABCCDEED rhyme, the argument moving from complaint to celebration to anticipation.

Themes

The beloved who surpasses even dreams of her, who is more than faith because she is present — and the pleasant prospect of dreaming of her again when she leaves.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633. One of Donne's most charming aubade-adjacent poems.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
