Merlin and the Gleam

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1889 Published 1889 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1889, one of Tennyson's last poems, this allegorical lyric traces the poet's career through Merlin's pursuit of the Gleam — the light of poetic inspiration — across many landscapes and through many dangers, to the final country beyond the horizon.

Quick Answer

"Merlin and the Gleam" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1889. Merlin follows the Gleam — the light of poetic inspiration — through fairy-land, following it through storm and sunshine, through all the changes of life, to the final country beyond the horizon where the spirit awaits.

O young Mariner,
You from the haven
Under the sea-cliff,
You that are watching
The gray Magician
With eyes of wonder,
I am Merlin,
And I am dying,
I am Merlin
Who follow The Gleam.

Mammon led me
Thro' the long valley
Of Mammon, led me
Into the clear sky,
Set me on heights
That no man can climb,
Large the low valley,
Large the high plain,
And I heard him say,
'Follow The Gleam.'

Not of the sunlight,
Not of the moonlight,
Not of the starlight!
O young Mariner,
Down to the haven,
Call your companions,
Launch your vessel,
And crowd your canvas,
And, ere it vanishes
Over the margin,
After it, follow it,
Follow The Gleam.

Public domain. Published 1889.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Ten stanzas of varying length in Tennyson's late lyric style.

Themes

The poet's lifelong pursuit of inspiration and the hope of final arrival at the country of poetry and spirit.

Historical Context

Published in 1889, Tennyson's penultimate collection. One of his most personal statements about his poetic career.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
