Ode to Memory

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1830 Published 1830 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1830, this early ode addresses Memory as an anchor that preserves the speaker's sense of self against the flow of time — return, return, thou wondrous one, and bathe him in thy living flood.

Quick Answer

"Ode to Memory" is an early poem by Tennyson, published in 1830. It addresses Memory as a goddess — the warden of the brain, the mother of the Muses — calling her to return and bathe the speaker in her flood, to anchor him with the images of his childhood home and the landscape of his youth.

Thou who stealest fire,
From the fountains of the past,
To glorify the present; O, haste,
Visit my low desire!
Strengthen me, enlighten me!
I faint in this obscurity,
Thou dewy dawn of memory.

Come not as thou camest of late,
Flinging the gloom of yesternight
On the white day; but robed in soften'd light
Of orient state.
Whilst I mused came Memory with sad eyes,
Holding the folded annals of my youth;
And turning o'er each page with solemn ruth,
She brought it to mine eyes.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five irregular stanzas of varying length in a Pindaric ode structure.

Themes

Memory as a source of identity and stability — the past preserved in memory as the foundation of the self.

Historical Context

Published in Poems, Chiefly Lyrical (1830). One of Tennyson's earliest important poems, showing his characteristic interest in memory as a philosophical theme.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
