By an Evolutionist

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1889 Published 1889 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1889, this poem meditates on the lessons of evolution — the speaker, now old, has struggled all his life to control his animal nature and raise himself toward the divine. Evolution is not just biological but spiritual.

Quick Answer

"By an Evolutionist" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1889. The speaker has lived through the age of evolution and reached old age. He has found that the Darwinian struggle is within the self — the fight to raise the animal to the spiritual. Old and weary, he lies waiting and the heavens open and the earth is young again.

The Lord let the house of a brute to the soul of a man,
And the man said, 'Am I your debtor?'
And the Lord—'Not yet: but make it as clean as you can,
And then I will let you a better.'

If my body come from brutes, tho' somewhat finer than their own,
I am heir, and this my kingdom. Shall the royal voice be mute?
No, but if the rebel subject seek to drag me from the throne,
Hold the sceptre, Human Soul, and rule thy Province of the brute.

I have climb'd to the snows of Age, and I gaze at a field in the Past,
Where I sank with the body at times in the sloughs of a low desire,
But I hear no yelp of the beast, and the Man is quiet at last,
As he stands on the heights of his life with a glimpse of a height that is higher.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five stanzas of four lines each in Tennyson's late lyric style.

Themes

The interpretation of evolution as a spiritual process — the soul evolving from the animal toward the divine through the conscious effort of a lifetime.

Historical Context

Published in 1889. Tennyson's response to Darwin was always to find in evolution a spiritual dimension.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
