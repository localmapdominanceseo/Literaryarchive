The Scholar Gipsy (Extract)

by Matthew Arnold

Written 1853 Published 1853 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1853, this poem is based on a seventeenth-century legend of an Oxford scholar who left his studies to live with the gypsies, waiting for the secret of their art. Arnold uses the legend to mourn the modern divided consciousness and celebrate the scholar's single-minded, uncorrupted purpose.

Quick Answer

"The Scholar Gipsy" is a poem by Matthew Arnold, published in 1853. A scholar left his Oxford studies to live with the gypsies and learn their lore. Arnold sees him still wandering the Oxfordshire countryside, unchanged, uncorrupted by modern doubt. He urges the scholar to fly the contagion of the modern world's divided, sick hurry and sick fatigue.

Go, for they call you, shepherd, from the hill;
Go, shepherd, and untie the wattled cotes!
No longer leave thy wistful flock unfed,
Nor let thy bawling fellows rack their throats,
Nor the cropped herbage shoot another head.
But when the fields are still,
And the tired men and dogs all gone to rest,
And only the white sheep are sometimes seen
Cross and recross the strips of moon-blanched green,
Come, shepherd, and again begin the quest!

Here, where the reaper was at work of late—
In this high field's dark corner, where he leaves
His coat, his basket, and his earthen cruse,
And in the sun all morning binds the sheaves,
Then here, at noon, comes back his stores to use—
Here will I sit and wait,
While to my ear from uplands far away
The bleating of the folded flocks is borne,
With distant cries of reapers in the corn—
All the live murmur of a summer's day.

Public domain. Published 1853.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-five ten-line stanzas in loose iambic pentameter with ABABCCDEED rhyme — the stanza Keats used in 'Ode to a Nightingale.'

Themes

The corruption of modern consciousness by doubt, by 'sick hurry' and 'sick fatigue,' contrasted with the scholar gipsy's single-minded, uncorrupted purpose. The poem is an elegy for an integrated selfhood impossible in the modern age.

Historical Context

Published in Poems: A New Edition (1853). Arnold drew on a passage in Joseph Glanvill's The Vanity of Dogmatizing (1661) for the legend of the scholar who left Oxford for the gypsies.

About Matthew Arnold

Matthew Arnold was born on December 24, 1822, in Laleham, Middlesex, England, the son of the famous headmaster Thomas Arnold of Rugby. He became both a significant poet and the foremost Victorian literary and cultural critic, arguing for culture as 'the best that has been thought and said.' His poetry, including 'Dover Beach' and 'The Scholar Gipsy,' engages directly with the crisis of religious faith in the modern world. He died in Liverpool in 1888.
