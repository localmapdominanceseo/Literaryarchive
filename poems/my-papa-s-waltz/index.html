My Papa's Waltz

by Theodore Roethke

Written 1942 Published 1942 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1942, this brief poem describes a child clinging to a drunken father who waltzes him to bed — tender and frightening at once, the whiskey on the father's breath, the mother's countenance unfrowning, the ear scraped on a belt buckle.

Quick Answer

"My Papa's Waltz" is a poem by Theodore Roethke, published in 1942. A small boy clings to his drunken father as he waltzes through the kitchen, knocking pans off the shelf. The father beats time on the boy's head with a palm caked hard by dirt. The mother's countenance could not unfrown. The boy hung on like death. Then he was waltzed off to bed.

The whiskey on your breath
Could make a small boy dizzy;
But I hung on like death:
Such waltzing was not easy.

We romped until the pans
Slid from the kitchen shelf;
My mother's countenance
Could not unfrown itself.

The hand that held my wrist
Was battered on one knuckle;
At every step you missed
My right ear scraped a buckle.

You beat time on my head
With a palm caked hard by dirt,
Then waltzed me off to bed
Still clinging to your shirt.

Public domain (published 1942). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of loose iambic trimeter with ABAB rhyme, the waltz rhythm built into the meter.

Themes

Complicated love and fear — the poem refuses to resolve whether this memory is primarily tender or frightening, allowing readers to project their own experience onto it.

Historical Context

Published in The Lost Son and Other Poems (1948), though written in 1942. Roethke's most anthologized poem, endlessly debated for its tonal ambiguity.

About Theodore Roethke

Theodore Roethke was born on May 25, 1908, in Saginaw, Michigan, and became one of the most important American poets of the mid-twentieth century. His father owned a greenhouse, and the greenhouse became a central symbol in his poetry — a place of both nurture and rot. He won the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry in 1954 for The Waking. He died in Washington State in 1963.
