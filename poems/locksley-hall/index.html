Locksley Hall (Extract)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1837 Published 1842 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1842, Locksley Hall is a dramatic monologue spoken by a young man revisiting the house where he grew up, nursing a broken heart and alternately raging against the cousin who rejected him for a richer man and celebrating the promise of the future.

Quick Answer

"Locksley Hall" is a dramatic monologue by Tennyson, published in 1842. The speaker revisits Locksley Hall where he fell in love with Amy, who rejected him for a wealthier man. He rages, mourns, and then looks forward — forecasting airplanes, a Parliament of Man, the Federation of the World. For I dipt into the future as far as human eye could see.

Comrades, leave me here a little, while as yet 'tis early morn:
Leave me here, and when you want me, sound upon the bugle-horn.

'Tis the place, and all around it, as of old, the curlews call,
Dreary gleams about the moorland flying over Locksley Hall;

Locksley Hall, that in the distance overlooks the sandy tracts,
And the hollow ocean-ridges roaring into cataracts.

Many a night from yonder ivied casement, ere I went to rest,
Did I look on great Orion sloping slowly to the West.

Many a night I saw the Pleiads, rising thro' the mellow shade,
Glitter like a swarm of fire-flies tangled in a silver braid.

Here about the beach I wander'd, nourishing a youth sublime
With the fairy tales of science, and the long result of Time;

When the centuries behind me like a fruitful land reposed;
When I clung to all the present for the promise that it closed:

When I dipt into the future far as human eye could see;
Saw the Vision of the world, and all the wonder that would be.—

In the Spring a fuller crimson comes upon the robin's breast;
In the Spring the wanton lapwing gets himself another crest;

In the Spring a livelier iris changes on the burnish'd dove;
In the Spring a young man's fancy lightly turns to thoughts of love.

Public domain. Published 1842.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Ninety-seven couplets of trochaic octameter, the driving rhythm giving the poem its emotional momentum.

Themes

The progress of science and democracy versus personal romantic failure — the speaker oscillates between despair at Amy's rejection and visionary excitement about the future of civilization.

Historical Context

Published in 1842. Contains some of Tennyson's most famous futuristic lines, including the vision of aerial warfare and a Parliament of Man.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
