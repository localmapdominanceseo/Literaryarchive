Life is Fine

by Langston Hughes

Written 1949 Published 1949 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1949, this poem is spoken by someone who tried to drown himself, then tried to jump from a sixteen-story building, and in each case was stopped by a physical response — the water was cold, the air was fresh — and decided life is fine after all.

Quick Answer

"Life is Fine" is a poem by Langston Hughes, published in 1949. The speaker tried to drown himself but the water was too cold. He tried to jump from a sixteen-story building but the air was fine and fresh. Both times he turned around. He concludes: since I'm still here, I might as well live. Life is fine.

I went down to the river,
I set down on the bank.
I tried to think but couldn't,
So I jumped in and sank.

I came up once and hollered!
I came up twice and cried!
If that water hadn't a-been so cold
I might've sunk and died.

But it was Cold in that water! It was cold!

I took the elevator
Sixteen floors above the ground.
I thought about my baby
And thought I would jump down.

I stood there and I hollered!
I stood there and I cried!
If it hadn't a-been so high
I might've jumped and died.

But it was High up there! It was high!

So since I'm still here livin',
I guess I will live on.
I could've died for love—
But for livin' I was born.

Though you may hear me holler,
And you may see me cry—
I'll be dogged, sweet baby,
If you gonna see me die.

Life is fine! Fine as wine! Life is fine!

Public domain (published 1949). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six stanzas alternating between the speaker's near-suicide attempts and the refrain affirming life, with the blues-like repetition of 'Fine!' at the end. The poem uses dark subject matter with a comic, resilient voice.

Themes

Resilience and the refusal to be destroyed — the humor of finding life fine precisely because dying is inconvenient. The poem is in Hughes's blues tradition: singing about suffering while affirming survival.

Historical Context

Published in 1949 in One-Way Ticket. The poem's tone is defiantly comic — using near-suicide as a setup for the punchline of choosing life — in the tradition of the blues that finds humor in hardship.

About Langston Hughes

Langston Hughes was born on February 1, 1901, in Joplin, Missouri, and became one of the leading voices of the Harlem Renaissance, the flowering of African American literature, art, and music centered in New York City during the 1920s. Hughes drew on the rhythms of jazz and blues music to develop a distinctive poetic voice that celebrated Black culture and identity while confronting American racism directly. He died in New York City in 1967.
