Early Spring

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1883 Published 1883 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1883, this short lyric celebrates early spring — the primrose and the violet, the lark and the linnet, the blossom of May — with a characteristic touch of elegy: spring comes, but the days of our youth are gone.

Quick Answer

"Early Spring" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1883. The season is fresh and new, the birds are singing, the primrose blossoms — once more the heavenly Power makes all things new. The woods that shivered to the rain are filling again. But the heart still grieves for what it cannot name.

Once more the Heavenly Power
Makes all things new,
And domes the red-plow'd hill
With loving blue;
The blackbirds have their wills,
The throstles too.

Opens a door in Heaven;
From skies of glass
A Jacob's ladder falls
On greening grass,
And o'er the mountain-walls
Young angels pass.

Before them fleets the shower,
And bursts the buds,
And shine the level lands,
And flash the floods;
The stars are from their hands
Flung thro' the woods,

The woods with living airs
How softly fann'd,
Light airs from where the deep,
All down the sand,
Is breathing in his sleep,
Heard by the land.

Public domain. Published 1883.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six stanzas of six lines each in alternating tetrameter and trimeter.

Themes

Spring renewal and the persistence of grief — the world is reborn but the speaker's sense of loss persists beneath the beauty.

Historical Context

Published in 1883 in The Lover's Tale and Other Poems.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
