The Negro Speaks of Rivers

by Langston Hughes

Written 1920 Published 1921 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Written in 1920 when Hughes was seventeen, while crossing the Mississippi River by train, and first published in The Crisis in June 1921, this is considered Hughes's signature poem and one of the foundational texts of the Harlem Renaissance.

Quick Answer

"The Negro Speaks of Rivers" is a poem by Langston Hughes, written in 1920 and first published in 1921. The speaker connects Black history and identity to the world's great rivers, from the Euphrates and the Nile to the Mississippi, asserting that the Black soul has grown deep through thousands of years of accumulated human history.

I've known rivers:
I've known rivers ancient as the world and older than the
flow of human blood in human veins.

My soul has grown deep like the rivers.

I bathed in the Euphrates when dawns were young.
I built my hut near the Congo and it lulled me to sleep.
I looked upon the Nile and raised the pyramids above it.
I heard the singing of the Mississippi when Abe Lincoln
went down to New Orleans, and I've seen its muddy
bosom turn all golden in the sunset.

I've known rivers:
Ancient, dusky rivers.

My soul has grown deep like the rivers.

Public domain (first published in The Crisis, June 1921). Text per multiple independent academic sources.

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem is written in free verse with no fixed rhyme scheme or meter, built instead around the repeated refrain 'I've known rivers' and the closing line 'My soul has grown deep like the rivers,' which frame the poem and give it a psalm-like, incantatory rhythm.

Themes

The poem's central theme is the depth and continuity of Black identity across history, expressed through an extended metaphor linking the speaker's soul to four great rivers spanning ancient Africa, the Middle East, and America. It moves from the dawn of civilization to the Mississippi and the era of Abraham Lincoln, asserting pride in a history that includes both achievement and suffering.

Historical Context

Hughes wrote the poem in about fifteen minutes at age seventeen, while crossing the Mississippi River on a train to visit his father in Mexico. It was first published in The Crisis, the NAACP's magazine, in June 1921, and later collected in his first book, The Weary Blues, in 1926. It is now considered one of the key poems of the Harlem Renaissance.

About Langston Hughes

Langston Hughes was born on February 1, 1901, in Joplin, Missouri, and became one of the leading voices of the Harlem Renaissance, the flowering of African American literature, art, and music centered in New York City during the 1920s. Hughes drew on the rhythms of jazz and blues music to develop a distinctive poetic voice that celebrated Black culture and identity while confronting American racism directly. He died in New York City in 1967.
