To Lucasta, Going to the Wars

by Richard Lovelace

Written 1649 Published 1649 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1649 in Lucasta, this short lyric explains to the poet's beloved why he must leave her to go to war — not inconstancy, but a greater love: of honour, which he loves more dearly than she.

Quick Answer

"To Lucasta, Going to the Wars" is a poem by Richard Lovelace, published in 1649. The speaker tells Lucasta not to think him unkind or inconstant for going to war — he is chasing a new mistress: the armed throngs and a stronger faith and love. He could not love her so much if he loved not honour more.

Tell me not, Sweet, I am unkind,
That from the nunnery
Of thy chaste breast and quiet mind
To war and arms I fly.

True, a new mistress now I chase,
The first foe in the field;
And with a stronger faith embrace
A sword, a horse, a shield.

Yet this inconstancy is such
As you too shall adore;
I could not love thee, Dear, so much,
Lov'd I not Honour more.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains of iambic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme, compressed and balanced, the final couplet clinching the paradox.

Themes

The conflict between love and military honour, resolved by the argument that honour is itself a form of love — that to love honour more than the beloved is what makes a man capable of loving at all.

Historical Context

Published in Lucasta (1649). Lovelace was a Cavalier poet who was imprisoned for his support of the King during the English Civil War. The poem is one of the most concise expressions of the Cavalier code.

About Richard Lovelace

Richard Lovelace was born on December 9, 1617, in Woolwich, England, and became the most celebrated of the Cavalier poets, known for poems of love, loyalty, and honour written during the English Civil War. He was imprisoned twice for his Royalist sympathies, and wrote his two most famous poems — 'To Lucasta, Going to the Wars' and 'To Althea, from Prison' — during these periods of confinement. He died in poverty in London in 1657.
