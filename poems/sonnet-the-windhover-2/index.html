Pied Beauty

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1877 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1877 and published posthumously in 1918, this curtal sonnet gives thanks for dappled things — skies of couple-colour, rose-moles on trout, finches' wings, and all things counter, original, spare, strange. Praise him.

Quick Answer

Pied Beauty is a poem by Gerard Manley Hopkins, published posthumously in 1918. Glory be to God for dappled things — for skies of couple-colour as a brinded cow, for rose-moles all in stipple upon trout that swim, for finches' wings, landscape plotted and pieced. All things counter, original, spare, strange — whatever is fickle, freckled (who knows how?) — He fathers-forth. Praise him.

Glory be to God for dappled things—
  For skies of couple-colour as a brinded cow;
    For rose-moles all in stipple upon trout that swim;
Fresh-firecoal chestnut-falls; finches' wings;
  Landscape plotted and pieced—fold, fallow, and plough;
    And all trades, their gear and tackle and trim.

All things counter, original, spare, strange;
  Whatever is fickle, freckled (who knows how?)
    With swift, slow; sweet, sour; adazzle, dim;
He fathers-forth whose beauty is past change:
                        Praise him.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A curtal sonnet — ten and a half lines in Hopkins's sprung rhythm, a shortened version of the Petrarchan sonnet.

Themes

Celebrating the variety and particularity of natural beauty as evidence of God's creative abundance. 'Counter, original, spare, strange' are all things that resist reduction to the uniform.

Historical Context

Written in 1877 at St Beuno's College, Wales, during Hopkins's training as a Jesuit priest. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
