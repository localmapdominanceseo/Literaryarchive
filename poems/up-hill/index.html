Up-Hill

by Christina Rossetti

Written 1858 Published 1862 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1862, this dialogue poem poses questions about the journey of life — does the road wind up-hill all the way? — and receives answers about the inn at the end where all who have travelled are welcome.

Quick Answer

Up-Hill is a poem by Christina Rossetti, published in 1862. Does the road wind up-hill all the way? Yes, to the very end. Will the day's journey take the whole long day? From morn to night, my friend. Will there be beds for all who come tired? Yes, beds for all who come.

Does the road wind up-hill all the way?
Yes, to the very end.
Will the day's journey take the whole long day?
From morn to night, my friend.

But is there for the night a resting-place?
A roof for when the slow, dark hours begin.
May not the darkness hide it from my face?
You cannot miss that inn.

Shall I find comfort, travel-sore and weak?
Of labour you shall find the sum.
Will there be beds for me and all who seek?
Yea, beds for all who come.

Public domain. Published 1862.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of alternating questions and answers in iambic pentameter and tetrameter, ABAB rhyme.

Themes

Life as a difficult uphill journey toward death, and the certainty of rest at the end.

Historical Context

Published in Goblin Market and Other Poems (1862).

About Christina Rossetti

Christina Rossetti was born on December 5, 1830, in London, the youngest of the four Rossetti children. She is the most admired Victorian woman poet, known for her lyrical grace, her devout Anglican faith, and her poems of love, death, and longing. Her first major collection Goblin Market and Other Poems (1862) was immediately celebrated. Her brother Dante Gabriel Rossetti was a central figure in the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. She died in London in 1894.
