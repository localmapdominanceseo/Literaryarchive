Now Sleeps the Crimson Petal

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1847 Published 1847 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1847 as a song within The Princess, this lyric is one of Tennyson's most beautiful — describing the night falling, flowers sleeping, the firefly waking, and asking the beloved: now fold me from the dark, and take and keep me.

Quick Answer

"Now Sleeps the Crimson Petal" is a lyric by Tennyson published in 1847. Now sleeps the crimson petal, now the white; nor waves the cypress in the palace walk; nor winks the gold fin in the porphyry font. The fire-fly wakens; waken thou with me. Now folds the lily all her sweetness up. Now slides the silent meteor on. Take me, fold me from the dark.

Now sleeps the crimson petal, now the white;
Nor waves the cypress in the palace walk;
Nor winks the gold fin in the porphyry font:
The fire-fly wakens: waken thou with me.

Now droops the milk-white peacock like a ghost,
And like a ghost she glimmers on to me.

Now lies the Earth all Danaë to the stars,
And all thy heart lies open unto me.

Now slides the silent meteor on, and leaves
A shining furrow, as thy thoughts in me.

Now folds the lily all her sweetness up,
And slips into the bosom of the lake:
So fold thyself, my dearest, thou, and slip
Into my bosom and be lost in me.

Public domain. Published 1847 in The Princess.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Ten lines of blank verse with a lyric, hypnotic rhythm created through syntactic parallelism.

Themes

The mystery of night and the invitation to the beloved to merge with the speaker as all things fold into the dark.

Historical Context

Published as a song within The Princess (1847). One of the most frequently anthologized of all Tennyson's shorter lyrics.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
