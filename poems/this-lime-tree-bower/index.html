This Lime-Tree Bower My Prison

by Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Written 1797 Published 1800 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1800, this conversation poem was written when Coleridge was unable to join Wordsworth and Charles Lamb on a walk, due to a burn on his foot. Sitting in his lime-tree bower, he imagines them walking through the landscape and realizes that the bower is not a prison after all.

Quick Answer

"This Lime-Tree Bower My Prison" is a poem by Coleridge, published in 1800. Unable to join his friends on a walk, Coleridge sits in the lime-tree bower. He imagines their walk through the dell and over the hill — then realizes that beauty is everywhere, even in his bower. No plot so narrow but nature is there. He is no longer imprisoned.

Well, they are gone, and here must I remain,
This lime-tree bower my prison! I have lost
Beauties and feelings, such as would have been
Most sweet to my remembrance even when age
Had dimm'd mine eyes to blindness! They, meanwhile,
Friends, whom I never more may meet again,
On springy heath, along the hill-top edge,
Wander in gladness, and wind down, perchance,
To that still roaring dell, of which I told;
The roaring dell, o'erwooded, narrow, deep,
And only speckled by the mid-day sun;
Where its slim trunk the ash from rock to rock
Flings arching like a bridge;—that branchless ash,
Unsunned and damp, whose few poor yellow leaves
Ne'er tremble in the gale, yet tremble still,
Fann'd by the water-fall! and there my friends
Behold the dark green file of long lank weeds,
That all at once (a most fantastic sight!)
Still nod and drip beneath the dripping edge
Of the blue clay-stone.

Public domain. Published 1800.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Seventy-six lines of blank verse, a conversation poem addressed to Charles Lamb.

Themes

The liberation of imagination from physical constraint — the bower that seemed a prison becomes a place of revelation when Coleridge learns to see it properly. Sympathy with friends' experience also provides a form of participation.

Historical Context

Written in 1797 at Nether Stowey. Published in 1800. The 'Sara' who spilled the boiling milk is his wife Sara Fricker; 'gentle-hearted Charles' is Charles Lamb.

About Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was born on October 21, 1772, in Ottery St Mary, Devon, England, and became one of the founders of the English Romantic movement alongside his friend William Wordsworth. Together they published Lyrical Ballads in 1798, a collection that launched Romanticism in English literature. Coleridge's own most celebrated contributions to the collection, 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' and 'Kubla Khan,' are among the most distinctive and widely studied poems in the language. He died in Highgate, London, in 1834.
