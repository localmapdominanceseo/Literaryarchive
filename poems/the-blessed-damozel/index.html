The Blessed Damozel

by Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Written 1847 Published 1850 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1850, this poem describes a young woman in heaven leaning out over the golden bar watching for her earthly lover to join her.

Quick Answer

The Blessed Damozel is a poem by Dante Gabriel Rossetti, published in 1850. The damozel leans out from heaven's golden bar, her eyes deeper than still waters, watching for her lover who has not yet died.

The blessed damozel leaned out
From the gold bar of Heaven;
Her eyes were deeper than the depth
Of waters stilled at even;
She had three lilies in her hand,
And the stars in her hair were seven.

Her robe, ungirt from clasp to hem,
No wrought flowers did adorn,
But a white rose of Mary's gift,
For service meetly worn;
Her hair that lay along her back
Was yellow like ripe corn.

It was the rampart of God's house
That she was standing on;
By God built over the sheer depth
The which is Space begun;
So high, that looking downward thence
She scarce could see the sun.

Public domain. Published 1850.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-four six-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter.

Themes

Longing and separation even in paradise — the dead beloved watches and waits, heaven incomplete without her lover.

Historical Context

Published in The Germ (1850), the Pre-Raphaelite journal. Written when Rossetti was eighteen.

About Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Dante Gabriel Rossetti was born on May 12, 1828, in London, and became both a celebrated painter and poet, a central figure in the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. His poems, including the sonnet sequence The House of Life, are among the most elaborately crafted of the Victorian period. He died at Birchington-on-Sea in 1882.
