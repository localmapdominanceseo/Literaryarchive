The Eolian Harp

by Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Written 1795 Published 1796 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1796, this conversation poem meditates on the wind harp by the window, whose music leads to speculation about whether all nature is animated by the same breath — then pulls back from this pantheism at his wife's rebuke.

Quick Answer

"The Eolian Harp" is a poem by Coleridge, published in 1796. Sitting with Sara by the cottage window, Coleridge hears the wind harp and meditates — what if all animated nature is like harps played by the intellectual breeze? One life and intellectual soul everywhere. Then Sara rebukes him and he returns to orthodox faith.

My pensive Sara! thy soft cheek reclined
Thus on mine arm, most soothing sweet it is
To sit beside our Cot, our Cot o'ergrown
With white-flowered Jasmin, and the broad-leaved Myrtle,
(Meet emblems they of Innocence and Love!)
And watch the clouds, that late were rich with light,
Slow saddening round, and mark the star of eve
Serenely brilliant (such should Wisdom be)
Shine opposite! How exquisite the scents
Snatched from yon bean-field! and the world so hushed!
The stilly murmur of the distant Sea
Tells us of silence.

And that simplest Lute,
Placed length-ways in the clasping casement, hark!
How by the desultory breeze caressed,
Like some coy maid half yielding to her lover,
It pours such sweet upbraiding, as must needs
Tempt to repeat the wrong! And now, its strings
Boldlier swept, the long sequacious notes
Over delicious surges sink and rise,
Such a soft floating witchery of sound
As twilight Elfins make, when they at eve
Voyage on gentle gales from Fairy-Land.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Sixty-four lines of blank verse, one of the earliest of Coleridge's conversation poems.

Themes

The temptation of pantheism — the idea that the divine animates all nature as wind animates a harp — and its conflict with orthodox Christian faith. The poem enacts the very tension it describes.

Historical Context

Published in 1796. Written at Clevedon, Somerset, on honeymoon with Sara Fricker. The Eolian (aeolian) harp was a wind harp placed in windows — the wind caused its strings to vibrate.

About Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was born on October 21, 1772, in Ottery St Mary, Devon, England, and became one of the founders of the English Romantic movement alongside his friend William Wordsworth. Together they published Lyrical Ballads in 1798, a collection that launched Romanticism in English literature. Coleridge's own most celebrated contributions to the collection, 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' and 'Kubla Khan,' are among the most distinctive and widely studied poems in the language. He died in Highgate, London, in 1834.
