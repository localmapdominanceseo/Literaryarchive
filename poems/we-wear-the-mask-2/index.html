Sympathy

by Paul Laurence Dunbar

Written 1899 Published 1899 Post-Reconstruction American Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1899, this poem describes a caged bird that beats its wings against the bars until they bleed — then beats them again at the opening of another beautiful day — and sings not out of joy but out of prayer, a plea to something beyond the cage.

Quick Answer

"Sympathy" is a poem by Paul Laurence Dunbar, published in 1899. The speaker knows what the caged bird feels when the first bud opens, when the wind stirs soft through the springing grass. He knows why the caged bird beats its wings against the bars until they bleed. He knows why the caged bird sings — not for joy, but as a prayer for something beyond its cage.

I know what the caged bird feels, alas!
When the sun is bright on the upland slopes;
When the wind stirs soft through the springing grass,
And the river flows like a stream of glass;
When the first bird sings and the first bud opes,
And the faint perfume from its chalice steals—
I know what the caged bird feels!

I know why the caged bird beats his wing
Till its blood is red on the cruel bars;
For he must fly back to his perch and cling
When he fain would be on the bough a-swing;
And a pain still throbs in the old, old scars
And they pulse again with a keener sting—
I know why he beats his wing!

I know why the caged bird sings, ah me,
When his wing is bruised and his bosom sore,—
When he beats his bars and he would be free;
It is not a carol of joy or glee,
But a prayer that he sends from his heart's deep core,
But a plea, that upward to Heaven he flings—
I know why the caged bird sings!

Public domain (published 1899). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three seven-line stanzas with ABAABCC rhyme, the refrain 'I know why the caged bird sings' launching each stanza and giving the poem its incantatory power.

Themes

Captivity and the desperate assertion of freedom through song. The poem draws on both the literal image of a caged bird and the metaphorical captivity of Black Americans under Jim Crow. Maya Angelou borrowed the poem's image for her autobiography I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings (1969).

Historical Context

Published in Lyrics of the Hearthside (1899). Dunbar was the first African American poet to gain national recognition, writing in both standard English and African American dialect.

About Paul Laurence Dunbar

Paul Laurence Dunbar was born on June 27, 1872, in Dayton, Ohio, the son of formerly enslaved parents, and became one of the first African American poets to achieve national and international recognition. He wrote both in standard English and in African American dialect verse, and his work captured the complexities of Black life in America in the decades following Reconstruction. He died in Dayton in 1906 at age 33.
