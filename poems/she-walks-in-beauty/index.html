She Walks in Beauty

by Lord Byron

Written 1814 Published 1815 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written in a single sitting on June 12, 1814, after Byron saw his cousin's wife at a London ball wearing a dark mourning gown spangled with silver, this poem praises her beauty as a perfect harmony of darkness and light.

Quick Answer

"She Walks in Beauty" is a poem by Lord Byron, written in 1814 and published in 1815 in his collection Hebrew Melodies. The speaker compares an unnamed woman's beauty to a clear, starry night, arguing that her perfection arises from a harmonious balance of darkness and light, and concludes that her outward beauty reflects an equally pure inner goodness.

She walks in beauty, like the night
Of cloudless climes and starry skies;
And all that's best of dark and bright
Meet in her aspect and her eyes;
Thus mellow'd to that tender light
Which heaven to gaudy day denies.

One shade the more, one ray the less,
Had half impair'd the nameless grace
Which waves in every raven tress,
Or softly lightens o'er her face;
Where thoughts serenely sweet express
How pure, how dear their dwelling-place.

And on that cheek, and o'er that brow,
So soft, so calm, yet eloquent,
The smiles that win, the tints that glow,
But tell of days in goodness spent,
A mind at peace with all below,
A heart whose love is innocent!

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three six-line stanzas written in iambic tetrameter with an ABABAB rhyme scheme, a meter Byron associated with hymns, fitting for a poem written for the religiously-themed Hebrew Melodies collection.

Themes

The harmony of opposites, with light and dark balanced so precisely that 'one shade the more, one ray the less' would spoil the effect. The poem moves from physical description toward an assumption of inner virtue, treating outward beauty as evidence of 'a mind at peace' and 'a heart whose love is innocent,' reflecting a more restrained, reverent mode than Byron's often more provocative work.

Historical Context

Byron wrote the poem the morning after meeting his cousin's wife, Anne Wilmot, at a London party on June 11, 1814, struck by the contrast between her dark mourning dress and her bright, animated appearance. It was published the following year in Hebrew Melodies, a collection of lyrics Byron wrote to be set to music by the composer Isaac Nathan.

About Lord Byron

George Gordon Byron, known as Lord Byron, was born on January 22, 1788, in London, and became one of the most famous and controversial figures of English Romanticism, celebrated equally for his poetry and his scandalous personal life. His work ranges from brief, polished lyrics to the sprawling satirical epic Don Juan, and the brooding, charismatic outsider figure he depicted in his writing gave rise to the term 'the Byronic hero.' He died in Greece in 1824 while supporting the Greek War of Independence.
