The Computation

by John Donne

Written 1601 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1633, this compressed poem computes how long the speaker has lived since his beloved left — his sighs and tears have aged him in hours to two hundred years.

Quick Answer

"The Computation" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. Since his beloved left, the speaker has lived through twenty hours — and in each hour, his sighs and tears have aged him ten years. He has lived two hundred years since she left. And twenty more must be his doom — and at that rate, when she returns, he will be dead.

For the first twenty years, since yesterday,
I scarce believed thou couldst be gone away;
For forty more, I fed on favours past,
And forty on hopes, that thou wouldst they might last;
Tearsdrowned one hundred, and sighs blew out two;
A thousand, I did neither think nor do,
Or not divide, all being one thought of you;
Or in a thousand more, forgot that too.
Yet call not this long life; but think that I
Am, by being dead, immortal; can ghosts die?

Analysis

Form and Structure

Ten lines of iambic pentameter rhyming ABABCDCDEE, the mathematical conceit compressed into a single stanza.

Themes

Hyperbolic love-time — the grief of separation measured not in hours but in centuries of suffering. Donne uses arithmetic to express the inexpressible weight of love's absence.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633. One of Donne's most compressed conceits — the lover's subjective time measured against clock time.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
