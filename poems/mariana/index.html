Mariana

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1830 Published 1830 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1830, based on Shakespeare's Measure for Measure, this poem describes a woman abandoned by her lover, living alone in a moated grange, repeating: He cometh not; I am aweary, I would that I were dead.

Quick Answer

Mariana is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1830. Based on Shakespeare's Mariana, it describes a woman alone in a decaying moated grange. The rusted nails, the blackened wall, her world decays as she decays. Her refrain: 'He will not come; I am aweary, aweary, I would that I were dead.'

With blackest moss the flower-plots
Were thickly crusted, one and all:
The rusted nails fell from the knots
That held the pear to the gable-wall.
The broken sheds look'd sad and strange:
Unlifted was the clinking latch;
Weeded and worn the ancient thatch
Upon the lonely moated grange.
She only said, 'My life is dreary,
He cometh not,' she said;
She said, 'I am aweary, aweary,
I would that I were dead!'

Public domain. Published 1830.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Seven twelve-line stanzas each ending with the refrain, the repetition enacting Mariana's circular, hopeless waiting.

Themes

Abandonment, stasis, and the decay of a life centered on a love that never arrives.

Historical Context

Published in Poems, Chiefly Lyrical (1830). Tennyson took the character from Shakespeare's Measure for Measure.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
