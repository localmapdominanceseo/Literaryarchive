Oenone (Extract)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1832 Published 1842 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1842, Oenone is a dramatic monologue spoken by the nymph Oenone, abandoned by Paris who chose Helen over her. She mourns her abandonment on Mount Ida and invokes the goddesses who watched Paris make his fatal choice.

Quick Answer

"Oenone" is a dramatic monologue by Tennyson, published in 1842. Oenone, the nymph abandoned by Paris for Helen, mourns on Mount Ida. She recalls the judgment of Paris — Hera offering power, Athena offering wisdom, Aphrodite offering the fairest woman alive — and how she begged Paris not to choose beauty. Now Troy will burn because of it.

There lies a vale in Ida, lovelier
Than all the valleys of Ionian hills.
The swimming vapour slopes athwart the glen,
Puts forth an arm, and creeps from pine to pine,
And loiters, slowly drawn. On either hand
The lawns and meadow-ledges midway down
Hang rich in flowers, and far below them roars
The long brook falling thro' the clov'n ravine
In cataract after cataract to the sea.
Behind the valley topmost Gargarus
Stands up and takes the morning: but in front
The gorges, opening wide apart, reveal
Troas and Ilion's column'd citadel,
The crown of Troas.

Hither came at noon
Mournful Oenone, wandering forlorn
Of Paris, once her playmate on the hills.
Her cheek had lost the rose, and round her neck
Floated her hair or seem'd to float in rest.
She, leaning on a fragment twined with vine,
Sang to the stillness, till the mountain-shade
Sloped downward to her seat from the upper cliff.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Blank verse dramatic monologue, one of Tennyson's classical poems.

Themes

Betrayal and the consequences of choosing beauty over wisdom — the abandonment of Oenone for Helen directly causes the Trojan War. The poem is both a lament and a warning.

Historical Context

First published in 1832, substantially revised for 1842. Tennyson's classical poems draw on Greek and Roman sources for Romantic-era emotional subjects.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
