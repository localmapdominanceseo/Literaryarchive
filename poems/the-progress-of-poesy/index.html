The Progress of Poesy (Extract)

by Thomas Gray

Written 1751 Published 1757 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1757, this Pindaric ode traces the progress of poetry from ancient Greece through Rome to England, culminating in praise of Shakespeare and Milton and the figure of the poet (Gray himself) who, though of lesser wing, still dares to mount to the peaks they climbed.

Quick Answer

"The Progress of Poesy" is an ode by Thomas Gray, published in 1757. It traces the progress of poetry through time and geography — from its origins in ancient Greece, through Rome, to England — celebrating Homer, Pindar, Dryden, Shakespeare, and Milton, and ending with the modest self-portrait of the poet himself, daring to mount on lesser wing than they.

Awake, Aeolian lyre, awake,
And give to rapture all thy trembling strings.
From Helicon's harmonious springs
A thousand rills their mazy progress take:
The laughing flowers that round them blow
Drink life and fragrance as they flow.
Now the rich stream of music winds along
Deep, majestic, smooth, and strong,
Thro' verdant vales and Ceres' golden reign:
Now rolling down the steep amain,
Headlong, impetuous, see it pour:
The rocks and nodding groves rebellow to the roar.

Oh! Sovereign of the willing soul,
Parent of sweet and solemn-breathing airs,
Enchanting shell! the sullen Cares
And frantic Passions hear thy soft control.
On Thracia's hills the Lord of War
Has curbed the fury of his car,
And dropped his thirsty lance at thy command.
Perching on the sceptered hand
Of Jove, thy magic lulls the feathered king
With ruffled plumes and flagging wing:
Quenched in dark clouds of slumber lie
The terror of his beak and lightnings of his eye.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three Pindaric odes each with strophe, antistrophe, and epode in elaborate irregular measures, Gray's most formally ambitious poem.

Themes

The transmission of poetic power across cultures and centuries, and the poet's humble place in that tradition. The poem is both a celebration of poetic history and a modest self-assessment.

Historical Context

Published in 1757 as one of the first publications of Horace Walpole's Strawberry Hill Press. Gray's odes were influential on later Romantic poets despite being initially misunderstood.

About Thomas Gray

Thomas Gray was born on December 26, 1716, in London, England, and spent most of his adult life as a scholar at Cambridge University. Though he published very little poetry, his 'Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard' (1751) became one of the most widely read and imitated poems in the English language. He died in Cambridge in 1771.
