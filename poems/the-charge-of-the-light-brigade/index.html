The Charge of the Light Brigade

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1854 Published 1854 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written and published within weeks of the disastrous cavalry charge at Balaclava during the Crimean War in October 1854, this poem celebrates the courage of the six hundred soldiers who rode into enemy artillery fire despite a catastrophically mistaken order, and commemorates their sacrifice.

Quick Answer

"The Charge of the Light Brigade" is a poem by Alfred Lord Tennyson, written and published in 1854. It commemorates the cavalry charge at the Battle of Balaclava during the Crimean War, in which six hundred British soldiers rode directly into Russian artillery fire because of a misunderstood order. The poem celebrates their courage and obedience while acknowledging the blunder that sent them to their deaths.

Half a league, half a league,
Half a league onward,
All in the valley of Death
Rode the six hundred.
'Forward, the Light Brigade!
Charge for the guns!' he said.
Into the valley of Death
Rode the six hundred.

'Forward, the Light Brigade!'
Was there a man dismayed?
Not though the soldier knew
Someone had blundered.
Theirs not to make reply,
Theirs not to reason why,
Theirs but to do and die.
Into the valley of Death
Rode the six hundred.

Cannon to right of them,
Cannon to left of them,
Cannon in front of them
Volleyed and thundered;
Stormed at with shot and shell,
Boldly they rode and well,
Into the jaws of Death,
Into the mouth of hell
Rode the six hundred.

Flashed all their sabres bare,
Flashed as they turned in air
Sabring the gunners there,
Charging an army, while
All the world wondered.
Plunged in the battery-smoke
Right through the line they broke;
Cossack and Russian
Reeled from the sabre stroke
Shattered and sundered.
Then they rode back, but not,
Not the six hundred.

Cannon to right of them,
Cannon to left of them,
Cannon behind them
Volleyed and thundered;
Stormed at with shot and shell,
While horse and hero fell.
They that had fought so well
Came through the jaws of Death,
Back from the mouth of hell,
All that was left of them,
Left of six hundred.

When can their glory fade?
O the wild charge they made!
All the world wondered.
Honour the charge they made!
Honour the Light Brigade,
Noble six hundred!

Public domain. Published December 1854.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six stanzas of varying length in dactylic dimeter, with the repeated refrain 'Rode the six hundred' creating a drumbeat rhythm that mimics the sound of charging horses. The poem's driving, relentless meter physically enacts the unstoppable momentum of the charge.

Themes

Military courage, obedience, and the honor of soldiers who follow orders without question even when those orders are fatal. The poem does not dwell on who gave the wrong order ('Someone had blundered') but on the soldiers' nobility in obeying without protest. It raises an implicit tension between heroic sacrifice and the callousness of military command.

Historical Context

Tennyson wrote the poem on December 2, 1854, reportedly in a few minutes after reading a newspaper account of the charge, published it almost immediately, and it became one of the most famous war poems in the English language. The charge itself resulted from a miscommunicated order during the Battle of Balaclava on October 25, 1854, and resulted in catastrophic British losses.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
