De Profundis

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1880 Published 1880 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1880, De Profundis is Tennyson's meditation on the soul's journey from the depths to God — a philosophical poem that begins with the birth of a soul into the world of time and ends with a call from the outer deep.

Quick Answer

Tennyson's De Profundis (1880) meditates on the soul's emergence from the cosmic abyss into human life, and its longing to return to the divine source. Out of the deep, my child, out of the deep, where all that was to be, in all that was, whirl'd for a million aeons thro' the vast waste dawn of multitudinous-eddying light.

Out of the deep, my child, out of the deep,
Where all that was to be, in all that was,
Whirl'd for a million aeons thro' the vast
Waste dawn of multitudinous-eddying light—
Out of the deep, my child, out of the deep,
Thro' all this changing world of changeless law,
And every phase of ever-heightening life,
And nine long months of antenatal gloom,
With this last moon, this crescent—her dark orb
Touch'd with earth's light—thou comest, darling boy;
Our own; a babe in lineament and limb
Perfect, and prophet of the perfect man;
Believed, but longing for belief to come:

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two sections of irregular verse, the first secular and the second a prayer.

Themes

The mystery of the soul's origin and destiny — from the cosmic void through human life back to God.

Historical Context

Published in 1880 in Ballads and Other Poems. Written partly for the birth of his grandson.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
