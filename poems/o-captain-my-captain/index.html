O Captain! My Captain!

by Walt Whitman

Written 1865 Published 1865 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written in 1865, this elegy mourns the assassination of President Abraham Lincoln, though the poem never names him directly. Whitman casts the nation's grief through the extended metaphor of a ship's captain who dies just as his vessel reaches safe harbor, and the poem became, ironically, the most conventional and most popular of his career.

Quick Answer

"O Captain! My Captain!" is an elegy Walt Whitman wrote in 1865 to mourn the assassination of President Abraham Lincoln, though Lincoln is never named. Using the extended metaphor of a ship's captain who dies just as his vessel reaches port, the poem contrasts a nation's victorious celebration with one sailor's private grief.

O Captain! my Captain! our fearful trip is done,
The ship has weather'd every rack, the prize we sought is won,
The port is near, the bells I hear, the people all exulting,
While follow eyes the steady keel, the vessel grim and daring;
But O heart! heart! heart!
O the bleeding drops of red,
Where on the deck my Captain lies,
Fallen cold and dead.

O Captain! my Captain! rise up and hear the bells;
Rise up- for you the flag is flung- for you the bugle trills,
For you bouquets and ribbon'd wreaths- for you the shores a-crowding,
For you they call, the swaying mass, their eager faces turning;
Here Captain! dear father!
This arm beneath your head!
It is some dream that on the deck,
You've fallen cold and dead.

My Captain does not answer, his lips are pale and still,
My father does not feel my arm, he has no pulse nor will,
The ship is anchor'd safe and sound, its voyage closed and done,
From fearful trip the victor ship comes in with object won;
Exult O shores, and ring O bells!
But I with mournful tread,
Walk the deck my Captain lies,
Fallen cold and dead.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Unusually for Whitman, who is best known for unrhymed free verse, this poem uses regular rhyme and a tight stanza structure with a refrain ('Fallen cold and dead'), giving it the singable, almost hymn-like quality that made it instantly popular and frequently anthologized.

Themes

The poem's central tension is between public triumph and private loss: the 'people all exulting' at the war's end stand in contrast to the speaker, who alone recognizes that the captain who guided the ship to safety will never see the celebration. It is widely read as a meditation on the cost of leadership and the way grief can isolate even amid shared victory.

Historical Context

Whitman wrote the poem shortly after Lincoln's assassination in April 1865, having spent much of the Civil War working as a volunteer nurse in Washington, D.C. army hospitals. It was first published in the Saturday Press that November and later included in Sequel to Drum-Taps, a collection of his Civil War poetry.

About Walt Whitman

Walt Whitman was born on May 31, 1819, on Long Island, New York. His landmark collection Leaves of Grass, first published in 1855 and expanded throughout his life, broke from traditional verse forms in favor of free verse and sweeping, democratic subject matter. Whitman served as a nurse during the American Civil War, an experience that shaped much of his later work, including his elegy for Abraham Lincoln. He is widely regarded, alongside Emily Dickinson, as one of the founders of a distinctly American poetic voice.
