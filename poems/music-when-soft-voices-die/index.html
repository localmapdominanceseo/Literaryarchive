Music, When Soft Voices Die

by Percy Bysshe Shelley

Written 1821 Published 1824 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written in 1821 and published posthumously in 1824, this four-line lyric argues that music lingers in the memory after it has stopped, violets live on in the scent they've left, and the speaker will live on in love's thoughts after he is gone.

Quick Answer

"Music, When Soft Voices Die" is a four-stanza lyric by Shelley, published posthumously in 1824. Music lingers in memory when the sound has died. Violets sicken when their odour is gone — but their scent lives on. Roses linger on the brow where they have been laid. And so the speaker will live in the beloved's thoughts when he is gone.

Music, when soft voices die,
Vibrates in the memory—
Odours, when sweet violets sicken,
Live within the sense they quicken.

Rose leaves, when the rose is dead,
Are heaped for the belovèd's bed;
And so thy thoughts, when thou art gone,
Love itself shall slumber on.

Public domain. Written 1821, published posthumously 1824.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of alternating tetrameter and trimeter, among the most compressed of Shelley's shorter lyrics.

Themes

The persistence of beauty and love in memory after the thing itself is gone — the argument that absence does not end love but transforms it.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1824. One of Shelley's most anthologized short lyrics.

About Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley was born on August 4, 1792, in Horsham, England, and became one of the major poets of English Romanticism despite controversy during his lifetime over his radical political and religious views. His poetry combines lyrical intensity with sustained engagement with politics, philosophy, and the natural world. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy in 1822, at the age of twenty-nine.
