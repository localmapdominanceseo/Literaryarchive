Meeting at Night

by Robert Browning

Written 1845 Published 1845 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1845, this two-stanza poem describes a lover's nocturnal journey across the sea to his beloved — the gray sea, the long black land, the warm sea-scented beach, and a voice less loud than two hearts beating.

Quick Answer

Meeting at Night is a poem by Browning, published in 1845. The gray sea, the long black land — a yellow half-moon — the slushy sand — a mile of warm sea-scented beach — a voice less loud than two hearts beating.

The gray sea and the long black land;
And the yellow half-moon large and low;
And the startled little waves that leap
In fiery ringlets from their sleep,
As I gain the cove with pushing prow,
And quench its speed i' the slushy sand.

Then a mile of warm sea-scented beach;
Three fields to cross till a farm appears;
A tap at the pane, the quick sharp scratch
And blue spurt of a lighted match,
And a voice less loud, thro' its joys and fears,
Than the two hearts beating each to each!

Public domain. Published 1845.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two six-line stanzas of iambic tetrameter with ABCCBA rhyme, remarkable for its compression and precision.

Themes

Erotic anticipation and the sensory experience of a nocturnal journey toward love.

Historical Context

Published in Dramatic Romances and Lyrics (1845). One of Browning's finest short lyrics.

About Robert Browning

Robert Browning was born on May 7, 1812, in Camberwell, London, and became one of the foremost Victorian poets, celebrated above all for his mastery of the dramatic monologue — a form in which a speaker inadvertently reveals more about themselves than they intend. His collection Men and Women (1855) and his verse novel The Ring and the Book (1868-1869) are considered his greatest achievements. He married Elizabeth Barrett in 1846 and they lived in Italy until her death in 1861. He died in Venice in 1889.
