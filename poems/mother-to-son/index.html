Mother to Son

by Langston Hughes

Written 1922 Published 1922 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1922 in The Crisis, this poem is spoken by a Black mother to her son, using the extended metaphor of a staircase — splinter-filled and worn, with no crystal steps — to tell him that despite hardship she has kept climbing, and so must he.

Quick Answer

"Mother to Son" is a poem by Langston Hughes, published in 1922. A mother tells her son that life for her has been no crystal stair — it has had tacks and splinters and broken boards and dark places with no light. But she has kept climbing, never sitting down, never turning back. She urges him to do the same, because she is still going.

Well, son, I'll tell you:
Life for me ain't been no crystal stair.
It's had tacks in it,
And splinters,
And boards torn up,
And places with no carpet on the floor—
Bare.
But all the time
I'se been a-climbin' on,
And reachin' landin's,
And turnin' corners,
And sometimes goin' in the dark
Where there ain't been no light.
So, boy, don't you turn back.
Don't you set down on the steps
'Cause you finds it's kinder hard.
Don't you fall now—
For I'se still goin', honey,
I'se still climbin',
And life for me ain't been no crystal stair.

Public domain (published December 1922 in The Crisis, pre-1927). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Free verse in African American vernacular speech, the mother's voice rendered in dialect that gives it authenticity and warmth. The central extended metaphor of the staircase — worn, broken, but still being climbed — carries the entire poem.

Themes

Perseverance through hardship, passed from parent to child. The crystal stair represents an easy, privileged life the speaker has never had. The real staircase — broken boards, tacks, splinters, darkness — is her actual life, and climbing it is her act of courage. The poem is as much about how to pass resilience on as it is about the hardships themselves.

Historical Context

Hughes published the poem in The Crisis in December 1922, when he was just twenty years old, and it was later collected in The Weary Blues (1926). It is one of his most widely anthologized early poems and an early example of his use of African American vernacular speech as a legitimate literary voice.

About Langston Hughes

Langston Hughes was born on February 1, 1901, in Joplin, Missouri, and became one of the leading voices of the Harlem Renaissance, the flowering of African American literature, art, and music centered in New York City during the 1920s. Hughes drew on the rhythms of jazz and blues music to develop a distinctive poetic voice that celebrated Black culture and identity while confronting American racism directly. He died in New York City in 1967.
