The Revenge: A Ballad of the Fleet

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1878 Published 1878 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1878, this ballad tells the story of the last fight of the Revenge under Sir Richard Grenville in 1591, when he and fifty-three sick men held off a Spanish fleet of fifty-three ships through the night.

Quick Answer

"The Revenge" is a ballad by Tennyson, published in 1878. Sir Richard Grenville and his sick crew of fifty-three hold the Revenge against a Spanish fleet of fifty-three ships through a whole night's fighting. Grenville is mortally wounded. The Revenge surrenders after a night of heroism. Sir Richard cried: 'I have fought for Queen and Faith like a valiant man and true; I have only done my duty as a man is bound to do.'

At Flores in the Azores Sir Richard Grenville lay,
And a pinnace, like a fluttered bird, came flying from far away:
'Spanish ships of war at sea! we have sighted fifty-three!'
Then sware Lord Thomas Howard: 'Fore God I am no coward;
But I cannot meet them here, for my ships are out of gear,
And the half my men are sick. I must fly, but follow quick.
We are six ships of the line; can we fight with fifty-three?'

Then spake Sir Richard Grenville: 'I know you are no coward;
You fly them for a moment to fight with them again.
But I've ninety men and more that are lying sick ashore.
I should count myself the coward if I left them, my Lord Howard,
To these Inquisition dogs and the devildoms of Spain.'

Public domain. Published 1878.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twelve stanzas of varying length in driving anapestic rhythm, the ballad meter giving the poem its relentless forward energy.

Themes

Heroic sacrifice and English national pride — Grenville's last fight against impossible odds as the model of English courage.

Historical Context

Published in 1878. Based on the historical fight of the Revenge on August 31, 1591. Tennyson's most successful narrative ballad after The Charge of the Light Brigade.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
