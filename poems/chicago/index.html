Chicago

by Carl Sandburg

Written 1914 Published 1914 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1914 in Poetry magazine, this poem addresses Chicago directly, acknowledging all the charges made against it — its brutal slaughterhouses, its women in prostitution, its hungry children — before defending it with fierce pride as the City of the Big Shoulders.

Quick Answer

"Chicago" is a poem by Carl Sandburg, published in 1914. The speaker addresses Chicago by its industrial nicknames — Hog Butcher, Tool Maker, Stacker of Wheat, City of the Big Shoulders — then acknowledges all the charges laid against it (crooked politicians, painted women, hungry children) before defending the city's raw, laughing, brutal vitality against those who would be soft.

Hog Butcher for the World,
Tool Maker, Stacker of Wheat,
Player with Railroads and the Nation's Freight Handler;
Stormy, husky, brawling,
City of the Big Shoulders:

They tell me you are wicked and I believe them, for I have seen your painted women under the gas lamps luring the farm boys.
And they tell me you are crooked and I answer: Yes, it is true I have seen the gunman kill and go free to kill again.
And they tell me you are brutal and my reply is: On the faces of women and children I have seen the marks of wanton hunger.
And having answered so I turn once more to those who sneer at this my city, and I give them back the sneer and say to them:
Come and show me another city with lifted head singing so proud to be alive and coarse and strong and cunning.
Flinging magnetic curses amid the toil of piling job on job, here is a tall bold slugger set vivid against the little soft cities;
Fierce as a dog with tongue lapping for action, cunning as a savage pitted against the wilderness,
Bareheaded,
Shoveling,
Wrecking,
Planning,
Building, breaking, rebuilding,
Under the smoke, dust all over his mouth, laughing with white teeth,
Under the terrible burden of destiny laughing as a young man laughs,
Laughing even as an ignorant fighter laughs who has never lost a battle,
Bragging and laughing that under his wrist is the pulse, and under his ribs the heart of the people,
Laughing!
Laughing the stormy, husky, brawling laughter of Youth, half-naked, sweating, proud to be Hog Butcher, Tool Maker, Stacker of Wheat, Player with Railroads and Freight Handler to the Nation.

Public domain. Published 1914 in Poetry magazine.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Free verse with long, energetic Whitmanesque lines that build through anaphora and catalog. The poem begins with short, punchy epithets and expands into long praise-and-defense sequences before returning to the nicknames in the closing lines.

Themes

Industrial urban vitality, civic pride, and the honest acknowledgment of a city's flaws alongside its strengths. The poem refuses to prettify Chicago or to condemn it — instead it finds the city's energy, brutality, and laughter all equally real and equally worthy of celebration.

Historical Context

Sandburg published 'Chicago' in Poetry magazine in March 1914. It was immediately controversial for its frank depiction of the city's vice and poverty alongside its industrial pride. The poem made Sandburg's reputation and gave Chicago one of its most enduring self-images: the City of the Big Shoulders.

About Carl Sandburg

Carl Sandburg was born on January 6, 1878, in Galesburg, Illinois, to Swedish immigrant parents, and became one of the most popular American poets of the twentieth century. He worked many jobs — farm worker, soldier, journalist — before establishing himself as a poet with Chicago Poems (1916). He won three Pulitzer Prizes, two for poetry and one for his biography of Abraham Lincoln. He died in Flat Rock, North Carolina, in 1967.
