The Vanity of Human Wishes (Extract)

by Samuel Johnson

Written 1749 Published 1749 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1749, this poem by Samuel Johnson is a Christian imitation of Juvenal's tenth satire, cataloguing the emptiness of human wishes — for wealth, power, learning, military glory, long life, and beauty — and finding in all of them only disappointment, corruption, or death.

Quick Answer

"The Vanity of Human Wishes" is a poem by Samuel Johnson, published in 1749. It surveys all the things humans wish for — wealth, power, fame, longevity, beauty — and shows in each case how those wishes lead only to corruption, suffering, or death. The conclusion is that we should ask heaven for what is truly good: a healthy mind in a healthy body, patience, faith, and resignation to God's will.

Let Observation with extensive View,
Survey Mankind, from China to Peru;
Remark each anxious Toil, each eager Strife,
And watch the busy Scenes of crouded Life;
Then say how Hope and Fear, Desire and Hate,
O'erspread with Snares the clouded Maze of Fate,
Where wav'ring Man, betray'd by vent'rous Pride,
To tread the dreary Paths without a Guide;
As treach'rous Phantoms in the Mist delude,
Shuns fancied Ills, or chases airy Good.
How rarely Reason guides the stubborn Choice,
Rules the bold Hand, or prompts the suppliant Voice,
How Nations sink, by darling Schemes oppress'd,
When Vengeance listens to the Fool's Request.
Fate wings with ev'ry Wish th'afflictive Dart,
Each Gift of Nature, and each Grace of Art,
With fatal Heat impetuous Courage glows,
With fatal Sweetness Elocution flows,
Impeachment stops the Speaker's pow'rful Breath,
And restless Fire precipitates on Death.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Heroic couplets in iambic pentameter, Johnson's most ambitious poem, modeled on Juvenal's Satire X.

Themes

The emptiness of all worldly ambitions and the sufficiency of resignation to divine providence. Johnson surveys scholars, soldiers, statesmen, and beauties and finds in each case that getting what they wished for brought ruin.

Historical Context

Published in 1749, the year Johnson published his dictionary prospectus. One of the great poems of the English moral tradition.

About Samuel Johnson

Samuel Johnson was born on September 18, 1709, in Lichfield, Staffordshire, England, and became the dominant literary critic and moralist of mid-eighteenth-century England, best known for his Dictionary of the English Language (1755) and his Lives of the Poets (1779-1781). His poem The Vanity of Human Wishes (1749) is his most sustained original work in verse. He died in London in 1784.
