The Song of Wandering Aengus

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1897 Published 1899 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1899 in The Wind Among the Reeds, this poem describes Aengus, the Irish god of love, who went fishing and caught a silver trout that became a girl who called his name and vanished — and he has wandered ever since seeking her through hollow lands and hilly lands.

Quick Answer

"The Song of Wandering Aengus" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1899. The speaker went to a hazel wood because a fire was in his head, cut and peeled a hazel wand, and caught a silver trout which became a girl who called his name and faded through the brightening air. He will spend his life searching for her, to pluck golden apples and silver apples and walk among long dappled grass.

I went out to the hazel wood,
Because a fire was in my head,
And cut and peeled a hazel wand,
And hooked a berry to a thread;
And when white moths were on the wing,
And moth-like stars were flickering out,
I dropped the berry in a stream
And caught a little silver trout.

When I had laid it on the floor
I went to blow the fire a-flame,
But something rustled on the floor,
And some one called me by my name:
It had become a glimmering girl
With apple blossom in her hair
Who called me by my name and ran
And faded through the brightening air.

Though I am old with wandering
Through hollow lands and hilly lands,
I will find out where she has gone,
And kiss her lips and take her hands;
And walk among long dappled grass,
And pluck till time and times are done,
The silver apples of the moon,
The golden apples of the sun.

Public domain (published 1899). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three six-line stanzas in loose iambic tetrameter with ABABCC rhyme, the ballad-like simplicity giving the mythological subject an accessible musical quality.

Themes

The pursuit of an ideal vision that can never quite be caught — Aengus's endless wandering for the girl who vanished. In the context of Yeats's life, this can be read as his pursuit of Maud Gonne, but it is primarily a myth about the nature of desire itself.

Historical Context

Published in The Wind Among the Reeds (1899), Yeats's most symbolist collection. Aengus (or Óengus) is the Irish god of love, youth, and poetic inspiration.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
