Because I could not stop for Death

by Emily Dickinson

Written 1863 Published 1890 American Romanticism Public Domain

Composed around 1863 and published posthumously in 1890, this is one of Emily Dickinson's most widely read poems. Death appears not as a terror but as a courteous gentleman caller, escorting the speaker on a slow carriage ride past the stages of life toward eternity.

Quick Answer

"Because I could not stop for Death" is a six-stanza poem by Emily Dickinson, written around 1863 and published posthumously in 1890. It personifies Death as a polite suitor who takes the speaker on a carriage ride past scenes of childhood, maturity, and old age before arriving at her grave, suggesting that death is not an ending but a quiet passage into eternity.

Because I could not stop for Death—
He kindly stopped for me—
The Carriage held but just Ourselves—
And Immortality.

We slowly drove—He knew no haste
And I had put away
My labor and my leisure too,
For His Civility—

We passed the School, where Children strove
At Recess—in the Ring—
We passed the Fields of Gazing Grain—
We passed the Setting Sun—

Or rather—He passed us—
The Dews drew quivering and chill—
For only Gossamer, my Gown—
My Tippet—only Tulle—

We paused before a House that seemed
A Swelling of the Ground—
The Roof was scarcely visible—
The Cornice—in the Ground—

Since then—'tis Centuries—and yet
Feels shorter than the Day
I first surmised the Horses' Heads
Were toward Eternity—

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem is written in Dickinson's signature ballad-like common meter, alternating lines of eight and six syllables, and uses her characteristic dashes to create pauses that slow the reader the way the unhurried carriage ride slows the speaker's sense of time.

Themes

The central theme is the reframing of death as courtship rather than violence: Death is 'kind,' shows 'civility,' and the speaker willingly abandons her 'labour' and 'leisure' for him. A second theme is the collapse of time, as the final stanza reveals that centuries have passed in what felt like a single afternoon, suggesting eternity is experienced differently than ordinary time.

Historical Context

Dickinson wrote prolifically about death and the afterlife throughout her life in Amherst, Massachusetts, though she rarely left her father's house in her later years. None of this poem's stanzas were published during her lifetime; it first appeared in the 1890 posthumous collection assembled by her friends and family, who heavily edited her unconventional punctuation for that edition.

About Emily Dickinson

Emily Dickinson was born on December 10, 1830, in Amherst, Massachusetts, and spent nearly her entire life there. Though she wrote nearly 1,800 poems, fewer than a dozen were published during her lifetime. Her work, marked by unconventional capitalization, slant rhyme, and dashes, was largely discovered and published after her death in 1886. She is now considered one of the most important and original voices in American poetry, known for her meditations on death, immortality, nature, and the inner life.
