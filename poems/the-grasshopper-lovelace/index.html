The Grasshopper

by Richard Lovelace

Written 1649 Published 1649 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1649 in Lucasta, this ode addresses the grasshopper — king of summer, drinking dew and making merry in the fields — who is overthrown when winter comes. Lovelace uses the grasshopper's seasonal fall to argue for Cavalier fellowship against the cold of the Puritan winter.

Quick Answer

"The Grasshopper" is a poem by Richard Lovelace, published in 1649. The grasshopper drinks dew and basks in the sun as king of a field — then winter comes and he falls. But the poem turns to friendship: the speaker and his friend Charles Cotton will make their own warmth, their own summer, in the cold Puritan winter. True friendship makes its own fire.

O thou that swing'st upon the waving hair
Of some well-fillèd oaten beard,
Drunk every night with a delicious tear
Dropped thee from heaven, where now th'art reared;

The joys of earth and air are thine entire,
That with thy feet and wings dost hop and fly;
And when thy poppy works, thou dost retire
To thy carved acorn-bed to lie.

Up with the day, the sun thou welcom'st then,
Sport'st in the gilt plats of his beams,
And all these merry days mak'st merry men,
Thyself, and melancholy streams.

But ah, the sickle! Golden ears are cropped;
Chill hoary fingers all your fields do seize,
And what reaps harvests? Winter, which being stopped,
The storm all your sweet merchandise.

Poor verdant fool! and now green ice! thy joys,
Large and as lasting as thy perch of grass,
Bid us lay in 'gainst winter rain, and poise
Their floods with an o'erflowing glass.

Thee we consider, fair fallen thing!
But not thyself t'have passed by us from hence;
For thou, alive, art with the fields away,
Which were thine own in their broad excellency.

So when another numbing winter's done,
I'll call to court the feeble, aged sun,
My wine shall give pale February life,
My roaring fires will warm his frozen veins,
His feathers and his flannel-flower of wife
Shall keep him warm, and shall revive his pains.

Dropt is thy merry, maggot-full delight
Of this world's riches: now thou'rt thine own god
Thyself thine own god, and thine own bright night,
And winter's self an April unod.

Now thou'st made thine own eternity!
I praise thee, Lovelace! Thou art freer than
The doting lover of his mistress be,
Or the philosopher and his tutor, man.

Public domain. Published 1649 in Lucasta.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight four-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABAB rhyme.

Themes

Cavalier resilience against Puritan winter — the grasshopper's seasonal fall is a metaphor for the Royalist cause's defeat, but true friendship creates its own warmth against the cold.

Historical Context

Published in Lucasta (1649). The poem is addressed to Charles Cotton the elder, a friend and fellow Royalist, and is one of the finest expressions of Cavalier fellowship.

About Richard Lovelace

Richard Lovelace was born on December 9, 1617, in Woolwich, England, and became the most celebrated of the Cavalier poets, known for poems of love, loyalty, and honour written during the English Civil War. He was imprisoned twice for his Royalist sympathies, and wrote his two most famous poems — 'To Lucasta, Going to the Wars' and 'To Althea, from Prison' — during these periods of confinement. He died in poverty in London in 1657.
