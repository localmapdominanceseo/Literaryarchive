To E.FitzGerald

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1883 Published 1885 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1885, this poem was Tennyson's address to his oldest friend Edward FitzGerald — the translator of the Rubáiyát — written on the occasion of FitzGerald's death, which Tennyson learned of only after sending him the poem.

Quick Answer

Tennyson's poem 'To E. FitzGerald' (1885) celebrates their long friendship and expresses hope they will meet again — but Tennyson learned only after sending it that FitzGerald had died. The poem became an elegy rather than a greeting. Old Fitz, who from your suburb grange enjoyed the peaceful days.

Old Fitz, who from your suburb grange,
Where once I tarried for a while,
Glory in self-content repose
With those old faces which I knew
In that half-forgotten day
When first you led me o'er the downs
To that dear Somersby vale, which now
You both have left so long ago,
I send a birthday line of greeting;
And faith, my friend, I know not why
I grow so old and shrivelled up,
But what the twelvemonth will unfold
Of this, or any other world
God knows. But, since we all
Must dwell some portion of our days,
With those who are no more, dear friend,
Send me a note to tell me you
Are still a living life for me
As I for you. God bless you, friend—
But after many a summer dies the swan.

Public domain. Published 1885.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A verse epistle in blank verse, informal and affectionate in tone.

Themes

Long friendship, aging, and the shock of bereavement — the poem was meant as a friendly letter but became an accidental elegy.

Historical Context

Published in 1885 in Tiresias and Other Poems. Edward FitzGerald (1809-1883), translator of the Rubáiyát, died while Tennyson was writing the poem to him.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
