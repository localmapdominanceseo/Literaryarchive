Song (When I Am Dead, My Dearest)

by Christina Rossetti

Written 1848 Published 1862 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1848 and published in 1862, this short poem asks the beloved not to sing sad songs when the speaker is dead — she may or may not be remembered, but she will not mind either way.

Quick Answer

Song (When I Am Dead) is a poem by Christina Rossetti, published in 1862. When she is dead, her dearest should sing no sad songs. She may or may not remember him — and she will not be sad either way.

When I am dead, my dearest,
Sing no sad songs for me;
Plant thou no roses at my head,
Nor shady cypress tree:
Be the green grass above me
With showers and dewdrops wet;
And if thou wilt, remember,
And if thou wilt, forget.

I shall not see the shadows,
I shall not feel the rain;
I shall not hear the nightingale
Sing on, as if in pain:
And dreaming through the twilight
That doth not rise nor set,
Haply I may remember,
And haply may forget.

Public domain. Published 1862.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two eight-line stanzas of alternating tetrameter and trimeter with ABABCBDB rhyme.

Themes

The acceptance of death and the release of the beloved from grief.

Historical Context

Published in Goblin Market and Other Poems (1862). Written when Rossetti was eighteen.

About Christina Rossetti

Christina Rossetti was born on December 5, 1830, in London, the youngest of the four Rossetti children. She is the most admired Victorian woman poet, known for her lyrical grace, her devout Anglican faith, and her poems of love, death, and longing. Her first major collection Goblin Market and Other Poems (1862) was immediately celebrated. Her brother Dante Gabriel Rossetti was a central figure in the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. She died in London in 1894.
