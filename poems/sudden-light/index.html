Sudden Light

by Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Written 1854 Published 1870 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1870, this poem describes a sudden sense of having lived this moment before — the beloved's face, the trees — and the speaker's wonder whether they have been here before and shall be here again.

Quick Answer

Sudden Light is a poem by Dante Gabriel Rossetti, published in 1870. The speaker has been here before — he knows the grass beyond the door, the sweet keen smell. Has this been thus before? And shall time's eddying flight still with their lives restore their love?

I have been here before,
But when or how I cannot tell:
I know the grass beyond the door,
The sweet keen smell,
The sighing sound, the lights around the shore.

You have been mine before,—
How long ago I may not know:
But just when at that swallow's soar
Your neck turned so,
Some veil did fall—I knew it all of yore.

Has this been thus before?
And shall not thus time's eddying flight
Still with our lives our love restore
In death's despite,
And day and night yield one delight once more?

Public domain. Published 1870.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three five-line stanzas in iambic pentameter with ABAAB rhyme.

Themes

Déjà vu and the longing for recurrence — the feeling that this perfect moment has happened before and may happen again.

Historical Context

Published in 1870. One of Rossetti's finest short lyrics.

About Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Dante Gabriel Rossetti was born on May 12, 1828, in London, and became both a celebrated painter and poet, a central figure in the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. His poems, including the sonnet sequence The House of Life, are among the most elaborately crafted of the Victorian period. He died at Birchington-on-Sea in 1882.
