America

by Claude McKay

Written 1921 Published 1921 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1921, this sonnet expresses a complex, contradictory relationship with America — the speaker hates its bitterness and its might, yet loves and is tested and strengthened by it, finding dark irony in the country's inevitable decline.

Quick Answer

"America" is a sonnet by Claude McKay, published in 1921. The speaker has a love-hate relationship with America: it feeds him bread of bitterness and sinks its tiger's tooth into his throat, yet he loves its cultured hell that tests his youth. He sees beneath its greatness the signs of inevitable decline — time's inevitable rebellion against its precious things.

Although she feeds me bread of bitterness,
And sinks into my throat her tiger's tooth,
Stealing my breath of life, I will confess
I love this cultured hell that tests my youth.
Her vigor flows like tides into my blood,
Giving me strength erect against her hate,
Her bigness sweeps my being like a flood.
Yet, as a rebel fronts a king in state,
I stand within her walls with not a shred
Of terror, malice, not a word of jeer.
Darkly I gaze into the days ahead,
And see her might and granite wonders there,
Predicted by the things that are,
In the path of the inevitable.

Public domain (published 1921). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. The volta arrives at line 9 with the turn from hatred/love to the longer vision of civilizational decline.

Themes

The paradox of loving a country that oppresses you, and finding strength and testing in that very oppression. The final vision of America's inevitable decline — its 'treasures sinking in the sand' — gives the speaker a dark equanimity: it will not last.

Historical Context

Published in The Liberator in 1921. McKay's complex relationship with America — hating its racism while being energized by its contradictions — is a recurring theme in his work.

About Claude McKay

Claude McKay was born on September 15, 1889, in Sunny Ville, Jamaica, and emigrated to the United States in 1912, becoming one of the earliest and most important voices of the Harlem Renaissance. His sonnet 'If We Must Die,' written during the Red Summer of 1919, became an anthem of Black resistance and was later cited by Winston Churchill during World War II. He died in Chicago in 1948.
