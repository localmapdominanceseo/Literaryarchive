Goblin Market (Extract)

by Christina Rossetti

Written 1859 Published 1862 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1862, Goblin Market is Christina Rossetti's narrative poem about two sisters and the goblin merchants who sell dangerous fruit. Laura eats the fruit and begins to pine; Lizzie sacrifices herself to bring their juices back to save her sister.

Quick Answer

Goblin Market is a poem by Christina Rossetti, published in 1862. Two sisters hear the goblin merchants calling — come buy, come buy. Laura eats their fruit and pines for more. Lizzie goes to the goblins and lets them press their fruit against her but does not open her lips. She returns to Laura and her sister is healed.

Morning and evening
Maids heard the goblins cry:
'Come buy our orchard fruits,
Come buy, come buy:
Apples and quinces,
Lemons and oranges,
Plump unpecked cherries,
Melons and raspberries,
Bloom-down-cheeked peaches,
Swart-headed mulberries,
Wild free-born cranberries,
Crab-apples, dewberries,
Pine-apples, blackberries,
Apricots, strawberries;—
All ripe together
In summer weather,—
Come buy, come buy:
Sweet to tongue and sound to eye;
Come buy, come buy.'

Public domain. Published 1862.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Long irregular lines with various rhyme schemes, the form as unpredictable and sensuous as the goblin market itself.

Themes

Temptation, redemption through sacrificial love, and female solidarity.

Historical Context

Published in Goblin Market and Other Poems (1862), Rossetti's first collection.

About Christina Rossetti

Christina Rossetti was born on December 5, 1830, in London, the youngest of the four Rossetti children. She is the most admired Victorian woman poet, known for her lyrical grace, her devout Anglican faith, and her poems of love, death, and longing. Her first major collection Goblin Market and Other Poems (1862) was immediately celebrated. Her brother Dante Gabriel Rossetti was a central figure in the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. She died in London in 1894.
