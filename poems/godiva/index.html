Godiva

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1840 Published 1842 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1842, this poem tells the legend of Lady Godiva, who rode naked through Coventry to persuade her husband to remit a burdensome tax on the townspeople. The townspeople kept their windows shut except for one Peeping Tom.

Quick Answer

"Godiva" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1842. Lady Godiva, shamed by her husband's cruelty to his people, rides naked through Coventry after securing his promise to remit the tax if she does so. The townspeople close their shutters out of respect. One man looks — and is struck blind. The earl keeps his promise.

I waited for the train at Coventry;
I hung with grooms and porters on the bridge,
To watch the three tall spires; and there I shaped
The city's ancient legend into this:

Not only we, the latest seed of Time,
New men, that in the flying of a wheel
Cry down the past, not only we, that prate
Of rights and wrongs, have loved the people well,
And loathed to see them overtax'd; but she
Did more, and underwent, and overcame,
The woman of a thousand summers back,
Godiva, wife to that grim Earl, who ruled
In Coventry: for when he laid a tax
Upon his town, and all the mothers brought
Their children, clamouring, 'If we pay, we starve!'
She sought her lord, and found him, where he strode
About the hall, among his dogs, alone,
His beard a foot before him, and his hair
A yard behind.

Analysis

Form and Structure

One hundred and twelve lines of blank verse.

Themes

Self-sacrifice for the common good, civic virtue, and the power of a woman's courage to shame male tyranny into justice.

Historical Context

Published in 1842. The Godiva legend has been localized in Coventry since at least the thirteenth century, though the historical basis is debated. Tennyson treats it as a straightforward heroic narrative.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
