Bright Star

by John Keats

Written 1819 Published 1838 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1819 and published posthumously in 1838, this sonnet expresses Keats's wish to have the permanence of a star while still experiencing human love — to be as steadfast as the North Star, but while lying in his beloved's arms, not in cold isolation.

Quick Answer

"Bright Star" is a sonnet by John Keats, written around 1819 and published posthumously in 1838. The speaker wishes he were as steadfast as the bright star — but not in the star's isolated, cold watchfulness. He wants that same permanence while lying pillowed on his beloved's breast, awake forever to feel the gentle rise and fall of her breathing, or else to die in that sweet moment.

Bright star, would I were stedfast as thou art—
Not in lone splendour hung aloft the night
And watching, with eternal lids apart,
Like nature's patient, sleepless Eremite,
The moving waters at their priestlike task
Of pure ablution round earth's human shores,
Or gazing on the new soft-fallen mask
Of snow upon the mountains and the moors—
No—yet still stedfast, still unchangeable,
Pillow'd upon my fair love's ripening breast,
To feel for ever its soft fall and swell,
Awake for ever in a sweet unrest,
Still, still to hear her tender-taken breath,
And so live ever—or else swoon to death.

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter with the rhyme scheme ABAB CDCD EFEF GG, structured as an octave describing the star's cold immortality, then a sestet describing the human warmth the speaker desires instead, ending in the paradox of eternal wakefulness or death.

Themes

The conflict between immortality and human warmth, with the speaker rejecting the star's kind of permanence — beautiful but cold, isolated, and inhuman — in favor of a permanence that includes love's physical closeness. The poem ends with the only resolution possible: either to be held forever in that moment or to die, suggesting that love's intensity cannot survive indefinite extension.

Historical Context

Keats wrote the poem around 1819, near the end of his brief creative life, during his relationship with Fanny Brawne. He was already aware of his declining health from tuberculosis. The poem was first published in 1838, seventeen years after his death at twenty-five, in a collection edited by his friend Richard Monckton Milnes.

About John Keats

John Keats was born on October 31, 1795, in London, England, and died of tuberculosis at just twenty-five, yet in a creative burst during 1819 alone produced some of the most celebrated odes in the English language, including 'Ode to a Nightingale' and 'Ode on a Grecian Urn.' His poetry is known for its rich sensory detail and its preoccupation with beauty, mortality, and the relationship between art and permanence. He died in Rome in 1821.
