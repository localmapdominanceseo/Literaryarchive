Duns Scotus's Oxford

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1879 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1879 and published posthumously in 1918, this sonnet mourns the encroachment of Victorian suburbs on the medieval Oxford Hopkins loved — but then celebrates the fact that the medieval philosopher Duns Scotus walked these streets and thought here.

Quick Answer

Duns Scotus's Oxford is a poem by Hopkins, published posthumously in 1918. Towery city and branchy between towers — whose base is really rock and river — Cuckoo-echoing, bell-swarmed, lark-charmed, rook-racked, river-rounded. But thou hast a quaint and crookèd ornament — even Duns Scotus who of all men most sways my spirits to peace.

Towery city and branchy between towers;
Cuckoo-echoing, bell-swarmèd, lark-charmèd, rook-racked, river-rounded;
The dapple-eared lily below thee; that country and town did
Once encounter in, here coped and poisèd powers;

Thou hast a base and brickish skirt there, sours
That neighbour-nature thy grey beauty is grounded
Best in; graceless growth, thou hast confounded
Rural rural keeping—folk, flocks, and flowers.

Yet ah! this air I gather and I release
He lived on; these weeds and waters, these walls are what
He haunted who of all men most sways my spirits to peace;

Of realty the rarest-veinèd unraveller; a not
Rivalled insight, be rival Italy or Greece;
Who fired France for Mary without spot.

Public domain. Written 1879, published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in sprung rhythm.

Themes

Grief at Victorian suburban expansion destroying medieval Oxford, consoled by the connection to Duns Scotus — whose philosophy Hopkins deeply admired.

Historical Context

Written March 1879 when Hopkins was at Oxford. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
