Sweet and Low

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1847 Published 1847 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1847 as a cradle song within The Princess, this lullaby asks the west wind and the tide to blow soft and rest while the father is away at sea, until he comes home to his baby.

Quick Answer

"Sweet and Low" is a lullaby by Alfred Lord Tennyson, published in 1847 in The Princess. It addresses the west wind and the tide, asking them to blow softly and let the child sleep while the father is away at sea — and to bring him home safely over the sea to his baby.

Sweet and low, sweet and low,
Wind of the western sea,
Low, low, breathe and blow,
Wind of the western sea!
Over the rolling waters go,
Come from the dying moon, and blow,
Blow him again to me;
While my little one, while my pretty one, sleeps.

Sleep and rest, sleep and rest,
Father will come to thee soon;
Rest, rest, on mother's breast,
Father will come to thee soon;
Father will come to his babe in the nest,
Silver sails all out of the west
Under the silver moon:
Sleep, my little one, sleep, my pretty one, sleep.

Public domain. Published 1847 in The Princess.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two six-line stanzas with a gentle musical rhythm that has made the poem one of the most frequently set to music of all Victorian lyrics.

Themes

Domestic tenderness and the anxiety of waiting for a loved one to return from sea. The poem captures Victorian middle-class family sentiment at its most refined.

Historical Context

Published as a song within The Princess (1847). Set to music by Joseph Barnby in 1863 and by many other composers, becoming one of the most popular Victorian parlor songs.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
