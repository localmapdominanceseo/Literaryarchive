Ask Me No More

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1847 Published 1847 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1847 as a song in The Princess, this lyric urges the beloved to ask no more questions — for the answer is silence, the last desire, and the spoken word is death.

Quick Answer

"Ask Me No More" is a lyric by Tennyson, published in 1847. Ask me no more: the moon may draw the sea; the cloud may stoop from heaven and take the shape with fold to fold, of mountain or of cape; but O too fond, when have I answer'd thee? Ask me no more.

Ask me no more: the moon may draw the sea;
The cloud may stoop from heaven and take the shape
With fold to fold, of mountain or of cape;
But O too fond, when have I answer'd thee?
Ask me no more.

Ask me no more: what answer should I give?
I love not hollow cheek or faded eye:
Yet, O my friend, I will not have thee die!
Ask me no more, lest I should bid thee live;
Ask me no more.

Ask me no more: thy fate and mine are seal'd:
I strove against the stream and all in vain:
Let the great river take me to the main:
No more, dear love, for at a touch I yield;
Ask me no more.

Public domain. Published 1847 in The Princess.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three stanzas of eight lines with a repeated refrain.

Themes

The silence at the heart of love — the lover who cannot or will not answer the beloved's questions, the exhaustion of passion.

Historical Context

Published as a song in The Princess (1847). One of Tennyson's most purely musical short lyrics.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
