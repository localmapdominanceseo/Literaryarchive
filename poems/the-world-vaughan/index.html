The World

by Henry Vaughan

Written 1650 Published 1650 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1650 in Silex Scintillans, this poem opens with one of the most celebrated lines in seventeenth-century poetry: 'I saw Eternity the other night / Like a great Ring of pure and endless light.' It then describes the futile activities of those who prefer the world to eternity.

Quick Answer

"The World" is a poem by Henry Vaughan, published in 1650. The speaker saw Eternity the other night like a great Ring of pure and endless light, calm as it was bright. Below it, Time like a vast shadow moved. The world and its lovers busy themselves with doting, plotting, and accumulating — while the Ring of Eternity shines above them all.

I saw Eternity the other night,
Like a great Ring of pure and endless light,
All calm, as it was bright;
And round beneath it, Time in hours, days, years,
Driv'n by the spheres
Like a vast shadow mov'd; in which the world
And all her train were hurl'd.
The doting Lover in his quaintest strain
Did there complain;
Near him, his Lute, his fancy, and his flights,
Wits sour delights,
With gloves, and knots, the silly snares of pleasure,
Yet his dear Treasure
All scattered lay, while he his eyes did pour
Upon a flower.

The darksome Statesman hung with weights and woe,
Like a thick midnight-fog mov'd there so slow,
He did not stay, nor go;
Condemning thoughts (like sad eclipses) scowl
Upon his soul,
And Clouds of crying witnesses without
Pursued him with one shout.
Yet digged the Mole, and lest his ways be found,
Work'd under ground,
Where he did clutch his prey; But one did see
That policie;
Churches and altars fed him; Perjuries
Were gnats and flies;
It rain'd about him bloud and tears, but he
Drank them as free.

Public domain. Published 1650 in Silex Scintillans.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four stanzas of varying length with irregular rhyme, the poem's formal variety matching the contrast between the perfect circle of Eternity and the chaotic activity below it.

Themes

The contrast between eternal divine reality and the futile busyness of worldly life. The poem catalogues the types of worldly men — the lover, the statesman, the miser — all moving in Time's shadow while Eternity's ring shines above them.

Historical Context

Published in Silex Scintillans (1650). The opening two lines are among the most quoted in the seventeenth-century tradition, influencing later writers from Blake to Thomas Traherne.

About Henry Vaughan

Henry Vaughan was born in 1621 in Brecknockshire, Wales, and became one of the finest poets of the Metaphysical tradition, deeply influenced by George Herbert. His collection Silex Scintillans (1650, 1655) contains some of the most beautiful devotional poems in English, including 'The World,' 'The Night,' and 'Regeneration.' He worked as a physician in Wales and died in 1695.
