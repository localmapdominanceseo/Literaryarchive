The Pilgrimage

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 in The Temple, this allegorical poem traces a spiritual journey through a Cave of Desperation, a Hill of Care, a Grove of Passions, the Wild of Passion, and finally to the place where Death meets the speaker — who finds it is the inn where he was promised rest.

Quick Answer

"The Pilgrimage" is a poem by George Herbert, published in 1633. The speaker travels through the Cave of Desperation, up the Hill of Care, through the Grove of Passions, and across the Wild of Passion — only to find Death waiting at the end. But Death tells him this is the Inn where he shall rest forever, for the land of Spices is just beyond.

I travelled on, seeing the hill, where lay
My expectation.
A long it was and weary way.
The gloomy cave of Desperation
I left on th'one, and on the other side
The rock of Pride.

And so I came to Fancy's meadow strowed
With many a flower:
Fain would I here have made abode,
But I was quickened by my hour.
So to Care's castle on I passed the fort,
And so the grove of those who hate no sport.

And more of Passions and things besides.
At last I came
To th' Hill of Grief, where I was tried,
And found that nought but death and fame
Could give me any standing. There I found
A gallant flower,

The Crown Imperial: Sure said I,
Peace at the last.
Yea, but the way to it and the plain
Through which I pass'd
Was rugged. And such a lofty height
Needs the whole day for to be bright.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five six-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABABCC rhyme, the ballad-like form suited to the journey narrative.

Themes

The Christian life as a pilgrimage through suffering toward rest in death. The poem's reversal — discovering that death is the Inn, not the end — is Herbert's characteristic transformation of apparent defeat into victory.

Historical Context

Published in The Temple (1633). The allegorical pilgrimage form draws on Bunyan's tradition (though Pilgrim's Progress was not published until 1678) and the medieval allegory of Piers Plowman.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
