The Eagle

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1851 Published 1851 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1851, this six-line poem describes an eagle clutching a crag with crooked hands, close to the sun, watching the wrinkled sea — then falling like a thunderbolt.

Quick Answer

The Eagle is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1851. The eagle clasps the crag with crooked hands, close to the sun in lonely lands, ringed with the azure world. The wrinkled sea beneath him crawls; he watches from his mountain walls, and like a thunderbolt he falls.

He clasps the crag with crooked hands;
Close to the sun in lonely lands,
Ring'd with the azure world, he stands.

The wrinkled sea beneath him crawls;
He watches from his mountain walls,
And like a thunderbolt he falls.

Public domain. Published 1851.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two tercets of iambic tetrameter with AAA BBB rhyme — six lines of almost perfect compression.

Themes

Power and solitude — the eagle commands his landscape from absolute height.

Historical Context

Published in 1851. One of the most anthologized short poems in English for its extreme compression and visual power.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
