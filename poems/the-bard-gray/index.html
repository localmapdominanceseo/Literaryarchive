The Bard (Extract)

by Thomas Gray

Written 1757 Published 1757 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1757, this ode is spoken by the last surviving Welsh bard who confronts King Edward I after his conquest of Wales and prophesies the eventual downfall of the Plantagenet dynasty before leaping to his death.

Quick Answer

"The Bard" is an ode by Thomas Gray, published in 1757. The last surviving Welsh bard, with disheveled hair and beard, confronts Edward I and prophesies the disasters that await the English kings descended from Edward — the Wars of the Roses, the execution of Richard II — before plunging from his crag into the river below.

'Ruin seize thee, ruthless king!
Confusion on thy banners wait,
Tho' fanned by Conquest's crimson wing,
They mock the air with idle state.
Helm, nor hauberk's twisted mail,
Nor even thy virtues, tyrant, shall avail
To save thy secret soul from nightly fears,
From Cambria's curse, from Cambria's tears!'
Such were the sounds that o'er the crested pride
Of the first Edward scattered wild dismay,
As down the steep of Snowdon's shaggy side
He wound with toilsome march his long array.
Stout Glo'ster stood aghast in speechless trance:
To arms! cried Mortimer, and couched his quivering lance.

On a rock, whose haughty brow
Frowns o'er old Conway's foaming flood,
Robed in the sable garb of woe,
With haggard eyes the Poet stood;
(Loose his beard and hoary hair
Streamed, like a meteor, to the troubled air)
And with a Master's hand and Prophet's fire,
Struck the deep sorrows of his lyre.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three parts in irregular Pindaric ode structure, each consisting of strophe, antistrophe, and epode, with Gray's characteristic dense, allusive verse.

Themes

The power of poetic prophecy and the endurance of Welsh (Celtic) cultural identity against political conquest. The bard embodies the idea that poetry outlives political power.

Historical Context

Published in 1757 alongside 'The Progress of Poesy.' Inspired by the historical tradition that Edward I ordered the execution of Welsh bards after his conquest of Wales.

About Thomas Gray

Thomas Gray was born on December 26, 1716, in London, England, and spent most of his adult life as a scholar at Cambridge University. Though he published very little poetry, his 'Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard' (1751) became one of the most widely read and imitated poems in the English language. He died in Cambridge in 1771.
