I Heard a Fly Buzz—When I Died

by Emily Dickinson

Written 1862 Published 1896 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1862 and published posthumously in 1896, this poem is narrated by a dead person recounting the moment of their death, focusing not on any grand spiritual vision but on the intrusion of a buzzing fly at the final moment, which blocks out the light.

Quick Answer

"I Heard a Fly Buzz—When I Died" is a poem by Emily Dickinson, written around 1862 and published posthumously in 1896. Narrated from beyond death, the poem describes the moment of dying: the room is solemn, the family gathered, the speaker ready for a divine presence — but instead, a fly buzzes between the speaker and the light, and that is the last thing perceived before consciousness ends.

I heard a Fly buzz—when I died—
The Stillness in the Room
Was like the Stillness in the Air—
Between the Heaves of Storm—

The Eyes around—had wrung them dry—
And Breaths were gathering firm
For that last Onset—when the King
Be witnessed—in the Room—

I willed my Keepsakes—Signed away
What portion of me be
Assignable—and then it was
There interposed a Fly—

With Blue—uncertain—stumbling Buzz—
Between the light—and me—
And then the Windows failed—and then
I could not see to see—

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains in common meter with slant rhyme, spoken from beyond death in a calm, matter-of-fact tone that makes the poem's subject matter more unsettling, not less.

Themes

The poem subverts the expected deathbed scene by replacing a divine final vision with a mundane, buzzing fly. Death does not arrive with ceremony or revelation but with the intrusion of something small and distracting. The fly's 'Blue—uncertain stumbling Buzz' suggests corruption and decay rather than transcendence, and the final darkness is total rather than transformative.

Historical Context

Dickinson wrote the poem around 1862, a period of intense creative productivity during which she wrote hundreds of poems. The poem was not published until 1896, ten years after her death, in the third series of posthumous collections. It is now one of her most widely studied poems for its spare, devastating reversal of conventional deathbed imagery.

About Emily Dickinson

Emily Dickinson was born on December 10, 1830, in Amherst, Massachusetts, and spent nearly her entire life there. Though she wrote nearly 1,800 poems, fewer than a dozen were published during her lifetime. Her work, marked by unconventional capitalization, slant rhyme, and dashes, was largely discovered and published after her death in 1886. She is now considered one of the most important and original voices in American poetry, known for her meditations on death, immortality, nature, and the inner life.
