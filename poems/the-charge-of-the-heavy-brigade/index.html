The Charge of the Heavy Brigade

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1882 Published 1882 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1882, this poem celebrates the Charge of the Heavy Brigade at the Battle of Balaclava (1854), a less famous but more successful engagement than the Charge of the Light Brigade — the Heavy Brigade broke through the Russian lines.

Quick Answer

"The Charge of the Heavy Brigade" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1882. At the Battle of Balaclava, three hundred of the Heavy Brigade charged uphill into the Russian forces. Three times they went into the Russian host and three times they scattered them. Honor the charge they made!

The charge of the gallant three hundred, the Heavy Brigade!
Down the hill, down the hill, thousands of Russians,
Thousands of horsemen, drew to the valley—and stayed;
For Scarlett and Scarlett's three hundred were riding by
When the points of the Russian lances arose in the sky;
And he call'd 'Left wheel into line!' and they wheel'd and obey'd.
Then he look'd at the host that had halted he knew not why,
And he turn'd half round, and he bade his trumpeter sound
To the charge, and he rode on ahead, as he waved his blade
To the gallant three hundred whose glory will never die—
'Follow!' and up the hill, up the hill, up the hill,
Follow'd the Heavy Brigade.

Public domain. Published 1882.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five sections of varying length in driving irregular meter.

Themes

Military heroism and national pride — the Heavy Brigade's successful charge as the model of British courage and discipline.

Historical Context

Published in 1882 in Tiresias and Other Poems. Written partly in response to criticisms that Tennyson had glorified the failed Charge of the Light Brigade — this poem celebrates a successful charge.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
