The Primrose

by John Donne

Written 1601 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1633, this poem describes walking on Primrose Hill and meditating on whether to wish a woman to be five-petaled (true woman) or four/six-petaled (too much or too little of woman) — concluding that a true woman, who is all, is the best.

Quick Answer

"The Primrose" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. Walking on Primrose Hill, the speaker meditates on the primrose's petals — five petals make a true woman, four makes her too little feminine, six too much. He would have her be all woman, the true number five, neither too much nor too little.

Upon this Primrose hill,
Where, if Heav'n would distil
A shoure of raine, each severall drop might goe
To his owne primrose, and grow Manna so;
And where their forme, and their infinite
Make a terrestriall Galaxie,
As the small starres doe in the skie:
I walke to finde a true Love; and I see
That 'tis not a mere woman, that is shee,
But must, or more, or lesse than woman bee.

Yet know I not, which flower
I wish; a sixe, or foure;
For should my true-Love lesse than woman bee,
She were scarse any thing; and then, should she
Be more than woman, shee would get above
All thought of sexe, and thinke to move
My heart to study her, and not to love;
Both these were monsters; Since there must reside
Falsehood in woman, I could more abide,
She were by art, than Nature falsifyed.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five six-line stanzas of iambic tetrameter with ABABCC rhyme, the botanical conceit sustained throughout.

Themes

A meditation on what constitutes true femininity, using the number of primrose petals as a numerological conceit — five is the perfect number for a woman, matching the five fingers of the hand.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633 in Songs and Sonnets. One of Donne's more playful Metaphysical conceits.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
