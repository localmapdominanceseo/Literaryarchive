Ave atque Vale (Extract)

by Algernon Charles Swinburne

Written 1867 Published 1868 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1868, this elegy for Charles Baudelaire addresses the dead poet directly — hail and farewell — acknowledging their kinship in the religion of beauty and suffering.

Quick Answer

Ave atque Vale is Swinburne's elegy for Baudelaire, published in 1868. Shall I strew on thee rose or rue or laurel? Or quiet sea-flower moulded by the sea? Hail and farewell — from lands of sun and sorrow, from twilight and the dead days, from the land of dreams.

Shall I strew on thee rose or rue or laurel,
Brother, on this that was the veil of thee?
Or quiet sea-flower moulded by the sea,
Or simplest growth of meadow-sweet or sorrel,
Such as the summer-sleepy Dryads weave,
Waked up by snow-soft sudden rains at eve?
Or wilt thou rather, as on earth before,
Half-faded fiery blossoms, pale with heat
And full of bitter summer, but more sweet
To thee than gleanings of a northern shore
Trod by no tropic feet?

Public domain. Published 1868.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Nineteen nine-line stanzas in a complex rhyme scheme, modeled on Catullus's own ave atque vale elegy.

Themes

The kinship of poets across death and the mourning of a fellow poet. Swinburne acknowledges Baudelaire as his master in the religion of beauty and pain.

Historical Context

Published in 1868. The title is from Catullus 101, his elegy for his brother. Baudelaire died in 1867.

About Algernon Charles Swinburne

Algernon Charles Swinburne was born on April 5, 1837, in London, and became one of the most technically accomplished and musically dazzling of all Victorian poets. His Poems and Ballads (1866) caused an immediate scandal for its paganism, eroticism, and attacks on conventional morality. His verse drama Atalanta in Calydon (1865) established his reputation. He died at Putney in 1909.
