The Brook's Song

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1855 Published 1855 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1855 as an interpolated song within The Brook, this famous lyric is spoken by the brook itself — I chatter, I bubble, I babble, and I flow — celebrating the brook's eternal flow against the brevity of human lives.

Quick Answer

"The Brook's Song" is a lyric by Tennyson, published in 1855. The brook speaks: I chatter over stony ways, in little sharps and trebles, I bubble into eddying bays, I babble on the pebbles. For men may come and men may go, but I go on for ever.

I come from haunts of coot and hern,
I make a sudden sally
And sparkle out among the fern,
To bicker down a valley.

By thirty hills I hurry down,
Or slip between the ridges,
By twenty thorps, a little town,
And half a hundred bridges.

Till last by Philip's farm I flow
To join the brimming river,
For men may come and men may go,
But I go on for ever.

I chatter over stony ways,
In little sharps and trebles,
I bubble into eddying bays,
I babble on the pebbles.

With many a curve my banks I fret
By many a field and fallow,
And many a fairy foreland set
With willow-weed and mallow.

I chatter, chatter, as I flow
To join the brimming river,
For men may come and men may go,
But I go on for ever.

Public domain. Published 1855.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six stanzas of eight lines each in alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter, the musical form enacting the brook's continuous movement.

Themes

The contrast between the permanence of nature and the transience of human life — the brook endures while men come and go.

Historical Context

Published within the poem The Brook (1855). The brook's refrain — 'For men may come and men may go, but I go on for ever' — became one of Tennyson's most quoted lines.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
