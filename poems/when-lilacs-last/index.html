When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd (Opening)

by Walt Whitman

Written 1865 Published 1865 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written in 1865 following the assassination of Abraham Lincoln and published the same year, this is the opening movement of Whitman's great elegy for Lincoln, establishing its three central symbols: the ever-returning lilac, the drooping western star (Lincoln), and the song of the hermit thrush.

Quick Answer

"When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd" is Walt Whitman's elegy for Abraham Lincoln, written and published in 1865. The poem opens with three recurring symbols: the lilac blooming annually in the dooryard, the western star (Lincoln) that fell in the night, and a hermit thrush singing in a swamp. These three strands weave through the poem as Whitman processes the grief of the nation alongside his own.

When lilacs last in the dooryard bloom'd,
And the great star early droop'd in the western sky in the night,
I mourn'd, and yet shall mourn with ever-returning spring.

Ever-returning spring, trinity sure to me you bring,
Lilac blooming perennial and drooping star in the west,
And thought of him I love.

O powerful western fallen star!
O shades of night—O moody, tearful night!
O great star disappear'd—O the black murk that hides the star!
O cruel hands that hold me powerless—O helpless soul of me!
O harsh surrounding cloud that will not free my soul.

In the dooryard fronting an old farm-house near the white-wash'd palings,
Stands the lilac-bush tall-growing with heart-shaped leaves of rich green,
With many a pointed blossom rising delicate, with the perfume strong I love,
With every leaf a miracle—and from this bush in the dooryard,
With delicate-color'd blossoms and heart-shaped leaves of rich green,
A sprig with its flower I break.

Public domain. Published 1865 in Drum-Taps.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Sixteen sections of long free verse lines, structured as a meditation moving through grief toward acceptance, much like the German lied or musical song cycle in structure. The poem weaves its three symbols through multiple returns rather than advancing linearly.

Themes

Public grief, the cycle of death and renewal in nature, and the role of the poet as a mourner who can transform national loss into art. The lilac's annual return enacts nature's indifference to individual death while simultaneously offering consolation. The hermit thrush's song of death-praise eventually becomes the poem's own song.

Historical Context

Whitman wrote the poem in the weeks after Lincoln's assassination on April 14, 1865. He had admired Lincoln deeply and had occasionally seen him in Washington. The poem was added to Leaves of Grass in the section called 'Memories of President Lincoln' and is considered one of the greatest elegies in American literature.

About Walt Whitman

Walt Whitman was born on May 31, 1819, on Long Island, New York. His landmark collection Leaves of Grass, first published in 1855 and expanded throughout his life, broke from traditional verse forms in favor of free verse and sweeping, democratic subject matter. Whitman served as a nurse during the American Civil War, an experience that shaped much of his later work, including his elegy for Abraham Lincoln. He is widely regarded, alongside Emily Dickinson, as one of the founders of a distinctly American poetic voice.
