Thou Art Indeed Just, Lord

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1889 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1889, the last year of Hopkins's life, and published posthumously in 1918, this sonnet quotes Jeremiah's complaint against God and adds Hopkins's own — he cannot create poetry while sinners thrive, and begs God to send his roots rain.

Quick Answer

Thou Art Indeed Just, Lord is a poem by Hopkins, published posthumously in 1918. Thou art indeed just, Lord, if I contend with thee — but why must sinners' ways prosper? The speaker strives and fails. Birds build but not he. Time's eunuch, and not breed one work that wakes. Mine, O thou lord of life, send my roots rain.

Thou art indeed just, Lord, if I contend
With thee; but, sir, so what I plead is just.
Why do sinners' ways prosper? and why must
Disappointment all I endeavour end?
    Wert thou my enemy, O thou my friend,
How wouldst thou worse, I wonder, than thou dost
Defeat, thwart me? Oh, the sots and thralls of lust
Do in spare hours more thrive than I that spend,
Sir, life upon thy cause. See, banks and brakes
Now, leavèd how thick! lacèd they are again
With fretty chervil, look, and fresh wind shakes
Them; birds build—but not I build; no, but strain,
Time's eunuch, and not breed one work that wakes.
Mine, O thou lord of life, send my roots rain.

Public domain. Written 1889, published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet with a Latin epigraph from Jeremiah 12, the poem's last line one of Hopkins's most moving.

Themes

The failure of poetic creativity and the sense of being abandoned by God — the speaker cannot write, cannot breed any work that wakes, while the world thrives around him.

Historical Context

Written March 17, 1889, three months before Hopkins's death from typhoid. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
