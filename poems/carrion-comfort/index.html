Carrion Comfort

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1885 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1885 and published posthumously in 1918, this sonnet resolves not to feast on despair — not to choose not to be — and describes wrestling with God like Jacob, then realising the wrestling was for his own good.

Quick Answer

Carrion Comfort is a poem by Hopkins, published posthumously in 1918. Not, I'll not, carrion comfort, Despair, not feast on thee. Not untwist these last strands of man in me — O thou terrible, why wouldst thou rude on me thy wring-world right foot rock? He cheers: me heaved heave-heartedly on my being. I kissed the rod. The speaker kissed it, not knowing it was God's hand.

Not, I'll not, carrion comfort, Despair, not feast on thee;
Not untwist—slack they may be—these last strands of man
In me ór, most weary, cry I can no more. I can;
Can something, hope, wish day come, not choose not to be.
But ah, but O thou terrible, why wouldst thou rude on me
Thy wring-world right foot rock? lay a lionlimb against me? scan
With darksome devouring eyes my bruisèd bones? and fan,
O in turns of tempest, me heaped there; me frantic to avoid thee and flee?

Why? That my chaff might fly; my grain lie, sheer and clear.
Nay in all that toil, that coil, since (seems) I kissed the rod,
Hand rather, my heart lo! lapped strength, stole joy, would laugh, chéer.
Cheer whom though? The hero whose heaven-handling flung me, foot tród
Me? or me that fought him? O which one? is it each one? That night, that year
Of now done darkness I wretch lay wrestling with (my God!) my God.

Public domain. Written c.1885, published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in sprung rhythm, one of the 'terrible sonnets.'

Themes

The resolve not to give in to despair, and the wrestling with God that turns out to be God's way of strengthening the soul — only recognized as such in retrospect.

Historical Context

Written c.1885 during Hopkins's years of spiritual desolation in Dublin. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
