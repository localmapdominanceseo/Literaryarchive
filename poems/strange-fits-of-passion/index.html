Strange Fits of Passion Have I Known

by William Wordsworth

Written 1799 Published 1800 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1800 in Lyrical Ballads, this is the first of the Lucy poems — the speaker rides toward Lucy's cottage and watches the moon drop toward her roof, and has a sudden irrational fear that she may be dead.

Quick Answer

Strange Fits of Passion is a poem by Wordsworth, published in 1800. The speaker rides to Lucy's cottage, watching the moon drop lower. When the moon had dropped behind the roof, a strange thought shot through his mind — what if Lucy should be dead?

Strange fits of passion have I known:
And I will dare to tell,
But in the Lover's ear alone,
What once to me befell.

When she I loved looked every day
Fresh as a rose in June,
I to her cottage bent my way,
Beneath an evening-moon.

Upon the moon I fixed my eye,
All over the wide lea;
With quickening pace my horse drew nigh
Those paths so dear to me.

And now we reached the orchard-plot;
And, as we climbed the hill,
The sinking moon to Lucy's cot
Came near, and nearer still.

In one of those sweet dreams I slept,
Kind Nature's gentlest boon!
And all the while my eyes I kept
On the descending moon.

My horse moved on; hoof after hoof
He raised, and never stopped:
When down behind the cottage roof,
At once, the bright moon dropped!

What fond and wayward thoughts will slide
Into a Lover's head!
'O mercy!' to myself I cried,
'If Lucy should be dead!'

Public domain. Published 1800 in Lyrical Ballads.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five four-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABAB rhyme.

Themes

The sudden, irrational fear of loss — the moon dropping below the roof of the beloved's cottage triggers a thought of her death that the speaker cannot explain.

Historical Context

Published in the second edition of Lyrical Ballads (1800). First of the five Lucy poems.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
