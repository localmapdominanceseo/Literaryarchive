The Hollow Men

by T.S. Eliot

Written 1925 Published 1925 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1925, this poem is spoken by hollow men who are the lost souls of those who failed to act decisively in life — neither good enough for Heaven nor bad enough for Hell. The poem ends with the famous lines about the world ending not with a bang but a whimper.

Quick Answer

"The Hollow Men" is a poem by T.S. Eliot, published in 1925. The hollow men are stuffed men, leaning together, with voices quiet and meaningless. They are those who crossed to death's kingdom without choosing good or evil decisively. The poem ends in the hollow men's twilight kingdom as their prayers disintegrate into nursery rhyme, and the world ends not with a bang but a whimper.

We are the hollow men

Our dried voices, when

As wind in dry grass

In our dry cellar

Paralysed force, gesture without motion;

Those who have crossed

Remember us—if at all—not as lost

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five sections of varying length in free verse, fragmenting progressively until the final section where a children's rhyme intrudes into the speaker's prayer, enacting the collapse of meaning.

Themes

Spiritual paralysis and the failure of will — the hollow men are those who neither chose good nor evil decisively and so exist in a limbo between life and death. The ending, 'Not with a bang but a whimper,' has become one of the most quoted lines in English poetry.

Historical Context

Published in 1925, a year after The Waste Land's enormous impact. Eliot drew on Conrad's Heart of Darkness, Dante's Purgatorio, and Guy Fawkes Day traditions.

About T.S. Eliot

Thomas Stearns Eliot was born on September 26, 1888, in St. Louis, Missouri, and became the most influential poet of English-language Modernism after moving to England and eventually becoming a British citizen. His landmark poem The Waste Land (1922) and the earlier 'The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock' (1915) redefined what poetry could do with fragmented form, multiple languages, and literary allusion. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1948 and died in London in 1965.
