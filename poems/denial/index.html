Denial

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 in The Temple, this poem enacts formal disorder to express spiritual disorder — each stanza ends with an incomplete, unrhymed final line until the last stanza, where the rhyme is completed as the speaker asks God to restore both his prayers and his poem.

Quick Answer

"Denial" is a poem by George Herbert, published in 1633. The speaker prays but God does not hear; his heart breaks and his sighs scatter — and the poem's formal structure enacts this disorder by leaving the final line of each stanza without a rhyme. Only when the speaker asks God to mend his rhyme does the final stanza's rhyme complete.

When my devotions could not pierce
Thy silent eares;
Then was my heart broken, as was my verse:
My breast was full of fears
And disorder:

My bent thoughts, like a brittle bow,
Did flie asunder:
Each took his way; some would to pleasures go,
Some to the warres and thunder
Of alarms:

As good go any where, they say,
As to benumme
Both knees and heart, in crying night and day,
Come, come, my God, O come,
But no hearing:

O that thou shouldst give dust a tongue
To crie to thee,
And then not heare it crying! all day long
My heart was in my knee,
But no hearing:

Therefore my soul lay out of sight,
Untuned, unstrung:
My feeble spirit, unable to look right,
Like a nipt blossome, hung
Discontented.

O cheer and tune my heartlesse breast,
Deferre no time;
That so thy favours granting my request,
They and my minde may chime,
And mend my ryme.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five six-line stanzas with ABABCC rhyme in the first four lines — but the final line of each stanza deliberately breaks the rhyme scheme until the last stanza, when God answering the prayer restores the rhyme.

Themes

Spiritual desolation and the formal enactment of disorder — Herbert uses the poem's structure to embody his subject, so that the reader experiences the broken rhyme as the speaker experiences the broken relationship with God.

Historical Context

Published in The Temple (1633). One of Herbert's most sophisticated formal experiments, using prosody as theology.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
