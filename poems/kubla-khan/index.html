Kubla Khan

by Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Written 1797 Published 1816 English Romanticism Public Domain

Composed around 1797 and published in 1816, this poem describes a vision of the pleasure palace of the Mongol emperor Kubla Khan, then shifts to the poet's lament that he cannot fully recapture or complete the vision, making the poem as much about the nature of poetic imagination as about any exotic landscape.

Quick Answer

"Kubla Khan" is a poem by Samuel Taylor Coleridge, composed around 1797 and published in 1816. It describes Kubla Khan's magnificent pleasure palace at Xanadu, with its sacred river, deep romantic chasm, and gardens, before shifting to the poet's wish that he could recover a vision of an Abyssinian maid whose music might allow him to rebuild the palace in song.

In Xanadu did Kubla Khan
A stately pleasure-dome decree:
Where Alph, the sacred river, ran
Through caverns measureless to man
Down to a sunless sea.
So twice five miles of fertile ground
With walls and towers were girdled round:
And there were gardens bright with sinuous rills,
Where blossomed many an incense-bearing tree;
And here were forests ancient as the hills,
Enfolding sunny spots of greenery.

But oh! that deep romantic chasm which slanted
Down the green hill athwart a cedarn cover!
A savage place! as holy and enchanted
As e'er beneath a waning moon was haunted
By woman wailing for her demon-lover!
And from this chasm, with ceaseless turmoil seething,
As if this earth in fast thick pants were breathing,
A mighty fountain momently was forced:
Amid whose swift half-intermitted burst
Huge fragments vaulted like rebounding hail,
Or chaffy grain beneath the thresher's flail:
And 'mid these dancing rocks at once and ever
It flung up momently the sacred river.
Five miles meandering with a mazy motion
Through wood and dale the sacred river ran,
Then reached the caverns measureless to man,
And sank in tumult to a lifeless ocean:
And 'mid this tumult Kubla heard from far
Ancestral voices prophesying war!

The shadow of the dome of pleasure
Floated midway on the waves;
Where was heard the mingled measure
From the fountain and the caves.
It was a miracle of rare device,
A sunny pleasure-dome with caves of ice!

A damsel with a dulcimer
In a vision once I saw:
It was an Abyssinian maid,
And on her dulcimer she played,
Singing of Mount Abora.
Could I revive within me
Her symphony and song,
To such a deep delight 'twould win me,
That with music loud and long,
I would build that dome in air,
That sunny dome! those caves of ice!
And all who heard should see them there,
And all should cry, Beware! Beware!
His flashing eyes, his floating hair!
Weave a circle round him thrice,
And close your eyes with holy dread,
For he on honey-dew hath fed,
And drunk the milk of Paradise.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Fifty-four lines of irregular meter and rhyme divided into three parts, moving from the external description of Xanadu, to a darker vision of the chasm and the river's plunge to a sunless sea, to the first-person voice of the poet lamenting his lost vision. Coleridge called it a fragment, claiming it was interrupted mid-composition by a visitor.

Themes

The tension between creative inspiration and its inevitable incompleteness. Xanadu is described with extraordinary sensory richness, but the poem's final section acknowledges the poet cannot fully reproduce or re-enter the vision, suggesting that artistic creation always falls short of the imagination that generated it. The poem also explores the uncanny: the pleasure dome floats above a measureless cavern, the sacred river plunges into a sunless sea, and the whole scene is shadowed by ancestral voices prophesying war.

Historical Context

Coleridge claimed the poem came to him complete in an opium-assisted dream around 1797, and that the famous interruption by 'a person from Porlock' broke his trance and left the poem unfinished, never to be recovered. Scholars debate whether the story of interrupted composition is literal or itself a literary device. Byron, who heard Coleridge recite it in 1815, urged him to publish it, and it appeared in 1816 alongside Christabel and 'The Pains of Sleep.'

About Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was born on October 21, 1772, in Ottery St Mary, Devon, England, and became one of the founders of the English Romantic movement alongside his friend William Wordsworth. Together they published Lyrical Ballads in 1798, a collection that launched Romanticism in English literature. Coleridge's own most celebrated contributions to the collection, 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' and 'Kubla Khan,' are among the most distinctive and widely studied poems in the language. He died in Highgate, London, in 1834.
