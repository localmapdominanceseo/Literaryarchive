Resolution and Independence

by William Wordsworth

Written 1802 Published 1807 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1807 in Poems in Two Volumes, this poem describes Wordsworth on a morning walk, moving from exhilaration to sudden anxiety about the future — and then being restored by an encounter with an old leech-gatherer who embodies quiet endurance.

Quick Answer

"Resolution and Independence" is a poem by Wordsworth, published in 1807. Walking on a moor after a storm, the speaker's joy suddenly gives way to anxiety about poets who begin in gladness but come to despondency and madness. He meets an old leech-gatherer at a pool, whose patient answers to his questions leave him with a sense of firm resolution.

There was a roaring in the wind all night;
The rain came heavily and fell in floods;
But now the sun is rising calm and bright;
The birds are singing in the distant woods;
Over his own sweet voice the Stock-dove broods;
The Jay makes answer as the Magpie chatters;
And all the air is filled with pleasant noise of waters.

All things that love the sun are out of doors;
The sky rejoices in the morning's birth;
The grass is bright with rain-drops;—on the moors
The hare is running races in her mirth;
And with her feet she from the plashy earth
Raises a mist; that, glittering in the sun,
Runs with her all the way, wherever she doth run.

I was a Traveller then upon the moor;
I saw the hare that raced about with joy;
I heard the woods and distant waters roar;
Or heard them not, as happy as a boy:
The pleasant season did my heart employ:
My old remembrances went from me wholly;
And all the ways of men, so vain and melancholy.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty stanzas in the rhyme royal stanza (ABABBCC), a traditional English stanza form Wordsworth adapted.

Themes

The recovery of resolution from despair through the example of endurance — the leech-gatherer asks for nothing and endures without complaint. The poem is partly about the poet's anxiety and partly about what ordinary people teach the artist.

Historical Context

Published in Poems in Two Volumes (1807). Wordsworth described the leech-gatherer as a real person he and Dorothy met on a moor. Coleridge received an early draft with mixed feelings.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
