Strange Meeting

by Wilfred Owen

Written 1918 Published 1920 World War I Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1918 and published posthumously in 1920, this poem describes a soldier who escapes the battle into a tunnel and encounters a dead German soldier he himself killed, who tells him they might have shared pity and truth with the world, but now it is too late.

Quick Answer

"Strange Meeting" is a poem by Wilfred Owen, written in 1918 and published posthumously in 1920. A soldier descends into a tunnel after battle and meets a figure who tells him they were enemies in life — the speaker killed him the day before. The German soldier laments what they could have shared with the world: pity, truth, the courage to speak clearly about war. His final words are: 'I am the enemy you killed, my friend.'

It seemed that out of battle I escaped
Down some profound dull tunnel, long since scooped
Through granites which titanic wars had groined.
Yet also there encumbered sleepers groaned,
Too fast in thought or death to be bestirred.
Then, as I probed them, one sprang up, and stared
With piteous recognition in fixed eyes,
Lifting distressful hands, as if to bless.
And by his smile, I knew that sullen hall,—
By his dead smile I knew we stood in Hell.
With a thousand fears that vision's face was grained;
Yet no blood reached there from the upper ground,
And no guns thumped, or down the flues made moan.
'Strange friend,' I said, 'here is no cause to mourn.'
'None,' said that other, 'save the undone years,
The hopelessness. Whatever hope is yours,
Was my life also; I went hunting wild
After the wildest beauty in the world,
Which lies not calm in eyes, or braided hair,
But mocks the steady running of the hour,
And if it grieves, grieves richlier than here.
For by my glee might many men have laughed,
And of my weeping something had been left,
Which must die now. I mean the truth untold,
The pity of war, the pity war distilled.
Now men will go content with what we spoiled,
Or, discontent, boil bloody, and be spilled.
They will be swift with swiftness of the tigress.
None will break ranks, though nations trek from progress.
Courage was mine, and I had mystery;
Wisdom was mine, and I had mastery:
To miss the march of this retreating world
Into vain citadels that are not walled.
Then, when much blood had clogged their chariot-wheels,
I would go up and wash them from sweet wells,
Even from wells we sunk too deep for war,
Even the sweetest wells that ever were.
I am the enemy you killed, my friend.
I knew you in this dark: for so you frowned
Yesterday through me as you jabbed and killed.
I parried; but my hands were loath and cold.
Let us sleep now....'

Analysis

Form and Structure

Forty-four lines of loosely iambic pentameter using Owen's distinctive pararhyme (near-rhyme matching consonants but not vowels: groined/groaned, hall/Hell, Years/yours), which creates a persistent sense of something slightly wrong, of harmony just missed.

Themes

The waste of war — not just lives but the pity, truth, and human understanding that die with combatants. The German soldier's speech describes a vision of what he and the British soldier might have done together: given the world 'the undone years, / The hopelessness.' The poem suggests that the real enemy of both soldiers is war itself.

Historical Context

Owen wrote the poem in early 1918 during his second period of service in France. It was published posthumously in 1920. It draws on classical underworld descents (Homer, Virgil, Dante) to frame the encounter, giving the meeting its dreamlike, mythic quality.

About Wilfred Owen

Wilfred Owen was born on March 18, 1893, in Oswestry, Shropshire, England, and is now considered the greatest poet of the First World War. He enlisted in 1915, served on the Western Front, and was sent to Craiglockhart War Hospital in 1917 suffering from shell shock, where he met Siegfried Sassoon, who encouraged and shaped his work. Owen was killed in action on November 4, 1918, exactly one week before the Armistice, at the age of twenty-five. Most of his poems were published posthumously in 1920.
