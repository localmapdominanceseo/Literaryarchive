To the Memory of My Beloved Author, Mr. William Shakespeare

by Ben Jonson

Written 1623 Published 1623 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1623 in the First Folio of Shakespeare's works, this poem by Ben Jonson is both a tribute to Shakespeare and a statement about literary immortality — Shakespeare was not of an age, but for all time.

Quick Answer

"To the Memory of My Beloved Author, Mr. William Shakespeare" is a poem by Ben Jonson, published in the 1623 First Folio. Jonson calls Shakespeare the Soul of the Age, the Applause and Wonder of our Stage, and declares he was not of an age but for all time. He shines as a Star of Poets.

To draw no envy, Shakespeare, on thy name,
Am I thus ample to thy book and fame;
While I confess thy writings to be such,
As neither man, nor muse, can praise too much.
'Tis true, and all men's suffrage. But these ways
Were not the paths I meant unto thy praise:
For silliest ignorance on these may light,
Which, when it sounds at best, but echoes right;
Or blind affection, which doth ne'er advance
The truth, but gropes, and urgeth all by chance;
Or crafty malice, might pretend this praise,
And think to ruin, where it seemed to raise.
These are, as some infamous bawd, or whore,
Should praise a matron. What could hurt her more?
But thou art proof against them, and, indeed,
Above th' ill fortune of them, or the need.
I therefore will begin. Soul of the age!
The applause! delight! the wonder of our stage!
My Shakespeare, rise! I will not lodge thee by
Chaucer, or Spenser, or bid Beaumont lie
A little further to make thee a room:
Thou art a monument without a tomb,
And art alive still, while thy book doth live,
And we have wits to read, and praise to give.
That I not mix thee so, my brain excuses,
I mean with great, but disproportioned Muses:
For if I thought my judgment were of years,
I should commit thee surely with thy peers,
And tell how far thou didst our Lyly outshine,
Or sporting Kyd, or Marlowe's mighty line.
And though thou hadst small Latin and less Greek,
From thence to honour thee, I would not seek
For names: but call forth thundering Aeschylus,
Euripides, and Sophocles to us,
Pacuvius, Accius, him of Cordova dead,
To life again, to hear thy buskin tread,
And shake a stage: or, when thy socks were on,
Leave thee alone, for the comparison
Of all that insolent Greece, or haughty Rome
Sent forth, or since did from their ashes come.
Triumph, my Britain, thou hast one to show,
To whom all scenes of Europe homage owe.
He was not of an age, but for all time!

Public domain. Published 1623 in the First Folio.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eighty lines of heroic couplets, the poem's formal weight matching its monumental subject.

Themes

Shakespeare's supremacy among English poets and his immortality — not just fame but genuine lasting greatness. Jonson's praise is notably specific about what makes Shakespeare great: his art, not just nature.

Historical Context

Written for and published in the First Folio (1623), the first collected edition of Shakespeare's plays, published seven years after his death. Jonson's poem is the most important contemporary statement about Shakespeare's achievement.

About Ben Jonson

Ben Jonson was born on June 11, 1572, in London, and became the foremost dramatist and poet of the Jacobean era, second only to Shakespeare in contemporary reputation. His plays Volpone (1606) and The Alchemist (1610) are among the greatest comedies in English. As a poet, his Epigrams, The Forest, and Under-wood collections display unrivaled technical mastery and classical learning. He died in London in 1637.
