Thyrsis (Extract)

by Matthew Arnold

Written 1865 Published 1866 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1866, Thyrsis is Arnold's pastoral elegy for his friend Arthur Hugh Clough, who died in 1861. Like Lycidas, it uses the pastoral convention to process grief, and like The Scholar Gipsy, it is set in the Oxfordshire countryside Arnold loved.

Quick Answer

"Thyrsis" is a pastoral elegy by Matthew Arnold, published in 1866, mourning his friend Arthur Hugh Clough. Arnold revisits the Oxfordshire countryside he and Clough walked together, finding the old tree still standing — a signal that the quest for truth is not over. The poem is Arnold's most personal and most beautiful lyric.

How changed is here each spot man makes or fills!
In the two Hinkseys nothing keeps the same;
The village street its haunted mansion lacks,
And from the sign is gone Sibylla's name,
And from the roofs the twisted chimney-stacks—
Are ye too changed, ye hills?
See, 'tis no foot of unfamiliar men
To-night from Oxford up your pathway strays!
Here came I often, often, in old days—
Thyrsis and I; we still had Thyrsis then.

Runs it not here, the track by Childsworth Farm,
Past the high wood, to where the elm-tree crowns
The hill behind whose ridge the sunset flames?
The signal-elm, that looks on Ilsley Downs,
The Vale, the three lone weirs, the youthful Thames?—
This winter-eve is warm,
Humped the old Berkshire Road into the rain.
Yet, come; for if not here, no shade I see
Of that dear friend, nor anywhere else he be,
This elm, and no other, is his sign.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-four ten-line stanzas in the same Keatsian stanza Arnold used in The Scholar Gipsy, ABABCCDEED.

Themes

Grief for a lost friend, the persistence of the quest for meaning despite loss, and the consolation of the natural world. The elm tree on the hill becomes a symbol that the quest Clough and Arnold shared is not ended.

Historical Context

Published in Macmillan's Magazine (1866). Clough died in Florence in 1861. Arnold returned to Oxfordshire to write the elegy and found the landscape both changed and consolingly permanent.

About Matthew Arnold

Matthew Arnold was born on December 24, 1822, in Laleham, Middlesex, England, the son of the famous headmaster Thomas Arnold of Rugby. He became both a significant poet and the foremost Victorian literary and cultural critic, arguing for culture as 'the best that has been thought and said.' His poetry, including 'Dover Beach' and 'The Scholar Gipsy,' engages directly with the crisis of religious faith in the modern world. He died in Liverpool in 1888.
