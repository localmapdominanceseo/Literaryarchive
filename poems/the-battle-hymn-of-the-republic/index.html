The Battle Hymn of the Republic

by Julia Ward Howe

Written 1861 Published 1862 American Romanticism Public Domain

Published 1862.

Quick Answer

The Battle Hymn of the Republic is a poem, published 1862.

Mine eyes have seen the glory of the coming of the Lord:
He is trampling out the vintage where the grapes of wrath are stored;
He hath loosed the fateful lightning of His terrible swift sword:
His truth is marching on.
Glory, glory, hallelujah!
His truth is marching on.

I have seen Him in the watch-fires of a hundred circling camps,
They have builded Him an altar in the evening dews and damps;
I can read His righteous sentence by the dim and flaring lamps:
His day is marching on.

In the beauty of the lilies Christ was born across the sea,
With a glory in his bosom that transfigures you and me:
As he died to make men holy, let us die to make men free,
While God is marching on.

Public domain. Published 1862.

About Julia Ward Howe

Julia Ward Howe was born on May 27, 1819, in New York City, and became one of the most prominent American reformers of the nineteenth century — a poet, playwright, lecturer, and abolitionist. Her Battle Hymn of the Republic (1862), written after visiting Union Army camps near Washington D.C., became the great marching song of the Civil War and one of the most celebrated American poems. She was the first woman elected to the American Academy of Arts and Letters. She died in Newport, Rhode Island, in 1910.
