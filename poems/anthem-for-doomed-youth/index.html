Anthem for Doomed Youth

by Wilfred Owen

Written 1917 Published 1920 World War I Poetry Public Domain

Written in September 1917 and published posthumously in 1920, this sonnet asks what passing-bells ring for those who die like cattle in the trenches, and answers that only the guns, the shells, and the sounds of battle mark their deaths — while at home, the only mourning comes in the eyes and faces of those who loved them.

Quick Answer

"Anthem for Doomed Youth" is a sonnet by Wilfred Owen, written in 1917 and published posthumously in 1920. It asks what funeral rites mark the death of soldiers killed in battle, and answers that only the sounds of war serve as their prayers and choir. The sestet turns to the mourners at home, finding their grief in candles of boys' eyes, their pall in girls' brows, and their flowers in the slow dusk that draws down blinds.

What passing-bells for these who die as cattle?
—Only the monstrous anger of the guns.
Only the stuttering rifles' rapid rattle
Can patter out their hasty orisons.
No mockeries now for them; no prayers nor bells;
Nor any voice of mourning save the choirs,—
The shrill, demented choirs of wailing shells;
And bugles calling for them from sad shires.

What candles may be held to speed them all?
Not in the hands of boys, but in their eyes
Shall shine the holy glimmers of good-byes.
The pallor of girls' brows shall be their pall;
Their flowers the tenderness of patient minds,
And each slow dusk a drawing-down of blinds.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in loose iambic pentameter with the volta arriving between octave (the battlefield) and sestet (the home front), using the contrast to show that war denies soldiers any proper ritual of mourning while burdening those at home with silent grief.

Themes

The desecration of death in modern warfare and the inadequacy of language, ritual, and ceremony to mark or mourn it properly. The irony of calling the poem an 'anthem' and 'doomed youth' juxtaposes military ceremony with its complete absence on the battlefield — the guns mock the bells, the shells serve for prayers, the wailing of shells replaces the choir.

Historical Context

Owen drafted the poem with the help of Siegfried Sassoon at Craiglockhart, where the two poets met in August 1917. Sassoon's suggestions, visible in surviving manuscripts, significantly shaped the final version. It was published posthumously in 1920 in Owen's collected poems.

About Wilfred Owen

Wilfred Owen was born on March 18, 1893, in Oswestry, Shropshire, England, and is now considered the greatest poet of the First World War. He enlisted in 1915, served on the Western Front, and was sent to Craiglockhart War Hospital in 1917 suffering from shell shock, where he met Siegfried Sassoon, who encouraged and shaped his work. Owen was killed in action on November 4, 1918, exactly one week before the Armistice, at the age of twenty-five. Most of his poems were published posthumously in 1920.
