Hymn to God My God, in My Sickness

by John Donne

Written 1623 Published 1635 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1623 during his severe illness and published posthumously in 1635, this hymn imagines Donne lying on his deathbed as a map the doctors are studying — and uses geographical imagery to argue that the straits of death lead to the Pacific of eternal life.

Quick Answer

"Hymn to God My God, in My Sickness" is a poem by John Donne, written in 1623 and published posthumously in 1635. Donne imagines himself as a flat map being studied by doctors — every strait his current illness could be the passage west that navigators sought. Whether via the straits of Fever, or the Pacific of peace, all meet in the other world. He is God's music and his fever, his tune.

Since I am coming to that holy room
Where, with thy choir of saints for evermore,
I shall be made thy music; as I come
I tune the instrument here at the door,
And what I must do then, think here before.

Whilst my physicians by their love are grown
Cosmographers, and I their map, who lie
Flat on this bed, that by them may be shown
That this is my south-west discovery,
Per fretum febris, by these straits to die.

I joy, that in these straits I see my west;
For, though those currents yield return to none,
What shall my west hurt me? As west and east
In all flat maps (and I am one) are one,
So death doth touch the resurrection.

Is the Pacific sea my home? Or are
The eastern riches? Is Jerusalem?
Anyan, and Magellan, and Gibraltar?
All straits, and none but straits, are ways to them,
Whether where Japhet dwelt, or Cham, or Shem.

We think that Paradise and Calvary,
Christ's cross and Adam's tree, stood in one place;
Look, Lord, and find both Adams met in me;
As the first Adam's sweat surrounds my face,
May the last Adam's blood my soul embrace.

So, in his purple wrapped, receive me, Lord;
By these his thorns, give me his other crown;
And as to others' souls I preached thy word,
Be this my text, my sermon to mine own:
Therefore that he may raise, the Lord throws down.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1635.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five six-line stanzas of iambic pentameter with ABABCC rhyme, the geographical conceit sustained across all five stanzas.

Themes

The deathbed as a map being read — the straits of illness as the Northwest Passage that leads to the Pacific (eternity). Donne's characteristic wit is here deployed in the face of death, finding in cartography a vehicle for Christian hope.

Historical Context

Written in December 1623 during the serious illness Donne also recorded in Devotions Upon Emergent Occasions. Published posthumously in 1635.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
