Song of Myself (Extract): I celebrate myself

by Walt Whitman

Written 1855 Published 1855 American Romanticism Public Domain

The opening lines of Walt Whitman's Song of Myself, first published in 1855 in Leaves of Grass, announce the poem's foundational premise: that the poet celebrates himself but in doing so celebrates every human being, because every atom belonging to him belongs also to the reader.

Quick Answer

The opening section of "Song of Myself" by Walt Whitman, published in 1855, announces the poem's democratic premise: the poet celebrates himself and sings himself, inviting the reader to assume what he assumes, because every atom belonging to him as good belongs to the reader. He will lean and loaf and observe a spear of summer grass.

I celebrate myself, and sing myself,
And what I assume you shall assume,
For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you.

I loafe and invite my soul,
I lean and loafe at my ease observing a spear of summer grass.

My tongue, every atom of my blood, formd from this soil, this air,
Born here of parents born here from parents the same, and their parents the same,
I, now thirty-seven years old in perfect health begin,
Hoping to cease not till death.

Creeds and schools in abeyance,
Retiring back a while sufficed at what they are, but never forgotten,
I harbor for good or bad, I permit to speak at every hazard,
Nature without check with original energy.

Public domain. From Leaves of Grass, 1855 first edition.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Free verse with long, expansive, catalog-driven lines characteristic of Whitman's revolutionary departure from conventional English verse forms. The poem has no regular meter or rhyme, drawing instead on biblical parallelism and the rhythms of oratory and natural speech.

Themes

Democratic selfhood — the idea that celebrating the individual self is simultaneously a celebration of all humanity. Whitman insists on the equality of all people, all experience, all atoms. The opening section establishes that the 'I' of the poem is simultaneously personal and universal: 'what I assume you shall assume.'

Historical Context

Whitman self-published Leaves of Grass in 1855, setting the type himself, and the first edition contained twelve untitled poems. Song of Myself was the longest and most ambitious, running to over 1,300 lines in its final 1891 version. It was radically unlike anything in American or British poetry at the time, and Ralph Waldo Emerson wrote to Whitman calling it 'the most extraordinary piece of wit and wisdom that America has yet contributed.'

About Walt Whitman

Walt Whitman was born on May 31, 1819, on Long Island, New York. His landmark collection Leaves of Grass, first published in 1855 and expanded throughout his life, broke from traditional verse forms in favor of free verse and sweeping, democratic subject matter. Whitman served as a nurse during the American Civil War, an experience that shaped much of his later work, including his elegy for Abraham Lincoln. He is widely regarded, alongside Emily Dickinson, as one of the founders of a distinctly American poetic voice.
