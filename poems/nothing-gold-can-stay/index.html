Nothing Gold Can Stay

by Robert Frost

Written 1923 Published 1923 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1923 in New Hampshire, this eight-line poem traces the pattern of inevitable loss: the first gold of spring leaves is the hardest gold to hold, Eden sank to grief, dawn goes down to day — nothing gold can stay.

Quick Answer

"Nothing Gold Can Stay" is a poem by Robert Frost, published in 1923. In eight lines, it traces the pattern of inevitable loss from the first gold of early spring leaves to Eden's fall to dawn fading into day — everything at its most perfect and earliest moment must fall away. Nothing gold can stay.

Nature's first green is gold,
Her hardest hue to hold.
Her early leaf's a flower;
But only so an hour.
Then leaf subsides to leaf.
So Eden sank to grief,
So dawn goes down to day.
Nothing gold can stay.

Public domain (published 1923). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight lines of iambic trimeter with AABBCCDD rhyme, compressed and formal — one of Frost's tightest poems in a career known for its conversational expansiveness.

Themes

The inevitability of loss at the moment of greatest perfection. The poem moves from nature to Eden to the daily cycle, arguing that the pattern of early perfection followed by fall is universal.

Historical Context

Published in New Hampshire (1923). The poem is widely known partly through its quotation in S.E. Hinton's novel The Outsiders (1967), where it becomes a touchstone for the characters' awareness of passing youth.

About Robert Frost

Robert Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California, though he is most closely associated with rural New England, where he lived and farmed for much of his life. His poetry uses plain, conversational language and traditional verse forms to explore deceptively simple rural scenes that often open onto deeper questions about choice, isolation, and mortality. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry and remains one of the most widely read American poets of the twentieth century. He died in Boston in 1963.
