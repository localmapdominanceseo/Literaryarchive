I Wake and Feel the Fell of Dark

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1885 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1885 during Hopkins's 'terrible sonnets' and published posthumously in 1918, this poem describes waking in the night to spiritual desolation — the fell of dark, the bitter taste of self, the horror of damnation.

Quick Answer

I Wake and Feel the Fell of Dark is a poem by Hopkins, published posthumously in 1918. The speaker wakes in the night, desolate, feeling the fell of dark hours upon him. What I feel now is the very taste of me — the bitter taste of the self that God has abandoned. The lost are like this, and their scourge is worse.

I wake and feel the fell of dark, not day.
What hours, O what black hours we have spent
This night! what sights you, heart, saw; ways you went!
And more must, in yet longer light's delay.

With witness I speak this. But where I say
Hours I mean years, mean life. And my lament
Is cries countless, cries like dead letters sent
To dearest him that lives alas! away.

I am gall, I am heartburn. God's most deep decree
Bitter would have me taste: my taste was me;
Bones built in me, flesh filled, blood brimmed the curse.

Selfyeast of spirit a dull dough sours. I see
The lost are like this, and their scourge to be
As I am mine, their sweating selves; but worse.

Public domain. Written c.1885, published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter, one of the 'terrible sonnets' of spiritual desolation.

Themes

Spiritual darkness and the fear of abandonment by God — the self as something bitter and unacceptable, glimpsing what damnation might be like.

Historical Context

Written c.1885 during Hopkins's years of depression and spiritual desolation in Dublin. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
