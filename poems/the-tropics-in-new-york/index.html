The Tropics in New York

by Claude McKay

Written 1920 Published 1920 Harlem Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1920, this poem describes a speaker in New York who sees tropical fruits in a window display and is suddenly, overwhelmingly transported by memory to his native Jamaica — where his eyes fill with tears.

Quick Answer

"The Tropics in New York" is a poem by Claude McKay, published in 1920. The speaker in New York sees tropical fruits — breadfruit, finger-figs, plantains, mangoes — in a window display and is immediately transported by memory to his native Jamaica, the land of his birth. His eyes grow dim with tears.

Bananas ripe and green, and ginger-root,
Cocoa in pods and alligator pears,
And tangerines and mangoes and grape fruit,
Fit for the highest prize at parish fairs,

Set in the window, bringing memories
Of fruit-trees laden by low-singing rills,
And dewy dawns, and mystical blue skies
In benediction over nun-like hills.

My eyes grew dim, and I could no more gaze;
A wave of longing through my body swept,
And, hungry for the old, familiar ways,
I turned aside and bowed my head and wept.

Public domain (published 1920). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains of iambic pentameter with ABAB rhyme, clear and traditional in structure for a poem whose emotional power comes from the contrast between the formal control and the sudden surge of grief.

Themes

Diaspora, homesickness, and the unexpected ambush of memory. The speaker is displaced in New York, and the sight of familiar fruit from home triggers a grief so acute it brings tears — a grief for a lost landscape that cannot be recovered.

Historical Context

McKay was born in Jamaica and emigrated to the United States in 1912. Published in Spring in New Hampshire (1920), this poem is one of his most personal expressions of the immigrant experience. McKay was a central figure of the Harlem Renaissance.

About Claude McKay

Claude McKay was born on September 15, 1889, in Sunny Ville, Jamaica, and emigrated to the United States in 1912, becoming one of the earliest and most important voices of the Harlem Renaissance. His sonnet 'If We Must Die,' written during the Red Summer of 1919, became an anthem of Black resistance and was later cited by Winston Churchill during World War II. He died in Chicago in 1948.
