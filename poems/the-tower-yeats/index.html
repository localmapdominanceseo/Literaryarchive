The Tower (Extract)

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1925 Published 1928 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1928 as the title poem of The Tower, this poem begins with Yeats's rage at old age and his declaration that he must choose between passion and philosophy — before summoning the shades of those who lived near his tower to ask them what they chose.

Quick Answer

"The Tower" is a long poem by W.B. Yeats published in 1928, of which this is the opening section. The aged poet, enraged at old age that has left him with an excited, passionate nature but a tattered body, declares he must choose the second of two impossible choices and set upon the growing boy imagination.

What shall I do with this absurdity—
O heart, O troubled heart—this caricature,
Decrepit age that has been tied to me
As to a dog's tail?
Never had I more
Excited, passionate, fantastical
Imagination, nor an ear and eye
That more expected the impossible—
No, not in boyhood when with rod and fly,
Or the humbler worm, I climbed Ben Bulben's back
And had the livelong summer day to spend.
It seems that I must bid the Muse go pack,
Choose Plato and Plotinus for a friend
Until imagination, ear and eye,
Can be content with argument and deal
In abstract things; or be derided by
A sort of battered kettle at the heel.

Public domain (published 1928). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three-part poem in Yeats's mature style, combining personal anger, historical summoning, and philosophical declaration. The opening section establishes the central tension of the poem.

Themes

Old age versus creative power; the rage of the passionate man against bodily decline. Yeats refuses to accept the conventional wisdom that old age should bring philosophic calm.

Historical Context

Published in The Tower (1928), considered Yeats's greatest collection and his mature masterpiece.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
