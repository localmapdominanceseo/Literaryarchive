The Tyger

by William Blake

Written 1794 Published 1794 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1794 in Blake's illustrated collection Songs of Experience, this poem consists entirely of unanswered questions about the nature of the tiger's creator.

Quick Answer

"The Tyger" is a poem by William Blake, published in 1794. Through unanswered rhetorical questions, the speaker wonders what kind of divine creator could have forged the tiger's fearsome power, asking whether the maker of the gentle lamb also made the tiger.

Tyger Tyger, burning bright,
In the forests of the night;
What immortal hand or eye,
Could frame thy fearful symmetry?

In what distant deeps or skies,
Burnt the fire of thine eyes?
On what wings dare he aspire?
What the hand, dare seize the fire?

And what shoulder, & what art,
Could twist the sinews of thy heart?
And when thy heart began to beat,
What dread hand? & what dread feet?

What the hammer? what the chain,
In what furnace was thy brain?
What the anvil? what dread grasp,
Dare its deadly terrors clasp!

When the stars threw down their spears
And water'd heaven with their tears:
Did he smile his work to see?
Did he who made the Lamb make thee?

Tyger Tyger burning bright,
In the forests of the night:
What immortal hand or eye,
Dare frame thy fearful symmetry?

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Seven quatrains of rhymed trochaic tetrameter, with a hammering rhythm that echoes the forge imagery within the poem. Nearly every line ends in a question mark.

Themes

The coexistence of beauty and violence within creation, imagined in industrial, blacksmith-like terms (hammer, chain, furnace, anvil). The closing question, linking the tiger to Blake's companion poem 'The Lamb,' leaves the tension between innocence and experience unresolved.

Historical Context

Published in Songs of Experience (1794) as a darker counterpart to 'The Lamb' from Songs of Innocence (1789). Blake engraved and hand-printed his own illustrated volumes, and remained largely unrecognized in his lifetime.

About William Blake

William Blake was born on November 28, 1757, in London, England, and worked as a poet, painter, and printmaker, producing illustrated volumes of poetry that he engraved, printed, and hand-colored himself. Largely unrecognized during his lifetime, he is now considered one of the founding figures of English Romanticism. His best-known collection, Songs of Innocence and of Experience, pairs poems of childlike wonder with darker counterparts exploring suffering and moral complexity. He died in London in 1827.
