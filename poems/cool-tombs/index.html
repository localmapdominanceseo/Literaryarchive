Cool Tombs

by Carl Sandburg

Written 1918 Published 1918 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1918 in Cornhuskers, this poem asks what the cool tombs say about Lincoln, about Pocahontas, about Ulysses Grant — the answer is nothing, because the grave asks only one question: tell me if the lovers are losers, tell me if any get more than the lovers.

Quick Answer

"Cool Tombs" is a poem by Carl Sandburg, published in 1918. Abraham Lincoln is in the cool tombs. So is Pocahontas. So is Ulysses Grant. And lovers — if any get more than the lovers in the cool tombs, in the rain and the gold, in the dust, in the cool tombs. The poem meditates on whether love is the only thing that matters at death.

When Abraham Lincoln was shoveled into the tombs,
he forgot the copperheads and the assassin...
in the dust, in the cool tombs.

And Ulysses Grant lost all thought of con men and Wall Street,
cash and collateral turned ashes...
in the dust, in the cool tombs.

Pocahontas' body, lovely as a poplar, sweet as a red haw
in November or a pawpaw in May, did she wonder?
does she remember?...in the dust, in the cool tombs?

Take any streetful of people buying clothes and groceries,
cheering a hero or throwing confetti and blowing tin horns...
tell me if the lovers are losers...tell me if any get more than
the lovers...in the dust...in the cool tombs.

Public domain (published 1918 in Cornhuskers).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four stanzas of free verse, the cool tombs refrain binding them together with a quiet, funereal repetition.

Themes

The equality of death — presidents, Native American women, generals, and lovers all go to the same cool tombs. The question at the end — whether lovers get more than anyone else — suggests love is the only criterion that matters.

Historical Context

Published in Cornhuskers (1918), the collection for which Sandburg won his first Pulitzer Prize.

About Carl Sandburg

Carl Sandburg was born on January 6, 1878, in Galesburg, Illinois, to Swedish immigrant parents, and became one of the most popular American poets of the twentieth century. He worked many jobs — farm worker, soldier, journalist — before establishing himself as a poet with Chicago Poems (1916). He won three Pulitzer Prizes, two for poetry and one for his biography of Abraham Lincoln. He died in Flat Rock, North Carolina, in 1967.
