Epithalamion (Extract)

by Edmund Spenser

Written 1594 Published 1595 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1595, Epithalamion is Spenser's wedding poem for his own marriage to Elizabeth Boyle. This is the celebrated opening invocation, calling on the Muses to help him sing his bride's praises on their wedding day.

Quick Answer

Spenser's Epithalamion (1595) is a long wedding poem for his own marriage. The opening calls on the learned sisters (Muses) to help him string his lute, wake the forest echoes, and make the woods and waters ring with his love's praises on this joyful day.

Ye learned sisters which have oftentimes
Beene to me ayding, others to adorne:
Whom ye thought worthy of your gracefull rymes,
That even the greatest did not greatly scorne
To heare theyr names sung in your simple layes,
But joyed in theyr prayse.
And when ye list your owne mishaps to mourne,
Which death, or love, or fortunes wreck did rayse,
Your string could soone to sadder tenor turne,
And teach the woods and waters to lament
Your dolefull dreriment.
Now lay those sorrowfull complaints aside,
And having all your heads with girland crownd,
Helpe me mine owne loves prayses to resound,
Ne let the same of any be envide:
So Orpheus did for his owne bride,
So I unto my selfe alone will sing,
The woods shall to me answer and my Eccho ring.

Public domain. Published 1595.

Analysis

Form and Structure

The full poem has 24 stanzas of varying length with a repeating refrain. This extract shows Spenser's elaborate invocation style and his characteristic blending of classical mythology with personal feeling.

Themes

The joy of marriage and the role of poetry in celebrating and preserving it. The poem was both a love poem to his bride and a demonstration of poetic skill.

Historical Context

Published in 1595 alongside Amoretti. Epithalamion was Spenser's wedding gift to his wife and himself — a poem that would immortalize their wedding day.

About Edmund Spenser

Edmund Spenser was born around 1552 in London and became one of the greatest poets of the English Renaissance, best known for his unfinished epic allegory The Faerie Queene (1590-1596), dedicated to Queen Elizabeth I. His sonnet sequence Amoretti (1595) and his wedding poem Epithalamion (1595) are his most personal works. He died in London in 1599.
