To a Mouse

by Robert Burns

Written 1785 Published 1786 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written in 1785 and published in 1786 in the Kilmarnock edition of Burns's poems, this poem is addressed to a field mouse whose nest the speaker has just accidentally destroyed while plowing. Burns apologizes to the mouse and reflects that he and the mouse are fellow mortals, both subject to plans going wrong.

Quick Answer

"To a Mouse" is a poem by Robert Burns, written in 1785 and published in 1786. After accidentally destroying a mouse's nest while plowing, the speaker apologizes to the mouse for mankind's dominion over nature, reflects that the mouse is not alone in seeing its carefully laid plans destroyed, and ends with the observation that the mouse has one advantage: unlike humans, it cannot look back with regret or forward with dread — it lives only in the present moment.

Wee, sleekit, cow'rin, tim'rous beastie,
O, what a panic's in thy breastie!
Thou need na start awa sae hasty,
Wi' bickerin brattle!
I wad be laith to rin an' chase thee,
Wi' murd'ring pattle!

I'm truly sorry man's dominion,
Has broken nature's social union,
An' justifies that ill opinion,
Which makes thee startle
At me, thy poor, earth-born companion,
An' fellow-mortal!

I doubt na, whiles, but thou may thieve;
What then? poor beastie, thou maun live!
A daimen icker in a thrave
'S a sma' request;
I'll get a blessin wi' the lave,
An' never miss't!

Thy wee bit housie, too, in ruin!
Its silly wa's the win's are strewin!
An' naething, now, to big a new ane,
O' foggage green!
An' bleak December's winds ensuin,
Baith snell an' keen!

Thou saw the fields laid bare an' waste,
An' weary winter comin fast,
An' cozie here, beneath the blast,
Thou thought to dwell—
Till crash! the cruel coulter past
Out thro' thy cell.

That wee bit heap o' leaves an' stibble,
Has cost thee mony a weary nibble!
Now thou's turn'd out, for a' thy trouble,
But house or hald,
To thole the winter's sleety dribble,
An' cranreuch cauld!

But, Mousie, thou art no thy lane,
In proving foresight may be vain:
The best-laid schemes o' mice an' men
Gang aft agley,
An' lea'e us nought but grief an' pain,
For promis'd joy!

Still thou art blest, compar'd wi' me!
The present only toucheth thee:
But och! I backward cast my e'e,
On prospects drear!
An' forward, tho' I canna see,
I guess an' fear!

Public domain. Published 1786 in the Kilmarnock Edition.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight six-line stanzas in the 'Standard Habbie' form (Burns's characteristic meter) written in the Scots dialect, with a lilting AAABAB rhyme scheme that gives the poem warmth despite its melancholy subject.

Themes

The best-laid plans of mice and men go often astray — Burns's poem gave English its most famous expression of this universal truth. The poem also reflects on the relationship between humans and other creatures, and the peculiar burden of human consciousness: we can remember the past and fear the future, while the mouse, living only in the present, is in some ways better off.

Historical Context

Burns wrote the poem in November 1785 while farming at Mossgiel, reportedly after actually plowing up a mouse's nest. The phrase 'The best-laid schemes o' Mice an' Men / Gang aft agley' became one of the most quoted lines in English literature and directly inspired the title of John Steinbeck's novel Of Mice and Men (1937).

About Robert Burns

Robert Burns was born on January 25, 1759, in Alloway, Scotland, and became Scotland's national poet, celebrated for poetry and songs written largely in the Scots dialect. He spent much of his life farming while writing prolifically, and in his later years devoted himself to collecting and adapting traditional Scottish folk songs and ballads, preserving them for future generations. He died in Dumfries in 1796 at age thirty-seven.
