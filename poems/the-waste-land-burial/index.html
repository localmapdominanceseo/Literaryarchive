The Waste Land: I. The Burial of the Dead

by T.S. Eliot

Written 1921 Published 1922 Modernism Public Domain

The first section of T.S. Eliot's landmark 1922 poem The Waste Land, opening with the famous line 'April is the cruellest month,' and moving through fragments of aristocratic memory, a premonition in the Hyacinth garden, a meeting with Madame Sosostris the clairvoyante, and a vision of London as an unreal city of the walking dead.

Quick Answer

"The Burial of the Dead" is the first section of T.S. Eliot's The Waste Land, published in 1922. It opens with the paradox that April, the month of revival, is the cruelest, because it forces life from dead ground — and winter's forgetful snow was preferable. The section moves through memory, prophecy, and a vision of London as a city populated by the walking dead, connecting post-World War I Europe to the myth of the Fisher King's wasted land.

April is the cruellest month, breeding
Lilacs out of the dead land, mixing
Memory and desire, stirring
Dull roots with spring rain.
Winter kept us warm, covering
Earth in forgetful snow, feeding
A little life with dried tubers.
Summer surprised us, coming over the Starnbergersee
With a shower of rain; we stopped in the colonnade,
And went on in sunlight, into the Hofgarten,
And drank coffee, and talked for an hour.
Bin gar keine Russin, stamm' aus Litauen, echt deutsch.
And when we were children, staying at the archduke's,
My cousin's, he took me out on a sled,
And I was frightened. He said, Marie,
Marie, hold on tight. And down we went.
In the mountains, there you feel free.
I read, much of the night, and go south in the winter.

Opening lines of The Waste Land (1922). T.S. Eliot died in 1965; the work entered the public domain in most jurisdictions in 2036 under life-plus-70 rules, but in the US the 1922 publication date means it is public domain under US copyright law.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Free verse mixing English, German, French, Latin, and Sanskrit fragments, shifting perspective and register without explanation, demanding the reader actively work to connect the fragments. The technique of juxtaposition without transition is the poem's central formal strategy.

Themes

Spiritual sterility, the aftermath of World War I, and the possibility of renewal. The title refers to Christian burial rites, the Tarot deck, and Frazer's anthropological study of dying-and-rising vegetation gods — all pointing to a culture that has lost the capacity for genuine regeneration. The 'Unreal City' of London echoes Dante's Hell.

Historical Context

Eliot wrote the poem during 1921 while suffering a mental breakdown, and Ezra Pound famously edited it down from a much longer draft, removing large sections. Published in 1922, the same year as Joyce's Ulysses, it became the defining poem of Anglo-American Modernism. It is in copyright in some jurisdictions but has passed into the public domain in others; this extract is provided for educational/reference purposes consistent with fair use commentary.

About T.S. Eliot

Thomas Stearns Eliot was born on September 26, 1888, in St. Louis, Missouri, and became the most influential poet of English-language Modernism after moving to England and eventually becoming a British citizen. His landmark poem The Waste Land (1922) and the earlier 'The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock' (1915) redefined what poetry could do with fragmented form, multiple languages, and literary allusion. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1948 and died in London in 1965.
