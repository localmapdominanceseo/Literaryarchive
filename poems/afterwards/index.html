Afterwards

by Thomas Hardy

Written 1917 Published 1917 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1917, this poem asks what people will say about Hardy after he is dead — whether they will notice he was someone who used to observe such things as the hawk at dusk or the hedgehog crossing the road.

Quick Answer

Afterwards is a poem by Thomas Hardy, published in 1917. When the present has latched its postern behind my tremulous stay — if a neighbour notices the May month's thin leaf-wings and asks 'He was a man who used to notice such things' — that will be his monument.

When the Present has latched its postern behind my tremulous stay,
And the May month flaps its glad green leaves like wings,
Delicate-filmed as new-spun silk, will the neighbours say,
'He was a man who used to notice such things'?

If it be in the dusk when, like an eyelid's soundless blink,
The dewfall-hawk comes crossing the shades to alight
Upon the wind-warped upland thorn, a gazer may think,
'To him this must have been a familiar sight.'

If I pass during some nocturnal blackness, mothy and warm,
When the hedgehog travels furtively over the lawn,
One may say, 'He strove that such innocent creatures should come to no harm,
But he could do little for them; and now he is gone.'

If, when hearing that I have been stilled at last, they stand at the door,
Watching the full-starred heavens that winter sees,
Will this thought rise on those who will meet my face no more,
'He was one who had an eye for such mysteries'?

And will any say when my bell of quittance is heard in the gloom,
And a crossing breeze cuts a pause in its outrollings,
Till they rise again, as they were a new bell's boom,
'He hears it not now, but used to notice such things'?

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five four-line stanzas of loose anapestic pentameter with ABAB rhyme, each stanza imagining a different season or time of day and a different neighbor's comment.

Themes

The hope to be remembered as someone attentive to nature's small details — not for great achievements but for the quality of observation and care.

Historical Context

Published in Moments of Vision (1917). One of Hardy's most beloved poems for its modesty and its precise natural imagery.

About Thomas Hardy

Thomas Hardy was born on June 2, 1840, in Higher Bockhampton, Dorset, England, and became one of the greatest novelists and poets of the Victorian and Edwardian eras. After Jude the Obscure (1895) was attacked for immorality, he stopped writing fiction and devoted himself entirely to poetry, producing eight volumes. His elegies for his first wife Emma, written after her sudden death in 1912, are considered among the finest in the English language. He died in Dorchester in 1928.
