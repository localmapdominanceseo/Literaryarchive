Crossing the Bar

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1889 Published 1889 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1889, three years before Tennyson's death, and published in his final collection Demeter and Other Poems, this short poem uses the image of a ship crossing the sandbar at the harbour mouth as a metaphor for death, expressing hope for a peaceful passage and reunion with God.

Quick Answer

"Crossing the Bar" is a poem by Alfred Lord Tennyson, written in 1889. It uses the metaphor of a ship crossing the sandbar at the harbour entrance — the transition from sheltered water to open sea — as an image of death. The speaker hopes for a calm, quiet passing with no mourning, and for reunion with his 'Pilot' (God) when he has crossed the bar.

Sunset and evening star,
And one clear call for me!
And may there be no moaning of the bar,
When I put out to sea,

But such a tide as moving seems asleep,
Too full for sound and foam,
When that which drew from out the boundless deep
Turns again home.

Twilight and evening bell,
And after that the dark!
And may there be no sadness of farewell,
When I embark;

For though from out our bourne of Time and Place
The flood may bear me far,
I hope to see my Pilot face to face
When I have crost the bar.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains alternating longer and shorter lines, with a gentle, wavelike rhythm that suits the maritime imagery. Tennyson asked that this poem always be placed last in any collection of his work, treating it as his final statement.

Themes

A serene acceptance of death as a passage rather than an ending. The bar — the sandbar at a harbour mouth that ships must cross to reach the open sea — represents the threshold of death. The 'Pilot' who waits beyond is widely understood as God or Christ, though Tennyson left the identification implicit. The poem's dominant emotion is peace rather than grief.

Historical Context

Tennyson wrote the poem in October 1889 on a crossing from Aldworth to Freshwater on the Isle of Wight, reportedly composing it within twenty minutes. He died in 1892. His instruction to place it last in all editions of his poetry was followed, and it became one of the most widely read Victorian poems on death and faith.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
