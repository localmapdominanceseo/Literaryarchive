A Psalm of Life

by Henry Wadsworth Longfellow

Written 1838 Published 1838 American Romanticism Public Domain

First published in 1838 and subtitled 'What the Heart of the Young Man Said to the Psalmist,' this poem rejects despair and the idea that life is meaningless, urging readers instead toward purposeful action and a legacy that outlasts death.

Quick Answer

"A Psalm of Life" is a poem by Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, first published in 1838. Subtitled 'What the Heart of the Young Man Said to the Psalmist,' it argues against viewing life as an empty dream heading only toward the grave, urging readers instead to act purposefully in the present and leave behind footprints that might inspire others.

Tell me not, in mournful numbers,
Life is but an empty dream!
For the soul is dead that slumbers,
And things are not what they seem.

Life is real! Life is earnest!
And the grave is not its goal;
Dust thou art, to dust returnest,
Was not spoken of the soul.

Not enjoyment, and not sorrow,
Is our destined end or way;
But to act, that each to-morrow
Find us farther than to-day.

Art is long, and Time is fleeting,
And our hearts, though stout and brave,
Still, like muffled drums, are beating
Funeral marches to the grave.

In the world's broad field of battle,
In the bivouac of Life,
Be not like dumb, driven cattle!
Be a hero in the strife!

Trust no Future, howe'er pleasant!
Let the dead Past bury its dead!
Act,—act in the living Present!
Heart within, and God o'erhead!

Lives of great men all remind us
We can make our lives sublime,
And, departing, leave behind us
Footprints on the sands of time;

Footprints, that perhaps another,
Sailing o'er life's solemn main,
A forlorn and shipwrecked brother,
Seeing, shall take heart again.

Let us, then, be up and doing,
With a heart for any fate;
Still achieving, still pursuing,
Learn to labor and to wait.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Nine quatrains alternating eight-syllable and seven-syllable lines, a meter closely associated with Protestant hymns, fitting for a poem that presents itself as a direct, sermon-like answer to a biblical psalm about life's brevity.

Themes

A direct rejection of fatalism and passive resignation in the face of mortality. Rather than accepting that 'dust thou art, to dust returnest' applies to the soul, the poem insists 'life is real, life is earnest,' and calls for heroic, purposeful action measured not by what comes after death but by the 'footprints' left behind to encourage others.

Historical Context

Longfellow wrote the poem in 1838, reportedly during a period of grief following the death of his first wife, Mary, in 1835. It was first published anonymously in The Knickerbocker magazine in October 1838 and then included in his first poetry collection, Voices of the Night, in 1839, quickly becoming one of the most widely recited and translated American poems of the nineteenth century.

About Henry Wadsworth Longfellow

Henry Wadsworth Longfellow was born on February 27, 1807, in Portland, Maine, and became the most popular and widely read American poet of the nineteenth century. His accessible style, traditional meters, and frequent use of historical and legendary American subject matter, as in 'Paul Revere's Ride' and The Song of Hiawatha, made his work a fixture of American education for generations. He taught modern languages at Harvard for many years and died in Cambridge, Massachusetts, in 1882.
