Sonnet 18 (Shall I Compare Thee to a Summer's Day?)

by William Shakespeare

Written 1595 Published 1609 English Renaissance Public Domain

Likely written in the 1590s and published in 1609 as part of Shakespeare's sequence of 154 sonnets, this is one of the most famous love poems in the English language, arguing that the beloved's beauty will be preserved forever, not in life, but within the poem itself.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 18, beginning 'Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?', is one of William Shakespeare's 154 sonnets, likely written in the 1590s and published in 1609. The speaker argues that the beloved surpasses a summer's day, which is brief and imperfect, because the beloved's beauty will live forever within the lines of the poem itself.

Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?
Thou art more lovely and more temperate:
Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,
And summer's lease hath all too short a date:
Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,
And often is his gold complexion dimm'd,
And every fair from fair sometime declines,
By chance, or nature's changing course untrimm'd:
But thy eternal summer shall not fade,
Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow'st,
Nor shall death brag thou wander'st in his shade,
When in eternal lines to time thou grow'st,
So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see,
So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.

Public domain. Text per the Folger Shakespeare Library, consistent with the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem follows the standard Shakespearean sonnet form: fourteen lines of iambic pentameter arranged in three quatrains and a closing couplet, with an ABAB CDCD EFEF GG rhyme scheme. The volta, or turn, arrives in line nine with 'But thy eternal summer shall not fade.'

Themes

The poem's central theme is the power of poetry to grant immortality. After comparing the beloved favorably to a summer's day, which is undermined by rough winds and its own brief 'lease,' the poem pivots to argue that the beloved will not fade because their beauty is now preserved permanently in verse, to be read 'so long as men can breathe, or eyes can see.'

Historical Context

Sonnet 18 is part of the 'Fair Youth' sequence (Sonnets 1-126), addressed to an unnamed young man whose identity remains debated among scholars. It was first published in the 1609 quarto Shake-speares Sonnets, though most scholars believe it, like the other sonnets, was written roughly a decade earlier.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
