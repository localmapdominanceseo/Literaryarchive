The Garden of Proserpine

by Algernon Charles Swinburne

Written 1866 Published 1866 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1866 in Poems and Ballads, this poem describes the speaker's longing for the sleep of Proserpine — where there is no love or life or light, but only rest and peace. From too much love of living, from hope and fear set free — we thank whatever gods may be that no life lives for ever.

Quick Answer

The Garden of Proserpine is a poem by Swinburne, published in 1866. The speaker longs for Proserpine's garden of rest and poppies. From too much love of living, from hope and fear set free, we thank with brief thanksgiving whatever gods may be that no life lives for ever, that dead men rise up never, that even the weariest river winds somewhere safe to sea.

Here, where the world is quiet;
Here, where all trouble seems
Dead winds' and spent waves' riot
In doubtful dreams of dreams;
I watch the green field growing
For reaping folk and sowing,
For harvest-time and mowing,
A sleepy world of streams.

I am tired of tears and laughter,
And men that laugh and weep;
Of what may come hereafter
For men that sow to reap:
I am weary of days and hours,
Brown bud and beated flowers,
Of all men's hopes and powers,
And all things but sleep.

From too much love of living,
From hope and fear set free,
We thank with brief thanksgiving
Whatever gods may be
That no life lives for ever;
That dead men rise up never;
That even the weariest river
Winds somewhere safe to sea.

Public domain. Published 1866 in Poems and Ballads.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twelve nine-line stanzas of alternating tetrameter and trimeter with complex rhyme, hypnotic and weary.

Themes

Exhaustion of life and longing for death as rest — Swinburne's pagan, nihilistic vision of death as the only real peace.

Historical Context

Published in Poems and Ballads (1866), the collection that caused immediate scandal.

About Algernon Charles Swinburne

Algernon Charles Swinburne was born on April 5, 1837, in London, and became one of the most technically accomplished and musically dazzling of all Victorian poets. His Poems and Ballads (1866) caused an immediate scandal for its paganism, eroticism, and attacks on conventional morality. His verse drama Atalanta in Calydon (1865) established his reputation. He died at Putney in 1909.
