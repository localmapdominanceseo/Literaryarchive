To Daffodils

by Robert Herrick

Written 1648 Published 1648 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1648 in Hesperides, this short lyric addresses daffodils that have come before the swallows dare and take the winds of March with beauty — but are gone too soon, like morning dew, like prayers, like the speaker and his friends.

Quick Answer

"To Daffodils" is a poem by Robert Herrick, published in 1648. The speaker asks the daffodils not to haste away so soon — they are leaving before midday. But he checks himself: they have short time to stay, as we have. We both have our summer's rain and then are gone. Our morning prayer and then away.

Fair Daffodils, we weep to see
You haste away so soon;
As yet the early-rising sun
Has not attain'd his noon.
Stay, stay,
Until the hasting day
Has run
But to the even-song;
And, having pray'd together, we
Will go with you along.

We have short time to stay, as you,
We have as short a Spring;
As quick a growth to meet decay
As you, or any thing.
We die,
As your hours do, and dry
Away
Like to the Summer's rain;
Or as the pearls of morning's dew
Ne'er to be found again.

Public domain. Published 1648 in Hesperides.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two ten-line stanzas of alternating tetrameter and dimeter with ABABCCDBBD rhyme, the short lines giving the poem a wistful, fluttering quality suited to its subject.

Themes

The brevity of life and the comfort — or melancholy — of knowing we share our transience with beautiful natural things. The daffodils and the speaker both have only a short time, and both must go.

Historical Context

Published in Hesperides (1648). One of Herrick's most anthologized poems, closely related to 'To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time' in its carpe diem awareness of transience.

About Robert Herrick

Robert Herrick was born in London in 1591 and became one of the most prolific of the Cavalier poets, writing elegant, sensuous lyrics drawing on classical models. His collection Hesperides (1648) contained over 1,400 poems, including his most famous work 'To the Virgins, to Make Much of Time.' He served as a vicar in Devon and died in 1674.
