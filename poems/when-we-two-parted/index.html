When We Two Parted

by Lord Byron

Written 1808 Published 1816 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1808 and published in 1816, this quiet, controlled poem describes the end of a secret love affair, with the speaker recalling the cold farewell and predicting that if they meet again in company, he will greet the person with silence and tears.

Quick Answer

"When We Two Parted" is a poem by Lord Byron, published in 1816. The speaker remembers parting in silence and tears from a lover whose name he now hears spoken in disgrace by others. He laments the coldness of that farewell, regrets that he loved someone who would prove faithless, and says that if they meet again, he will greet them only with silence and tears.

When we two parted
In silence and tears,
Half broken-hearted
To sever for years,
Pale grew thy cheek and cold,
Colder thy kiss;
Truly that hour foretold
Sorrow to this.

The dew of the morning
Sunk chill on my brow—
It felt like the warning
Of what I feel now.
Thy vows are all broken,
And light is thy fame;
I hear thy name spoken,
And share in its shame.

They name thee before me,
A knell in mine ear;
A shudder comes o'er me—
Why wert thou so dear?
They know not I knew thee,
Who knew thee too well:—
Long, long shall I rue thee,
Too deeply to tell.

In secret we met—
In silence I grieve,
That thy heart could forget,
Thy spirit deceive.
If I should meet thee
After long years,
How should I greet thee?—
With silence and tears.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four octaves of short, clipped lines in iambic dimeter, with an ABABCDCD rhyme scheme. The brevity and restraint of the lines mirrors the poem's emotional suppression — this is a poem about grief held back rather than expressed.

Themes

Betrayal, secret grief, and the bitterness of loving someone whose reputation then falls publicly. The speaker suffers twice: from the loss of the relationship, and from hearing the beloved's name spoken with contempt by others who do not know of his connection to them.

Historical Context

Byron is believed to have written the poem about Lady Frances Wedderburn Webster, with whom he had a brief affair around 1813-1814, though he disguised the date of composition by claiming it was written in 1808. It was published in 1816 in the collection Poems on His Domestic Circumstances.

About Lord Byron

George Gordon Byron, known as Lord Byron, was born on January 22, 1788, in London, and became one of the most famous and controversial figures of English Romanticism, celebrated equally for his poetry and his scandalous personal life. His work ranges from brief, polished lyrics to the sprawling satirical epic Don Juan, and the brooding, charismatic outsider figure he depicted in his writing gave rise to the term 'the Byronic hero.' He died in Greece in 1824 while supporting the Greek War of Independence.
