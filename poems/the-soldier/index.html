The Soldier

by Rupert Brooke

Written 1914 Published 1915 World War I Poetry Public Domain

Written in December 1914 and published in 1915, this sonnet is spoken by a soldier who contemplates his own death in a foreign field, imagining that the patch of ground where he is buried will become forever a piece of England — carrying English air, English flowers, and an English heartbeat into foreign soil.

Quick Answer

"The Soldier" is a sonnet by Rupert Brooke, published in 1915. The speaker asks the reader to think, if he should die in a foreign land, that there is some corner of a foreign field that is forever England — because his body, enriched by English air and rivers and sun, would make that earth English. The sestet imagines him as a pulse in an eternal English consciousness.

If I should die, think only this of me:
That there's some corner of a foreign field
That is for ever England. There shall be
In that rich earth a richer dust concealed;
A dust whom England bore, shaped, made aware,
Gave, once, her flowers to love, her ways to roam,
A body of England's, breathing English air,
Washed by the rivers, blest by suns of home.

And think, this heart, all evil shed away,
A pulse in the eternal mind, no less
Gives somewhere back the thoughts by England given;
Her sights and sounds; dreams happy as her day;
And laughter, learnt of friends; and gentleness,
In hearts at peace, under an English heaven.

Public domain. Published 1915.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter with an octave presenting the central conceit (an English body making foreign soil English) and a sestet imagining the speaker's spiritual afterlife as part of England's eternal being.

Themes

Patriotism, the idealization of England, and the hope of a meaningful death. The poem represents the idealistic early-war attitude before the realities of trench warfare fully registered — Brooke died of blood poisoning on his way to Gallipoli in April 1915, never having seen sustained combat.

Historical Context

Brooke wrote the poem in December 1914 as one of his 'War Sonnets' sequence. He died in April 1915. Winston Churchill wrote his obituary in The Times, describing him as fitting perfectly 'the image that England's young men had of themselves.' The poem became enormously popular as an expression of noble sacrifice but was later criticized as naive idealization of war.

About Rupert Brooke

Rupert Brooke was born on August 3, 1887, in Rugby, England, and became one of the most celebrated young poets of his generation for his patriotic war sonnets written in 1914. He died on April 23, 1915, of blood poisoning on a hospital ship near Skyros, Greece, before seeing significant combat. His death at twenty-seven and his idealistic, romantic poetry made him an emblem of the lost generation, though later poets like Wilfred Owen offered a starkly different vision of the war.
