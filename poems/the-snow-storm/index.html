The Snow-Storm

by Ralph Waldo Emerson

Written 1835 Published 1841 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written around 1835 and published in 1841, this poem describes a snowstorm arriving overnight and building fantastic architecture — announcing itself, filling all the air, transforming the familiar world. The next morning the wind has gone, the craftsman has vanished, and his work stands in mocking imitation of human art.

Quick Answer

"The Snow-Storm" is a poem by Ralph Waldo Emerson, published in 1841. A snowstorm arrives, announced by the north wind, and overnight builds fantastic shapes — mimicking human architecture with mad pranks, bending into graceful curves and whited walls. By morning the snow-piled art stands in the sun, and the mad artist has vanished behind the horizon, leaving his work behind.

Announced by all the trumpets of the sky,
Arrives the snow, and, driving o'er the fields,
Seems nowhere to alight: the whited air
Hides hills and woods, the river, and the heaven,
And veils the farm-house at the garden's end.
The sled and traveller stopped, the courier's feet
Delayed, all friends shut out, the housemates sit
Around the radiant fireplace, enclosed
In a tumultuous privacy of storm.

Come see the north wind's masonry.
Out of an unseen quarry evermore
Furnished with tile, the fierce artificer
Curves his white bastions with projected roof
Round every windward stake, or tree, or door.
Speeding, the myriad-handed, his wild work
So fanciful, so savage, nought cares he
For number or proportion. Mockingly,
On coop or kennel he hangs Parian wreaths;
A swan-like form invests the hidden thorn;
Fills up the farmer's lane from wall to wall,
Maugre the farmer's sighs; and, at the gate,
A tapering turret overtops the work.
And when his hours are numbered, and the world
Is all his own, retiring, as he were not,
Leaves, when the sun appears, astonished Art
To mimic in slow structures, stone by stone,
Built in an age, the mad wind's night-work,
The frolic architecture of the snow.

Public domain. Published 1841 in Essays: First Series.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-eight lines of blank verse in loose iambic pentameter, the open form suited to a poem about the wild, undirected energy of a storm.

Themes

Nature as the supreme artist — its creations are more beautiful and more inventive than anything human art can produce. The snow-storm's works mock the labor of human builders with their effortless, overnight creation.

Historical Context

Published in Essays: First Series (1841). The poem is one of Emerson's finest nature poems and a direct expression of his Transcendentalist conviction that nature surpasses human art.

About Ralph Waldo Emerson

Ralph Waldo Emerson was born on May 25, 1803, in Boston, Massachusetts, and became the central figure of American Transcendentalism — the philosophical and literary movement that emphasized the divinity of nature, individual intuition, and the unity of all things. His Essays (1841 and 1844) and his poetry exerted enormous influence on American literature and thought, and his lecture 'The American Scholar' (1837) has been called America's intellectual Declaration of Independence. He died in Concord, Massachusetts, in 1882.
