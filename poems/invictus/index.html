Invictus

by William Ernest Henley

Written 1875 Published 1888 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1875 while Henley was recovering in hospital from tuberculosis of the bone — having already had one foot amputated — and published in 1888, this poem asserts unconquerable will in the face of suffering and fate, ending with the declaration that the speaker is the master of his fate and the captain of his soul.

Quick Answer

"Invictus" is a poem by William Ernest Henley, published in 1888. Written from a place of intense physical suffering — Henley spent years in hospital and lost a foot to tuberculosis — it asserts that despite the darkness and blows of life, the speaker's spirit remains unbroken. The closing lines, 'I am the master of my fate: / I am the captain of my soul,' became one of the most quoted statements of human willpower in the English language.

Out of the night that covers me,
Black as the pit from pole to pole,
I thank whatever gods may be
For my unconquerable soul.

In the fell clutch of circumstance
I have not winced nor cried aloud.
Under the bludgeonings of chance
My head is bloody, but unbowed.

Beyond this place of wrath and tears
Looms but the Horror of the shade,
And yet the menace of the years
Finds and shall find me unafraid.

It matters not how strait the gate,
How charged with punishments the scroll,
I am the master of my fate,
I am the captain of my soul.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of iambic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme, tightly controlled and formally resilient in a way that mirrors the speaker's defiant composure.

Themes

Unconquerable willpower in the face of suffering, injustice, and death. The poem argues that while external circumstances (the 'bludgeonings of chance') can inflict pain, they cannot break the inner self. The title is Latin for 'unconquered.' The 'narrow fellow' of the soul is its irreducible, unkillable core.

Historical Context

Henley wrote the poem in 1875 while in Edinburgh Royal Infirmary, where he spent nearly two years recovering from tuberculosis of the bone after his foot was amputated at age seventeen. It was published in 1888 in his collection Book of Verses. The poem became enormously influential and was cited by Nelson Mandela as a source of strength during his imprisonment on Robben Island.

About William Ernest Henley

William Ernest Henley was born on August 23, 1849, in Gloucester, England. He lost a foot to tuberculosis of the bone as a teenager and spent nearly two years in Edinburgh Royal Infirmary, where he was treated by the surgeon Joseph Lister and wrote much of his early poetry, including 'Invictus.' He died in 1903. He was also a prominent literary editor and critic, and was reportedly the inspiration for Long John Silver in his friend Robert Louis Stevenson's Treasure Island.
