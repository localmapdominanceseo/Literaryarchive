Little Gidding (Extract)

by T.S. Eliot

Written 1942 Published 1942 Modernism Public Domain

Published 1942.

Quick Answer

Little Gidding (Extract) is a poem published 1942.

Midwinter spring is its own season
Sempiternal though sodden towards sundown,
Suspended in time, between pole and tropic.
When the short day is brightest, with frost and fire,
The brief sun flames the ice, on pond and ditches,
In windless cold that is the heart's heat,
Reflecting in a watery mirror
A glare that is blindness in the early afternoon.
And glow more intense than blaze of branch, or brazier,
Stirs the dumb spirit: no wind, but pentecostal fire
In the dark time of the year.

What we call the beginning is often the end
And to make an end is to make a beginning.
The end is where we start from.

We shall not cease from exploration
And the end of all our exploring
Will be to arrive where we started
And know the place for the first time.

About T.S. Eliot

Thomas Stearns Eliot was born on September 26, 1888, in St. Louis, Missouri, and became the most influential poet of English-language Modernism after moving to England and eventually becoming a British citizen. His landmark poem The Waste Land (1922) and the earlier 'The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock' (1915) redefined what poetry could do with fragmented form, multiple languages, and literary allusion. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1948 and died in London in 1965.
