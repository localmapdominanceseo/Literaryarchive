The Sick Rose

by William Blake

Written 1794 Published 1794 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1794 in Songs of Experience, this eight-line poem describes an invisible worm that has found a bed of crimson joy in a rose and destroys it — typically read as an allegory of secret, destructive desire corrupting innocent love.

Quick Answer

"The Sick Rose" is a poem by William Blake, published in 1794. An invisible worm, flying through the night in a howling storm, has found the rose's bed of crimson joy and with his dark secret love destroyed her life. The poem is a compressed allegory, most often read as representing how secret or repressed desire corrupts and destroys innocent love.

O Rose, thou art sick!
The invisible worm
That flies in the night,
In the howling storm,

Has found out thy bed
Of crimson joy:
And his dark secret love
Does thy life destroy.

Public domain. Published 1794 in Songs of Experience.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two quatrains of loose trochaic tetrameter with ABCB rhyme, short and compressed as a Blake lyric characteristically is. Every word carries symbolic weight.

Themes

The corruption of innocence by secret, possessive, or repressed desire. The worm's 'dark secret love' is the opposite of open, generous love — it is hidden, destructive, and night-flying. The rose, representing love or beauty, is destroyed precisely by what claims to love it.

Historical Context

Published in Songs of Experience (1794), paired against the more innocent Songs of Innocence. The poem's allegorical content has been read as sexual repression destroying love, as political tyranny corrupting liberty, and as rationalism destroying imagination — Blake's symbolism is deliberately multilayered.

About William Blake

William Blake was born on November 28, 1757, in London, England, and worked as a poet, painter, and printmaker, producing illustrated volumes of poetry that he engraved, printed, and hand-colored himself. Largely unrecognized during his lifetime, he is now considered one of the founding figures of English Romanticism. His best-known collection, Songs of Innocence and of Experience, pairs poems of childlike wonder with darker counterparts exploring suffering and moral complexity. He died in London in 1827.
