On His Blindness

by John Milton

Written 1655 Published 1673 English Renaissance Public Domain

Written around 1655 after Milton had become completely blind, and published in 1673, this sonnet meditates on whether God expects Milton to use the poetic talent he has been given even now that blindness prevents him from writing directly — and arrives at the famous answer that God does not need man's work, but those who stand and wait also serve.

Quick Answer

"On His Blindness" is a sonnet by John Milton, written around 1655 after he lost his sight entirely. The speaker worries that his blindness has rendered his God-given talent useless — would God punish him for not using it? Patience answers that God has no need of man's work or gifts; those who bear their lot in faith and stand ready also serve, and 'they also serve who only stand and wait.'

When I consider how my light is spent,
Ere half my days in this dark world and wide,
And that one talent which is death to hide
Lodged with me useless, though my soul more bent
To serve therewith my Maker, and present
My true account, lest He returning chide;
"Doth God exact day-labour, light denied?"
I fondly ask. But Patience, to prevent
That murmur, soon replies, "God doth not need
Either man's work or his own gifts; who best
Bear his mild yoke, they serve him best. His state
Is kingly: thousands at his bidding speed,
And post o'er land and ocean without rest;
They also serve who only stand and wait."

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter with the characteristic turn at line 9 where Patience speaks, answering the speaker's anxious question with a theological argument about divine sufficiency.

Themes

The relationship between human ability, divine expectation, and the acceptance of limitation. Milton had devoted himself to becoming a great poet and then lost his sight before writing his greatest works (Paradise Lost was composed blind, dictated to secretaries). The poem's resolution — that passive, faithful waiting is itself a form of service — is both consolation and conviction.

Historical Context

Milton went completely blind around 1651-1652 from what was probably glaucoma. He wrote the sonnet around 1655 during the Interregnum period while serving as Latin Secretary to Oliver Cromwell's government (which he did despite his blindness). Paradise Lost, his greatest work, was composed entirely blind through dictation, and not published until 1667.

About John Milton

John Milton was born on December 9, 1608, in London, England, and became one of the greatest poets in the English language, best known for his epic Paradise Lost (1667). He served as Latin Secretary to Oliver Cromwell's government during the English Interregnum and went completely blind around 1651-1652, composing his greatest works through dictation. He died in London in 1674.
