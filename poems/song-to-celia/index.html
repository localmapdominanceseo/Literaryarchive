Song: To Celia

by Ben Jonson

Written 1616 Published 1616 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1616 in The Forest, this song asks Celia to drink to the speaker only with her eyes — a toast that needs no wine. He sent her a wreath of roses and she sent it back, but it still smells not of roses but of her.

Quick Answer

"Song: To Celia" is a poem by Ben Jonson, published in 1616. The speaker asks Celia to drink to him only with her eyes — he'll pledge with his. Leave a kiss but in the cup and he'll not look for wine. He sent her a wreath of roses; she sent it back, but it smells not of itself but of her.

Drink to me only with thine eyes,
And I will pledge with mine;
Or leave a kiss but in the cup,
And I'll not look for wine.
The thirst that from the soul doth rise
Doth ask a drink divine;
But might I of Jove's nectar sup,
I would not change for thine.

I sent thee late a rosy wreath,
Not so much honouring thee
As giving it a hope that there
It could not withered be;
But thou thereon didst only breathe,
And sent'st it back to me;
Since when it grows, and smells, I swear,
Not of itself but thee!

Public domain. Published 1616 in The Forest.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two eight-line stanzas in alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter, ABABCDCD, the simplicity and melody of the form making it one of the most song-like poems in English.

Themes

Love so pure it needs no physical wine — a look from the beloved is intoxicating enough. The wreath that retains her scent is a delicate image of how she transforms everything she touches.

Historical Context

Published in The Forest (1616). Set to music many times and now one of the best-known songs of the Renaissance. The poem is loosely based on letters of the Greek writer Philostratus.

About Ben Jonson

Ben Jonson was born on June 11, 1572, in London, and became the foremost dramatist and poet of the Jacobean era, second only to Shakespeare in contemporary reputation. His plays Volpone (1606) and The Alchemist (1610) are among the greatest comedies in English. As a poet, his Epigrams, The Forest, and Under-wood collections display unrivaled technical mastery and classical learning. He died in London in 1637.
