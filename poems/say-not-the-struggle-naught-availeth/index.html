Say Not the Struggle Naught Availeth

by Arthur Hugh Clough

Written 1849 Published 1862 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1849 and published posthumously in 1862, this poem argues against despair in the face of apparent failure, using two extended analogies — tides coming in on a far beach, and the sun rising in the west before it appears in the east — to suggest that progress is happening even when you cannot see it.

Quick Answer

"Say Not the Struggle Naught Availeth" is a poem by Arthur Hugh Clough, published posthumously in 1862. It tells those struggling in a cause not to despair at apparent failure: as tides rise on a far shore you cannot see, and as the sun lightens the west before dawn appears in the east, so the cause advances even when you cannot perceive it. England may be rising even as you look westward.

Say not the struggle naught availeth,
The labour and the wounds are vain,
The enemy faints not, nor faileth,
And as things have been they remain.

If hopes were dupes, fears may be liars;
It may be, in yon smoke concealed,
Your comrades chase e'en now the fliers,
And, but for you, possess the field.

For while the tired waves, vainly breaking,
Seem here no painful inch to gain,
Far back, through creeks and inlets making,
Comes silent, flooding in, the main.

And not by eastern windows only,
When daylight comes, comes in the light,
In front the sun climbs slow, how slowly,
But westward, look, the land is bright.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1862.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of loose iambic pentameter with ABAB rhyme, simple and clear — a poem designed to be remembered and repeated as encouragement.

Themes

Perseverance and faith in progress despite apparent failure. The poem became famous when Winston Churchill quoted its final stanza in a speech during World War II, becoming one of the most cited Victorian poems of the twentieth century.

Historical Context

Clough wrote the poem around 1849 during the failed European revolutions of 1848, published posthumously in 1862. Churchill's use of it during the Second World War ('And not by eastern windows only / When daylight comes, comes in the light; / In front the sun climbs slow, how slowly, / But westward, look, the land is bright') made the final stanza famous in modern times.

About Arthur Hugh Clough

Arthur Hugh Clough was born on January 1, 1819, in Liverpool, England, and became a Victorian poet known for intellectual honesty, religious doubt, and a skeptical voice unusual among his contemporaries. He was a close friend of Matthew Arnold, who wrote the elegy Thyrsis in his memory. His poem 'Say Not the Struggle Naught Availeth' became famous when Winston Churchill quoted it in a wartime speech. He died in Florence in 1861.
