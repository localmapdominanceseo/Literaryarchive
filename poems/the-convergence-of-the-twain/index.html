The Convergence of the Twain

by Thomas Hardy

Written 1912 Published 1912 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1912, written for the sinking of the Titanic, this poem describes the great ship lying on the ocean floor while the iceberg that would sink it was being shaped simultaneously by the Immanent Will — the two were predestined to meet.

Quick Answer

The Convergence of the Twain is a poem by Thomas Hardy, published in 1912. The Titanic lies on the ocean floor, its gilded mirrors reflecting fish. On the other side of the earth the iceberg was growing — shaped by the Immanent Will that stirs and urges everything — to be the mate for the ship's blaze of size. The two were meant to meet.

In a solitude of the sea
Deep from human vanity,
And the Pride of Life that planned her, stilly couches she.

Steel chambers, late the pyres
Of her salamandrine fires,
Cold currents thrid, and turn to rhythmic tidal lyres.

Over the mirrors meant
To glass the opulent
The sea-worm crawls—grotesque, slimed, dumb, indifferent.

Jewels in joy designed
To ravish the sensuous mind
Lie lightless, all their sparkles bleared and black and blind.

Dim moon-eyed fishes near
Gaze at the gilded gear
And query: 'What does this vaingloriousness down here?'

Well: while was fashioning
This creature of cleaving wing,
The Immanent Will that stirs and urges everything

Prepared a sinister mate
For her—so gaily great—
A Shape of Ice, for the time far and dissociate.

And as the smart ship grew
In stature, grace, and hue,
In shadowy silent distance grew the Iceberg too.

And as the smart ship grew
In stature, grace, and hue,
So the sinister mate grew also, day by day,
Till the Spinner of the Years
Said 'Now!' And each one hears,
And consummation comes, and jars two hemispheres.

Public domain. Published 1912.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eleven three-line stanzas of two short lines and one long with AAA rhyme — the visual shape of the stanzas on the page resembles a ship.

Themes

Fate and the Immanent Will — Hardy's deterministic universe in which great catastrophes are planned and prepared for long before they happen. Human pride and luxury are irrelevant to the cosmic plan.

Historical Context

Published in 1912 in a program for a Titanic disaster benefit. Included in Satires of Circumstance (1914).

About Thomas Hardy

Thomas Hardy was born on June 2, 1840, in Higher Bockhampton, Dorset, England, and became one of the greatest novelists and poets of the Victorian and Edwardian eras. After Jude the Obscure (1895) was attacked for immorality, he stopped writing fiction and devoted himself entirely to poetry, producing eight volumes. His elegies for his first wife Emma, written after her sudden death in 1912, are considered among the finest in the English language. He died in Dorchester in 1928.
