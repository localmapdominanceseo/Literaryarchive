The Rape of the Lock (Extract)

by Alexander Pope

Written 1712 Published 1714 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in expanded form in 1714, The Rape of the Lock is Alexander Pope's mock-heroic poem about Lord Petre cutting a lock of hair from Miss Arabella Fermor without permission — treated with the full apparatus of classical epic. This is the celebrated opening canto.

Quick Answer

"The Rape of the Lock" is a mock-heroic poem by Alexander Pope, published in expanded form in 1714. Lord Petre cuts a lock of hair from Miss Arabella Fermor's head. Pope treats this trivial social incident with the full machinery of classical epic — divine intervention, supernatural guardians, heroic battles — to satirize the vanity and triviality of fashionable society.

What dire Offence from am'rous Causes springs,
What mighty Contests rise from trivial Things,
I sing — This Verse to Caryll, Muse! is due:
This, ev'n Belinda may vouchsafe to view:
Slight is the Subject, but not so the Praise,
If She inspire, and He approve my Lays.
Say what strange Motive, Goddess! cou'd compel
A well-bred Lord t'assault a gentle Belle?
Oh say what stranger Cause, yet unexplor'd,
Cou'd make a gentle Belle reject a Lord?
In Tasks so bold, can Little Men engage,
And in soft Bosoms dwells such mighty Rage?

Sol thro' white Curtains shot a tim'rous Ray,
And op'd those Eyes that must eclipse the Day:
Now Lap-dogs give themselves the rowzing Shake,
And sleepless Lovers, just at Twelve, awake:
Thrice rung the Bell, the Slipper knock'd the Ground,
And the press'd Watch return'd a silver Sound.
Belinda still her downy Pillow prest,
Her Guardian Sylph prolong'd the balmy Rest.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Heroic couplets in iambic pentameter throughout, the elevated form deliberately mismatched to the trivial subject for comic effect.

Themes

The mockery of fashionable society's treatment of trivial matters with epic gravity. Pope uses the gap between the grandeur of classical epic and the smallness of the social incident to satirize both the characters involved and the epic convention itself.

Historical Context

Pope wrote the poem at the request of a mutual friend to heal a quarrel between two families caused by the incident. First published in two cantos in 1712, expanded to five cantos in 1714.

About Alexander Pope

Alexander Pope was born on May 21, 1688, in London, and became the dominant English poet of the early eighteenth century, celebrated for his mastery of the heroic couplet, his satirical wit, and the technical perfection of his verse. His most celebrated works include The Rape of the Lock (1714), An Essay on Man (1733-1734), and The Dunciad (1728). He died at Twickenham in 1744.
