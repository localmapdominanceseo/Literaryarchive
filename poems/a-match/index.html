A Match

by Algernon Charles Swinburne

Written 1866 Published 1866 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1866 in Poems and Ballads, this lyric compares the speaker's love to wind, fire, rain, snow, and day — and asks the beloved to be the same in return.

Quick Answer

A Match is a poem by Swinburne, published in 1866. If love were what the rose is and I were like the leaf, we should grow together in sun and shower. If you were queen of pleasure and I were king of pain, we should stand as the two towers, together facing the rain.

If love were what the rose is,
And I were like the leaf,
Our lives would grow together
In sad or singing weather,
Blown fields or flowerful closes,
Green pleasure or grey grief;
If love were what the rose is,
And I were like the leaf.

If I were what the words are,
And love were like the tune,
With double sound and single
Delight our lips would mingle,
With kisses glad as birds are
That get sweet rain at noon;
If I were what the words are
And love were like the tune.

Public domain. Published 1866 in Poems and Ballads.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five eight-line stanzas of alternating tetrameter and trimeter with ABABCDCD rhyme.

Themes

The complementary nature of love — each stanza proposes a different natural pairing for the lovers.

Historical Context

Published in Poems and Ballads (1866). One of Swinburne's lighter and more charming lyrics.

About Algernon Charles Swinburne

Algernon Charles Swinburne was born on April 5, 1837, in London, and became one of the most technically accomplished and musically dazzling of all Victorian poets. His Poems and Ballads (1866) caused an immediate scandal for its paganism, eroticism, and attacks on conventional morality. His verse drama Atalanta in Calydon (1865) established his reputation. He died at Putney in 1909.
