The Rime of the Ancient Mariner (Extract)

by Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Written 1797 Published 1798 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1798 in Lyrical Ballads, The Rime of the Ancient Mariner is Coleridge's great ballad of a sailor who shoots an albatross and is cursed. This extract covers the most famous passages: the shooting, the death of the crew, the water snakes, and the blessing.

Quick Answer

The Rime of the Ancient Mariner is Coleridge's 1798 ballad in which a mariner shoots an albatross and brings a curse on his ship — all the crew die, and he is left alone on a rotting sea with water water everywhere and ne'er a drop to drink. Only when he blesses the water snakes unawares is the curse broken.

It is an ancient Mariner,
And he stoppeth one of three.
'By thy long grey beard and glittering eye,
Now wherefore stopp'st thou me?

The Bridegroom's doors are opened wide,
And I am next of kin;
The guests are met, the feast is set:
Mayst hear the merry din.'

He holds him with his skinny hand,
'There was a ship,' quoth he.
'Hold off! unhand me, grey-beard loon!'
Eftsoons his hand dropt he.

He holds him with his glittering eye—
The Wedding-Guest stood still,
And listens like a three years' child:
The Mariner hath his will.

The Wedding-Guest sat on a stone:
He cannot choose but hear;
And thus spake on that ancient man,
The bright-eyed Mariner.

'The ship was cheered, the harbour cleared,
Merrily did we drop
Below the kirk, below the hill,
Below the lighthouse top.

'God save thee, ancient Mariner!
From the fiends, that plague thee thus!—
Why look'st thou so?'—With my cross-bow
I shot the Albatross.

Water, water, every where,
And all the boards did shrink;
Water, water, every where,
Nor any drop to drink.

The very deep did rot: O Christ!
That ever this should be!
Yea, slimy things did crawl with legs
Upon the slimy sea.

O Wedding-Guest! this soul hath been
Alone on a wide wide sea:
So lonely 'twas, that God himself
Scarce seemed there to be.

He prayeth best, who loveth best
All things both great and small;
For the dear God who loveth us,
He made and loveth all.

Public domain. Published 1798 in Lyrical Ballads.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A long ballad in four-line stanzas with ABCB rhyme, using archaic diction and the traditional ballad's supernatural machinery.

Themes

Guilt, punishment, and grace — the mariner's inexplicable killing of the albatross brings cosmic retribution, and his blessing of the water snakes (an act of love toward unchosen creatures) is the turning point of his redemption.

Historical Context

Published in Lyrical Ballads (1798), the collaboration with Wordsworth that launched English Romanticism. Coleridge claimed to have written it almost entirely from a dream-state.

About Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was born on October 21, 1772, in Ottery St Mary, Devon, England, and became one of the founders of the English Romantic movement alongside his friend William Wordsworth. Together they published Lyrical Ballads in 1798, a collection that launched Romanticism in English literature. Coleridge's own most celebrated contributions to the collection, 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' and 'Kubla Khan,' are among the most distinctive and widely studied poems in the language. He died in Highgate, London, in 1834.
