Peace

by Henry Vaughan

Written 1650 Published 1650 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1650 in Silex Scintillans, this short poem asks where true peace is to be found — not in the world, not in commerce or pleasure — but in a country far beyond the stars, where a new spring blossoms, a rose which no sun's eye did ever see.

Quick Answer

"Peace" is a poem by Henry Vaughan, published in 1650. The speaker asks where he will find rest and full peace. Not in the world, not in fame. There is a country far beyond the stars, a new spring blossoming in eternal summer, where no war is heard and where the shady city of palm trees waits.

My soul, there is a country
Far beyond the stars,
Where stands a winged sentry
All skilful in the wars:
There above noise and danger
Sweet Peace sits crowned with smiles,
And One born in a manger
Commands the beauteous files.
He is thy gracious friend,
And (O my soul awake!)
Did in pure love descend,
To die here for thy sake.
If thou canst get but thither,
There grows the flower of peace,
The rose that cannot wither,
Thy fortress and thy ease.
Leave then thy foolish ranges,
For none can thee secure
But One who never changes,
Thy God, thy life, thy cure.

Public domain. Published 1650 in Silex Scintillans.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five four-line stanzas of alternating tetrameter and trimeter with ABAB rhyme, the compressed, hymn-like form suited to its subject.

Themes

The search for spiritual peace beyond the transient pleasures and conflicts of the world. The 'country far beyond the stars' is heaven, but Vaughan presents it with sensory vividness — a rose, a spring, a shady city.

Historical Context

Published in Silex Scintillans (1650). One of Vaughan's most lyrical short poems, often paired with 'The Retreat' and 'The World' as expressions of his mystical longing.

About Henry Vaughan

Henry Vaughan was born in 1621 in Brecknockshire, Wales, and became one of the finest poets of the Metaphysical tradition, deeply influenced by George Herbert. His collection Silex Scintillans (1650, 1655) contains some of the most beautiful devotional poems in English, including 'The World,' 'The Night,' and 'Regeneration.' He worked as a physician in Wales and died in 1695.
