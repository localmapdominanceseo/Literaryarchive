The Eagle

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1851 Published 1851 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1851, this six-line poem is one of the most compressed masterpieces in Victorian verse, describing an eagle perched on a mountain crag, scanning the sea below, before dropping like a thunderbolt.

Quick Answer

"The Eagle" is a short poem by Alfred Lord Tennyson, published in 1851. In six lines, it places an eagle high on a mountain crag, close to the sun, scanning the wrinkled sea below with his crooked hands — before he falls like a thunderbolt.

He clasps the crag with crooked hands;
Close to the sun in lonely lands,
Ring'd with the azure world, he stands.

The wrinkled sea beneath him crawls;
He watches from his mountain walls,
And like a thunderbolt he falls.

Public domain. Published 1851.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two tercets of iambic tetrameter with AAA BBB rhyme — three-line stanzas, each rhyme complete, giving the poem the compactness of a painting.

Themes

Power, height, and the sudden violence of natural predation. The eagle is presented with grandeur — he is near the sun, the sea wrinkles far below him — before the final line collapses all that height into sudden, explosive action.

Historical Context

Published in 1851 as a fragment in a collection. It is one of Tennyson's most frequently anthologized poems precisely because of its extraordinary compression.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
