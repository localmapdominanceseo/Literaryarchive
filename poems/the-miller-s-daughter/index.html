The Miller's Daughter (Extract)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1832 Published 1842 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1842, The Miller's Daughter is a narrative poem told by an old man to his wife — the miller's daughter he loved and married — recalling their courtship with extraordinary tenderness.

Quick Answer

The Miller's Daughter (1842) is Tennyson's narrative of a man recalling his courtship of a miller's daughter to whom he is now happily married. He asks: do you remember how you used to sit and take his hand, and say 'I love you, Alice'? Sweet is every sound, sweeter thy voice, but every sound is sweet.

It is the miller's daughter,
And she is grown so dear, so dear,
That I would be the jewel
That trembles at her ear:
For hid in ringlets day and night,
I'd touch her neck so warm and white.

And I would be the girdle
About her dainty dainty waist,
And her heart would beat against me,
In sorrow and in rest:
And I should know if it beat right,
I'd clasp it round so close and tight.

And I would be the necklace,
And all day long to fall and rise
Upon her balmy bosom,
With her laughter or her sighs,
And I would lie so light, so light,
I scarce should be unclasp'd at night.

Public domain. Published 1842.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Multiple stanzas in varied meters including the famous song 'Love that hath us in the net' in iambic tetrameter.

Themes

Domestic happiness and the memory of courtship — the poem is unusual in Tennyson for its contented ending, celebrating a love that was fulfilled rather than lost.

Historical Context

First published in 1832, revised for 1842. One of Tennyson's most affectionate domestic poems.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
