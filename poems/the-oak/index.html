The Oak

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1892 Published 1892 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1892 in The Death of Oenone, Tennyson's last collection, this eight-line poem about an oak tree growing from a sapling to a great tree is often taken as a meditation on a life well lived.

Quick Answer

"The Oak" is a short poem by Tennyson, published in 1892. Live thy Life, young and old, like yon oak, bright in spring, living gold, summer-rich, then autumn-changed and soberer-hued, and all the way through to leafless, all the way to the winter season, still is full, still is good.

Live thy Life,
Young and old,
Like yon oak,
Bright in spring,
Living gold;

Summer-rich
Then; and then
Autumn-changed,
Soberer-hued
Gold again.

All his leaves
Fall'n at length,
Look, he stands,
Trunk and bough,
Naked strength.

Public domain. Published 1892.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight lines of trochaic dimeter, one of the most compressed of Tennyson's poems.

Themes

The fullness of a life lived through all its seasons — not diminishing with age but completing.

Historical Context

Published in 1892 in The Death of Oenone, his final collection, written in the months before his death.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
