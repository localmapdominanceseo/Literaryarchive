To a Butterfly

by William Wordsworth

Written 1802 Published 1807 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1807 in Poems in Two Volumes, this short poem asks a butterfly to stay still a moment and share the poet's garden — for it has come from the same field where he and his sister chased butterflies as children.

Quick Answer

To a Butterfly is a poem by Wordsworth, published in 1807. Stay near me — do not take thy flight! A little longer stay in sight! I have been watching thee for hours. This plot of orchard-ground is ours — my sister Emma and I used to chase thee once, and that sweet time is past.

Stay near me—do not take thy flight!
A little longer stay in sight!
Much converse do I find in thee,
Historian of my infancy!
Float near me; do not yet depart!
Dead times revive in thee:
Thou bring'st, gay creature as thou art!
A solemn image to my heart,
My father's family!

Oh! pleasant, pleasant were the days,
The time, when, in our childish plays,
My sister Emmeline and I
Together chased the butterfly!
A very hunter did I rush
Upon the prey:—with leaps and springs
I followed on from brake to bush;
But she, God love her! feared to brush
The dust from off its wings.

Public domain. Published 1807.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two eight-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and dimeter, the poem light and domestic.

Themes

Childhood memory and the tenderness of the domestic moment — the butterfly connecting present and past.

Historical Context

Published in Poems in Two Volumes (1807). Written while Wordsworth and his sister Dorothy watched a butterfly in their garden.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
