Michael (Extract)

by William Wordsworth

Written 1800 Published 1800 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1800 in Lyrical Ballads, Michael is Wordsworth's narrative poem about an aged shepherd whose son Luke is sent to the city to restore the family's fortunes, falls into disgrace, and never returns — leaving the old man to die with the sheepfold unfinished.

Quick Answer

"Michael" is a narrative poem by Wordsworth, published in 1800. Michael is an aged shepherd who has worked his hillside for eighty years. When family debts threaten his land, his son Luke is sent to the city to work. Luke falls into dissipation and is never heard from again. Michael continues to go to the unfinished sheepfold alone until his death.

If from the public way you turn your steps
Up the tumultuous brook of Greenhead Ghyll,
You will suppose that with an upright path
Your feet must struggle; in such bold ascent
The pastoral mountains front you, face to face.
But, courage! for around that boisterous brook
The mountains have all opened out themselves,
And made a hidden valley of their own.
No habitation can be seen; but they
Who journey thither find themselves alone
With a few sheep, with rocks and stones, and kites
That overhead are sailing in the sky.
It is in truth an utter solitude;
Nor should I have made mention of this Dell
But for one object which you might pass by,
Might see and notice not. Beside the brook
Appears a straggling heap of unhewn stones!
And to that simple object appertains
A story—unenriched with strange events,
Yet not unfit, I deem, for the fireside,
Or for the summer shade.

Public domain. Published 1800 in Lyrical Ballads.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four hundred and ninety-one lines of blank verse, one of the great narrative poems in the English language.

Themes

The destruction of the rural way of life by economic forces — the city takes Luke from the hills and ruins him. The unfinished sheepfold becomes a monument to broken bonds and lost inheritance.

Historical Context

Published in the second edition of Lyrical Ballads (1800). Based on a local Lake District story. Wordsworth considered it one of his most important poems for its treatment of the lives of the rural poor.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
