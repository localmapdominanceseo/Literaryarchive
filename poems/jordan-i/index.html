Jordan (I)

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 in The Temple, this poem defends plain religious verse against the elaborate conceits of secular poetry, asking why fiction should be dressed in elaborate disguise when the plainness of truth is its own beauty.

Quick Answer

"Jordan (I)" is a poem by George Herbert, published in 1633, defending plain devotional poetry. Must poetry always be dressed in allegorical disguise? Must truth be veiled? Herbert says no — he will plainly say 'My God, My King,' and that directness is its own glory.

Who says that fictions onely and false hair
Become a verse? Is there in truth no beautie?
Is all good structure in a winding stair?
May no lines passe, except they do their dutie
Not to a true, but painted chair?

Is it no verse, except enchanted groves
And sudden arbours shadow course-spunne lines?
Must purling streams refresh a lovers loves?
Must all be vail'd, while he that reades, divines,
Catching the sense at two removes?

Sherlocke away: My God, My King.
Who says My God, My King? O blessed man!
Who says My God, My King? Why that is all
That truth requires of me. My God, My King
I praise, I love. Thus simply I begin.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three five-line stanzas in iambic pentameter, the poem's formal simplicity enacting its argument for plain speech.

Themes

The aesthetics of devotional poetry — the argument that direct, simple language about God is more appropriate and more powerful than elaborate Petrarchan conceits applied to religious subjects.

Historical Context

The title alludes to the River Jordan, the boundary of the Promised Land — crossing Jordan meant leaving behind the elaborate secular world of pastoral poetry for the direct country of devotion.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
