The Altar

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 as the first poem in The Temple's main section, this pattern poem is printed in the shape of an altar, describing a broken altar of stone made from the poet's broken heart and cemented with tears.

Quick Answer

"The Altar" is a pattern poem by George Herbert, published in 1633, printed in the shape of an altar. The speaker has built an altar made from his hard and broken heart, cemented with tears. Only God's power can cut and shape this stone; if God consecrates it, it will praise him forever.

A broken Altar, Lord, thy servant rears,
Made of a heart, and cemented with teares:
Whose parts are as thy hand did frame;
No workmans tool hath touch'd the same.
     A Heart alone
     Is such a stone,
     As nothing but
     Thy pow'r doth cut.
     Wherefore each part
     Of my hard heart
     Meets in this frame,
To praise thy Name.
That, if I chance to hold my peace,
These stones to praise thee may not cease.
O let thy blessed Sacrifice be mine,
And sanctifie this Altar to be thine.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Printed in the shape of an altar — wider at the top and bottom, narrow in the middle — making the visual form itself part of the poem's meaning.

Themes

The paradox of the broken heart as the proper material for worship — the stone that cannot be shaped by man must be shaped by God. The poem echoes Psalm 51:17: 'a broken and a contrite heart, O God, thou wilt not despise.'

Historical Context

Placed first in 'The Church' section of The Temple (1633), The Altar sets the theme for the entire collection: the offering of a broken self to God.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
