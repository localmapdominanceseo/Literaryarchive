Ode on Solitude

by Alexander Pope

Written 1700 Published 1717 English Renaissance Public Domain

Written around 1700 when Pope was about twelve years old and published in 1717, this short ode celebrates the man who lives quietly on his own land, raising his own food, breathing his own air, and dying without fame or notice.

Quick Answer

"Ode on Solitude" is an early poem by Alexander Pope, written around age twelve and published in 1717. It celebrates the man whose wishes are modest — he asks only to live on his paternal land, raise his own cattle and crops, breathe his own fields, and die without a groan, leaving no stone marking his name.

Happy the man, whose wish and care
A few paternal acres bound,
Content to breathe his native air,
In his own ground.

Whose herds with milk, whose fields with bread,
Whose flocks supply him with attire,
Whose trees in summer yield him shade,
In winter fire.

Blest, who can unconcern'dly find
Hours, days, and years slide soft away,
In health of body, peace of mind,
Quiet by day,

Sound sleep by night; study and ease,
Together mixt; sweet recreation;
And Innocence, which most does please
With meditation.

Thus let me live, unseen, unknown,
Thus unlamented let me die,
Steal from the world, and not a stone
Tell where I lie.

Public domain. Published 1717.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five quatrains of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter, simple and musical, the formal simplicity mirroring the simple life it celebrates.

Themes

The classical ideal of the self-sufficient rural life, drawn from Horace's Beatus ille (happy the man who lives by his own land). The poem is remarkable for having been written by a child.

Historical Context

Pope reportedly wrote this when he was about twelve years old, making it one of the most impressive juvenilia in English literature. Published in his Works (1717).

About Alexander Pope

Alexander Pope was born on May 21, 1688, in London, and became the dominant English poet of the early eighteenth century, celebrated for his mastery of the heroic couplet, his satirical wit, and the technical perfection of his verse. His most celebrated works include The Rape of the Lock (1714), An Essay on Man (1733-1734), and The Dunciad (1728). He died at Twickenham in 1744.
