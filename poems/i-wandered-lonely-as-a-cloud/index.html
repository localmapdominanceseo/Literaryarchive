I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud

by William Wordsworth

Written 1804 Published 1815 English Romanticism Public Domain

Also known as 'Daffodils,' this poem was inspired by a walk Wordsworth took with his sister Dorothy in 1802, during which they came upon a long stretch of daffodils beside Ullswater in England's Lake District. First published in 1807 and revised in 1815, it has become one of the most widely anthologized poems in the English language.

Quick Answer

"I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud," also known as "Daffodils," is a poem by William Wordsworth first published in 1807 and revised in 1815. It describes the speaker encountering a vast field of daffodils beside a lake, an image that later returns to comfort him in solitary, low moments, illustrating Wordsworth's belief in nature's lasting power over memory and imagination.

I wandered lonely as a cloud
That floats on high o'er vales and hills,
When all at once I saw a crowd,
A host, of golden daffodils;
Beside the lake, beneath the trees,
Fluttering and dancing in the breeze.

Continuous as the stars that shine
And twinkle on the milky way,
They stretched in never-ending line
Along the margin of a bay:
Ten thousand saw I at a glance,
Tossing their heads in sprightly dance.

The waves beside them danced; but they
Out-did the sparkling waves in glee:
A poet could not but be gay,
In such a jocund company:
I gazed—and gazed—but little thought
What wealth the show to me had brought:

For oft, when on my couch I lie
In vacant or in pensive mood,
They flash upon that inward eye
Which is the bliss of solitude;
And then my heart with pleasure fills,
And dances with the daffodils.

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org), 1815 revised edition.

Analysis

Form and Structure

The poem is written in four six-line stanzas (sestets) with an ABABCC rhyme scheme and iambic tetrameter, giving it a steady, dance-like rhythm that mirrors the daffodils 'tossing their heads in sprightly dance' described within the poem itself.

Themes

A defining theme of English Romanticism runs through the poem: that an intense experience of nature can be stored in memory and recalled later to restore the mind. The final stanza's 'inward eye' shows the daffodils sustaining the speaker emotionally long after the original moment has passed, in solitude rather than company.

Historical Context

The poem was inspired by an 1802 walk Wordsworth took with his sister Dorothy around Ullswater in England's Lake District, where Dorothy's own journal recorded the daffodils in detail. Wordsworth drafted the poem around 1804, published an early version in 1807, and revised it into its now-familiar form, adding an entire stanza, for the 1815 edition.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
