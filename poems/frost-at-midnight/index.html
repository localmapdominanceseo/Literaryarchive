Frost at Midnight

by Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Written 1798 Published 1798 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1798, this conversation poem describes Coleridge sitting up alone at midnight with his sleeping infant son, meditating on the frost outside and his own childhood — and wishing for his son a childhood in nature that he himself never had.

Quick Answer

"Frost at Midnight" is a poem by Coleridge, published in 1798. At midnight, frost works in secret ministry while everyone sleeps, including his infant son. Coleridge meditates on his own city childhood, longing to see his native countryside — then resolves that his son shall have what he lacked: a childhood of lakes and mountains, where God will teach him through all the seasons.

The Frost performs its secret ministry,
Unhelped by any wind. The owlet's cry
Came loud—and hark, again! loud as before.
The inmates of my cottage, all at rest,
Have left me to that solitude, which suits
Abstruser musings: save that at my side
My cradled infant slumbers peacefully.
'Tis calm indeed! so calm, that it disturbs
And vexes meditation with its strange
And extreme silentness. Sea, hill, and wood,
This populous village! Sea, and hill, and wood,
With all the numberless goings-on of life,
Inaudible as dreams! the thin blue flame
Lies on my low-burnt fire, and quivers not;
Only that film, which fluttered on the grate,
Still flutters there, the sole unquiet thing.
Methinks, its motion in this hush of nature
Gives it dim sympathies with me who live,
Making it a companionable form,
Whose puny flaps and freaks the idling Spirit
By its own moods interprets, every where
Echo or mirror seeking of itself,
And makes a toy of Thought.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Seventy-four lines of blank verse in loose iambic pentameter, one of the conversation poems in which the speaker meditates in apparent solitude.

Themes

The contrast between city and nature as formative environments, the desire to give one's child better than one received, and the way frost's secret ministry parallels the poet's meditative activity.

Historical Context

Published in 1798. Written at Nether Stowey, Somerset, where Coleridge lived near Wordsworth. The infant is his son Hartley, who appears in several of Coleridge and Wordsworth's poems.

About Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was born on October 21, 1772, in Ottery St Mary, Devon, England, and became one of the founders of the English Romantic movement alongside his friend William Wordsworth. Together they published Lyrical Ballads in 1798, a collection that launched Romanticism in English literature. Coleridge's own most celebrated contributions to the collection, 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' and 'Kubla Khan,' are among the most distinctive and widely studied poems in the language. He died in Highgate, London, in 1834.
