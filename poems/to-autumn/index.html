To Autumn

by John Keats

Written 1819 Published 1820 English Romanticism Public Domain

Composed on September 19, 1819, after a walk near Winchester, and published in 1820, this ode is widely considered Keats's most perfect poem and one of the greatest short poems in English, celebrating autumn's richness without the anguished tension of his other odes.

Quick Answer

"To Autumn" is an ode by John Keats, written in 1819 and published in 1820. Addressing autumn directly, the poem traces the season through three stages: the warm ripening of fruit and grain; the drowsy, harvesting middle of the season; and finally the soft, sound-filled days of late autumn, accepting the season's gentle decline with serenity rather than grief.

Season of mists and mellow fruitfulness,
Close bosom-friend of the maturing sun;
Conspiring with him how to load and bless
With fruit the vines that round the thatch-eves run;
To bend with apples the moss'd cottage-trees,
And fill all fruit with ripeness to the core;
To swell the gourd, and plump the hazel shells
With a sweet kernel; to set budding more,
And still more, later flowers for the bees,
Until they think warm days will never cease,
For Summer has o'er-brimm'd their clammy cells.

Who hath not seen thee oft amid thy store?
Sometimes whoever seeks abroad may find
Thee sitting careless on a granary floor,
Thy hair soft-lifted by the winnowing wind;
Or on a half-reap'd furrow sound asleep,
Drows'd with the fume of poppies, while thy hook
Spares the next swath and all its twined flowers:
And sometimes like a gleaner thou dost keep
Steady thy laden head across a brook;
Or by a cyder-press, with patient look,
Thou watchest the last oozings hours by hours.

Where are the songs of Spring? Ay, where are they?
Think not of them, thou hast thy music too,--
While barred clouds bloom the soft-dying day,
And touch the stubble-plains with rosy hue;
Then in a wailful choir the small gnats mourn
Among the river sallows, borne aloft
Or sinking as the light wind lives or dies;
And full-grown lambs loud bleat from hilly bourn;
Hedge-crickets sing; and now with treble soft
The red-breast whistles from a garden-croft;
And gathering swallows twitter in the skies.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three eleven-line stanzas in iambic pentameter, with a rhyme scheme that begins ABABCDEDCE in each stanza. Unlike Keats's earlier odes, the poem is notable for the absence of the speaker's own anxious questioning, achieving what Keats called 'negative capability': absorption in the sensory world without reaching after resolution.

Themes

The beauty of maturity and decline, treated as fulfillment rather than loss. Where Keats's other 1819 odes struggle between the ideal and the real, 'To Autumn' finds the two perfectly unified in the season's abundance. The poem's final question, 'Where are the songs of Spring? Ay, where are they?', is immediately dismissed: autumn has its own music in the bleating lambs, singing crickets, and twittering swallows, a music that accepts the approach of winter without protest.

Historical Context

Keats composed the poem in September 1819 after a walk through the water-meadows near Winchester. In a letter to his friend John Hamilton Reynolds, he described the scene as extraordinarily beautiful and noted that he had composed a poem on it. It was published in his 1820 collection alongside Lamia and The Eve of St. Agnes, which also appeared in print about a year before Keats died of tuberculosis in Rome at twenty-five.

About John Keats

John Keats was born on October 31, 1795, in London, England, and died of tuberculosis at just twenty-five, yet in a creative burst during 1819 alone produced some of the most celebrated odes in the English language, including 'Ode to a Nightingale' and 'Ode on a Grecian Urn.' His poetry is known for its rich sensory detail and its preoccupation with beauty, mortality, and the relationship between art and permanence. He died in Rome in 1821.
