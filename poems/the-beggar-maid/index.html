The Beggar Maid

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1842 Published 1842 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1842, this short poem retells the legend of King Cophetua who fell in love with a beggar maid — a story of love that transcends social rank.

Quick Answer

"The Beggar Maid" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1842. Her arms across her breast she laid; she was more fair than words can say: barefooted came the beggar maid before the King Cophetua. In robe and crown the king stept down, to meet and greet her on her way; 'It is no wonder,' said the lords, 'She is more beautiful than day.'

Her arms across her breast she laid;
She was more fair than words can say:
Barefooted came the beggar maid
Before the king Cophetua.
In robe and crown the king stept down,
To meet and greet her on her way;
'It is no wonder,' said the lords,
'She is more beautiful than day.'

As shines the moon in clouded skies,
She in her poor attire was seen:
One praised her ankles, one her eyes,
One her dark hair and lovesome mien.
So sweet a face, such angel grace,
In all that land had never been:
Cophetua sware a royal oath:
'This beggar maid shall be my queen!'

Public domain. Published 1842.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two stanzas of eight lines each in iambic tetrameter with ABAB CDCD rhyme.

Themes

Love that transcends social rank — the king who abandons his dignity to marry a beggar maid because her beauty surpasses all worldly wealth.

Historical Context

Published in 1842. Based on the legend of King Cophetua, the subject of a famous Pre-Raphaelite painting by Burne-Jones.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
