The Oxen

by Thomas Hardy

Written 1915 Published 1915 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1915, this poem recalls the Christmas Eve belief that oxen kneel in their barns at midnight in memory of the Nativity — and says that if someone asked Hardy to come and see them kneel, he would go in the hope that it might be true.

Quick Answer

The Oxen is a poem by Thomas Hardy, published in 1915. Christmas Eve, and the elder sitting by the fire says the oxen are kneeling now in their straw at midnight. In those years we all believed it. Now if someone came and said 'Come see the oxen kneel,' Hardy would go in the hoping it might be so.

Christmas Eve, and twelve of the clock.
'Now they are all on their knees,'
An elder said as we sat in a flock
By the embers in hearthside ease.

We pictured the meek mild creatures where
They dwelt in their strawy pen,
Nor did it occur to one of us there
To doubt they were kneeling then.

So fair a fancy few would weave
In these years! Yet, I feel,
If someone said on Christmas Eve,
'Come; see the oxen kneel

'In the lonely barton by yonder coomb
Our childhood used to know,'
I should go with him in the gloom,
Hoping it might be so.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABAB rhyme, simple and direct.

Themes

Lost faith and its residue — Hardy no longer believes in the Christmas miracle, but the hope that he might still find it true has not entirely died. The poem is one of the most moving expressions of secular wistfulness in English.

Historical Context

Published in The Times on Christmas Eve 1915.

About Thomas Hardy

Thomas Hardy was born on June 2, 1840, in Higher Bockhampton, Dorset, England, and became one of the greatest novelists and poets of the Victorian and Edwardian eras. After Jude the Obscure (1895) was attacked for immorality, he stopped writing fiction and devoted himself entirely to poetry, producing eight volumes. His elegies for his first wife Emma, written after her sudden death in 1912, are considered among the finest in the English language. He died in Dorchester in 1928.
