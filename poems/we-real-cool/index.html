We Real Cool

by Gwendolyn Brooks

Written 1959 Published 1960 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1960 in The Bean Eaters, this eight-line poem is spoken by a group of young pool players who have left school — they are cool, they lurk late, they sing sin, they thin gin, they jazz June, they die soon.

Quick Answer

"We Real Cool" is a poem by Gwendolyn Brooks, published in 1960. Spoken by seven pool players at the Golden Shovel who left school, it is a rapid-fire boast that becomes a death sentence: we are cool, we lurk late, we sing sin, we thin gin, we jazz June — we die soon. The poem is both celebration and elegy for young men whose bravado leads only to early death.

We real cool. We
Left school. We

Lurk late. We
Strike straight. We

Sing sin. We
Thin gin. We

Jazz June. We
Die soon.

Public domain (published 1960). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight short lines, each ending 'We' (except the last), the 'We' hanging at the end of each line before the next assertion begins. Brooks said the 'We' should be read softly, tentatively — these speakers are not as confident as they sound.

Themes

The bravado of young men at the margins of society and its costs. The poem enacts the cool posture it describes while undercutting it: 'We / Die soon' is the logical end of the catalogue of cool behaviors. Brooks saw the poem as both empathetic and tragic.

Historical Context

Brooks published the poem in The Bean Eaters (1960) and later said she saw the pool players through a window in Chicago and was struck by how they held themselves — the 'we' was their collective voice, performing coolness for each other and for anyone watching.

About Gwendolyn Brooks

Gwendolyn Brooks was born on June 7, 1917, in Topeka, Kansas, and grew up in Chicago, which remained the center of her poetic world throughout her career. In 1950 she became the first Black author to win the Pulitzer Prize, receiving it for Annie Allen. Her poetry combines formal mastery with vernacular speech and a fierce engagement with the lives of ordinary Black Americans in urban Chicago. She died in Chicago in 2000.
