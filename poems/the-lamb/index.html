The Lamb

by William Blake

Written 1789 Published 1789 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1789 in Songs of Innocence, this short poem is spoken by a child addressing a lamb, asking who made it and then answering with a theological argument that collapses the distance between lamb, child, and Christ into a single act of divine naming.

Quick Answer

"The Lamb" is a poem by William Blake, published in 1789 in Songs of Innocence. A child addresses a lamb, asking who created it, then answers that its creator is Jesus, who is also called a Lamb and who 'became a little child,' creating a chain of identity linking lamb, child, and Christ.

Little Lamb, who made thee?
Dost thou know who made thee?
Gave thee life, and bid thee feed
By the stream and o'er the mead;
Gave thee clothing of delight,
Softest clothing, woolly, bright;
Gave thee such a tender voice,
Making all the vales rejoice?
Little Lamb, who made thee?
Dost thou know who made thee?

Little Lamb, I'll tell thee,
Little Lamb, I'll tell thee:
He is called by thy name,
For he calls himself a Lamb.
He is meek, and he is mild;
He became a little child.
I a child, and thou a lamb,
We are called by his name.
Little Lamb, God bless thee!
Little Lamb, God bless thee!

Public domain. Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two stanzas of ten lines each, with a gentle AABB rhyme scheme and a simple, song-like trochaic meter. Each stanza is framed by a repeated couplet refrain, giving the poem its hymn-like, catechistic structure of question followed by answer.

Themes

The innocence and goodness of creation, seen through a child's eyes. The poem argues that God's nature, as expressed through the gentle, meek Christ who 'became a little child,' is essentially tender and nurturing rather than fearsome. It stands in deliberate contrast to 'The Tyger' in Songs of Experience, which asks the same question, 'Who made thee?', about a creature defined by power and terror rather than gentleness.

Historical Context

Blake published Songs of Innocence in 1789, hand-engraving and printing the illustrated volume himself. In 1794 he published Songs of Experience and combined the two into a single volume, Songs of Innocence and of Experience, pairing poems in deliberate contrast. 'The Lamb' and 'The Tyger' are the most famous contrasting pair, together forming Blake's central meditation on the nature of divine creation.

About William Blake

William Blake was born on November 28, 1757, in London, England, and worked as a poet, painter, and printmaker, producing illustrated volumes of poetry that he engraved, printed, and hand-colored himself. Largely unrecognized during his lifetime, he is now considered one of the founding figures of English Romanticism. His best-known collection, Songs of Innocence and of Experience, pairs poems of childlike wonder with darker counterparts exploring suffering and moral complexity. He died in London in 1827.
