A Slumber Did My Spirit Seal

by William Wordsworth

Written 1799 Published 1800 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1800 in Lyrical Ballads, this is the most compressed of the Lucy poems — eight lines mourning a girl who seemed immortal when alive and is now rolled round with rocks and stones and trees.

Quick Answer

A Slumber Did My Spirit Seal is a poem by Wordsworth, published in 1800. A slumber sealed the speaker's spirit — he had no human fears for her; she seemed a thing that could not feel the touch of earthly years. Now she is dead; no motion has she now, no force. She is rolled round with rocks and stones and trees.

A slumber did my spirit seal;
I had no human fears:
She seemed a thing that could not feel
The touch of earthly years.

No motion has she now, no force;
She neither hears nor sees;
Rolled round in earth's diurnal course,
With rocks, and stones, and trees.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two quatrains of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABAB rhyme — eight lines of absolute compression.

Themes

The shock of death — the speaker was complacent about the beloved's immortality while she lived; now she is absorbed into the indifferent motions of the natural world.

Historical Context

Published in Lyrical Ballads (1800). The most philosophical of the Lucy poems.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
