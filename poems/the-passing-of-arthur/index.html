The Passing of Arthur (Extract)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1869 Published 1869 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1869 as the final poem in Idylls of the King, The Passing of Arthur expands Morte d'Arthur with a frame narrative and the famous conclusion: The old order changeth, yielding place to new.

Quick Answer

In The Passing of Arthur (1869), Arthur's final words to Bedivere before departing for Avalon include the famous lines: The old order changeth, yielding place to new, and God fulfils himself in many ways, lest one good custom should corrupt the world. Pray for my soul. More things are wrought by prayer than this world dreams of.

Then from the dawn it seemed there came, but faint
As from beyond the limit of the world,
Like the last echo born of a great cry,
Sounds, as if some fair city were one voice
Around a king returning from his wars.

Thereat once more he moved about, and clomb
Even to the highest he could climb, and saw,
Straining his eyes beneath an arch of hand,
Or thought he saw, the speck that bare the King,
Down that long water opening on the deep
Somewhere far off, pass on and on, and go
From less to less and vanish into light.
And the new sun rose bringing the new year.

'The old order changeth, yielding place to new,
And God fulfils himself in many ways,
Lest one good custom should corrupt the world.'

Public domain. Published 1869 in Idylls of the King.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Blank verse, expanding the earlier Morte d'Arthur with a frame narrative.

Themes

The passing of greatness and the necessity of change — Arthur's wisdom is to recognize that even the best order must yield to the new, and that prayer sustains what human effort cannot.

Historical Context

Published in 1869 as the conclusion to Idylls of the King, the sequence of twelve Arthurian poems that occupied Tennyson for decades.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
