She Dwelt Among the Untrodden Ways

by William Wordsworth

Written 1799 Published 1800 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1800 in Lyrical Ballads, this is one of the Lucy poems — describing a girl who lived unknown by all but the speaker, and whose death made a difference to him only.

Quick Answer

She Dwelt Among the Untrodden Ways is a Lucy poem by Wordsworth, published in 1800. Lucy dwelt near the springs of Dove, unknown and unprais'd. She was a violet by a mossy stone, a star when only one is shining. She lived unknown, and few could know when Lucy ceased to be. But she is in her grave, and oh the difference to me.

She dwelt among the untrodden ways
Beside the springs of Dove,
A Maid whom there were none to praise
And very few to love:

A violet by a mossy stone
Half hidden from the eye!
—Fair as a star, when only one
Is shining in the sky.

She lived unknown, and few could know
When Lucy ceased to be;
But she is in her grave, and, oh,
The difference to me!

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABAB rhyme.

Themes

The death of someone known only to the speaker and the immensity of private grief for someone unknown to the world.

Historical Context

Published in Lyrical Ballads (1800). Part of the Lucy sequence; Lucy may be an idealized figure rather than a real person.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
