Channel Firing

by Thomas Hardy

Written 1914 Published 1914 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in April 1914, just before the outbreak of World War I, this poem imagines the dead in a coastal churchyard being woken by gunnery practice in the Channel — God explains it is just the nations preparing for war again, and the dead lie down again.

Quick Answer

Channel Firing is a poem by Thomas Hardy, published in April 1914. The guns wake the dead in a churchyard. God says: be not appalled — these are nations doing what they always do, practicing to annihilate each other. The dead lie down again. The guns go on roaring. All the way to Stourton Tower and Camelot.

That night your great guns, unawares,
Shook all our coffins as we lay,
And broke the chancel window-squares,
We thought it was the Judgement-day

And sat upright. While drearisome
Arose the howl of wakened hounds:
The mouse let fall the altar-crumb,
The worms drew back into the mounds,

The glebe cow drooled. Till God called, 'No;
It's gunnery practice out at sea
Just as before you went below;
The world is as it used to be:

'All nations striving strong to make
Red war yet redder. Mad as hatters
They do no more for Christés sake
Than you who are helpless in such matters.

'That this is not the judgment-hour
For some of them's a blessed thing,
For if it were they'd have to scour
Hell's floor for so much threatening....

'Ha, ha. It will be warmer when
I blow the trumpet (if indeed
I ever do; for you are men,
And rest eternal sorely need).'

So down we lay again. 'I wonder,
Will the world ever saner be,'
Said one, 'than when He sent us under
In our indifferent century!'

And many a skeleton shook his head.
'Instead of preaching forty year,'
My neighbour Parson Thirdly said,
'I wish I had stuck to pipes and beer.'

Again the guns disturbed the hour,
Roaring their readiness to avenge,
As far inland as Stourton Tower,
And Camelot, and starlit Stonehenge.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Seven quatrains of iambic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme, the poem's ordinary stanza form contrasting with the extraordinary content.

Themes

The eternal recurrence of war and the indifference of history — even God cannot stop it. The poem ends with the sounds of guns echoing all the way to the mythical past.

Historical Context

Published in the Fortnightly Review in May 1914, months before war broke out. Hardy seemed to have anticipated it.

About Thomas Hardy

Thomas Hardy was born on June 2, 1840, in Higher Bockhampton, Dorset, England, and became one of the greatest novelists and poets of the Victorian and Edwardian eras. After Jude the Obscure (1895) was attacked for immorality, he stopped writing fiction and devoted himself entirely to poetry, producing eight volumes. His elegies for his first wife Emma, written after her sudden death in 1912, are considered among the finest in the English language. He died in Dorchester in 1928.
