To Elsie

by William Carlos Williams

Written 1923 Published 1923 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1923 in Spring and All, this poem describes the pure products of America going crazy — devoid of imagination, driven by isolation and poverty to murder or strange accidents — personified in Elsie, a girls home worker who expresses what it means to be a beautiful thing in a degraded world.

Quick Answer

"To Elsie" is a poem by William Carlos Williams, published in 1923. The pure products of America go crazy — devil may care men, isolated mountain folk, degraded by isolation and ignorance. Elsie, devoid of anything to distinguish her except the dash of Indian blood, expresses what it means to be a beautiful thing devoured by circumstance. No one to witness and adjust, no one to drive the car.

The pure products of America
go crazy—
mountain folk from Kentucky

or the ribbed north end of
Jersey
with its isolate lakes and

valleys, its deaf-mutes, thieves
old names
and promiscuity between

devil-may-care men who have taken
to railroading
out of sheer lust of adventure—

and young slatterns, bathed
in filth
from Monday to Saturday

to be tricked out that night
with gauds
from imaginations which have no

peasant traditions to give them
character
but flutter and flaunt

sheer rags—succumbing without
emotion
save numbed terror

under some hedge of choke-cherry
or viburnum—
which they cannot express—

Unless it be that marriage
perhaps
with a dash of Indian blood

will throw up a girl so desolate
so hemmed round
with disease or murder

that she'll be rescued by an
agent—
reared by the state and

placed with some thrust
of wings
into the air that way.

No one
to witness
and adjust, no one to drive the car

Public domain (published 1923 in Spring and All).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-seven three-line stanzas of free verse, the poem building through accumulating images of American degradation to its bleak conclusion.

Themes

The failure of the American democratic promise — 'the pure products of America' should be something fine but are instead driven crazy by poverty, isolation, and the lack of culture. The poem is both diagnosis and elegy.

Historical Context

Published in Spring and All (1923). The Elsie of the title was a domestic worker employed in Williams's household.

About William Carlos Williams

William Carlos Williams was born on September 17, 1883, in Rutherford, New Jersey, and practiced medicine as a pediatrician for most of his adult life while also becoming one of the most influential American Modernist poets. He rejected the allusive, European-influenced style of T.S. Eliot in favor of the American idiom, ordinary speech, and concrete sensory images drawn from everyday life. He died in Rutherford in 1963.
