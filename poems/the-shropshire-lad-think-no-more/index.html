Think No More, Lad

by A.E. Housman

Written 1896 Published 1896 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1896 in A Shropshire Lad, this poem urges a young man not to think about the darkness — to drink and dance and laugh and lie, for the world is not good enough to worry about.

Quick Answer

Think No More, Lad is a poem by A.E. Housman, published in 1896. Think no more, lad; laugh, be jolly. Why should men make haste to die? Empty heads and tongues a-talking make the rough road easy walking, and the feather pate of folly bears the falling sky.

Think no more, lad; laugh, be jolly:
Why should men make haste to die?
Empty heads and tongues a-talking
Make the rough road easy walking,
And the feather pate of folly
Bears the falling sky.

Oh, 'tis jesting, dancing, drinking
Spins the heavy world around.
If young hearts were not so clever,
Oh, they would be young for ever:
Think no more; 'tis only thinking
Lays lads underground.

Public domain. Published 1896 in A Shropshire Lad.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four six-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter.

Themes

The Epicurean strategy of not thinking — refusing to dwell on mortality by filling life with noise and drink and laughter.

Historical Context

Published in A Shropshire Lad (1896), poem XLIX.

About A.E. Housman

Alfred Edward Housman was born on March 26, 1859, in Fockbury, Worcestershire, England, and became both one of the greatest classical scholars and one of the most beloved English poets of his era. A Shropshire Lad (1896), published at his own expense after being rejected by publishers, became one of the most popular poetry collections ever published in English. He died in Cambridge in 1936.
