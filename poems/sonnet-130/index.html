Sonnet 130 (My Mistress' Eyes Are Nothing Like the Sun)

by William Shakespeare

Written 1595 Published 1609 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1609, this anti-Petrarchan sonnet deliberately refuses every conventional compliment — her eyes are nothing like the sun, her lips are not as red as coral — before declaring in the couplet that his love is as rare as any woman falsely praised through overblown metaphors.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 130 is a Shakespeare sonnet published in 1609. The speaker methodically denies his mistress every conventional poetic compliment — her eyes aren't like the sun, her hair is like black wires, her breath reeks — before the couplet reveals the point: he loves her as truly as any poet who uses ridiculous overstatements to flatter his beloved.

My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun;
Coral is far more red than her lips' red;
If snow be white, why then her breasts are dun;
If hairs be wires, black wires grow on her head.
I have seen roses damasked, red and white,
But no such roses see I in her cheeks;
And in some perfumes is there more delight
Than in the breath that from my mistress reeks.
I love to hear her speak, yet well I know
That music hath a far more pleasing sound;
I grant I never saw a goddess go;
My mistress, when she walks, treads on the ground.
And yet, by heaven, I think my love as rare
As any she belied with false compare.

Public domain. Text per the Folger Shakespeare Library.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Shakespearean sonnet in iambic pentameter, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. The poem is structured as a sustained anti-blazon — a parody of the blazon form that catalogued a beloved's physical perfections — dismantling each conventional comparison before the couplet reframes the whole enterprise.

Themes

Honest love versus conventional flattery. The poem satirizes Petrarchan love poetry, which compared beloveds to suns, coral, roses, snow, and perfume. Shakespeare argues that real love does not require false praise — that loving a real person honestly is more valuable than idealizing an impossible standard.

Historical Context

Part of the Dark Lady sequence (Sonnets 127-154), addressed to a woman with dark hair and eyes rather than the fair, idealized beloved of conventional Renaissance love poetry. The poem has been read as both satire of poetic convention and genuine declaration of grounded, honest love.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
