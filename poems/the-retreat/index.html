The Retreat

by Henry Vaughan

Written 1650 Published 1650 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1650 in Silex Scintillans, this poem looks back on the speaker's childhood as a time of uncorrupted spiritual vision — when he could see angelic faces and divine light — and longs to return to that Edenic state before the world's shadow fell.

Quick Answer

"The Retreat" is a poem by Henry Vaughan, published in 1650. The speaker recalls his childhood as a time of divine vision, before he began to sin — when he could see the shadows of the eternal world, celestial faces, and angelic forms. Now he longs to travel back to that first, uncorrupted state, before he made his first, sinful step.

Happy those early days, when I
Shined in my Angel-infancy!
Before I understood this place
Appointed for my second race,
Or taught my soul to fancy aught
But a white, celestial thought;
When yet I had not walked above
A mile or two from my first Love,
And looking back—at that short space—
Could see a glimpse of His bright face;
When on some gilded cloud or flower
My gazing soul would dwell an hour,
And in those weaker glories spy
Some shadows of eternity;
Before I taught my tongue to wound
My conscience with a sinful sound,
Or had the black art to dispense
A several sin to every sense,
But felt through all this fleshly dress
Bright shoots of everlastingness.
O how I long to travel back,
And tread again that ancient track!
That I might once more reach that plain
Where first I left my glorious train;
From whence th' enlightened spirit sees
That shady City of Palm-trees.
But ah! my soul with too much stay
Is drunk, and staggers in the way!
Some men a forward motion love,
But I by backward steps would move;
And when this dust falls to the urn,
In that state I came, return.

Public domain. Published 1650 in Silex Scintillans.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Thirty-three lines of iambic tetrameter rhyming couplets, the poem's continuous forward motion expressing the speaker's longing to go back.

Themes

The Platonic idea of pre-existence and the loss of spiritual vision through growing up — closely related to Wordsworth's 'Immortality Ode,' which it anticipates by nearly 150 years. Childhood is a state of grace; adulthood is a fall.

Historical Context

Published in Silex Scintillans (1650). Often cited as an anticipation of Wordsworth's 'Immortality Ode' and Traherne's Centuries of Meditations. Vaughan is the first major English poet to treat childhood as spiritually superior to adulthood.

About Henry Vaughan

Henry Vaughan was born in 1621 in Brecknockshire, Wales, and became one of the finest poets of the Metaphysical tradition, deeply influenced by George Herbert. His collection Silex Scintillans (1650, 1655) contains some of the most beautiful devotional poems in English, including 'The World,' 'The Night,' and 'Regeneration.' He worked as a physician in Wales and died in 1695.
