A Song in the Front Yard

by Gwendolyn Brooks

Written 1945 Published 1945 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1945 in A Street in Bronzeville, this poem is spoken by a girl who has spent her whole life in the safe front yard and longs to go into the back yard where the rough, free children play — and maybe even go into the bad part of town.

Quick Answer

"A Song in the Front Yard" is a poem by Gwendolyn Brooks, published in 1945. A girl who has spent her life in the respectable front yard longs to play in the back yard where the rough children are, to get away from the polite flowers and go where the charity children play. She wants to be bad, to wear stockings with sagas on them, to walk after dark.

I've stayed in the front yard all my life.
I want a peek at the back
Where it's rough and untended and hungry weed grows.
A girl gets sick of a rose.

I want to go in the back yard now
And maybe down the alley,
To where the charity children play.
I want a good time today.

They do some wonderful things.
They have some wonderful fun.
My mother sneers, but I say it's fine
How they don't have to go in at quarter to nine.
My mother, she tells me that Johnnie Mae
Will grow up to be a bad woman.
That George'll be taken to Jail soon or late
(On account of last winter he sold our back gate).

But I say it's fine. Honest, I do.
And I'd like to be a bad woman, too,
And wear the brave stockings of night-black lace
And strut down the streets with paint on my face.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four stanzas of loose iambic lines with irregular rhyme, the easy, conversational voice of a child yearning for freedom beyond what's permitted.

Themes

The desire to escape respectability and safety for the freedom of the margins. The front yard is the domestic space of good behavior; the back yard is where the real children play. The poem is both comic and genuinely wistful.

Historical Context

Published in A Street in Bronzeville (1945), Brooks's debut collection, which established her as a major voice by depicting the lives of Black Americans on Chicago's South Side with precision and empathy.

About Gwendolyn Brooks

Gwendolyn Brooks was born on June 7, 1917, in Topeka, Kansas, and became the first African American to win the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry, awarded for Annie Allen in 1950. She was the Poet Laureate of Illinois for most of her adult life and one of the most influential American poets of the twentieth century. She died in Chicago in 2000.
