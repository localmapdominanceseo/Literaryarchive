Concord Hymn

by Ralph Waldo Emerson

Written 1836 Published 1837 American Romanticism Public Domain

Written in 1836 and published in 1837, this hymn was sung at the completion of the Concord Battle Monument, commemorating the first battle of the American Revolutionary War. It contains the famous line about the shot heard round the world.

Quick Answer

"Concord Hymn" is a poem by Ralph Waldo Emerson, published in 1837 for the dedication of the Concord Battle Monument. It commemorates the minute-men who stood at the rude bridge and fired the shot heard round the world in April 1775, and asks that their spirit be preserved in the monument now raised to them.

By the rude bridge that arched the flood,
Their flag to April's breeze unfurled,
Here once the embattled farmers stood,
And fired the shot heard round the world.

The foe long since in silence slept;
Alike the conqueror silent sleeps;
And Time the ruined bridge has swept
Down the dark stream which seaward creeps.

On this green bank, by this soft stream,
We set to-day a votive stone;
That memory may their deed redeem,
When, like our sires, our sons are gone.

Spirit, that made those heroes dare
To die, and leave their children free,
Bid Time and Nature gently spare
The shaft we raise to them and thee.

Public domain. Published 1837.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of iambic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme, written as a hymn — simple, formal, intended to be sung.

Themes

The preservation of the memory of sacrifice and the founding act of American liberty. The 'shot heard round the world' became one of the defining phrases of American identity.

Historical Context

Emerson wrote the poem for the July 4, 1837 dedication of a monument at the North Bridge in Concord, Massachusetts, where the first armed confrontation of the Revolutionary War took place on April 19, 1775. It was sung to the tune of 'Old Hundred.'

About Ralph Waldo Emerson

Ralph Waldo Emerson was born on May 25, 1803, in Boston, Massachusetts, and became the central figure of American Transcendentalism — the philosophical and literary movement that emphasized the divinity of nature, individual intuition, and the unity of all things. His Essays (1841 and 1844) and his poetry exerted enormous influence on American literature and thought, and his lecture 'The American Scholar' (1837) has been called America's intellectual Declaration of Independence. He died in Concord, Massachusetts, in 1882.
