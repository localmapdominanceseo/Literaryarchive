Love Unknown

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 in The Temple, this poem is structured as a complaint: the speaker tells a friend of three painful experiences — his heart thrown into a boiling cauldron, washed in a font, thrown into a bed of thorns — only to learn that each was an act of purification by his Lord.

Quick Answer

"Love Unknown" is a poem by George Herbert, published in 1633. The speaker complains to a friend of three inexplicable afflictions — his heart boiled, washed, thrown on thorns. The friend explains: it was all your Lord's doing, to cleanse and soften and humble your heart. Your Lord is kind; he uses hard remedies. The end is good.

Dear friend, sit down, the tale is long and sad:
And in my faintings I presume your love
Will more comply than help. A Lord I had,
And have, of whom some grounds which may improve,
I hold for two lives, and both lives in me.
To him I brought a dish of fruit one day,
And in the middle placed my heart. But he
(I sigh to say)
Looked on a servant, who did know his eye
Better than you know me, or (which is one)
Than I myself. The servant instantly,
Quitting the fruit, seized on my heart alone,
And threw it in a font, wherein did fall
A stream of bloud, which issued from the side
Of a great rock: I well remember all,
And have good cause: there it was dipt and dried,
And washt, and wrung: the very wringing yet
Enforceth tears. Your heart was foul, I fear.
Indeed 'tis true. I did and do commit
Many a fault more than my lease will bear;
Yet still asked pardon, and was not denied.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A long narrative poem of seventy-six lines in iambic pentameter with irregular rhyme, structured as a dialogue between the speaker and an unnamed friend.

Themes

Divine chastisement as a form of love — the painful experiences the speaker endures are all acts of purification, not punishment. The poem argues that the hardest spiritual experiences are the most loving.

Historical Context

Published in The Temple (1633). One of Herbert's longest and most elaborately plotted poems, the narrative structure allowing a more complex unfolding of spiritual experience than his shorter lyrics.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
