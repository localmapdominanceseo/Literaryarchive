The Red Wheelbarrow

by William Carlos Williams

Written 1923 Published 1923 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1923 in Spring and All, this poem of sixteen words arranged into four couplets asserts, with complete seriousness, that 'so much depends upon' a red wheelbarrow glazed with rain water beside the white chickens.

Quick Answer

"The Red Wheelbarrow" is a poem by William Carlos Williams, published in 1923. In sixteen words across four short couplets, the speaker declares that 'so much depends upon' a red wheelbarrow glazed with rain beside white chickens — and then stops. The poem is one of the most discussed short poems in American literature, representing the Imagist belief that the thing itself, precisely observed, is the poem.

so much depends
upon

a red wheel
barrow

glazed with rain
water

beside the white
chickens.

Public domain. Published 1923 in Spring and All.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four two-line stanzas, each breaking after the first word of a compound noun (wheelbarrow, rain, water), creating a small visual delay that makes the reader look at each word as an individual object, not merely a reading unit.

Themes

The sufficiency of the particular, concrete, physical world as subject matter for poetry. Williams was reacting against what he saw as the over-intellectualized abstractions of Eliot's The Waste Land. 'So much depends upon' is both absurdly understated and completely earnest — the poem argues that close attention to humble, specific, real things is itself a form of meaning.

Historical Context

Published in Spring and All (1923), a hybrid prose-and-poetry collection. Williams wrote the poem while visiting a gravely ill child, a fact that some scholars use to explain the poem's quiet urgency. It became the foundational example of the American Imagist or Objectivist approach to poetry.

About William Carlos Williams

William Carlos Williams was born on September 17, 1883, in Rutherford, New Jersey, and practiced medicine as a pediatrician for most of his adult life while also becoming one of the most influential American Modernist poets. He rejected the allusive, European-influenced style of T.S. Eliot in favor of the American idiom, ordinary speech, and concrete sensory images drawn from everyday life. He died in Rutherford in 1963.
