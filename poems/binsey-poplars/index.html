Binsey Poplars

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1879 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1879 and published posthumously in 1918, this poem mourns the felling of a row of poplar trees along the Thames near Oxford — they were felled to the ground, and the rural scene they made is gone. After-comers cannot guess the beauty been.

Quick Answer

Binsey Poplars is a poem by Hopkins, published posthumously in 1918. My aspens dear, whose airy cages quelled the leaping sun are felled, felled — all felled. The sweet especial scene is gone. What we touch we wound. After-comers cannot guess the beauty been.

My aspens dear, whose airy cages quelled,
Quelled or quenched in leaves the leaping sun,
All felled, felled, are all felled;
  Of a fresh and following folded rank
            Not spared, not one
            That dandled a sandalled
         Shadow that swam or sank
On meadow and river and wind-wandering weed-winding bank.

  O if we but knew what we do
         When we delve or hew—
     Hack and rack the growing green!
          Since country is so tender
       To touch, her being só slender,
     That, like this sleek and seeing ball
     But a prick will make no eye at all,
     Where we, even where we mean
             To mend her we end her,
         When we hew or delve:
 After-comers cannot guess the beauty been.
  Ten or twelve, only ten or twelve
     Strokes of havoc únselve
           The sweet especial scene,
     Rural scene, a rural scene,
     Sweet especial rural scene.

Public domain. Written 1879, published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Free verse in sprung rhythm, the poem's unusual line breaks and repetition expressing grief through form.

Themes

The irreversible destruction of natural beauty — once a tree is felled, the landscape it created is gone for ever. The poem anticipates modern environmentalism.

Historical Context

Written March 1879, when Hopkins saw the poplars along the Cherwell at Oxford had been felled. Published posthumously in 1918.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
