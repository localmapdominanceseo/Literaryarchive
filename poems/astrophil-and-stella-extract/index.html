Astrophil and Stella: Sonnet 1

by Sir Philip Sidney

Written 1582 Published 1591 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1591, this opening sonnet of Sir Philip Sidney's sequence Astrophil and Stella describes a poet struggling to find the right words to express his love, trying many approaches, until his Muse tells him to look in his own heart and write.

Quick Answer

Sonnet 1 of Astrophil and Stella by Sir Philip Sidney (published 1591) opens a sequence about unrequited love. The poet tries to write about his love but cannot find the right words, studies the work of others, and racks his brains — until his Muse says: 'Fool, look in thy heart and write.'

Loving in truth, and fain in verse my love to show,
That she, dear she, might take some pleasure of my pain,
Pleasure might cause her read, reading might make her know,
Knowledge might pity win, and pity grace obtain,
I sought fit words to paint the blackest face of woe;
Studying inventions fine her wits to entertain,
Oft turning others' leaves, to see if thence would flow
Some fresh and fruitful showers upon my sun-burned brain.
But words came halting forth, wanting invention's stay;
Invention, Nature's child, fled step-dame Study's blows;
And others' feet still seemed but strangers in my way.
Thus, great with child to speak, and helpless in my throes,
Biting my truant pen, beating myself for spite,
'Fool,' said my Muse to me, 'look in thy heart, and write!'

Public domain. Published posthumously 1591.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A fourteen-line sonnet in iambic hexameter (alexandrines), Sidney's modification of the standard English sonnet, ABBA ABBA CDCD EE.

Themes

The paradox that the most direct route to poetic expression is through personal experience, not imitation of other poets. The Muse's final injunction — 'look in thy heart and write' — became one of the defining mottos of Renaissance love poetry.

Historical Context

Written around 1582 and published posthumously in 1591. The sequence is addressed to Stella (Lady Penelope Rich) through the persona of Astrophil (star-lover). Sidney died in battle in 1586 at age thirty-one.

About Sir Philip Sidney

Sir Philip Sidney was born on November 30, 1554, in Penshurst, Kent, England, and became the ideal Renaissance courtier — soldier, diplomat, patron, and poet. His sonnet sequence Astrophil and Stella (published posthumously 1591) is one of the founding texts of the English sonnet tradition. He died of wounds received in battle at Zutphen in the Netherlands in 1586 at the age of thirty-one.
