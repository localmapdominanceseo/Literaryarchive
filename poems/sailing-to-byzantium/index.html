Sailing to Byzantium

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1926 Published 1928 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1928 in The Tower, this poem is spoken by an aging man who has sailed away from the country of the young to Byzantium, the ancient capital of art and golden mosaics, seeking to escape mortality by becoming a work of art himself.

Quick Answer

"Sailing to Byzantium" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1928. An old man, feeling out of place in the country of the young where everything is caught in sensual music, sails to Byzantium — the ancient city of gold mosaics and eternal art. He asks the sages in the holy fire to teach his soul to sing and to gather him into the artifice of eternity, becoming a golden bird singing on a golden bough.

That is no country for old men. The young
In one another's arms, birds in the trees,
—Those dying generations—at their song,
The salmon-falls, the mackerel-crowded seas,
Fish, flesh, or fowl, commend all summer long
Whatever is begotten, born, and dies.
Caught in that sensual music all neglect
Monuments of unageing intellect.

An aged man is but a paltry thing,
A tattered coat upon a stick, unless
Soul clap its hands and sing, and louder sing
For every tatter in its mortal dress,
Nor is there singing school but studying
Monuments of its own magnificence;
And therefore I have sailed the seas and come
To the holy city of Byzantium.

O sages standing in God's holy fire
As in the gold mosaic of a wall,
Come from the holy fire, perne in a gyre,
And be the singing-masters of my soul.
Consume my heart away; sick with desire
And fastened to a dying animal
It knows not what it is; and gather me
Into the artifice of eternity.

Once out of nature I shall never take
My bodily form from any natural thing,
But such a form as Grecian goldsmiths make
Of hammered gold and gold enamelling
To keep a drowsy Emperor awake;
Or set upon a golden bough to sing
To lords and ladies of Byzantium
Of what is past, or passing, or to come.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four eight-line stanzas in ottava rima (ABABABCC), an Italian stanza form associated with epic poetry, giving the poem formal grandeur.

Themes

The conflict between mortal flesh and immortal art; the old man's desire to transcend bodily decay by becoming an artifact. Byzantium represents the highest achievement of human art — golden mosaics, jeweled birds — and the speaker wishes to escape the biological cycle entirely by becoming such an artifact.

Historical Context

Published in The Tower (1928), written when Yeats was sixty-one and acutely conscious of aging. The poem engages with his system of history and art developed in A Vision.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
