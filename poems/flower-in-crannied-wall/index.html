Flower in the Crannied Wall

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1869 Published 1869 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1869, this six-line poem describes plucking a flower from a wall and meditating: if I could understand this flower, root and all, I should know what God and man is.

Quick Answer

Flower in the Crannied Wall is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1869. The speaker plucks a flower from a wall, root and all, holds it in hand: little flower, if I could understand what you are, root and all, I should know what God and man is.

Flower in the crannied wall,
I pluck you out of the crannies,
I hold you here, root and all, in my hand,
Little flower—but if I could understand
What you are, root and all, and all in all,
I should know what God and man is.

Public domain. Published 1869.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six lines of irregular verse, compressed to the point of a koan.

Themes

The argument from natural complexity to metaphysical mystery — a complete plant, understood fully, would contain all answers.

Historical Context

Published in 1869. One of Tennyson's most compressed philosophical poems.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
