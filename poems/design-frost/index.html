Design

by Robert Frost

Written 1912 Published 1936 American Modernism Public Domain

Written around 1912 and published in 1936 in A Further Range, this sonnet describes finding a white spider on a white heal-all flower holding a white moth — an unusual triple coincidence of whiteness — and asks whether this design of darkness was purposive or whether there is no design at all.

Quick Answer

"Design" is a sonnet by Robert Frost, published in 1936. The speaker finds a fat white spider on a white heal-all flower, holding up a dead white moth — three things that should not be white (the heal-all is usually blue, spiders usually not white) all together by apparent coincidence. He asks: what brought the three together? What but design of darkness to appall — if design govern in a thing so small.

I found a dimpled spider, fat and white,
On a white heal-all, holding up a moth
Like a white piece of rigid satin cloth—
Assorted characters of death and blight
Mixed ready to begin the morning right,
Like the ingredients of a witches' broth—
A snow-drop spider, a flower like a froth,
And dead wings carried like a paper kite.

What had that flower to do with being white,
The wayside blue and innocent heal-all?
What brought the kindred spider to that height,
Then steered the white moth thither in the night?
What but design of darkness to appall?—
If design govern in a thing so small.

Public domain (published 1936). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter — an unusually formal structure for Frost, which itself signals that the poem is doing something philosophically serious.

Themes

The question of whether the natural world operates by design (theological or otherwise) or by pure chance. The discovery of these three white things together suggests either that some 'design of darkness' arranged them — or that if even such an apparently purposeful convergence is meaningless, then there may be no design at all in things so small.

Historical Context

Frost wrote an early version called 'In White' around 1912, then revised it substantially for publication in A Further Range (1936). It is considered one of his most philosophically serious poems.

About Robert Frost

Robert Frost was born on March 26, 1874, in San Francisco, California, though he is most closely associated with rural New England, where he lived and farmed for much of his life. His poetry uses plain, conversational language and traditional verse forms to explore deceptively simple rural scenes that often open onto deeper questions about choice, isolation, and mortality. Frost won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry and remains one of the most widely read American poets of the twentieth century. He died in Boston in 1963.
