The Cloud

by Percy Bysshe Shelley

Written 1820 Published 1820 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1820, this lyric is spoken by the cloud itself, describing its journeys — bringing fresh showers for the flowers, bearing light from the sun, laughing and leaping through the sky, and, after the storm, building its dome again.

Quick Answer

"The Cloud" is a poem by Shelley, published in 1820, spoken by the cloud itself. It brings fresh showers for the flowers, sheathes the sun in a cloud of fire, pilots the sun's bark over the sea of air. After thunder and lightning, the cloud dissolves — and from the rain and dew and blue sky, I build my dome again.

I bring fresh showers for the thirsting flowers,
From the seas and the streams;
I bear light shade for the leaves when laid
In their noonday dreams.
From my wings are shaken the dews that waken
The sweet buds every one,
When rocked to rest on their mother's breast,
As she dances about the sun.
I wield the flail of the lashing hail,
And whiten the green plains under,
And then again I dissolve it in rain,
And laugh as I pass in thunder.

I sift the snow on the mountains below,
And their great pines groan aghast;
And all the night 'tis my pillow white,
While I sleep in the arms of the blast.
Sublime on the towers of my skiey bowers,
Lightning my pilot sits;
In a cavern under is fettered the thunder,
It struggles and howls at fits;
Over earth and ocean, with gentle motion,
This pilot is guiding me,
Lured by the love of the genii that move
In the depths of the purple sea;
Over the rills, and the crags, and the hills,
Over the lakes and the plains,
Wherever he dream, under mountain or stream,
The Spirit he loves remains;
And I all the while bask in Heaven's blue smile,
Whilst he is dissolving in rains.

Public domain. Published 1820.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six stanzas of varying length in anapestic rhythm, the cloud's voice ranging freely through its different states.

Themes

The life cycle of the cloud as a metaphor for natural transformation — the cloud never truly dies but changes form, dissolving into rain and re-forming from the vapor. The poem celebrates endless transformation rather than permanence.

Historical Context

Published in Prometheus Unbound (1820). One of Shelley's finest nature lyrics, remarkable for its scientific accuracy about cloud formation alongside its mythological vividness.

About Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley was born on August 4, 1792, in Horsham, England, and became one of the major poets of English Romanticism despite controversy during his lifetime over his radical political and religious views. His poetry combines lyrical intensity with sustained engagement with politics, philosophy, and the natural world. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy in 1822, at the age of twenty-nine.
