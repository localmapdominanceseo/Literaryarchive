Northern Farmer, New Style

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1869 Published 1869 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1869 in The Holy Grail and Other Poems, this poem is a dramatic monologue spoken by a wealthy Yorkshire farmer who warns his son not to marry for love but for money — 'Don't thou marry for munny, but goä wheer munny is!'

Quick Answer

"Northern Farmer, New Style" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1869, spoken in broad Yorkshire dialect by a wealthy farmer. He lectures his son not to marry a farmer's daughter for love — the land and money are what matter. Property, property, property — that's the word. Don't marry for money, but go where money is.

Dosn't thou 'ear my 'erse's legs, as they canters awaäy?
Proputty, proputty, proputty—that's what I 'ears 'em saäy.
Proputty, proputty, proputty—Sam, thou's an ass for thy paaïns:
Theer's moor sense i' one o' 'is legs nor in all thy braaïns.

Woa—theer's a craw to pluck wi' tha, Sam: yon's parson's 'ouse—
Dosn't thou knaw that a man mun be eäther a man or a mouse?
Time to think on it then; for thou'll be twenty to weeäk.
Proputty, proputty—woa then woa—let ma 'ear mysen speäk.

Me an' thy muther, Sammy, 'as beän a-talkin' o' thee;
Thou's beän talkin' to muther, an' she beän a tellin' it me.
Thou'll not marry for munny—thou's sweet upo' parson's lass—
Noa—thou'll marry for luvv—an' we boäth on us thinks tha an ass.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twelve stanzas in Yorkshire dialect, the colloquial voice creating comic distance from the speaker's blunt materialism.

Themes

Victorian materialism and the conflict between romance and economics in marriage, rendered comically through the farmer's entirely unselfconscious money-worship.

Historical Context

Published in 1869. Tennyson was fascinated by Lincolnshire dialect from his own childhood and wrote several dialect poems.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
