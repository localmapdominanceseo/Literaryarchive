Easter Hymn

by A.E. Housman

Written 1900 Published 1936 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in More Poems (1936), this poem addresses Christ directly — if he did indeed rise from the dead, then the speaker asks him to make good his promise and take the dying with him. If he did not rise, then let the dead lie quiet.

Quick Answer

Easter Hymn is a poem by A.E. Housman, published posthumously in 1936. If in that Syrian garden, ages slain, you sleep, and know not you are dead in vain — nor even in dreams behold how white celestial faces, come to lead his race back, smiling, from the brink. Make haste or stay. If he indeed came back, then make good his promise to the dead who trusted him.

If in that Syrian garden, ages slain,
You sleep, and know not you are dead in vain,
Nor even in dreams behold how white celestial
OfFaces, come to lead you hence, grow pale,
And that high sign by which the nations hail
Your name is writ in water on the gale
And all the starry multitude grows stale—
If this with you remain,
If you arose and are not where you lay,
The empty tomb speaks Easter to the sky;
O get you hence to Galilee again.
And make good—since you rose—the good you promised,
And teach the mourners that believe they lie
Not all in vain beneath the vault of sky.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1936.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Fourteen lines in iambic pentameter — a modified sonnet.

Themes

Skeptical engagement with the Resurrection — if it is true, then Christ has obligations to those who died believing in him; if it is false, then everyone is equally deceived.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in More Poems (1936).

About A.E. Housman

Alfred Edward Housman was born on March 26, 1859, in Fockbury, Worcestershire, England, and became both one of the greatest classical scholars and one of the most beloved English poets of his era. A Shropshire Lad (1896), published at his own expense after being rejected by publishers, became one of the most popular poetry collections ever published in English, carried in the pockets of thousands of soldiers in World War I. He died in Cambridge in 1936.
