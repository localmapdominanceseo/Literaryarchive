Ode on the Death of the Duke of Wellington (Extract)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1852 Published 1852 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1852 for Wellington's funeral, this ode celebrates the Duke's career and mourns his passing — arguing that he was the greatest leader England produced since Nelson.

Quick Answer

Tennyson's Ode on the Death of the Duke of Wellington (1852) celebrates Wellington's life of service and mourns England's greatest soldier — he who saved the sum of things for pay, who labored long and great, and whose trumpet-voice the people heard at Waterloo.

Bury the Great Duke
With an empire's lamentation,
Let us bury the Great Duke
To the noise of the mourning of a mighty nation,
Mourning when their leaders fall,
Warriors carry the warrior's pall,
And sorrow darkens hamlet and hall.

Where shall we lay the man whom we deplore?
Here, in streaming London's central roar.
Let the sound of those he wrought for,
And the feet of those he fought for,
Echo round his bones for evermore.

Lead out the pageant: sad and slow,
As fits an universal woe,
Let the long long procession go,
And let the sorrowing crowd about it grow,
And let the mournful martial music blow;
The last great Englishman is low.

Public domain. Published 1852.

Analysis

Form and Structure

An irregular ode in varying stanza forms, the formal variety matching the public occasion.

Themes

Public mourning, national greatness, and the duty of service — Wellington as the model of the public servant who sacrifices self for nation.

Historical Context

Published in 1852 for Wellington's state funeral. One of Tennyson's major public poems as Poet Laureate.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
