When I Was One-and-Twenty

by A.E. Housman

Written 1896 Published 1896 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1896 in A Shropshire Lad, this short poem describes a wise man's advice — give away rubies but not your heart — which the speaker ignored at twenty-one, and is now learning at twenty-two was true.

Quick Answer

When I Was One-and-Twenty is a poem by A.E. Housman, published in 1896. A wise man told the speaker when he was twenty-one to give pearls and rubies away but keep his fancy free. But at twenty-two, a twelvemonth on, he knows the man spoke true: oh, tis true, tis true.

When I was one-and-twenty
I heard a wise man say,
'Give crowns and pounds and guineas
But not your heart away;
Give pearls away and rubies
But keep your fancy free.'
But I was one-and-twenty,
No use to talk to me.

When I was one-and-twenty
I heard him say again,
'The heart out of the bosom
Was never given in vain;
'Tis paid with sighs a plenty
And sold for endless rue.'
And I am two-and-twenty,
And oh, 'tis true, 'tis true.

Public domain. Published 1896 in A Shropshire Lad.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two eight-line stanzas of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABABCDCD rhyme.

Themes

The knowledge that comes too late — the advice about not giving the heart away was good advice, but the speaker could only learn it by experience.

Historical Context

Published in A Shropshire Lad (1896), poem XIII. One of Housman's most popular and most frequently set to music.

About A.E. Housman

Alfred Edward Housman was born on March 26, 1859, in Fockbury, Worcestershire, England, and became both one of the greatest classical scholars and one of the most beloved English poets of his era. A Shropshire Lad (1896), published at his own expense after being rejected by publishers, became one of the most popular poetry collections ever published in English. He died in Cambridge in 1936.
