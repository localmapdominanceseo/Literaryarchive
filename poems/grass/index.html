Grass

by Carl Sandburg

Written 1918 Published 1918 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1918 in Cornhuskers, this short poem is spoken by the grass of battlefields, asking to be let alone to work — to cover the dead of Austerlitz, Waterloo, Gettysburg, Ypres, Verdun — while passengers on a train two years later ask what place this is and the conductor says he does not know.

Quick Answer

"Grass" is a poem by Carl Sandburg, published in 1918. The grass of battlefields speaks, asking to be allowed to cover the dead of history's great battles — Austerlitz, Waterloo, Gettysburg, Ypres, Verdun. Train passengers two years later look out at the flat landscape and ask what place it is. The conductor does not know. The grass has done its work.

Pile the bodies high at Austerlitz and Waterloo.
Shovel them under and let me work—
I am the grass; I cover all.

And pile them high at Gettysburg
And pile them high at Ypres and Verdun.
Shovel them under and let me work.
Two years, ten years, and passengers ask the conductor:
What place is this?
Where are we now?

I am the grass.
Let me work.

Public domain. Published 1918 in Cornhuskers.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Ten lines of free verse in the grass's voice, the personification of the natural world as an indifferent but relentless force that buries all human violence under green.

Themes

History's erasure of its own violence through time and the indifference of nature to human suffering. The grass does not mourn or celebrate — it simply covers. The conductor who does not know the name of the place represents how thoroughly nature and time bury even the largest human catastrophes.

Historical Context

Published in Cornhuskers (1918), the year World War I ended. The poem connects ancient battles (Austerlitz, Waterloo) to recent ones (Ypres, Verdun), suggesting that all military history follows the same pattern of violence followed by natural erasure.

About Carl Sandburg

Carl Sandburg was born on January 6, 1878, in Galesburg, Illinois, to Swedish immigrant parents, and became one of the most popular American poets of the twentieth century. He worked many jobs — farm worker, soldier, journalist — before establishing himself as a poet with Chicago Poems (1916). He won three Pulitzer Prizes, two for poetry and one for his biography of Abraham Lincoln. He died in Flat Rock, North Carolina, in 1967.
