Despair

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1881 Published 1881 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1881, Despair is a dramatic monologue spoken by a man rescued from a suicide attempt — he and his wife had jumped into the sea after losing their faith. He attacks both the Calvinist hell-theology and the empty atheism that replaced it.

Quick Answer

Despair (1881) is a dramatic monologue by Tennyson. A man and his wife have jumped into the sea — the man is rescued, the wife drowned. He attacks both Calvinist theology (which preaches a God who sends most souls to hell) and modern materialism (which gives no meaning to life at all). We had not found God in either.

Have I crazed myself over their horrible infidel writings?
O, yes, I am sure that I spoke what I felt, tho' it seems
That my words, when I mutter'd them to you, were harsh in the hearing—
The faith that is lost in the chaos of thought is lost in the dream!

Yes, and I talk of it now—I am sure that I found no God
In your Godless deep, but I found some spark of his light in the night
When you lay at the foot of the grave, and I laid your hand in mine,
For a moment heard your last prayer and cried out: God 'forgive,' God 'forgive!'

Public domain. Published 1881.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twenty-one stanzas of eight lines each in iambic hexameter, the long lines enacting the speaker's anguished flood of words.

Themes

The spiritual crisis of the Victorian age — caught between a God who damns and a universe without meaning. The poem attacks both extremes while offering no resolution.

Historical Context

Published in 1881 in Ballads and Other Poems. One of Tennyson's most anguished dramatic monologues.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
