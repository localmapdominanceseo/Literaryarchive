The Sisters

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1880 Published 1880 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1880 in Ballads and Other Poems, this dramatic monologue is spoken by a woman who killed the Earl who seduced and abandoned her sister — she gave him poison in his wine and watched him die, calling her sister's name.

Quick Answer

"The Sisters" is a dramatic monologue by Tennyson, published in 1880. The speaker tells how the Earl seduced and abandoned her elder sister, who died of grief. She waited and planned — and when the Earl came courting her, she gave him hemlock in his wine and watched him die calling out her sister's name. Now she waits for her own death.

We were two daughters of one race:
She was the fairest in the face:
The wind is blowing in turret and tree.
They were together, and she fell;
Therefore revenge became me well.
O the Earl was fair to see!

She lived a moon in that low lodge with him:
His boat was often seen from our cove and cliff:
My father came from the fields of May:
The wind is howling in turret and tree.
Who—but—I—what was the Earl to me?
He hanged his arm in glittering ring:
O the Earl was fair to see!

Analysis

Form and Structure

A dramatic monologue in blank verse with ballad-like qualities.

Themes

Revenge, grief, and the bond between sisters — the speaker's love for her dead sister drives her to murder and self-sacrifice.

Historical Context

Published in 1880 in Ballads and Other Poems. One of Tennyson's darkest dramatic monologues.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
