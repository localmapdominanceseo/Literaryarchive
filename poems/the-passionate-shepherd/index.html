The Passionate Shepherd to His Love

by Christopher Marlowe

Written 1599 Published 1599 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1599, this pastoral lyric is one of the most famous love poems of the English Renaissance, spoken by a shepherd who invites his beloved to come and live with him in the country, promising her beds of roses, embroidered dresses made of flowers, and a cap of flowers.

Quick Answer

"The Passionate Shepherd to His Love" is a poem by Christopher Marlowe, published in 1599. A shepherd invites his love to come live with him in the countryside, promising her all the pleasures the season offers: beds of roses, a cap made from flowers, embroidered kirtle, golden buckles, and a belt of straw and ivy buds. The poem is a pure fantasy of perpetual springtime and endless rural delight.

Come live with me and be my love,
And we will all the pleasures prove,
That valleys, groves, hills, and fields,
Woods or steepy mountain yields.

And we will sit upon the rocks,
Seeing the shepherds feed their flocks,
By shallow rivers to whose falls
Melodiou birds sing madrigals.

And I will make thee beds of roses
And a thousand fragrant posies,
A cap of flower, and a kirtle
Embroidered all with leaves of myrtle;

A gown made of the finest wool
Which from our pretty lambs we pull;
Fair lined slippers for the cold,
With buckles of the purest gold;

A belt of straw and ivy buds,
With coral clasps and amber studs;
And if these pleasures may thee move,
Come live with me, and be my love.

The shepherd's swains shall dance and sing
For thy delight each May morning:
If these delights thy mind may move,
Then live with me, and be my love.

Public domain. Published 1599.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six quatrains in iambic tetrameter with AABB rhyme, light and musical in structure. The poem is a classic pastoral invitation, drawing on a long tradition of classical pastoral poetry (Theocritus, Virgil) translated into an idealized English countryside.

Themes

The pastoral ideal — the fantasy of a simple, perfect country life in permanent springtime, free from time, aging, and hardship. The shepherd makes elaborate promises that are only possible in a world where seasons never change and everything is in flower simultaneously. The poem was so famous that Sir Walter Raleigh wrote a reply, 'The Nymph's Reply to the Shepherd,' gently pointing out that time will pass and the flowers will fade.

Historical Context

Published in 1599 in The Passionate Pilgrim, a collection attributed (dubiously) to Shakespeare. The poem became enormously famous and spawned multiple replies and parodies. It remains the most celebrated example of the Renaissance pastoral lyric in English.

About Christopher Marlowe

Christopher Marlowe was born in February 1564 in Canterbury, England, and became the foremost English playwright before Shakespeare, writing Doctor Faustus, Tamburlaine, and The Jew of Malta. He was also a significant lyric poet. He died in a tavern brawl in Deptford in May 1593 at the age of twenty-nine.
