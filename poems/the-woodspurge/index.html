The Woodspurge

by Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Written 1856 Published 1870 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1870, this brief lyric describes a man in deep grief sitting with his head between his knees — and what he remembers of that hour is only the woodspurge, a weed with a cup of three. From perfect grief there need not be wisdom or relief.

Quick Answer

The Woodspurge is a poem by Dante Gabriel Rossetti, published in 1870. The speaker walks in grief, sits with his head between his knees. He looks up and sees the woodspurge with a cup of three. From perfect grief there need not be wisdom or even relief: only the weed's cup of three.

The wind flapped loose, the wind was still,
Shaken out dead from tree and hill:
I had walked on at the wind's will,—
I sat now, for the wind was still.

Between my knees my forehead was,—
My lips, drawn in, said not Alas!
My hair was over in the grass,
My naked ears heard the day pass.

My eyes, wide open, had the run
Of some ten weeds to fix upon;
Among those few, out of the sun,
The woodspurge flowered, three cups in one.

From perfect grief there need not be
Wisdom or even memory:
One thing then learnt remains to me,—
The woodspurge has a cup of three.

Public domain. Published 1870.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of iambic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme, formally simple contrasting with its disturbing insight.

Themes

The random, specific, indelible quality of grief's memories — not wisdom, not consolation, just an accidental detail.

Historical Context

Published in 1870. One of the finest short poems about grief in the Victorian period.

About Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Dante Gabriel Rossetti was born on May 12, 1828, in London, and became both a celebrated painter and poet, a central figure in the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. His poems, including the sonnet sequence The House of Life, are among the most elaborately crafted of the Victorian period. He died at Birchington-on-Sea in 1882.
