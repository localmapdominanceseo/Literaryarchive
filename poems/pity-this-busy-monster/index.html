pity this busy monster, manunkind

by E.E. Cummings

Written 1944 Published 1944 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1944, this poem attacks Progress — a comfortable disease — and the civilized world built on it, ending with the plea to listen: there is a hell of a good universe next door; let's go.

Quick Answer

"pity this busy monster, manunkind" is a poem by E.E. Cummings, published in 1944. Progress is a comfortable disease whose helpless victim cries 'we can never be born enough.' Electrons deify one razorblade into a mountain range. The speaker says: pity poor mankind, but don't, because there's a hell of a good universe next door. Let's go.

pity this busy monster, manunkind,

not. Progress is a comfortable disease:
your victim (death and life safely beyond)

plays with the bigness of his littleness
— electrons deify one razorblade
into a mountainrange; lenses extend

unwish through curving wherewhen till unwish
returns on its unself.
                        A world of made
is not a world of born — pity poor flesh

and trees, poor stars and stones, but never this
fine specimen of hypermagical

ultraomniportence. We doctors know

a hopeless case if — listen: there's a hell
of a good universe next door; let's go

Analysis

Form and Structure

Cummings's characteristic lowercase free verse with unconventional punctuation, the parenthetical asides and compound words ('manunkind', 'ultraomnipotence') doing the satirical work.

Themes

A savage attack on technological progress and the civilized world's self-congratulation — the poem argues that progress has made mankind less than human, and the only escape is to leave for a better universe entirely.

Historical Context

Published in 1 x 1 (1944), written during World War II. One of Cummings's most quoted political poems.

About E.E. Cummings

Edward Estlin Cummings was born on October 14, 1894, in Cambridge, Massachusetts, and became one of the most distinctive American poets of the twentieth century, known for his radical experiments with typography, punctuation, capitalization, and syntax. He published his first major collection, Tulips and Chimneys, in 1923, and continued to innovate formally throughout his career. His love poems, particularly 'i carry your heart with me,' are now among the most frequently quoted in American literature. He died in North Conway, New Hampshire, in 1962.
