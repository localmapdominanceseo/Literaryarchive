The Charge of the Light Brigade

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1854 Published 1854 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1854, written just six weeks after the disastrous charge at Balaclava during the Crimean War, this poem celebrates the courage of the six hundred cavalrymen who rode into artillery fire due to a miscommunicated order.

Quick Answer

The Charge of the Light Brigade is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1854. Half a league onward, all in the valley of Death rode the six hundred. Cannon to the right, cannon to the left, cannon in front. Theirs not to reason why, theirs but to do and die.

Half a league, half a league,
Half a league onward,
All in the valley of Death
Rode the six hundred.
'Forward, the Light Brigade!
Charge for the guns!' he said:
Into the valley of Death
Rode the six hundred.

'Forward, the Light Brigade!'
Was there a man dismay'd?
Not tho' the soldier knew
Some one had blunder'd:
Their's not to make reply,
Their's not to reason why,
Their's but to do and die:
Into the valley of Death
Rode the six hundred.

Cannon to right of them,
Cannon to left of them,
Cannon in front of them
Volley'd and thunder'd;
Storm'd at with shot and shell,
Boldly they rode and well,
Into the jaws of Death,
Into the mouth of Hell
Rode the six hundred.

When can their glory fade?
O the wild charge they made!
All the world wonder'd.
Honour the charge they made!
Honour the Light Brigade,
Noble six hundred!

Public domain. Published 1854.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six stanzas of varying length in dactylic dimeter with irregular rhyme, the driving rhythm enacting the cavalry charge.

Themes

Military courage in the face of certain death and a disastrous command failure. The poem celebrates the men who obeyed.

Historical Context

Published in The Examiner on December 9, 1854. The charge occurred on October 25, 1854, at the Battle of Balaclava during the Crimean War.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
