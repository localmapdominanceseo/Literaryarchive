God's Grandeur

by Gerard Manley Hopkins

Written 1877 Published 1918 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Written in 1877 and published posthumously in 1918, this Petrarchan sonnet laments that despite the world being charged with God's grandeur, humanity has bleared and smeared it through industrial exploitation — yet nature is never spent, because the Holy Ghost broods over the world like a bird over its nest.

Quick Answer

"God's Grandeur" is a poem by Gerard Manley Hopkins, written in 1877 and published posthumously in 1918. The poem declares that the world is charged with God's grandeur, then asks why humanity has refused to heed it, smearing the earth through generations of industrial toil until nature seems exhausted. The sestet answers: nature is never spent, because the Holy Ghost broods with warm breast over the bent world, renewing it.

The world is charged with the grandeur of God.
It will flame out, like shining from shook foil;
It gathers to a greatness, like the ooze of oil
Crushed. Why do men then now not reck his rod?
Generations have trod, have trod, have trod;
And all is seared with trade; bleared, smeared with toil;
And wears man's smudge and shares man's smell: the soil
Is bare now, nor can foot feel, being shod.

And for all this, nature is never spent;
There lives the dearest freshness deep down things;
And though the last lights off the black West went
Oh, morning, at the brown brink eastward, springs—
Because the Holy Ghost over the bent
World broods with warm breast and with ah! bright wings.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1918.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in sprung rhythm, dividing the problem (humanity's degradation of the earth, octave) from the hope (God's inexhaustible renewal, sestet). Hopkins uses 'shining from shook foil' — electrical imagery of charged gold leaf — to describe divine energy flooding the world.

Themes

The conflict between divine creation and human industrial destruction, resolved by faith in nature's inexhaustible renewal through God. The poem is one of Hopkins's most direct statements of his 'inscape' theory — the inner essence of things that reveals divine presence — applied to an environmental lament.

Historical Context

Written in February 1877 during Hopkins's theological training at St. Beuno's College in Wales. It was not published during his lifetime; Robert Bridges edited and published his collected poems in 1918. The poem reflects both Hopkins's Jesuit theology and his deep attention to the natural world.

About Gerard Manley Hopkins

Gerard Manley Hopkins was born on July 28, 1844, in Stratford, Essex, England, and became one of the most innovative poets of the Victorian era, though almost none of his work was published during his lifetime. He converted to Catholicism in 1866 and was ordained as a Jesuit priest in 1877, the year he wrote some of his finest poems. Hopkins invented 'sprung rhythm,' a metrical system based on stressed syllables rather than a fixed pattern, and his poetry's intense verbal compression and sound effects were a major influence on twentieth-century verse. He died in Dublin in 1889.
