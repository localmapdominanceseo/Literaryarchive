The Chestnut Casts His Flambeaux

by A.E. Housman

Written 1922 Published 1922 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in Last Poems (1922), this poem watches the chestnut trees in bloom in the wind and meditates on the futility of complaining about fate — the troubles of our proud and angry dust are from eternity.

Quick Answer

The Chestnut Casts His Flambeaux is a poem by A.E. Housman, published in 1922. The chestnut casts his flambeaux in the wind. Earth and high heaven are fixed of old and founded strong. The troubles of our proud and angry dust are from eternity, and shall not fail. Bear them we can, and if we can we must.

The chestnut casts his flambeaux, and the flowers
Stream from the hawthorn on the wind away,
The doors clap to, the pane is blind with showers.
Pass me the can, lad; there's an end of May.

There's one spoilt spring to scant our mortal lot,
One season ruined of our little store.
May will be fine next year as like as not:
Oh ay, but then we shall be twenty-four.

We for a certainty are not the first
Have sat in taverns while the tempest hurled
Their hopeful plans to emptiness, and cursed
Whatever brute and blackguard made the world.

It is in truth iniquity on high
To cheat our sentenced souls of aught they crave,
And mar the merriment as you and I
Stomp at the devil's ball of blame and stave.

The troubles of our proud and angry dust
Are from eternity, and shall not fail.
Bear them we can, and if we can we must.
Shoulder the sky, my lad, and drink your ale.

Public domain. Published 1922 in Last Poems.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Five four-line stanzas of iambic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme.

Themes

Stoic acceptance of suffering — the troubles of humanity are eternal and cannot be argued away. We must bear them because there is nothing else.

Historical Context

Published in Last Poems (1922).

About A.E. Housman

Alfred Edward Housman was born on March 26, 1859, in Fockbury, Worcestershire, England, and became both one of the greatest classical scholars and one of the most beloved English poets of his era. A Shropshire Lad (1896), published at his own expense after being rejected by publishers, became one of the most popular poetry collections ever published in English. He died in Cambridge in 1936.
