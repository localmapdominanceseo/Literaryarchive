Down by the Salley Gardens

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1888 Published 1889 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1889 in The Wanderings of Oisin, this short song poem is Yeats's adaptation of a fragment of an old Irish air he heard, describing a young man who failed to take his love's advice to take love easy, and now stands full of tears.

Quick Answer

"Down by the Salley Gardens" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1889. A young man met his love in the salley gardens and she told him to take love easy as the leaves grow on the tree, but he was young and foolish and disagreed. In a field by a river she told him to take life easy as the grass grows on the weirs, but he was young and foolish and now he is full of tears.

Down by the salley gardens my love and I did meet;
She passed the salley gardens with little snow-white feet.
She bid me take love easy, as the leaves grow on the tree;
But I, being young and foolish, with her did not agree.

In a field by the river my love and I did stand,
And on my leaning shoulder she laid her snow-white hand.
She bid me take life easy, as the grass grows on the weirs;
But I was young and foolish, and now am full of tears.

Public domain (published 1889). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two quatrains of alternating tetrameter and trimeter in ballad form, the simple folk song structure suiting Yeats's adaptation of a traditional fragment.

Themes

The regret of youth that would not take love or life easily, and must now live with the consequence of that refusal — not dramatic tragedy but quiet, full tears.

Historical Context

Yeats based the poem on a fragment of song he heard from an old peasant woman and published it in The Wanderings of Oisin (1889). It has been set to music many times and is one of the most sung of all Yeats's poems.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
