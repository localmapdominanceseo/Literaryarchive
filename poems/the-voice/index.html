The Voice

by Thomas Hardy

Written 1912 Published 1914 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1914 in Satires of Circumstance, this poem is part of Hardy's elegies for his first wife Emma who died in 1912. He hears her voice calling him and wonders if it is really her or only the wind.

Quick Answer

The Voice is a poem by Thomas Hardy, published in 1914. Woman much missed, how you call to me, call to me. Hardy hears his dead wife's voice saying she is not as she was when she changed from the one who was all to him — but just the woman who used to be. He strains to hear her but only hears the wind.

Woman much missed, how you call to me, call to me,
Saying that now you are not as you were
When you had changed from the one who was all to me,
But as at first, when our day was fair.

Can it be you that I hear? Let me view you, then,
Standing as when I drew near to the town
Where you would wait for me: yes, as I knew you then,
Even to the original air-blue gown!

Or is it only the breeze, in its listlessness
Travelling across the wet mead to me here,
You being ever dissolved to wan wistlessness,
Heard no more again far or near?

Thus I; faltering forward,
Leaves around me falling,
Wind oozing thin through the thorn from norward,
And the woman calling.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four quatrains of dactylic tetrameter with ABAB rhyme, the unusual meter giving the poem its haunted, uncertain quality.

Themes

Grief and the ambiguity of perceived presence — Hardy cannot tell if he really hears his wife's voice or only the wind. The poem captures the hallucinations of grief.

Historical Context

Part of the Poems of 1912-13 (also called Emma poems), written after Emma Hardy's sudden death in November 1912.

About Thomas Hardy

Thomas Hardy was born on June 2, 1840, in Higher Bockhampton, Dorset, England, and became one of the greatest novelists and poets of the Victorian and Edwardian eras. After Jude the Obscure (1895) was attacked for immorality, he stopped writing fiction and devoted himself entirely to poetry, producing eight volumes. His elegies for his first wife Emma, written after her sudden death in 1912, are considered among the finest in the English language. He died in Dorchester in 1928.
