The Pulley

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1633 in The Temple, this poem describes God giving humanity all gifts — strength, beauty, wisdom, honor, pleasure — but withholding rest, so that restlessness itself becomes the pulley that draws humanity back to God.

Quick Answer

"The Pulley" is a poem by George Herbert, published in 1633. God pours out all his gifts on humanity except rest — keeping that in his glass. Without rest, humanity will be rich but restless, and that restlessness will be the pulley that draws them back to God: if goodness cannot win the heart, weariness may toss it to God's breast.

When God at first made man,
Having a glasse of blessings standing by;
Let us (said he) poure on him all we can:
Let the worlds riches, which dispersed lie,
Contract into a span.

So strength first made a way;
Then beautie flow'd, then wisdome, honour, pleasure:
When almost all was out, God made a stay,
Perceiving that alone of all his treasure
Rest in the bottome lay.

For if I should (said he)
Bestow this jewell also on my creature,
He would adore my gifts in stead of me,
And rest in Nature, not the God of Nature:
So both should losers be.

Yet let him keep the rest,
But keep them with repining restlessnesse:
Let him be rich and wearie, that at least,
If goodnesse leade him not, yet wearinesse
May tosse him to my breast.

Public domain. Published 1633 in The Temple.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four five-line stanzas of iambic pentameter and tetrameter, the conceit sustained across all four stanzas with careful precision.

Themes

Divine strategy: by withholding rest, God ensures that no earthly satisfaction can fully content the human soul, so that restlessness always draws it back toward its source. The pulley works in reverse — instead of lifting the soul by force, God lets weariness lower it to rest.

Historical Context

Published in The Temple (1633). One of Herbert's most famous conceits — the mechanical image of the pulley as a metaphor for the mechanism of salvation.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
