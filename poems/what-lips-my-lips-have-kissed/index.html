What Lips My Lips Have Kissed

by Edna St. Vincent Millay

Written 1920 Published 1923 American Modernism Public Domain

Written around 1920 and published in 1923 in The Harp-Weaver and Other Poems, this sonnet meditates on forgotten lovers — the speaker cannot remember their names or faces, only that they came and went, leaving a winter emptiness in the soul like a tree stripped of birds.

Quick Answer

"What Lips My Lips Have Kissed" is a sonnet by Edna St. Vincent Millay, published in 1923. The speaker lies awake listening to rain, remembering unnamed, forgotten lovers who came and went. She can no longer recall their faces or names, only that they were there. She compares herself to a tree that once held singing birds in summer but now stands silent in winter, not remembering what birds have gone.

What lips my lips have kissed, and where, and why,
I have forgotten, and what arms have lain
Under my head till morning; but the rain
Is full of ghosts tonight, that tap and sigh
Upon the glass and listen for reply,
And in my heart there stirs a quiet pain
For unremembered lads that not again
Will turn to me at midnight with a cry.

Thus in winter stands the lonely tree,
Nor knows what birds have vanished one by one,
Yet knows its boughs more silent than before:
I cannot say what loves have come and gone,
I only know that summer sang in me
A little while, that in me sings no more.

Public domain (published 1923). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter, the octave establishing the speaker's loss and vague memory, the sestet expanding to the tree metaphor and the final acceptance of solitary winter.

Themes

Memory, loss, and the passage of passionate youth. The poem does not mourn specific people but the general fact of forgetting — that the heart that was once full of love and sensation is now quiet and winter-bare, unable even to remember what it has lost.

Historical Context

Published in The Harp-Weaver and Other Poems (1923), the collection for which Millay won the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry in 1923 — the first woman to do so.

About Edna St. Vincent Millay

Edna St. Vincent Millay was born on February 22, 1892, in Rockland, Maine, and became one of the most celebrated American poets of the 1920s, known for her sonnets and her embodiment of the free-spirited 'New Woman' of the Jazz Age. She was the first woman to win the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry, receiving it in 1923 for The Harp-Weaver and Other Poems. She died at Steepletop, her farm in New York, in 1950.
