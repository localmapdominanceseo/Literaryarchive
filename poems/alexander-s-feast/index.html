Alexander's Feast

by John Dryden

Written 1697 Published 1697 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1697 as an ode for St. Cecilia's Day, this poem describes Timotheus the musician playing at Alexander the Great's feast after the conquest of Persia, controlling Alexander's emotions entirely through his music — raising him to glory, then plunging him to grief, then rousing him to burn Persepolis.

Quick Answer

"Alexander's Feast" is a St. Cecilia's Day ode by John Dryden, published in 1697. Timotheus the musician plays at Alexander the Great's feast and completely controls the conqueror's emotions through music: rousing him to pride, then to love of Thais, then to grief for Darius, then to fury in which Alexander burns Persepolis. Music proved greater than arms.

'Twas at the royal feast, for Persia won,
By Philip's warlike son:
Aloft in awful state
The god-like hero sate
On his imperial throne:
His valiant peers were placed around,
Their brows with roses and with myrtles bound:
(So should desert in arms be crowned;)
The lovely Thais, by his side,
Sate like a blooming Eastern bride,
In flower of youth and beauty's pride:
Happy, happy, happy pair!
None but the brave,
None but the brave,
None but the brave deserves the fair!

Timotheus, placed on high
Amid the tuneful quire,
With flying fingers touched the lyre:
The trembling notes ascend the sky,
And heavenly joys inspire.
The song began from Jove,
Who left his blissful seats above,
(Such is the power of mighty love!)
A dragon's fiery form belied the god:
Sublime on radiant spires he rode,
When he to fair Olympia pressed,
And while he sought her snowy breast:
Then round her slender waist he curled,
And stamped an image of himself, a sovereign of the world.
The listening crowd admire the lofty sound,
'A present deity,' they shout around;
'A present deity,' the vaulted roofs rebound:
With ravished ears
The monarch hears,
Assumes the god,
Affects to nod,
And seems to shake the spheres.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Pindaric ode in irregular stanzas with varying line lengths and rhyme schemes, designed to be set to music. Handel famously set it to music in 1736.

Themes

The power of music over the human passions — and by extension, the power of the arts over political and military greatness. Timotheus controls the world-conqueror completely through song.

Historical Context

Published in 1697. Dryden wrote two St. Cecilia's Day odes; this is the second and is generally considered the finer. Handel's 1736 setting made it one of the most performed English odes.

About John Dryden

John Dryden was born on August 9, 1631, in Aldwincle, Northamptonshire, England, and became the dominant literary figure of the Restoration period, serving as Poet Laureate from 1668 to 1688. He excelled in satire, translation, and occasional poetry. His translations of Virgil and his St. Cecilia's Day odes (including Alexander's Feast) were enormously influential. He died in London in 1700.
