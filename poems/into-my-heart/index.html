Into My Heart an Air That Kills

by A.E. Housman

Written 1896 Published 1896 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1896 in A Shropshire Lad, this short poem describes an air that blows into the speaker's heart from the lost land — the land of lost content, where he cannot go again.

Quick Answer

Into My Heart is a poem by A.E. Housman, published in 1896. Into my heart an air that kills from yon far country blows. What are those blue remembered hills? What spires, what farms are those? That is the land of lost content, I see it shining plain — the happy highways where I went and cannot come again.

Into my heart an air that kills
From yon far country blows:
What are those blue remembered hills,
What spires, what farms are those?

That is the land of lost content,
I see it shining plain,
The happy highways where I went
And cannot come again.

Public domain. Published 1896 in A Shropshire Lad.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two quatrains of iambic tetrameter and trimeter with ABAB rhyme, the poem compressed to its essential image.

Themes

Nostalgia for a lost childhood paradise — the 'land of lost content' that can only be seen from a distance and can never be returned to.

Historical Context

Published in A Shropshire Lad (1896), poem XL. One of Housman's most beloved short poems.

About A.E. Housman

Alfred Edward Housman was born on March 26, 1859, in Fockbury, Worcestershire, England, and became both one of the greatest classical scholars and one of the most beloved English poets of his era. A Shropshire Lad (1896), published at his own expense after being rejected by publishers, became one of the most popular poetry collections ever published in English. He died in Cambridge in 1936.
