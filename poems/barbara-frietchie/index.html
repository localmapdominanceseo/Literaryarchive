Barbara Frietchie

by John Greenleaf Whittier

Written 1863 Published 1863 American Romanticism Public Domain

Published 1863.

Quick Answer

Barbara Frietchie is a poem, published 1863.

Up from the meadows rich with corn,
Clear in the cool September morn,

The clustered spires of Frederick stand
Green-walled by the hills of Maryland.

'Shoot, if you must, this old gray head,
But spare your country's flag,' she said.

A shade of sadness, a blush of shame,
Over the face of the leader came;

The nobler nature within him stirred
To life at that woman's deed and word;

'Who touches a hair of yon gray head
Dies like a dog! March on!' he said.

All day long through Frederick street
Sounded the tread of marching feet:

Up rose old Barbara Frietchie then,
Bowed with her fourscore years and ten;

Brave heart! that strain would never be heard
In her grave, or yon blue sky.

About John Greenleaf Whittier

John Greenleaf Whittier was born on December 17, 1807, in Haverhill, Massachusetts, and became both a prominent abolitionist and one of the most popular American poets of the nineteenth century. His long poem Snow-Bound (1866), a winter idyll and elegy for his lost family, is considered his masterpiece. He was a Quaker and used his poetry extensively in the service of the anti-slavery cause. He died in Hampton Falls, New Hampshire, in 1892.
