Come, My Celia

by Ben Jonson

Written 1606 Published 1616 English Renaissance Public Domain

Published in 1616, this song from the play Volpone (1606) is sung by Volpone to seduce Celia, drawing on Catullus's Vivamus, mea Lesbia to argue that life is short and kisses should not be counted or numbered.

Quick Answer

"Come, My Celia" is a song by Ben Jonson from Volpone (1606), published in 1616. The speaker urges Celia to let them prove while we may the sports of love — the sun rises again, but when their little light is set, one long night will remain. Let them not count their kisses. Sport as free as the fair wind.

Come, my Celia, let us prove,
While we may, the sports of love;
Time will not be ours for ever,
He at length our good will sever.
Spend not then his gifts in vain:
Suns that set may rise again,
But if once we lose this light,
'Tis with us perpetual night.

Why should we defer our joys?
Fame and rumour are but toys.
Cannot we delude the eyes
Of a few poor household spies?
Or his easier ears beguile,
Thus removed by our wile?
'Tis no sin love's fruit to steal,
But the sweet thefts to reveal.

To be taken, to be seen,
These have crimes accounted been.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three stanzas of eight lines each in iambic tetrameter, the song adapted closely from Catullus 5.

Themes

Carpe diem — the urgency of seizing love's pleasures before death's long night. The dramatic context (Volpone singing this to seduce an unwilling woman) gives the poem an ironic edge absent from Catullus's original.

Historical Context

From the play Volpone (performed 1606, published 1607). Collected in The Forest (1616). A close adaptation of Catullus 5.

About Ben Jonson

Ben Jonson was born on June 11, 1572, in London, and became the foremost dramatist and poet of the Jacobean era, second only to Shakespeare in contemporary reputation. His plays Volpone (1606) and The Alchemist (1610) are among the greatest comedies in English. As a poet, his Epigrams, The Forest, and Under-wood collections display unrivaled technical mastery and classical learning. He died in London in 1637.
