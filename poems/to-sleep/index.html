To Sleep

by William Wordsworth

Written 1807 Published 1807 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1807 in Poems in Two Volumes, this sonnet addresses Sleep as a gentle thing — the speaker has called on it without success, and lists the sounds of night around him, and asks it to come.

Quick Answer

To Sleep is a sonnet by Wordsworth, published in 1807. A flock of sheep that leisurely pass by, one after one; the sound of rain, and bees murmuring; a clock striking the hours one and two — and none of them has brought the speaker sleep. Come, blessed barrier between day and day, dear mother of fresh thoughts and joyous health!

A flock of sheep that leisurely pass by,
One after one; the sound of rain, and bees
Murmuring; the fall of rivers, winds and seas,
Smooth fields, white sheets of water, and pure sky;
I have thought of all by turns, and yet do lie
Sleepless! and soon the small birds' melodies
Must hear, first uttered from my orchard trees;
And the first cuckoo's melancholy cry.
Even thus last night, and two nights more, I lay
And could not win thee, Sleep! by any stealth:
So do not let me wear to-night away:
Without Thee what is all the morning's wealth?
Come, blessed barrier between day and day,
Dear mother of fresh thoughts and joyous health!

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter.

Themes

The blessing of sleep as a restorative — the speaker who cannot sleep celebrates what he lacks.

Historical Context

Published in Poems in Two Volumes (1807).

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
