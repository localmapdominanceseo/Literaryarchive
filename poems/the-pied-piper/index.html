Home-Thoughts, from Abroad

by Robert Browning

Written 1845 Published 1845 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1845 in Dramatic Romances and Lyrics, this short lyric is spoken by someone in a foreign country in April, who suddenly longs for England — for the brushwood sheaf, the elm with its rattling twigs, the buttercup, the wise thrush that sings each song twice over.

Quick Answer

"Home-Thoughts, from Abroad" is a poem by Robert Browning, published in 1845. The speaker, abroad in spring, thinks of England: the small white flowers, the chaffinch on the orchard bough, the wise thrush who sings each song twice over to recapture its first careless rapture — oh, to be in England, now that April's there.

Oh, to be in England
Now that April's there,
And whoever wakes in England
Sees, some morning, unaware,
That the lowest boughs and the brushwood sheaf
Round the elm-tree bole are in tiny leaf,
While the chaffinch sings on the orchard bough
In England—now!

And after April, when May follows,
And the whitethroat builds, and all the swallows!
Hark, where my blossomed pear-tree in the hedge
Leans to the field and scatters on the clover
Blossoms and dewdrops—at the bent spray's edge—
That's the wise thrush; he sings each song twice over,
Lest you should think he never could recapture
The first fine careless rapture!
And though the fields look rough with hoary dew,
All will be gay when noontide wakes anew
The buttercups, the little children's dower
—Far brighter than this gaudy melon-flower!

Analysis

Form and Structure

Two stanzas of irregular rhyming lines, the first eight lines and the second sixteen, with a light, lyrical movement.

Themes

Nostalgia for England's spring seen from abroad — not for grand landscapes but for the small, precise details of English spring: specific birds, specific flowers, specific sounds. The 'wise thrush' who sings each song twice to recapture its first rapture is one of Browning's finest images.

Historical Context

Published in Dramatic Romances and Lyrics (1845). Browning spent many years in Italy after his elopement with Elizabeth Barrett, and this poem reflects genuine homesickness for the English spring.

About Robert Browning

Robert Browning was born on May 7, 1812, in Camberwell, London, and became one of the foremost Victorian poets, celebrated above all for his mastery of the dramatic monologue — a form in which a speaker inadvertently reveals more about themselves than they intend. His collection Men and Women (1855) and his verse novel The Ring and the Book (1868-1869) are considered his greatest achievements. He married Elizabeth Barrett in 1846 and they lived in Italy until her death in 1861. He died in Venice in 1889.
