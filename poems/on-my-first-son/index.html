On My First Son

by Ben Jonson

Written 1603 Published 1616 English Renaissance Public Domain

Written in 1603 after the death of his son Benjamin at age seven and published in 1616 in Epigrams, this short elegy says farewell to the child — the father's best piece of poetry — and resolves never again to like what he makes or loves too much.

Quick Answer

"On My First Son" is a poem by Ben Jonson, published in 1616. Jonson addresses his dead son Benjamin — his joy, his best piece of poetry, lent from God and now returned. The boy has escaped the misery of age. Jonson vows henceforth not to like too much what he loves.

Farewell, thou child of my right hand, and joy;
My sin was too much hope of thee, loved boy.
Seven years thou wert lent to me, and I thee pay,
Exacted by thy fate, on the just day.
O, could I lose all father, now! For why
Will man lament the state he should envy?
To have so soon 'scaped world's, and flesh's rage,
And, if no other misery, yet age!
Rest in soft peace, and, asked, say, Here doth lie
Ben Jonson his best piece of poetry.
For whose sake, henceforth, all his vows be such,
As what he loves may never like too much.

Public domain. Published 1616 in Epigrams.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twelve lines of iambic pentameter rhyming AABBCCDDEEFF, an epigram in the strict sense.

Themes

Parental grief and the theological consolation that a child is only lent from God — a consolation the poem simultaneously accepts and struggles with. The resolution not to love too much is both Stoic and heartbroken.

Historical Context

Published in Epigrams (1616). Benjamin Jonson died on his seventh birthday in 1603, the same year as a London plague epidemic. Jonson is said to have seen his son's apparition in a dream the night the child died.

About Ben Jonson

Ben Jonson was born on June 11, 1572, in London, and became the foremost dramatist and poet of the Jacobean era, second only to Shakespeare in contemporary reputation. His plays Volpone (1606) and The Alchemist (1610) are among the greatest comedies in English. As a poet, his Epigrams, The Forest, and Under-wood collections display unrivaled technical mastery and classical learning. He died in London in 1637.
