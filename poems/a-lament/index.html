A Lament

by Percy Bysshe Shelley

Written 1821 Published 1824 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1824, this six-line lyric asks the world, time, and life why they have changed — things that were sweet are now bitter, the world is wearier.

Quick Answer

"A Lament" is a six-line poem by Shelley, published posthumously in 1824. O world! O life! O time! On whose last steps I climb. Out of the day and night a joy has taken flight. Fresh spring, summer, and autumn and winter — not again. O thou who are gone into the world of darkness — not again.

O world! O life! O time!
On whose last steps I climb,
Trembling at that where I had stood before;
When will return the glory of your prime?
No more—Oh, never more!

Out of the day and night
A joy has taken flight;
Fresh spring, and summer, and winter hoar,
Move my faint heart with grief, but with delight
No more—Oh, never more!

Public domain. Published posthumously 1824.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Six lines of irregular length, the poem compressed to its barest emotional content.

Themes

Grief and the loss of joy — the world has not changed but the speaker's capacity for joy has gone.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1824. One of Shelley's most compressed lyrics.

About Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley was born on August 4, 1792, in Horsham, England, and became one of the major poets of English Romanticism despite controversy during his lifetime over his radical political and religious views. His poetry combines lyrical intensity with sustained engagement with politics, philosophy, and the natural world. He drowned in a boating accident off the coast of Italy in 1822, at the age of twenty-nine.
