Come into the Garden, Maud

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1855 Published 1855 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1855 in Maud: A Monodrama, this is the most famous lyric from that poem-sequence — the speaker calling Maud to come into the garden where the black bat Night has flown, and the planets and stars dance, waiting for her.

Quick Answer

"Come into the Garden, Maud" is a lyric by Tennyson, published in 1855, from the dramatic sequence Maud. The speaker calls Maud to come into the garden where the black bat Night has flown, the planets are glittering bright, the planet of Love is on high, and the rose is all alive. She is his queen, his life, his fate.

Come into the garden, Maud,
For the black bat, Night, has flown,
Come into the garden, Maud,
I am here at the gate alone;
And the woodbine spices are wafted abroad,
And the musk of the rose is blown.

For a breeze of morning moves,
And the planet of Love is on high,
Beginning to faint in the light that she loves
On a bed of daffodil sky,
To faint in the light of the sun she loves,
To faint in his light, and to die.

All night have the roses heard
The flute, violin, bassoon;
All night has the casement jessamine stirr'd
To the dancers dancing in tune;
Till a silence fell with the waking bird,
And a hush with the setting moon.

I said to the lily, 'There is but one
With whom she has heart to be gay.
When will the dancers leave her alone?
She is weary of dance and play.'
Now half to the setting moon are gone,
And half to the rising day;
Low on the sand and loud on the stone
The last wheel echoes away.

I said to the rose, 'The brief night goes
In babble and revel and wine.
O young lord-lover, what sighs are those,
For one that will never be thine?
But mine, but mine,' so I sware to the rose,
'For ever and ever, mine.'

Public domain. Published 1855 in Maud: A Monodrama.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Eight stanzas of varying length in a loose, ecstatic trochaic rhythm, the formal freedom matching the speaker's emotional intensity.

Themes

Romantic rapture and the lover's anticipation — the garden at night waiting for Maud is a paradise of sensation.

Historical Context

Published in Maud: A Monodrama (1855), Tennyson's experimental poem-sequence about a passionate, unstable speaker whose love for Maud ends in tragedy.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
