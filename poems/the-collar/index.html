The Collar

by George Herbert

Written 1633 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1633 in The Temple, this poem dramatizes a speaker in fierce rebellion against religious duty, railing against the constraints of a life of service — before being silenced in the final four lines by hearing God call his name.

Quick Answer

"The Collar" is a poem by George Herbert, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker, in angry rebellion against God and religious duty, rails that he is enslaved by a 'collar' of constraint — there is wine and harvest and freedom to be had, so why does he remain bound by sighing and service? The poem's sustained fury collapses in the final four lines when the speaker hears God calling 'Child,' and answers simply: 'My Lord.'

I struck the board, and cried, 'No more;
I will abroad!'
What? shall I ever sigh and pine?
My lines and life are free, free as the road,
Loose as the wind, as large as store.
Shall I be still in suit?
Have I no harvest but a thorn
To let me blood, and not restore
What I have lost with cordial fruit?
Sure there was wine
Before my sighs did dry it; there was corn
Before my tears did drown it.
Is the year only lost to me?
Have I no bays to crown it,
No flowers, no garlands gay? All blasted?
All wasted?
Not so, my heart; but there is fruit,
And thou hast hands.
Recover all thy sigh-blown age
On double pleasures: leave thy cold dispute
Of what is fit and not; forsake thy cage,
Thy rope of sands,
Which petty thoughts have made, and made to thee
Good cable, to enforce and draw,
And be thy law,
While thou didst wink and wouldst not see.
Away! take heed;
I will abroad.
Call in thy death's-head there, tie up thy fears;
He that forbears
To suit and serve his need
Deserves his load.'
But as I raved and grew more fierce and wild
At every word,
Methought I heard one calling, 'Child!'
And I replied 'My Lord.'

Analysis

Form and Structure

Thirty-six lines of varying length and irregular rhyme, the formal disorder mirroring the speaker's mental and spiritual turmoil. The poem's chaos of line lengths resolves abruptly into the calm quatrain of the ending, formally enacting the sudden submission it describes.

Themes

Spiritual rebellion and submission. The 'collar' of the title carries multiple meanings: the literal collar of a priest's vestment, a yoke of servitude, and a pun on 'choler' (anger). The poem is remarkable for how fully it inhabits the rebellious voice before surrendering it — Herbert is not simply condemning rebellion but dramatizing its force, making the submission at the end feel like a real psychological event rather than a foregone conclusion.

Historical Context

Herbert wrote the poem as part of The Temple, a collection he entrusted to a friend before his death in 1633 with instructions to publish it if it might help any 'dejected poor soul,' or otherwise burn it. It was published the same year and became one of the most influential collections of devotional English poetry.

About George Herbert

George Herbert was born on April 3, 1593, in Montgomery, Wales, and became one of the leading Metaphysical poets, known for devotional verse of striking formal ingenuity. He served as a Church of England priest in his final years and prepared his entire collection, The Temple, for posthumous publication before his death in 1633. His poetry combines intense personal piety with wit and formal experimentation.
