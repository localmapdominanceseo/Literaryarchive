The Daisy

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1853 Published 1855 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1855, The Daisy is an epistle to his wife Emily written from Edinburgh, accompanying a daisy he picked on their Italian tour twelve years earlier — recalling the Italian landscapes they saw together.

Quick Answer

"The Daisy" is a poem by Tennyson, published in 1855. Written to his wife Emily from Edinburgh, it accompanies a daisy pressed from their Italian honeymoon journey twelve years before. The poem recalls the landscapes of Italy — the Lombard plain, Como, Sirmione — where he first found the daisy.

O love, what hours were thine and mine,
In lands of palm and southern pine;
In lands of palm, of orange-blossom,
Of olive, aloe, and maize and vine.

What Roman strength Turbìa show'd
In ruin, by the mountain road;
How like a gem, beneath, the city
Of little Monaco, basking, glow'd.

How richly down the rocky dell
The torrent vineyard streaming fell
To meet the sun and sunny waters,
That only heaved with a summer swell.

What slender campanili grew
By bays, the peacock's neck in hue;
Where, here and there, on sandy beaches
A milky-bell'd amaryllis blew.

Public domain. Published 1855.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Sixteen stanzas of eight lines each in alternating tetrameter and trimeter.

Themes

Memory, travel, and the preservation of shared experience — the pressed daisy as a tangible link between past joy and present distance.

Historical Context

Published in 1855 in Maud and Other Poems. One of Tennyson's most personal and affectionate poems.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
