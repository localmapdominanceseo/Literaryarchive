To Althea, from Prison

by Richard Lovelace

Written 1642 Published 1649 English Renaissance Public Domain

Written in 1642 while Lovelace was imprisoned for his Royalist activities and published in 1649, this poem argues that the speaker is freer in prison than the birds or fish — for his mind is free, and stone walls do not a prison make nor iron bars a cage.

Quick Answer

"To Althea, from Prison" is a poem by Richard Lovelace, written in 1642 and published in 1649. While imprisoned, the speaker argues that he is freer than the birds or the fish — Althea visits him, they drink wine and sing the king's cause, and his mind remains free. Stone walls do not a prison make, nor iron bars a cage; minds innocent and quiet are that hermitage.

When Love with unconfined wings
Hovers within my gates,
And my divine Althea brings
To whisper at the grates;
When I lie tangled in her hair
And fettered to her eye,
The birds that wanton in the air
Know no such liberty.

When flowing cups run swiftly round
With no allaying Thames,
Our careless heads with roses bound,
Our hearts with loyal flames;
When thirsty grief in wine we steep,
When healths and draughts go free—
Fishes that tipple in the deep
Know no such liberty.

When, like committed linnets, I
With shriller throat shall sing
The sweetness, mercy, majesty,
And glories of my King;
When I shall voice aloud how good
He is, how great should be,
Enlargèd winds, that curl the flood,
Know no such liberty.

Stone walls do not a prison make,
Nor iron bars a cage;
Minds innocent and quiet take
That for an hermitage;
If I have freedom in my love
And in my soul am free,
Angels alone, that soar above,
Enjoy such liberty.

Public domain. Written 1642, published 1649 in Lucasta.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Four eight-line stanzas of alternating tetrameter and trimeter in ballad-like ABABCDCD rhyme, the light form contrasting with the gravity of imprisonment.

Themes

The freedom of the mind over the imprisonment of the body — and the Cavalier loyalty to King and love as sources of inner freedom that no prison can take away.

Historical Context

Written when Lovelace was imprisoned in 1642 for presenting a Royalist petition to Parliament. Published in Lucasta (1649). The final stanza's 'stone walls do not a prison make' became one of the most quoted lines in English literature.

About Richard Lovelace

Richard Lovelace was born on December 9, 1617, in Woolwich, England, and became the most celebrated of the Cavalier poets, known for poems of love, loyalty, and honour written during the English Civil War. He was imprisoned twice for his Royalist sympathies, and wrote his two most famous poems — 'To Lucasta, Going to the Wars' and 'To Althea, from Prison' — during these periods of confinement. He died in poverty in London in 1657.
