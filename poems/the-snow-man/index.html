The Snow Man

by Wallace Stevens

Written 1921 Published 1921 American Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1921 in Poetry magazine, this single-sentence poem of fifteen lines asks what it means to have 'a mind of winter' — to perceive a snow-covered landscape without projecting human feeling onto it, and to behold nothing that is not there, and the nothing that is.

Quick Answer

"The Snow Man" is a poem by Wallace Stevens, published in 1921. In a single long sentence, it describes the kind of perception required to truly see a winter landscape: one must have a mind of winter, cold enough not to project misery or beauty onto the bare trees and frozen boughs — and in that state, perceive 'nothing that is not there and the nothing that is.'

One must have a mind of winter
To regard the frost and the boughs
Of the pine-trees crusted with snow;

And have been cold a long time
To behold the junipers shagged with ice,
The spruces rough in the distant glitter

Of the January sun; and not to think
Of any misery in the sound of the wind,
In the sound of a few leaves,

Which is the sound of the land
Full of the same wind
That is blowing in the same bare place

For the listener, who listens in the snow,
And, nothing himself, beholds
Nothing that is not there and the nothing that is.

Public domain (published 1921). Text per Poetry Foundation.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Fifteen lines in five tercets, written as a single sentence, the syntax unfolding slowly through a series of relative clauses before arriving at the final paradox. The form enacts the poem's content: the slow, patient attention required to empty the mind of projection.

Themes

The difference between perception colored by human emotion and pure, cold perception that sees only what is actually there. The 'nothing that is not there' is the misery that a cold observer would not project onto the wind; 'the nothing that is' is the actual emptiness of the winter scene, perceived clearly at last.

Historical Context

Published in Poetry magazine in October 1921 and collected in Harmonium (1923). The poem is one of Stevens's most philosophical, exploring the relationship between mind and reality that would preoccupy him throughout his career.

About Wallace Stevens

Wallace Stevens was born on October 2, 1879, in Reading, Pennsylvania, and spent most of his career as a lawyer and insurance executive while producing some of the most philosophically ambitious poetry of the American twentieth century. He published his first major collection, Harmonium, in 1923, and won the Pulitzer Prize for poetry in 1955, the year of his death in Hartford, Connecticut.
