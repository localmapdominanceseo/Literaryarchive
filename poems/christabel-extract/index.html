Christabel (Extract)

by Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Written 1797 Published 1816 English Romanticism Public Domain

Written in 1797-1800 and published in 1816, Christabel is Coleridge's unfinished Gothic narrative poem about a young woman who encounters a mysterious lady in the forest and brings her home, not realizing she is a supernatural creature.

Quick Answer

Christabel is Coleridge's unfinished Gothic narrative poem, begun in 1797 and published in 1816. At midnight in the wood, Christabel meets the beautiful Geraldine, who claims to have been abducted by men. Christabel brings her home — but Geraldine has a supernatural power over her, and her eyes have strange effects on those who see them.

'Tis the middle of night by the castle clock,
And the owls have awakened the crowing cock;
Tu—whit! Tu—whoo!
And hark, again! the crowing cock,
How drowsily it crew.

Sir Leoline, the Baron rich,
Hath a toothless mastiff bitch;
From her kennel beneath the rock
She maketh answer to the clock,
Four for the quarters, and twelve for the hour;
Ever and aye, by shine and shower,
Sixteen short howls, not over loud;
Some say, she sees my lady's shroud.

Is the night chilly and dark?
The night is chilly, but not dark.
The thin gray cloud is spread on high,
It covers but not hides the sky.
The moon is behind, and at the full;
And yet she looks both small and dull.
The night is chill, the cloud is gray:
'Tis a month before the month of May,
And the Spring comes slowly up this way.

The lovely lady, Christabel,
Whom her father loves so well,
What makes her in the wood so late,
A furlong from the castle gate?
She had dreams all yesternight
Of her own betrothèd knight;
And she in the midnight wood will pray
For the weal of her lover that's far away.

Public domain. Published 1816.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Accentual verse with varying numbers of syllables per line but generally four stresses — an innovative meter Coleridge invented for the poem.

Themes

Innocence menaced by the supernatural, the ambiguity of evil that appears beautiful, and the failure of innocence to recognize danger.

Historical Context

Published in 1816 alongside Kubla Khan. Coleridge never finished the poem. It influenced Gothic literature enormously and introduced the idea of the female vampire to English literature.

About Samuel Taylor Coleridge

Samuel Taylor Coleridge was born on October 21, 1772, in Ottery St Mary, Devon, England, and became one of the founders of the English Romantic movement alongside his friend William Wordsworth. Together they published Lyrical Ballads in 1798, a collection that launched Romanticism in English literature. Coleridge's own most celebrated contributions to the collection, 'The Rime of the Ancient Mariner' and 'Kubla Khan,' are among the most distinctive and widely studied poems in the language. He died in Highgate, London, in 1834.
