Surprised by Joy

by William Wordsworth

Written 1812 Published 1815 English Romanticism Public Domain

Published in 1815 in Poems, this sonnet was written after the death of Wordsworth's daughter Catherine in 1812. The speaker is surprised by joy, turns to share it with someone — and then remembers she is dead.

Quick Answer

Surprised by Joy is a sonnet by Wordsworth, published in 1815. Surprised by joy — impatient as the wind — the speaker turned to share the transport with someone, then remembered that she who was the most grievous loss was gone. His most grievous loss was that he forgot even for a moment.

Surprised by joy—impatient as the Wind
I turned to share the transport—Oh! with whom
But Thee, deep buried in the silent tomb,
That spot which no vicissitude can find?
Love, faithful love, recalled thee to my mind—
But how could I forget thee? Through what power,
Even for the least division of an hour,
Have I been so beguiled as to be blind
To my most grievous loss!—That thought's return
Was the worst pang that sorrow ever bore,
Save one, one only, when I stood forlorn,
Knowing my heart's best treasure was no more;
That neither present time, nor years unborn
Could to my sight that heavenly face restore.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter.

Themes

The particular cruelty of forgetting grief for a moment and then remembering — the double loss of the beloved and the moment of forgetting.

Historical Context

Published in Poems (1815). Written after the death of his daughter Catherine in 1812.

About William Wordsworth

William Wordsworth was born on April 7, 1770, in Cockermouth, England. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, he helped launch the English Romantic movement with the 1798 publication of Lyrical Ballads. Wordsworth's poetry championed plain language, the beauty of the natural world, and the inner life of ordinary people. He served as England's Poet Laureate from 1843 until his death in 1850, and his long autobiographical poem The Prelude is considered one of the central achievements of English Romanticism.
