In Memoriam A.H.H. (Selected Sections)

by Alfred Lord Tennyson

Written 1849 Published 1850 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1850, In Memoriam A.H.H. is Tennyson's elegy for Arthur Henry Hallam, written over seventeen years. These selected sections include the famous tis better to have loved and lost, and the Ring out wild bells passage.

Quick Answer

In Memoriam A.H.H. is Tennyson's great elegy for Arthur Hallam, published in 1850. Its most famous lines include 'Tis better to have loved and lost / Than never to have loved at all' and the Ring out, wild bells section calling for the new year to ring in the true.

I hold it true, whate'er befall;
I feel it, when I sorrow most;
'Tis better to have loved and lost
Than never to have loved at all.

— Section XXVII

Ring out, wild bells, to the wild sky,
The flying cloud, the frosty light:
The year is dying in the night;
Ring out, wild bells, and let him die.

Ring out the old, ring in the new,
Ring, happy bells, across the snow:
The year is going, let him go;
Ring out the false, ring in the true.

— Section CVI

Strong Son of God, immortal Love,
Whom we, that have not seen thy face,
By faith, and faith alone, embrace,
Believing where we cannot prove;

— Prologue

Analysis

Form and Structure

One hundred and thirty-three sections in four-line stanzas of iambic tetrameter with ABBA rhyme — the In Memoriam stanza.

Themes

Grief, religious doubt, evolutionary science, friendship, and eventual faith.

Historical Context

Published in 1850. Queen Victoria said it was her greatest consolation after Prince Albert's death.

About Alfred Lord Tennyson

Alfred Lord Tennyson was born on August 6, 1809, in Somersby, Lincolnshire, England, and became the dominant poet of the Victorian era, serving as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death in 1892. His work ranges from brief lyrics and dramatic monologues to the epic In Memoriam A.H.H., his long elegy for his close friend Arthur Henry Hallam. He died at Aldworth, Surrey, in 1892.
