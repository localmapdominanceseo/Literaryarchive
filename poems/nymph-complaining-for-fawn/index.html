The Nymph Complaining for the Death of Her Fawn

by Andrew Marvell

Written 1650 Published 1681 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1681, this long lyric is spoken by a nymph whose pet fawn has been shot dead by troopers. She mourns it, recalls its innocence, and plans to lie down beside it and die with it.

Quick Answer

"The Nymph Complaining for the Death of Her Fawn" is a poem by Andrew Marvell, published posthumously in 1681. A nymph mourns her fawn shot dead by troopers — the fawn she loved better than any gift, whose silver feet printed roses among the lilies. She will die with it and their tears and blood will be carved together in marble.

The wanton troopers riding by
Have shot my fawn and it will die.
Ungentle men! They cannot thrive
To kill thee. Thou ne'er didst alive
Them any harm: alas, nor could
Thy death yet do them any good.
I'm sure I never wished them ill;
Nor do I for all this; nor will:
But if my simple prayers may yet
Prevail with heaven to forget
Thy murder, I will join my tears
Rather than fail. But O my fears!
It cannot die so. Heaven's King
Keeps register of everything:
And nothing may we use in vain.
Even beasts must be with justice slain,
Else men are made their deodands.
Though they should wash their guilty hands
In this warm life-blood, which doth part
From thine, and wound me to the heart,
Yet could they not be clean: their stain
Is dyed in such a purple grain.
There is not such another in
The world, to offer for their sin.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1681.

Analysis

Form and Structure

One hundred and twenty-two lines of iambic tetrameter rhyming couplets, the form creating a sustained, elegiac momentum.

Themes

Innocence destroyed by violence, the grief of the innocent, and the pastoral convention of the beloved pet as stand-in for a more complex grief. The poem has been read allegorically (the fawn as Christ, as the English church), but its primary power is as a simple, moving lament.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1681. One of Marvell's finest lyrics, combining pastoral sweetness with genuine emotional depth.

About Andrew Marvell

Andrew Marvell was born on March 31, 1621, in Winestead-in-Holderness, Yorkshire, England, and became one of the most significant Metaphysical poets as well as a prominent political figure, serving as a Member of Parliament. Most of his lyric poetry, including 'To His Coy Mistress,' was published posthumously in 1681. He died in London in 1678.
