Hertha (Extract)

by Algernon Charles Swinburne

Written 1870 Published 1871 Victorian Poetry Public Domain

Published in 1871, this poem is spoken by Hertha, the Norse earth goddess, who declares herself the root and source of all life — I am that which began, out of me the years roll, out of me God and man.

Quick Answer

Hertha is a poem by Swinburne, published in 1871. The earth goddess Hertha speaks: I am that which began, out of me the years roll, out of me God and man. I the grain and the furrow, the plough-cloven clod, and the ploughshare drawn thorough, the germ and the sod. I am before thee, behind thee, beside thee, within thee.

I am that which began;
Out of me the years roll;
Out of me God and man;
I am equal and whole;
God changes, and man, and the form of them bodily; I am the soul.

Before ever land was,
Before ever the sea,
Or soft hair of the grass,
Or fair limbs of the tree,
Or the flesh-coloured fruit of my branches, I was, and thy soul was in me.

First life on my sources
First drifted and swam;
Out of me are the forces
That save it or damn;
Out of me man and woman, and wild-beast and bird; before God was, I am.

Public domain. Published 1871 in Songs Before Sunrise.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Long anapestic stanzas, the goddess's voice sweeping and incantatory.

Themes

Pantheism and the primacy of the natural world over all human constructions including God — Hertha is more ancient and more real than any deity.

Historical Context

Published in Songs Before Sunrise (1871), Swinburne's most politically radical collection, celebrating liberty and republicanism.

About Algernon Charles Swinburne

Algernon Charles Swinburne was born on April 5, 1837, in London, and became one of the most technically accomplished and musically dazzling of all Victorian poets. His Poems and Ballads (1866) caused an immediate scandal for its paganism, eroticism, and attacks on conventional morality. His verse drama Atalanta in Calydon (1865) established his reputation. He died at Putney in 1909.
