Holy Sonnet XIV (Batter My Heart)

by John Donne

Written 1609 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1609 and published posthumously in 1633, this sonnet asks God to take violent action on the speaker's soul — not to knock and breathe, but to batter, break, blow, and burn — because gentle persuasion has not worked, and the speaker is imprisoned by the enemy.

Quick Answer

"Batter My Heart, Three-Person'd God" is a Holy Sonnet by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. The speaker asks God not to merely knock and breathe at his heart but to break, blow, burn, and make him new. He is God's captured town, betrothed to God's enemy, and cannot be free unless God ravishes him.

Batter my heart, three-person'd God; for you
As yet but knock; breathe, shine, and seek to mend;
That I may rise, and stand, o'erthrow me, and bend
Your force, to break, blow, burn, and make me new.
I, like an usurpt town, to another due,
Labour to admit you, but Oh, to no end,
Reason your viceroy in me, me should defend,
But is captiv'd, and proves weak or untrue.
Yet dearly I love you, and would be loved fain,
But am betroth'd unto your enemie:
Divorce me, untie, or break that knot again;
Take me to you, imprison me, for I,
Except you enthrall me, never shall be free,
Nor ever chaste, except you ravish me.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter, the octave making the request with violent military and blacksmithing metaphors, the sestet explaining why gentler means have failed and ending with the paradox that only by being ravished can the speaker be made chaste.

Themes

The paradox of divine force in salvation — the soul needs violent overthrow because gentle persuasion has not broken its attachment to sin. The closing paradoxes (imprisoned/free, enthralled/chaste) are characteristic Metaphysical conceits applied to religious experience.

Historical Context

Part of Donne's Holy Sonnets, a sequence of nineteen religious poems probably written around 1609-1610 and published posthumously in 1633.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
