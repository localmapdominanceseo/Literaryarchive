No Second Troy

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1908 Published 1910 Modernism Public Domain

Published in 1910 in The Green Helmet, this short poem asks why Yeats should blame Maud Gonne for filling his days with misery — she was made for another age, a noble and simple beauty like Helen of Troy, and there was no second Troy for her to burn.

Quick Answer

"No Second Troy" is a short poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1910. The speaker asks why he should blame Maud Gonne for making his life miserable, for teaching the ignorant violent ways, for filling the days with misery. What could she have done, being what she is? Was there another Troy for her to burn?

Why should I blame her that she filled my days
With misery, or that she would of late
Have taught to ignorant men most violent ways,
Or hurled the little streets upon the great,
Had they but courage equal to desire?
What could have made her peaceful with a mind
That nobleness made simple as a fire,
With beauty like a tightened bow, a kind
That is not natural in an age like this,
Being high and solitary and most stern?
Why, what could she have done, being what she is?
Was there another Troy for her to burn?

Public domain (published 1910). Text per Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Twelve lines of loose iambic pentameter in rhyming couplets, the compressed, rhetorical structure of a series of questions that answer themselves.

Themes

The fascination of destructive beauty — Maud Gonne as Helen of Troy, too great and too violent for the small stage of Edwardian Ireland. The poem simultaneously blames and exonerates her.

Historical Context

Published in The Green Helmet (1910). One of many poems Yeats addressed to Maud Gonne, who had long refused his marriage proposals.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
