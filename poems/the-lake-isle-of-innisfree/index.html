The Lake Isle of Innisfree

by W.B. Yeats

Written 1888 Published 1890 Modernism Public Domain

Written in 1888 and published in 1890, this is one of Yeats's earliest celebrated poems, in which the speaker, standing on a grey London pavement, hears in his heart the lapping water of Lough Gill in his native County Sligo, and resolves to go there and live in solitude.

Quick Answer

"The Lake Isle of Innisfree" is a poem by W.B. Yeats, published in 1890. The speaker vows to arise and go to Innisfree, a small island on Lough Gill in County Sligo, where he will build a small cabin, keep bees, live alone in peace, and hear the lake water lapping. Standing on grey pavements, he hears this sound always in his heart.

I will arise and go now, and go to Innisfree,
And a small cabin build there, of clay and wattles made;
Nine bean-rows will I have there, a hive for the honey-bee,
And live alone in the bee-loud glade.

And I shall have some peace there, for peace comes dropping slow,
Dropping from the veils of the morning to where the cricket sings;
There midnight's all a glimmer, and noon a purple glow,
And evening full of the linnet's wings.

I will arise and go now, for always night and day
I hear lake water lapping with low sounds by the shore;
While I stand on the roadway, or on the pavements grey,
I hear it in the deep heart's core.

Public domain (published 1890). Text per the Academy of American Poets (poets.org).

Analysis

Form and Structure

Three quatrains of loose iambic hexameter with an ABAB rhyme scheme. The long, flowing lines and the repetition of 'I will arise and go now' give the poem a meditative, incantatory quality.

Themes

The longing for escape into natural simplicity and solitude. The poem is Yeats's most direct statement of the Romantic pastoral impulse — the city-dweller dreaming of retreat — but its final line grounds the vision in the speaker's own heartbeat rather than the actual island, making it as much about interior longing as actual place.

Historical Context

Yeats wrote the poem in London in 1888, homesick for County Sligo, and was inspired partly by Thoreau's Walden. He later said he wrote it while watching a small fountain in a shop window in Fleet Street and was struck by how much the sight of moving water made him miss Sligo. It was published in 1890 in The National Observer.

About W.B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats was born on June 13, 1865, near Dublin, Ireland, and became one of the most significant poets of the twentieth century and a central figure in the Irish Literary Revival. His poetry moved from early lyrical, mystically influenced verse toward a sparer, more powerful style concerned with history, aging, and Irish politics. Yeats won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 and served as a senator in the Irish Free State. He died in France in 1939.
