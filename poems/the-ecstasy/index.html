The Ecstasy

by John Donne

Written 1601 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Written around 1601 and published posthumously in 1633, this long poem describes two lovers lying together on a violet bank, their hands joined and souls in contact, and proceeds to argue (at considerable philosophical length) that the soul needs the body to express itself — so they should return to their bodies.

Quick Answer

"The Ecstasy" is a poem by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633. Two lovers lie on a bank, hands joined, eyes locked — their souls have left their bodies and are negotiating between them. The poem argues that love has purified them through this ecstasy, but their souls must re-enter their bodies so that weaker observers can understand their spiritual love through its physical expression.

Where, like a pillow on a bed,
A pregnant bank swelled up, to rest
The violet's reclining head,
Sat we two, one another's best.
Our hands were firmly cemented
With a fast balm, which thence did spring;
Our eye-beams twisted, and did thread
Our eyes upon one double string.
So to engraft our hands, as yet
Was all the means to make us one;
And pictures in our eyes to get
Was all our propagation.
As 'twixt two equal armies Fate
Suspends uncertain victory,
Our souls (which to advance their state,
Were gone out) hung 'twixt her and me.
And whilst our souls negotiate there,
We like sepulchral statues lay;
All day, the same our postures were,
And we said nothing, all the day.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

Seventy-six lines of iambic tetrameter rhyming couplets, the poem's length and philosophical argument making it one of Donne's most ambitious lyrics.

Themes

The relationship between soul and body in love — the poem argues that spiritual love must manifest through the body, that the soul cannot be known except through the body's expression of it. The ecstasy (literally, standing outside the body) is resolved by a return to the body.

Historical Context

Published posthumously in 1633. One of the most discussed of Donne's poems for its attempt to reconcile Neoplatonic spiritual love with erotic physical love.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
