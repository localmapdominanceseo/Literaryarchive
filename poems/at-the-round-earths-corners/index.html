At the round earth's imagined corners

by John Donne

Written 1609 Published 1633 Metaphysical Poetry Public Domain

Published posthumously in 1633, this Holy Sonnet (VII) opens with angels at the four corners of the earth blowing their trumpets to summon the dead for Judgment — but asks God to hold off until the speaker has repented enough.

Quick Answer

Holy Sonnet VII by John Donne, published posthumously in 1633, addresses the angels at the four corners of the earth who will blow their Last Trump to raise the dead — but asks God to let the speaker repent first. Death by war, flood, age, agues, tyranny, and despair — all have killed their millions. Now God must teach the speaker to repent, so he can be raised worthy.

At the round earth's imagined corners, blow
Your trumpets, angels, and arise, arise
From death, you numberless infinities
Of souls, and to your scattered bodies go;
All whom the flood did, and fire shall, o'erthrow,
All whom war, dearth, age, agues, tyrannies,
Despair, law, chance hath slain, and you whose eyes
Shall behold God and never taste death's woe.
But let them sleep, Lord, and me mourn a space;
For, if above all these my sins abound,
'Tis late to ask abundance of thy grace
When we are there. Here on this lowly ground,
Teach me how to repent; for that's as good
As if thou hadst sealed my pardon with thy blood.

Public domain. Published posthumously 1633.

Analysis

Form and Structure

A Petrarchan sonnet in iambic pentameter, the octave describing the universal resurrection, the sestet pivoting to the speaker's personal need for more time to repent.

Themes

The terrifying grandeur of the Last Judgment and the speaker's anxious awareness of his own unreadiness for it. The sonnet swings from cosmic scale to personal need in the turn.

Historical Context

Part of Donne's Holy Sonnets (c.1609-1617), published posthumously in 1633.

About John Donne

John Donne was born in 1572 in London, England, and became the leading figure among the Metaphysical poets, a group known for intellectual wit, elaborate conceits, and the fusion of intense feeling with rigorous argument. After an early career writing love poetry, Donne was ordained as an Anglican priest in 1615 and eventually became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral in London, where he was renowned for his sermons. Much of his poetry, including his Holy Sonnets, circulated only in manuscript during his lifetime and was first published after his death in 1631.
