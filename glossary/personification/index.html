Figurative Language

Personification

A figure of speech in which a non-human thing — an object, animal, or abstract idea — is given human qualities, actions, or feelings.

In Plain English

Personification works by mapping human behavior onto something that cannot literally act or feel, which makes abstract or impersonal subjects easier to relate to emotionally. A poem that says the wind 'whispered' or grief 'visited' is using personification: wind cannot whisper and grief cannot knock on a door, but describing them this way makes the experience vivid and immediate for the reader.

Examples

Emily Dickinson personifies Death throughout "Because I could not stop for Death," portraying him as a polite, patient gentleman caller who 'kindly stopped' for the speaker and shows her 'civility' on their carriage ride.

William Wordsworth personifies the daffodils in "I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud," describing them as 'tossing their heads in sprightly dance,' giving the flowers human movement and apparent joy.
