Sound and Structure

Slant Rhyme

A rhyme in which the final sounds of two words are similar but not identical, also called near rhyme or imperfect rhyme.

In Plain English

Unlike a perfect rhyme (where the ending sounds match exactly, as in 'cat' and 'hat'), a slant rhyme only partially matches the sound, as in 'soul' and 'all,' or 'Ring' and 'Sun' (matching only on a single consonant or vowel sound). Slant rhyme creates a subtler, less musical effect than perfect rhyme, and was used distinctively by certain poets to create tension or unease rather than resolution.

Examples

Emily Dickinson relied heavily on slant rhyme throughout her work, including in "Because I could not stop for Death," where end words like 'Sun' and 'Ground,' or 'Tulle' and 'Ground,' chime without fully matching, giving the poem an unsettled, suspended quality appropriate to its subject.
