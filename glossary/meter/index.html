Sound and Structure

Meter

The rhythmic pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables in a line of poetry.

In Plain English

Meter is measured in units called feet, each made up of a specific pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables (an iamb, for example, is one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable, as in the word 'be-TRAY'). A line's meter is named by combining the type of foot with how many feet it contains per line — for example, 'iambic tetrameter' means four iambic feet per line. Meter gives a poem its underlying rhythm, the way a time signature underlies a piece of music.

Examples

Emily Dickinson wrote in a ballad-like common meter, alternating lines of eight and six syllables, as seen throughout "Because I could not stop for Death."

William Wordsworth wrote "I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud" in iambic tetrameter, giving the poem a steady, walking rhythm appropriate to a poem about a country walk.
