The Tempest

William Shakespeare · Written 1610, Published 1623

Written around 1610-11 and published in the First Folio of 1623, The Tempest is probably Shakespeare's last solo play. The sorcerer Prospero, exiled with his daughter Miranda on a remote island, uses his magic and his spirit servant Ariel to bring his enemies to the island by shipwreck and engineer the marriage of Miranda and Ferdinand.

Quick Summary

The Tempest is a romance by Shakespeare, written c.1610-11. Prospero, the rightful Duke of Milan, was usurped by his brother Antonio and exiled to an island with his daughter Miranda. Using magic, he raises a tempest to bring his enemies to the island. Ferdinand, son of the King of Naples, falls in love with Miranda. Prospero forgives his enemies, breaks his staff, and prepares to return to Milan.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act romance, one of Shakespeare's late plays, observing the classical unities of time, place, and action.

Themes

Power, colonialism, and the moral questions of Prospero's authority over Caliban and Ariel; forgiveness versus revenge; art and magic as analogues for theatrical power; freedom and servitude.

Historical Context

Written c.1610-11, possibly influenced by accounts of the Sea Venture shipwreck in Bermuda (1609). Prospero's renunciation of magic is often read as Shakespeare's farewell to the stage.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

Prospero raises a storm. He tells Miranda their history: they were exiled twelve years ago. He enslaved Caliban and made Ariel his servant. Ferdinand arrives and is charmed by Miranda.

Act 2

The rest of the shipwrecked party — Alonso, Sebastian, Antonio, Gonzalo — are on the island. Antonio and Sebastian plot to kill Alonso while he sleeps. Caliban meets the drunken butler Stephano and plans a rebellion against Prospero.

Act 3

Ferdinand and Miranda declare their love and agree to marry. Caliban, Stephano, and Trinculo plot to kill Prospero. A spirit banquet appears before the court and vanishes.

Act 4

Prospero blesses the young couple with a masque of goddesses, then suddenly dismisses it, remembering Caliban's plot. Caliban's conspiracy is foiled.

Act 5

Prospero confronts his enemies and forgives them. Alonso is reunited with Ferdinand. Prospero renounces his magic, breaks his staff, and drowns his book. Caliban recognizes his foolishness. All prepare to return to Milan. Prospero delivers the epilogue asking the audience to release him with their applause.

Key Quotes

We are such stuff as dreams are made on, and our little life is rounded with a sleep.

Prospero — Act 4, Scene 1

Prospero's meditation on the insubstantiality of life and art after the masque dissolves.

O brave new world, that has such people in't!

Miranda — Act 5, Scene 1

Miranda seeing other people for the first time — her naivety is ironic, since these are Prospero's treacherous enemies.

You taught me language, and my profit on't is I know how to curse.

Caliban — Act 1, Scene 2

Caliban's bitter complaint about Prospero's colonizing education.

Be not afeard; the isle is full of noises.

Caliban — Act 3, Scene 2

Caliban's most lyrical speech — describing the music of the island.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
