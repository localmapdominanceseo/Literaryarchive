King Lear

William Shakespeare · Written 1606, Published 1608

Written around 1605-06 and published in 1608, King Lear is widely considered Shakespeare's greatest tragedy. The aging King Lear divides his kingdom among his three daughters based on their professions of love. His two eldest daughters Goneril and Regan flatter him; his youngest Cordelia speaks plainly and is disinherited. The play follows Lear's descent into madness as his older daughters strip him of power — alongside the parallel story of the Earl of Gloucester, whose bastard son Edmund betrays him.

Quick Summary

King Lear is a tragedy by Shakespeare, written c.1605-06. Lear divides his kingdom, disowning his honest daughter Cordelia and giving power to Goneril and Regan, who immediately reduce his retinue and cast him out into a storm. Lear goes mad on the heath. Gloucester is blinded by Regan's husband for helping Lear. Cordelia returns with a French army but is captured. Goneril poisons Regan and kills herself. Edmund orders Cordelia hanged. Lear arrives with her dead body and dies. Almost everyone is dead.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act blank verse tragedy with a double plot — Lear and his daughters, Gloucester and his sons — that mirror each other.

Themes

The madness and wisdom that suffering brings; parental love and ingratitude; the nature of authority and power stripped bare; blindness (literal and metaphorical); nothingness — 'Nothing will come of nothing.'

Historical Context

Written c.1605-06, based on the legend of King Leir. Shakespeare made the story much darker — the source has a happy ending. The storm scenes and Edgar's disguise as Poor Tom are Shakespeare's additions.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

Lear divides his kingdom, disowns Cordelia, and banishes Kent for defending her. Edmund deceives his father Gloucester into distrusting his legitimate son Edgar. Goneril begins reducing Lear's retinue.

Act 2

Edgar flees and disguises himself as Poor Tom, a mad beggar. Regan and Goneril unite against their father. Lear is cast out into a violent storm.

Act 3

Lear rages on the heath in the storm, accompanied by the Fool and Kent in disguise. He begins to go mad. Gloucester, trying to help Lear, is betrayed by Edmund, seized, and blinded by Cornwall.

Act 4

Edgar guides his blind father. Cordelia returns with a French army to rescue Lear. Goneril and Regan compete for Edmund. Lear and Cordelia are reunited.

Act 5

Cordelia's forces are defeated. She and Lear are captured. Edmund orders Cordelia hanged. Goneril poisons Regan and kills herself. Edgar kills Edmund in combat. Edmund's repeal order arrives too late — Cordelia is dead. Lear dies over her body.

Key Quotes

Nothing will come of nothing. Speak again.

Lear — Act 1, Scene 1

Lear to Cordelia after she refuses to compete with her sisters' flattery.

I am a man more sinned against than sinning.

Lear — Act 3, Scene 2

Lear in the storm, raging against the heavens.

As flies to wanton boys are we to the gods; they kill us for their sport.

Gloucester — Act 4, Scene 1

The blinded Gloucester expressing nihilistic despair after losing his eyes.

Ripeness is all.

Edgar — Act 5, Scene 2

Edgar's stoic acceptance — readiness for death is the most one can achieve.

Never, never, never, never, never!

Lear — Act 5, Scene 3

Lear over Cordelia's dead body — five repetitions of 'never' expressing total desolation.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
