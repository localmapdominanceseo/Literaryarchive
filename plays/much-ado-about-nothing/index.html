Much Ado About Nothing

William Shakespeare · Written 1598, Published 1600

Written around 1598-99 and published in 1600, Much Ado About Nothing is Shakespeare's wittiest comedy. Two plots interweave in Messina: the courtship of Claudio and Hero, sabotaged by the villain Don John; and the battle of wits between Benedick and Beatrice, who are tricked into admitting they love each other.

Quick Summary

Much Ado About Nothing is a comedy by Shakespeare, written c.1598-99. Claudio falls for Hero; Benedick and Beatrice trade insults but are secretly in love. Don John tricks Claudio into thinking Hero is unfaithful; Claudio publicly shames her at the altar. Hero faints and is thought dead. The plot is uncovered; Hero is cleared. Benedick and Beatrice finally admit their love. The play ends with a double marriage.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act comedy — the Beatrice and Benedick plot is the most celebrated in Shakespeare and was probably more popular with Elizabethan audiences than the main Claudio-Hero plot.

Themes

The battle of the sexes and the comedy of pride; the dangers of male honor culture that allows Claudio to publicly shame Hero; slander and reputation; noting/observing (the play is full of eavesdropping).

Historical Context

Written c.1598-99. The role of Beatrice is one of Shakespeare's greatest comic female roles.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

Don Pedro and his soldiers arrive in Messina. Claudio falls for Hero. Benedick and Beatrice resume their war of words. Don John plans mischief.

Act 2

Don Pedro woos Hero on Claudio's behalf. The gulling plots begin: friends trick Benedick into thinking Beatrice loves him, and trick Beatrice into thinking Benedick loves her.

Act 3

Don John's scheme: his man Borachio courts Margaret at Hero's window at night; Claudio and Don Pedro are made to watch, believing it is Hero. The watch overhear Borachio boasting of the trick but fail to communicate it in time.

Act 4

Claudio denounces Hero at the altar; she collapses. Friar Francis devises a plan: announce Hero dead. Benedick and Beatrice confess their love. Benedick agrees to challenge Claudio.

Act 5

Borachio's plot is uncovered. Claudio, in penance, is told he must marry Hero's 'cousin.' He agrees. The 'cousin' is Hero. Benedick and Beatrice finally stop quarrelling and agree to marry.

Key Quotes

Sigh no more, ladies, sigh no more, men were deceivers ever.

Balthasar — Act 2, Scene 3

A song sung while Benedick is tricked into thinking Beatrice loves him.

I do love nothing in the world so well as you — is not that strange?

Benedick — Act 4, Scene 1

Benedick finally confessing his love to Beatrice after Claudio has shamed Hero.

Kill Claudio.

Beatrice — Act 4, Scene 1

Beatrice's response when Benedick asks what she wants him to do to prove his love — the most startling line in the play.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
