Macbeth

William Shakespeare · Written 1606, Published 1623

Written around 1606 and published in the First Folio of 1623, Macbeth is Shakespeare's shortest tragedy and most concentrated study of ambition and its consequences. The Scottish general Macbeth is prophesied to become King of Scotland by three witches. Spurred by his wife, he murders King Duncan and seizes the throne — then spirals into tyranny and paranoia until he is killed by Macduff.

Quick Summary

Macbeth is a tragedy by Shakespeare, written c.1606. Three witches prophesy that Macbeth will be King of Scotland. Lady Macbeth pushes him to murder King Duncan while he sleeps as their guest. Macbeth takes the throne and murders Banquo to prevent his prophesied line from inheriting it. Lady Macbeth is destroyed by guilt and dies. Macbeth retreats to Dunsinane Castle. Macduff, whose family Macbeth has killed, leads an army against him. Macbeth is killed, and Malcolm becomes king.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act blank verse tragedy — the shortest of Shakespeare's major tragedies at around 2,100 lines. Features the witches, sleepwalking, hallucinations, and prophecy.

Themes

Ambition and its corrupting power; the unnaturalness of regicide; the relationship between masculinity and violence; guilt and its psychological effects; fate versus free will — do the witches determine Macbeth's fate or merely reveal it?

Historical Context

Written c.1606, possibly performed before James I (who was fascinated by witchcraft and claimed descent from Banquo). The play has a reputation for being unlucky — theatrical tradition calls it 'the Scottish play.'

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

Three witches prophesy that Macbeth will be Thane of Cawdor and then King. The first prophecy immediately comes true. Lady Macbeth reads Macbeth's letter and resolves to push him to murder King Duncan, who is coming to stay with them.

Act 2

Macbeth murders King Duncan in his sleep. He is shaken; Lady Macbeth returns the daggers to frame the guards. Duncan's sons Malcolm and Donalbain flee, making them suspects. Macbeth is named king.

Act 3

Macbeth, fearing Banquo's descendants will inherit the throne, arranges Banquo's murder. At a banquet, Banquo's ghost appears to Macbeth, causing a terrifying breakdown. The witches warn Macbeth further.

Act 4

Macbeth visits the witches again and receives three more prophecies. He orders the murder of Macduff's family. Malcolm and Macduff plan their campaign against Macbeth.

Act 5

Lady Macbeth, destroyed by guilt, sleepwalks and dies. An army marches on Dunsinane, carrying branches from Birnam Wood — fulfilling one prophecy. Macbeth is killed by Macduff — who was born by caesarean section, not 'of woman born' — fulfilling another.

Key Quotes

Is this a dagger which I see before me, the handle toward my hand?

Macbeth — Act 2, Scene 1

Macbeth's hallucination of a dagger before he goes to murder Duncan.

Out, damned spot! Out, I say!

Lady Macbeth — Act 5, Scene 1

Lady Macbeth sleepwalking, trying to wash imaginary blood from her hands.

Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow, creeps in this petty pace from day to day.

Macbeth — Act 5, Scene 5

Macbeth's nihilistic meditation on life after hearing of Lady Macbeth's death.

Double, double toil and trouble; fire burn and cauldron bubble.

The Witches — Act 4, Scene 1

The three witches stirring their cauldron and preparing their prophecies for Macbeth.

By the pricking of my thumbs, something wicked this way comes.

Second Witch — Act 4, Scene 1

A witch sensing Macbeth's approach — he has become 'something wicked.'

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
