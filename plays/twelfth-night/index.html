Twelfth Night

William Shakespeare · Written 1601, Published 1623

Written around 1601-02 and published in the First Folio of 1623, Twelfth Night is Shakespeare's most melancholy comedy. Viola, shipwrecked in Illyria, disguises herself as a boy named Cesario and serves Duke Orsino, who loves Countess Olivia. Olivia falls in love with Cesario. Orsino falls in love with Cesario. Viola's twin brother Sebastian arrives and marries Olivia. Cesario is revealed as Viola, and Orsino transfers his love to her.

Quick Summary

Twelfth Night is a comedy by Shakespeare, written c.1601-02. Viola disguises as a boy, Cesario, serving Duke Orsino. Orsino sends Cesario to woo Countess Olivia — who falls for Cesario instead. Viola falls for Orsino. Viola's twin Sebastian arrives; Olivia marries him thinking he is Cesario. Viola's identity is revealed. Orsino, realising he loves Viola, proposes to her. The subplot features the humiliation of the pompous steward Malvolio.

Analysis

Form & Structure

Five-act comedy — the cross-dressing plot, the romantic confusion, and the Malvolio subplot make it one of Shakespeare's most structurally complex comedies.

Themes

The instability of love and identity; disguise as liberation and confusion; melancholy and excess — the subtitle is 'What You Will'; social humiliation (Malvolio) as both comedy and cruelty.

Historical Context

Written c.1601-02. The play ends with Malvolio's bitter exit — 'I'll be revenged on the whole pack of you' — a darker note than most Shakespearean comedy endings.

Act-by-Act Summary

Act 1

Viola is shipwrecked and disguises as Cesario to serve Orsino. Olivia, in mourning for her brother, receives Cesario (Orsino's messenger) and falls for him.

Act 2

Viola/Cesario is in an impossible position: Olivia loves Cesario, Cesario loves Orsino. The subplot: Sir Toby and Maria forge a love letter to trick Malvolio.

Act 3

Malvolio appears before Olivia in yellow stockings and cross-garters. Sebastian arrives in Illyria with his friend Antonio. Sir Andrew challenges Cesario to a duel.

Act 4

Olivia mistakes Sebastian for Cesario and proposes to him; he accepts. Malvolio is locked up as a madman.

Act 5

The confusion is resolved when Viola and Sebastian appear together. Orsino proposes to Viola. Malvolio is released, bitter, and exits threatening revenge. Feste sings the closing song.

Key Quotes

If music be the food of love, play on, give me excess of it.

Orsino — Act 1, Scene 1

Orsino's opening lines — his love of Olivia is self-indulgent and theatrical.

She never told her love, but let concealment, like a worm i' the bud, feed on her damask cheek.

Viola — Act 2, Scene 4

Viola describing, obliquely, her own hidden love for Orsino.

Some are born great, some achieve greatness, and some have greatness thrust upon 'em.

Malvolio (reading the letter) — Act 2, Scene 5

The forged letter that tricks Malvolio into thinking Olivia loves him.

About William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare was born in April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and is widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's preeminent dramatist. Beyond his plays, he wrote 154 sonnets, most addressed to an unnamed beloved or a mysterious 'Dark Lady,' which explore love, beauty, time, and mortality with a technical mastery that established the Shakespearean sonnet form. He died in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1616.
